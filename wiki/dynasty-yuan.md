# Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368 CE)

The first dynasty to rule all of China from outside China. The Mongol Empire under Khubilai Khan was the largest land empire in human history; the Yuan was its Chinese wing. It lasted less than a century — shorter than any major dynasty — before being expelled by a peasant rebellion that founded the Ming.

---

## The Mongol conquests

The process that produced the Yuan took nearly seventy years.

**Chinggis Khan** (c. 1162–1227) united the Mongolian steppe tribes in 1206, declared himself universal ruler (*qaghan*), and immediately began expanding. He targeted the Xi Xia (Tangut state of Gansu and Ordos) and the Jurchen Jin dynasty of northern China before he died in 1227 — some say from a fall from a horse during the Xi Xia campaign, though the sources are contradictory. The Xi Xia was obliterated so thoroughly that its language was not deciphered until the 20th century.

The **Jin dynasty** (northern China's Jurchen rulers) fell in 1234, two years after Chinggis's death, under his successor Ögedei. This left the Mongols bordering the Southern Song.

The Southern Song proved far harder. It had the Yangzi River as a natural barrier, a sophisticated naval tradition, and — crucially — the inland fortress city of **Xiangfan**, which controlled the main corridor between north and south.

---

## The siege of Xiangfan (1267–1273)

Khubilai Khan, who had named his dynasty "Yuan" from the *Yi-jing* hexagram for "the creative primal force," began the systematic conquest of Song in 1267 with the siege of Xiangfan. The city guarded the Han River; taking it meant the Yangzi basin was open.

It held for **six years**.

The Song mounted relief efforts, including one by a Colonel Chang who led a hundred paddle-wheel warships and a thousand men down the Han River carrying supplies. He made it through once. The second attempt failed under a hail of fire arrows. Xiangfan's twin city across the river fell first; Xiangfan itself held.

The breakthrough came when Khubilai imported Muslim siege engineers from Persia — men who built **counterweight trebuchets** capable of hurling 150 kg projectiles with terrifying accuracy. The psychological effect of the first bombardment was apparently decisive: Xiangfan negotiated surrender in 1273.

With Xiangfan gone, the road to Hangzhou was open.

---

## The fall of Song (1276–1279)

The Southern Song capital **Hangzhou** surrendered in 1276 after negotiations — Khubilai, to his credit, allowed the city's extraordinary civilization to continue intact. The boy emperor and his mother were taken north with dignity.

Two loyalist officials, **Lu Xiufu** and **Zhang Shijie**, continued resistance with a rump court moving by ship down the coast. The Song claimant died; they proclaimed a new emperor — the same seven-year-old boy at the center of the previous narrative. A year later, off the Pearl River Delta near modern Hong Kong, the Mongol admiral Zhang Hongfan cornered the Song fleet of 800 ships.

On 19 March 1279, as the battle was clearly lost, Lu Xiufu strapped the seven-year-old emperor to his back and jumped into the sea. The fleet followed. Perhaps 100,000 people drowned. The Song dynasty was over.

## Khubilai Khan's China

Khubilai (r. 1260–1294) was the most Chinese-oriented of the Mongol khans — he spoke some Chinese, consulted Chinese advisors, and built a Chinese-style capital at **Dadu** (modern Beijing), which became his primary seat. The Mongol homeland's capital Karakorum was abandoned.

The Yuan administered China through explicit social hierarchy:

| Tier | Group |
|------|-------|
| 1st | Mongols |
| 2nd | *Semu ren* (Central Asians, Persians, Arabs) |
| 3rd | Northern Chinese (former Jin subjects) |
| 4th | Southern Chinese (former Song subjects) |

Han Chinese were largely excluded from senior government positions. The **examination system was suspended** for most of the Yuan period — a catastrophe for the Confucian scholar class, whose entire path to status and power ran through it.

The unintended cultural consequence was a flowering of **drama**. Literate men who couldn't pursue official careers wrote plays instead. The Yuan *zaju* theatrical tradition produced over 150 extant works, including what are considered the masterpieces of Chinese drama. Art and scholarship continued; they just moved sideways.

## Marco Polo

The Venetian merchant **Marco Polo** visited Khubilai Khan's court in the 1270s–80s and spent seventeen years in China. His account described a civilization of staggering wealth: Hangzhou ("the greatest city in the world"), the Grand Canal, the paper money system (incomprehensible to Europeans), the coal that people burned in their fireplaces (also incomprehensible). When he returned to Venice and dictated his account, nobody believed him. His nickname was "Marco Millions" — the man who always exaggerated.

Historians still debate which parts of the *Travels* are firsthand and which were added by his ghostwriter. He never mentions tea, foot-binding, or the Great Wall. But much of what he describes has been confirmed by Chinese sources.

## The fall (1368)

The Yuan's collapse came from the combination of natural disaster and misgovernment that had ended most Chinese dynasties. Floods, famines, epidemic disease, and crushing taxation produced the now-familiar pattern: peasant rebellion, messianic religious movements, regional fragmentation. 

The founder of the Ming, **Zhu Yuanzhang**, was a beggar-orphan who had survived a plague that killed his entire family. He joined a Red Turban rebellion, rose through the ranks by military talent, and eventually expelled the last Yuan emperor to Mongolia in 1368 — where a "Northern Yuan" continued to claim legitimacy for decades.

---

## Legacy

The Yuan's short duration conceals its long effects:

- **Beijing as capital**: Khubilai's choice of Dadu as capital established Beijing's centrality. The Ming rebuilt it; the Qing inherited it; it remains China's capital today.
- **The Grand Canal extended**: Khubilai rebuilt and extended the Grand Canal to run directly to Dadu, bypassing the old route; this alignment survives.
- **Drama**: the Yuan dramatic tradition was foundational for later Chinese opera and theater.
- **Pax Mongolica**: under Mongol domination, the overland routes between China and Europe were safer than at any previous time. This enabled the Black Death's transmission west in the 1340s — and also the flow of ideas, goods, and people that may have contributed to the European Renaissance.
