# *Fengjian* 封建 — The Zhou Enfeoffment System

**Period:** Western Zhou, c. 1045–771 BCE
**Often mistranslated as:** "Feudalism"
**Li Feng's term:** "Settlement-based state founded on delegatory rules"

---

## What it was

After conquering the Shang, the Zhou faced a stark problem: how to govern a territory far larger than anything previously administered in China. Their solution was to dispatch trusted relatives and commanders to key strategic points throughout the eastern plain, granting each the resources to establish a self-governing regional state (*guo* 国).

The practice — *fengjian* 封建, literally "to seal/mark off and establish" — produced about 26 major states founded by brothers of Kings Wu and Cheng, or by sons of the [[figure-duke-of-zhou]], spread across the old Shang heartland and its peripheries. These were not placed randomly: states were established at former Shang strongholds, at key transportation nodes, and at the approaches to potentially hostile regions. They formed a defensive web as much as an administrative one. The major states that would later compete in the Warring States period — Qi 齐, Lu 鲁, Jin 晋, Yan 燕, Chu 楚, Wei 卫, Song 宋 — were all products of this founding moment.

---

## What a founding looked like

A bronze inscription — the Yihou Ze *gui* 宜侯夨簋, excavated in the 1950s — records an actual founding ceremony in detail. The king inspects a map of former Shang campaigns, then announces the grant:

> "Transfer and be the ruler (*hou*) at Yi. I award you X-fragrant wine of one *yu*-jar, and award *zan*-jade, one red-lacquered bow and one hundred red-lacquered arrows; ten black bows and one thousand black arrows. I award you land, the *zhen*-fields... the residential settlements of which are thirty-five... and seven clans of the king's men at Yi, and the seven Elders (*bo*) of Zheng... and fifty men."

Land, people, weapons, jades, wine — everything needed to constitute and run a state. The regional ruler received full autonomy over domestic matters in return for participating in military campaigns when summoned, sending tribute, and visiting the Zhou capital periodically.

## Why it is NOT feudalism

For most of the twentieth century, Western and Chinese scholars alike described the Zhou system as "feudalism" — a cluster of proto-independent entities loosely bound by contracted lord-vassal obligations. Li Feng published a 2003 article in the *Harvard Journal of Asiatic Studies* arguing this is a fundamental misreading.

The key difference lies in the *basis* of obligation:

| Western feudalism | Zhou *fengjian* |
|---|---|
| Based on contract between lord and vassal | Based on kinship obligation to a primary lineage |
| Vassal owes lord military service by agreement | Regional ruler owes king because of genealogical position |
| Lord's domain is limited by the terms of contract | Zhou king regarded the whole territory as his responsibility |
| No inherent hierarchy beyond the contractual | Primary lineage (royal) has absolute genealogical precedence |

Regional rulers obeyed the Zhou king not because they had contracted to do so, but because lineage logic required it: the Zhou king was head of the primary lineage from which all other lineages branched. To defy him was to defy the same genealogical order that gave your own title its legitimacy.

The Zhou also stationed royal inspectors in regional states and retained the right to intervene in succession disputes — practices incompatible with genuine feudal autonomy.

## The state as a web of settlements

Li Feng describes the Western Zhou as a "settlement-based state": defined not by territory (lines on a map) but by the clusters of settlements (*yi* 邑) it controlled. There were empty spaces within what the Zhou considered their domain, and even overlaps between neighboring states. It was a web of nodes, not a continuous landmass.

The regional states did not exist as defined geographic territories but as networks of settlements, each with a political center surrounded by affiliated villages and farms. The state existed wherever the settlements did.

---

## The built-in weakness

The system contained its own slow-motion collapse. Genealogical distance between the regional rulers and the Zhou king grew with every generation. The bonds of obligation weakened correspondingly. Regional rulers developed local identities, economic interests, and political ambitions that had nothing to do with the Wei River valley court.

The bronze inscriptions show the king increasingly unable to grant large parcels of land (it had all been distributed) and forced to hand out scattered fields piecemeal. Land disputes and even theft of land products were being filed at the Zhou court and recorded on bronzes. By the mid-Western Zhou period, competition for land resources had become intense and the centrifugal forces were already visible.

By the Spring and Autumn period (after 771 BCE), the Zhou king had become a ceremonial figure. The states the *fengjian* system created had become effectively sovereign — and would eventually tear each other apart in the Warring States.
