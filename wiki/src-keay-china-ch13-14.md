# Keay, *China: A History* — Chapters 13–14

**Source**: John Keay, *China: A History* (2008)  
**Chapters**: 13 ("The Rites of Ming," 1405–1620), 14 ("The Manchu Conquest," 1620–1760)  
**Coverage**: Late Ming through the early Qing "Three Emperors"

---

## What this source covers

Chapter 13 covers the Yongle Emperor's founding of Beijing and the Forbidden City, Zheng He's seven voyages (1405–1433) and their abandonment, the Vietnam debacle, the Tumu Incident (1449, emperor captured by Mongols), the Great Rites Controversy (1521–24), Ming population growth (85m to 275m), the construction of the Great Wall as we know it, European contact (Portuguese at Macao, Matteo Ricci and the Jesuits), the silver-based economy, and the Wanli Emperor's decades-long withdrawal from government.

Chapter 14 covers the fall of the Ming — Li Zicheng's rebellion, the last emperor's suicide on Coal Hill, Wu Sangui opening the Shanhaiguan gate, the Manchu occupation of the Forbidden City; the bloody pacification of southern China, the Yangzhou massacre, the "queue order" and its resistance; Zheng Chenggong (Coxinga)'s loyalist sea empire and Taiwan; the Qing "Three Emperors" (Kangxi, Yongzheng, Qianlong); the Treaty of Nerchinsk with Russia; and the Zunghar genocide — the deliberate extermination of 600,000 people in Xinjiang.

---

## What's most interesting

**The abandonment of Zheng He's voyages** — the most discussed "what if" in Chinese history. Keay's explanation is structural, not conspiratorial: the voyages were ritual-tributary exercises, not commercial or exploratory in the European sense. When the Vietnam war ended and the strategic rationale evaporated, there was nothing left to justify the enormous expense. "More money, power, and ceremony applied in the same old way was simply bound to fail."

**The Tumu Incident (1449)** — the emperor surrounded at a post-station, 30,000 cavalry destroyed in an ambush, the guards all dead, the emperor still sitting on the ground when a Mongol prince noticed he was "someone special" and led him into captivity. No precedent in Chinese history for Heaven's Son as a hostage. Beijing's court simply elected a brother as emperor and defended the city without him.

**The Great Rites Controversy (1521–24)** — a fifteen-year-old prince insisted on honoring his natural parents over his adopted imperial lineage; two hundred senior officials knelt at the throne-room door chanting the names of great Ming emperors; the Jiajing Emperor had them beaten with rods; nineteen died under the flogging. About as much as ritual propriety could bear.

**The last Ming emperor** — sometime after midnight on 25 April 1644, the Chongzhen Emperor climbed Coal Hill with only an old eunuch, surveyed the fires in the suburbs, retired to the Hat and Girdle Department, and hanged himself from a crossbeam. Li Zicheng's forces found the gates unlocked.

**The Yangzhou massacre** — Shi Kefa defending Yangzhou with unimpeachable loyalty; the walls too narrow for the guns; the platforms collapsing; survivors describing people "falling like leaves." Ten-day sack, most notorious in the Qing conquest.

**Zheng Chenggong (Coxinga)** — half-Japanese, born in Nagasaki, educated in Nanjing; controlled the entire coastal sea-lane from Guangdong to Zhejiang; nearly retook Nanjing; expelled the Dutch from Taiwan; died in his thirties convulsed with grief when news arrived that Wu Sangui had had the last Ming pretender strangled. Later worshipped simultaneously by the KMT, the communists, and the Japanese.

**The Zunghar genocide** — the Qianlong Emperor's deliberate extermination of 600,000 Zunghars in Xinjiang: 40% died of smallpox (introduced as a weapon), 30% killed by Qing armies, 20% fled. "Zungharia was left as a blank social space." One authority does not hesitate to call it "the final solution." Replaced by state-sponsored Han, Manchu, and Hui settlement.

## New information vs. existing wiki

- First full Ming treatment: Yongle, Zheng He, Tumu, Great Rites, Wang Yangming's political applications, Wanli's collapse
- First detailed treatment of the Ming-Qing transition: Li Zicheng, Wu Sangui, the Yangzhou massacre, queue order, Southern Ming resistance
- First full coverage of Coxinga/Zheng Chenggong and Taiwan's early history
- First substantial Qing material: the "Three Emperors," Nerchinsk treaty, Zunghar genocide, Tibet relationship
