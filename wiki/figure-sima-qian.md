# Sima Qian — Grand Historian of China

**Born:** c. 145 BC
**Died:** c. 86 BC
**Title:** Grand Astrologer (a role encompassing history, astronomy, and divination); author of the *Shiji*
**Period:** Western Han, reign of [[figure-han-wudi|Han Wudi]]

---

## The work

The *Shiji* 史记 ("Records of the Grand Historian") is one of the most ambitious historical works ever written. 130 chapters spanning 3,000 years — from the mythical Five Emperors through the reign of Han Wudi. It was the first of China's 24 "Standard Histories" and served as their template. No other Standard History covers more than a single dynasty; the *Shiji* covers everything that came before.

Its structure is unusual and demanding. Twelve chapters of "Basic Annals" provide a chronological framework. Thirty chapters on "Hereditary Houses" cover the states and kingdoms. Seventy biographical chapters cover notable individuals. To understand any given moment fully, you need all three — it's like piecing together a play by reading each actor's lines separately.

This fragmented approach wasn't accidental. Sima Qian was not simply recording events; he was creating what one scholar calls "a rival to the First Emperor's tomb" — a microcosm of the known world inscribed in bamboo, regulated by scholarship and morality rather than bronze and force. Where the First Emperor sought to control the past by burning books, Sima Qian sought to preserve it by writing the one book that mattered.

## The castration

In 99 BC, during a campaign against the Xiongnu, a general named Li Ling fought on with 5,000 infantry against impossible odds until not one arrow remained — then surrendered. The court condemned him. Sima Qian, who knew Li Ling and respected his courage, interceded with the emperor on his behalf. He meant only "to broaden His Majesty's views."

The emperor did not see it that way. Sima Qian was accused of trying to exonerate Li Ling in order to disparage the great General Li Guangli — which in turn was construed as an attempt to deceive the emperor, a capital offence.

An official facing such a charge was expected to commit suicide: honor partially redeemed, the emperor unimplicated, the Confucian ideal of self-correction satisfied. Alternatively, commutation could be purchased — at a price Sima Qian could not afford.

There was a third option. Worse than execution, more humiliating than suicide, too shameful to name directly in the long letter he wrote explaining his decision. Judicial castration. "The punishment of rottenness."

He chose it. The *Shiji* was not finished.

## The letter

In a letter to a friend, Sima Qian explained what he had done and why. It is one of the most remarkable documents in Chinese literature:

> "How can I, his son, dare to neglect his will?" (referring to his father, who had begun the *Shiji* and charged him with completing it)

> "When I have truly completed this work, I shall deposit it in the Famous Mountain. If it may be handed down to men who will appreciate it, and penetrate to the villages and the great cities, then though I should suffer a thousand mutilations, what regret should I have?"

Filial piety — the Confucian obligation to complete what his father had started — and personal conviction kept him alive. The physical shame, the public humiliation, the contempt of his contemporaries, he accepted. The work mattered more.

## The bias question

Sima Qian wrote under the Han dynasty, which had every reason to demonize Qin. He wrote about Wudi, the emperor who had castrated him, which gave him every reason to insert coded criticisms. He was well aware of both problems and partly transparent about them.

On the First Emperor: he quoted a long diatribe condemning him, but then added his own view — "Qin's seizure of power was accompanied by much violence, yet it did manage to change with the times and its accomplishments were great." On Xiang Yu, who lost the civil war to Liu Bang's Han: Sima Qian's admiration for the general bursts through on almost every page, despite the political need to uphold the Han founder.

He also included enough innuendo about Wudi's failings to alert posterity that his official account of the reign should be read critically. For a historian who had been castrated by his subject, this is remarkable restraint and remarkable courage.

---

## The *Shiji* as a model of the world

One scholar has argued that the *Shiji* was structured as a deliberate counterpart to the First Emperor's tomb: where the tomb contained a physical model of the empire (mercury rivers, constellations of pearls, crossbow booby traps), the *Shiji* contained a textual model — rivers and waterways described, provinces catalogued, officials biographied, the heavens and their influence tracked. "If Sima's creation could not match the First Emperor's in political power, it far surpassed it in influence."

The *Shiji*'s model of the world proved more durable. The tomb is still sealed. The *Shiji* has been read continuously for 2,100 years.
