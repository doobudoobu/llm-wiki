# Oracle Bones (*Jiaguwen* 甲骨文)

**What they are:** Cattle shoulder blades and turtle belly shells used by the Shang dynasty for divination, inscribed with the earliest confirmed Chinese writing
**Where found:** Primarily Anyang, Henan province (the late Shang capital)
**Discovery:** 1899, traced from a Beijing pharmacy where they were being ground up as medicine for malaria

---

## The discovery story

In 1899 a Beijing pharmacist was selling "dragon bones" — powdered animal remains used in traditional medicine to treat fever. A scholar named Wang Yirong was buying some when he noticed scratched marks on the bones that looked like archaic writing. His brother happened to be a noted expert in early Chinese scripts. The marks, it turned out, were real: the brothers had stumbled onto fragments of the Shang dynasty's royal divination archive, more than three thousand years old.

Word spread. Collectors — many of them foreign — found a surprising number of the bones for sale in Beijing's antique markets. The supply seemed inexhaustible. So many fakes were produced that one historian estimated "a hundred forgeries for every genuine piece." Collections of bones "not one of which was genuine" were selling for hundreds of dollars.

Eventually the bones were traced to villagers near Anyang. Excavations began in the late 1920s and have continued, with interruptions for wars and revolutions, ever since. More than 100,000 fragments — constituting roughly 7,000 scapulas and plastrons — have been recovered. Over a quarter came from a single underground storage deposit, suggesting deliberate archival preservation.

## Physical preparation

The materials used were of two kinds: cattle scapulae (shoulder blades), and turtle plastrons (the flat bottom shell). The use of bones for divination goes back to the Longshan period; the earliest divinatory use of turtle shells is found at the Jiahu site, dating to the pre-Yangshao period (*c.* 6500–5500 BC). By the late Shang, cattle bones were drawn largely from local sources, but a large proportion of the turtle shells found at Anyang were imported from far-outlying regions in the south.

Before use, both materials required careful preparation. For scapulae, the round joint was cut off and the long spine on the back trimmed. For plastrons, the bridges connecting the top shell to the bottom were carefully cut away. In both cases, oval holes intercepted by a round hole were carved on the back of the bone or shell — a row of these pre-cut receptors arranged in sequence, ready to receive heat.

## The divination process

A complete oracle-bone inscription in its fully stated form had four parts: **preface, charge, prognostication, and verification**.

1. **Preface:** date and diviner's name
2. **Charge:** the matter to be divined, posed as a statement ("This year Shang will receive harvest")
3. **Prognostication:** the king's reading of the crack
4. **Verification:** what actually happened

The actual procedure: a metal stick was heated to a high degree in fire; when it touched the joint of the oval and round holes, smoke would rise and produce a crack in the shape of the Chinese character *bu* 卜 ("to divine" — the character literally depicts a cracked bone) on the front of the bone or shell. It was the king — not the diviner — who examined the crack and made the prognostication. The inscription was then carved near the relevant crack.

Charges were very often posed in complementary pairs — positive and negative — placed on opposing sides of the plastron: "Will it rain?" opposite "Will it not rain?" Multiple pairs (sometimes three or four) could be posed on the same bone for a single matter.

**Verifications are extremely rare.** Only 1.2% of inscriptions from the Bin-group diviners include a verification. The king may have preferred oral prognostication to avoid a written record of being proved wrong; or he may have kept the decision private; or — as some scholars argue — the charges were phrased not as genuine questions but as "wishes," making prognostication unnecessary.

## The corpus and its dating

There are approximately 100,000 pieces of oracle bone in collections inside and outside China. Nearly half belonged to King Wu Ding — the most powerful Shang king in Anyang — reflecting the enormous scale of his royal activities.

~120 diviner names are recorded on the bones and shells. The key to dating the corpus was worked out by Dong Zuobin: by tracking which diviner names appeared together on the same shells, and cross-referencing the temple names of Shang kings mentioned in the inscriptions, he divided the corpus into five chronological periods. This five-period scheme has been largely confirmed by subsequent excavations and is the standard framework in the field.

## The non-royal oracle bones: a secret within a secret

For most of the twentieth century, scholars assumed oracle bones were exclusively a royal institution. A discovery in 1991 — fully published in 2003 — overturned this.

Some 1,583 pieces of inscribed shells and bones were found at Huayuanzhuang-east, a location just 300 meters south of the palace zone in Anyang. These were not royal divination records. The protagonist who appears most frequently on the Huayuanzhuang-east shells was a "Prince" (*zi*) — most scholars identify him as a brother of King Wu Ding. He even divined on his own shells on as many as twenty-six occasions — something the Shang king would never do.

The Huayuanzhuang-east bones differed from royal bones in striking ways:
- Inscriptions were laid out irregularly, often going around the crack rather than beside it
- They used time-of-day markers (*ze* 昃 "late afternoon," *xi* 夕 "evening") not found in royal bones
- They frequently noted whether a bone was "used" or "not used"
- Fourteen non-royal diviners produced records for the prince

The most striking implication: **divination was a highly secret proceeding**. The diviners producing these shells 300 meters from the palace almost certainly had never been exposed to the standards and vocabulary of the royal divinatory institution. An entirely parallel tradition operated in the shadow of the royal one, apparently without any knowledge of it.

## What they asked

The subjects of divination range from the cosmic to the personal. Common charges:

- **Weather:** "Will it rain this ten-day period?" (often paired: "Will it not rain?")
- **Harvest:** "This year Shang will receive harvest" — asked four times, for each of the Four Lands (east, west, south, north) surrounding the Central Shang
- **Military campaigns:** "Should we attack the Gong Fang?" / "Will the servitors be captured?"
- **Royal health:** illness, toothache, shoulder pain
- **Ritual:** whether to perform a sacrifice, which type, to which ancestor
- **Childbirth:** Lady Hao's pregnancy is recorded in a famous sequence — Wu Ding asked whether the birth would be auspicious; the response was ambiguous; "after 31 days, she gave birth and it was not good; it was a girl"

The example below shows a verified inscription where the king was proven correct and one where he was not:

> *Crack-making on the jimao day, Que divined: "Will it rain?" The king prognosticated: "If it will rain, it will be on a ren day." On the renwu day, it really did rain.*

> *Crack-making on the guiyou day, Huan divined: "Will the servitors be captured?" The king prognosticated: "If they will be captured, it will be on a jia day or a yi day." On the jiaxu day, the servitors crossed the river in boats and went to Zou. They were not reported. After ten and five days, on the dinghai day, they were shackled.* [The king was wrong about the day.]

## The writing system

About 4,000 individual characters of "Archaic Chinese" script have been isolated from the oracle-bone inscriptions. Around half have been translated or identified with varying certainty.

The oracle-bone scripts reflect a language that was characteristically **Sinitic** — an archaic form of the single-syllable languages spoken in various regional dialectal forms across modern China. The scripts are hand-carved and display a higher degree of freedom than the inscriptions on bronze vessels. It is not unusual for a modern reader familiar with traditional Chinese characters to make out the meaning of a few graphs.

There is a genuine scholarly debate about whether the system emerged suddenly (structured from the beginning by consistent rules, achievable in "a relatively short period of time, perhaps over a few generations") or gradually (implying centuries of unrecovered precursor stages). A recent discovery of multiple graphs written on pottery jars from a middle Shang sacrificial site near the Yellow River suggests writing was in use before the Anyang period.

On the oracle bones, learning to write appears to have taken place within **individual scribal workshops** that served the Shang king specifically to produce and preserve divinatory records.

## Shang religion through the bones

The most important topic addressed in the oracle-bone inscriptions was **royal sacrifice** — the central religious and political act of the Shang king.

### The High God (*Shangdi* / *di*)

The Shang supreme deity was "High God" — *Shangdi* 上帝 or simply *di* 帝. He had power over both natural and human phenomena: rainfall, harvest, military outcomes. Crucially, no sacrificial offerings were ever made to *di* directly — unlike the ancestor spirits — suggesting a fundamental distinction between the two types of supernatural authority.

Recent scholarship has proposed a striking identification: the oracle-bone character *di* 帝 appears to have been derived from the star formation around the celestial Northern Pole, the hollow center around which all constellations revolve. If this theory holds — and it has been argued independently by two recent scholars — then the Shang High God was literally the pivot of the sky. By the end of the dynasty, at least two kings had assumed this sacred title for themselves: **Di Yi** 帝乙 (King 28) and **Di Xin** 帝辛 (King 29), the last Shang king.

### Natural deities and the Four Winds

Below *di* in the Shang pantheon were natural deities: Earth, River (the Yellow River to the east of Anyang), and Mountain (the Taihang Mountain to the west). These three received standard offerings of cattle, sheep, and pigs alongside the wood-burning ritual.

The Shang also named and worshipped the four directional winds, associating each with a particular direction and asking them for good harvests. To the Shang, the landscape was not merely a physical setting — it was inhabited by spirits whose cooperation had to be maintained.

### The five sacrifices and the sacrificial circle

The most regularly maintained cult was directed at the **Shang royal ancestors**, who received five different types of sacrifice (*yi* 彡, *ji* 祭, *zai* 再, *xie* 燮, *yong* 肜) on a rotating schedule throughout the year. Beginning with the pre-dynastic ancestor Shang Jia (P1) and working through the full king list, each ancestor received each type of sacrifice in turn. When all five types had been applied to all ancestors, one full "sacrificial circle" — one year — had been completed.

The oracle bones record consecutive divinations about this schedule:

> *Crack-making on the bingyin day, the king divined: "On the next day, dingmao, when the king hosts Da Ding (King 2) and performs the zai sacrifice (type 3), will there be no harm?" ... [same question repeated for King 3, King 7, King 12, King 13]*

These five consecutive records appear on the same piece of bone from bottom to top, spanning a period of forty-four days.

### Human sacrifice

War prisoners from the **Qiang Fang** — a people located to the west of Shang, probably in western Shaanxi and eastern Gansu — were among the most common human offerings. The scale was not small:

> *Offering to Da Ding, Da Jia, Zu Yi: hundred cups of wine, hundred Qiang prisoners, and three hundred cattle, three hundred sheep, and three hundred pigs.*

More than 2,000 sacrificial pits containing the skeletons of human victims have been found at Xibeigang, around the royal tombs to the north of the Huan River. Animal carcasses used in sacrifice were likely consumed by the Shang elite afterward — their bones recycled to the bone workshops. Human victims did not receive this treatment; they went directly to the burial pits.

## The Shang royal lineage

The oracle bones allow scholars to reconstruct the Shang succession rules in surprising detail.

Before the dynasty was founded, the Shang appear to have practiced simple father-to-son succession. After Tang/Da Yi (King 1) established the Shang dynasty, a new rule emerged: the throne was passed **among brothers in the same generation**, then given back to the oldest son of the oldest brother for the next generation. The likely rationale was practical — ensuring an adult always held the throne.

This system held for roughly twelve generations. But the oracle bones reveal three moments where it broke down, each coinciding with a major political change:

- **Zu Yi (King 12)** refused to return the throne to his brother Jian Jia's son, passing it to his own son Zu Xin (King 13). This corresponded with the transfer of the Shang capital from Xiang to Xing.
- **Wu Ding (King 21)** succeeded his father Xiao Yi (King 20), bypassing the brother rotation entirely. Wu Ding was the most powerful king of late Shang; new evidence suggests he moved the capital to south of the Huan River.
- **Wu Yi (King 26)** and his successors all passed the throne directly to their sons — establishing, by repeated violation of the older rule rather than by decree, the **primogeniture** that most later Chinese dynasties would follow.

Each Shang king received a name from the ten Heavenly Stems (*jia* 甲, *yi* 乙, *bing* 丙, *ding* 丁, *wu* 戊, *ji* 己, *geng* 庚, *xin* 辛, *ren* 壬, *gui* 癸). One influential theory (K.C. Chang) holds that the Shang royal lineage was organized into two exogamous groups — "Yi-group" 乙组 and "Ding-group" 丁组 — that alternated the kingship, with kings named according to which group they were born into. This would explain regularities in the distribution of stem names across the king list.

## The Shang government as seen through the bones

The oracle-bone inscriptions reveal a government of striking **rudimentary character**. The Shang king showed little interest in establishing differentiated administrative offices. The bones mention "many officers," "many horses," and "many captains," but these appear to be status descriptors rather than formal titles — there is no way to determine superior-subordinate relations among the people referred to.

By the very end of Anyang, two administrative roles do appear: "Document Maker" and "Superintendent" (possibly a palace official). But specific administrative offices had not been differentiated before this late stage. The Shang government was essentially the king and his diviners — with the diviners serving as personal attendants who occasionally handled administrative matters brought to court.

This "peripatetic" government suited a peripatetic king. The Shang king spent many days each year on hunting trips away from the capital — hunting that was simultaneously military display, political strategy, and ritual performance.

## The Shang state beyond Anyang

The oracle bones reveal that the Shang conceived their territory as the **"Four Lands" (*situ* 四土)** — eastern, western, southern, and northern — surrounding the Central Shang at Anyang. Local leaders recognized Shang supremacy through the title "Archer-Lord" (*hou* 侯) or "Prince" (*zi* 子). The political relationship was maintained not by permanent administration but by the Shang king's continuous display of military might through hunting campaigns and punitive expeditions.

The Shang state had **no permanent perimeter**. It expanded when a powerful king could bring distant groups into submission; it contracted when he couldn't. At its height under Wu Ding, the network extended to the Fen River valley in the west and possibly the Wei River valley and western Shandong. For most of the late Shang period, royal activity was concentrated in central and northern Henan.

Beyond this network were the **Fang polities** — independent foreign entities the Shang king campaigned against. The Gui Fang and Gong Fang shared the Bronze Age culture of the Lower Ordos region (northern Shaanxi/Shanxi). The Qiang Fang supplied the majority of human sacrifice victims. The Hu Fang ("Polity of Tiger") may have been located south of the Yangzi in modern Jiangxi and Hunan. The Ren Fang appears in the archaeology of southwestern Shandong.

## Legacy

The oracle bones connected the worlds of archaeology, linguistics, and history in a single find. They:

- Authenticated the Shang dynasty, which had previously been as mythologically suspect as the Xia
- Established the antiquity and continuity of the Chinese script
- Revealed the structure of Shang religion, court life, political geography, and royal succession
- Provided dated biographical details (names of kings, consorts, enemies) cross-referenceable with later textual tradition
- Demonstrated that writing in China may have emerged initially in a **religious context** — in service of communicating with spirits — rather than the economic/administrative contexts that drove writing in Mesopotamia and Egypt

They are also a reminder that the foundations of Chinese civilization were built on a practice that looks, to modern eyes, like magic: systematically heating bones and reading the cracks.
