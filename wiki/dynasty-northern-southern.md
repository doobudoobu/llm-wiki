# Period of Disunion and Northern/Southern Dynasties (304–589 CE)

For nearly three centuries, China had two parallel civilizations — a Han Chinese court culture in the south and non-Han (primarily Xianbei) states in the north. Each called the other barbarians; each claimed to be the real China. The period ended when the Sui reunified the country in 589, and what emerged from this crucible of chaos was the cosmopolitan Tang.

| Region | Major States | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| North | 16 Kingdoms, then Northern Wei, Eastern Wei, Western Wei, Northern Qi, Northern Zhou | Dominated by non-Han rulers; massive Sinicization under Northern Wei |
| South | Eastern Jin, then Liu Song, Southern Qi, Liang, Chen (the "Six Dynasties") | Han Chinese courts at Jiankang (Nanjing); literary and Buddhist flowering |

## Why Keay calls it *not* dark ages

Historians used to dismiss the Period of Disunion as China's equivalent of the European Dark Ages — Rome falling to barbarians, political fragmentation, cultural regression. Keay argues forcefully against this. The period was, in his phrase, "illumined by dazzling literary achievements, by the emergence of Daoism as an indigenous counterpoint to Confucian orthodoxy, and by the adoption of Buddhism, the greatest religio-cultural import in the history of pre-modern China."

The parallel with the Warring States period is apt: endemic strife had earlier produced Confucius, Mozi, Laozi, and the hundred schools of thought. Once again, instability catalyzed creativity. The Tang's golden age would have been impossible without what was discovered and synthesized during these centuries of division.

## The north: alien rulers and a great experiment

The **Northern Wei** (386–534), founded by the Tabgach/Tuoba clan of the Xianbei, was by far the most important northern dynasty. By 439 they had reunited all China north of the Huai River, ruling from Pingcheng (modern Datong).

Their fundamental problem was the same as every non-Han dynasty's: how to retain the loyalty of a steppe warrior class while governing a population that was overwhelmingly Han Chinese. The Northern Wei's first answer was essentially segregation — the *guoren* ("compatriots") remained a ruling class with their own ranks, privileges, language, and monopoly on military command; Han Chinese provided the tax base and civilian administration.

### The equal-field system

Their most consequential innovation was agrarian. To get depopulated land back into cultivation — the north had lost perhaps 30% of its population during the centuries of war — the Northern Wei introduced the **equal-field system** (*juntian*) in the 470s. Each household was entitled to a fixed allocation of farmland plus mulberry groves for silkworms. The mulberry land was heritable; the farmland reverted to the state on the farmer's death.

The system was designed to make it worth farmers' while to stay put rather than flee. It worked well enough that the Sui adopted it unchanged, and the Tang kept it until the mid-8th century — at which point "Chinese attempts at state regulation of land finally came to an end and were not effectively renewed until the communism of the twentieth century" (Keay). One of the longest-running land policies in history.

### Dowager Empress Feng and the great reversal

The transformative moment came in the 470s–490s when the formidable **Dowager Empress Feng** gradually took control of the Northern Wei government and reversed course entirely. Instead of preserving Xianbei identity, she and her step-grandson Emperor Xiaowen embarked on aggressive Sinicization: Xianbei nobles were ordered to abandon their language, dress, and customs; intermarriage with Han Chinese families was encouraged; the capital was moved from Pingcheng — "a place on the verge of the Mongolian steppe, difficult to supply with grain" — to a rebuilt Luoyang, steeped in Han imperial associations.

The move to Luoyang was made by sleight of hand. Emperor Xiaowen led an army south as if to attack the Southern Qi, then halted near Luoyang in pouring rain. When his troops pleaded to abandon the campaign, he announced that very well, they could stay where they were and begin rebuilding the capital. The new Luoyang rose in nine years: 18 square kilometers, 1,300 monasteries, over half a million inhabitants.

### The problem with sinicization

A new class of mixed Han-Xianbei landowners emerged from this process. These hybrid elites — military men turned Confucian bureaucrats, steppe warriors turned farmers — would provide most of the leading figures of the 6th and 7th centuries, including the founders of the Sui and Tang. The Tang's Li family was a direct product of this northern frontier crucible.

But the frontier Xianbei garrisons resented being told to become Chinese. In 534 they rebelled, the Northern Wei split, and Luoyang was abandoned. By 535 the city was a ghost town again. From the wreckage emerged the **Northern Zhou** dynasty based in Chang'an — the state whose institutional innovations made Sui and Tang possible.

## The south: refinement and stagnation

The southern courts at Jiankang maintained elaborate Confucian ritual, produced great poets and calligraphers, and preserved the literary tradition. The aristocratic clans who had fled south after Luoyang's fall in 311 dominated cultural life; standards of scholarship were zealously maintained against the day — which never came — when the exiles would return north.

Culturally the south was spectacular. Daoist and Buddhist philosophy intertwined in sophisticated court debate. But politically the Six Dynasties were fragile — each overthrown by a military commander who then started a new dynasty. The Liang under Emperor Wu (r. 502–549) came closest to real stability. Wu funded hundreds of monasteries and called himself a "Buddhist Son of Heaven." His long reign ended in catastrophe when a 548 rebellion resulted in a siege of Jiankang; brocaded aristocrats, "unacquainted with the mechanics of cooking," reportedly starved to death in their lacquered apartments.

## Buddhism conquers China

This is the period when Buddhism transformed from an exotic import into the dominant religion of Chinese civilization. By 310 CE there were 180 Buddhist establishments in Luoyang alone; by 510 the rebuilt Luoyang had 1,300 monasteries. The cave temples at **Yungang** (near Datong) and **Longmen** (near Luoyang), carved by tens of thousands of laborers, remain among the greatest Buddhist monuments in Asia.

Non-Han rulers found Buddhism genuinely useful: it offered legitimacy without requiring Chinese ancestry. "Buddha, being a foreign god, is the very one we should worship," as the psychopathic Shi Hu of the Later Zhao put it with unusual clarity. Mahayana Buddhism also offered salvation to everyone regardless of race, education, or gender — a universal message that Confucianism, with its rigidly hierarchical social vision, could not match.

## The Northern Zhou: forging the Tang's foundations

The Northern Zhou dynasty (557–581), based at Chang'an, is the least celebrated but arguably most consequential of the northern states. Under Yuwen Tai, the Northern Zhou developed the institutional framework the Sui and Tang would inherit nearly intact:

- The **fubing** system: territorial militias of volunteer soldiers exempt from taxes, rotated through palace guard duty. Backbone of Sui and Tang armies.
- Reunification of the north by 577 with forces put at 570,000.
- A brief Confucian-fundamentalist ideological experiment (archaic Zhou-dynasty revival, quickly junked by the Sui) that helped legitimize non-Han rule by claiming to restore ancient Chinese purity.

The man who overthrew the Northern Zhou — Yang Jian, who became Sui Wendi — was himself part of this northern aristocracy. He took the institutional framework the Northern Zhou had built and used it to conquer the south. See [[dynasty-sui]].

## The long arc

Looking back from the Tang, the Period of Disunion looks like three centuries of painful but necessary transformation:
- Non-Han peoples were absorbed into the Chinese cultural and ethnic mix, producing the hybrid elites of Sui and Tang.
- Buddhism was domesticated and made Chinese — no longer a foreign import but a central pillar of civilization.
- Daoism crystallized into an organized religion with canonical texts, clergy, and institutional life.
- The equal-field system gave China a workable agrarian foundation for centuries.
- The Grand Canal, examination system, and legal codes of Sui and Tang all had roots in this era.

The "dark ages" comparison misses the point. The chaos was real, the suffering immense — but what came out of it was the Tang.
