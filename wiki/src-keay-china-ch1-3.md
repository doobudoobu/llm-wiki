# Keay, *China: A History* — Chapters 1–3

**Author:** John Keay | **Published:** 2008 | **Chapters covered:** 1–3 (pre-c. 1050 BC through 210 BC)

John Keay is a British historian of Asia (*India: A History*, *The Honourable Company*). This book is a single-volume sweep from Chinese prehistory to the present. The prose is crisp and opinionated, and Keay is good at flagging where textual tradition and archaeology disagree — which turns out to be often.

---

## What these chapters cover

- **Chapter 1 — "Rites to Writing" (pre-c. 1050 BC):** Neolithic cultures, the Xia and Shang dynasties, the oracle bones, bronze-casting, and a controversy about Caucasoid mummies in Xinjiang.
- **Chapter 2 — "Sages and Heroes" (c. 1050–c. 250 BC):** The Zhou dynasty — Western Zhou, Spring & Autumn, Warring States. The Mandate of Heaven. Confucius. The rise of Qin.
- **Chapter 3 — "The First Empire" (c. 250–210 BC):** Qin's conquest of Sichuan, unification of China, Qin Shi Huangdi's standardizations, the book burning, the Great Wall, and the terracotta army.

## Most interesting and surprising details

**The oracle bones' origin story.** In 1899 a Beijing pharmacist was selling powdered "dragon bones" as a cure for malaria. A scholar-patient noticed the bones had archaic writing scratched on them — and eventually traced them to Anyang. What the pharmacist had been grinding up was the Shang royal divination archive, 3,000 years old.

**The Tarim Mummies.** In 1988 an American scholar walked into a museum in Xinjiang and found life-size, well-preserved mummies of Caucasoid people — some with Celtic-weave tartan, one wearing rainbow-coloured felt socks. DNA-certified proto-Tocharian speakers, dating to ~2000 BC. The mummies are politically explosive: Uighur separatists have used them to dispute Beijing's historical claim to Xinjiang.

**The Mandate of Heaven was likely invented by the Zhou.** The Shang's supreme deity was "Di" — "Heaven" barely appears in their oracle bones. The Duke of Zhou articulated the doctrine *after* the conquest, to explain why overthrowing the Shang was cosmically correct. It became the justification for every dynastic change for two millennia.

**The Stone Cattle Road.** To invade Sichuan, the king of Qin commissioned five life-size stone cows and spattered their backsides with gold, then let Shu's ambassadors see them. The credulous Shu king demanded the gold-dunging cattle as a gift. Qin graciously offered to build a road for them first. The road was then used for invasion.

**Sima Qian never mentioned the Terracotta Army.** China's greatest historian wrote extensively about the First Emperor's tomb — mercury rivers, crossbow booby-traps, a ceiling of stars — but not a word about the thousands of clay soldiers outside it. Some well-diggers found them in 1974.

## A central tension Keay highlights

The written texts (compiled from the 4th–3rd centuries BC onward) portray a neat linear pedigree: Xia → Shang → Zhou, each centered on the Yellow River's Central Plain. Archaeology tells a messier story: overlapping timelines, multiple contemporaneous bronze cultures across a huge area, no clear "cradle" in the north. This tension — official narrative vs. material evidence — runs through all of early Chinese history and has modern political stakes (who counts as "Chinese"?).
