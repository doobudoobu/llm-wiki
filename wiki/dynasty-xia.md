# Xia Dynasty

**Period:** c. 2100–1600 BCE (traditional dates; archaeology suggests possibly a few centuries later)
**Location:** Lower Yellow River basin — the "Central Plain" stretching across northern China
**Status:** First of the "Three Dynasties" (Xia, Shang, Zhou) — possibly historical, possibly legendary

---

## The founding myth

In Chinese tradition, the Xia are founded by Yu, last of the mythical "Five Emperors." The Five Emperors were semi-divine rulers whose conduct was so exemplary that they served more as moral archetypes than historical figures. What distinguished Yu from his predecessors was that he broke a tradition: instead of passing power to the worthiest candidate, he named his own son as heir. Hereditary succession — and with it, dynastic rule — was born.

Before the Xia there was no dynasty in the strict sense; there were only emperors. Yu created kings.

---

## What we actually know (and don't)

The honest answer is: very little. The Xia left no writing that can be attributed to them with certainty, and no material remains that archaeologists can definitively assign to their dynasty. Even China's earliest historians — writing more than a thousand years later — found "comparatively little to say about them."

What exists is:

- **Textual references** in classics like the *Shangshu* (Book of Documents, 4th century BC) and the *Shiji* (Sima Qian's history, 1st century BC) — but these were compiled long after the Xia period and may reflect later political agendas.
- **Archaeological candidate:** The site of **Erlitou**, near Luoyang in Henan province, dates to c. 1900–1600 BC and is the strongest candidate for Xia material remains. It was a true state-level society (500 ha, population 18,000–30,000), with palace foundations, bronze workshops, wheeled vehicle tracks (earliest in China), and copper sourced from mountains 200 km away. But no written evidence from any Erlitou site mentions "Xia." See [[concept-erlitou]] for the full debate.

## The Erlitou–Xia debate in detail

Li Feng's nuanced position: the identification cannot be proven *or* disproven. The oracle bones never mention Xia — but oracle bones were divinatory records entirely focused on current affairs (did Di favor the harvest? will the queen have a son?); they show no interest in past enemies at all. The argument from silence is much weaker than it looks.

The **Bingong xu** bronze vessel (mid Western Zhou, c. 950–850 BC) carries a long inscription praising Great Yu — the legendary Xia founder — in terms that match the received tradition closely. This shows that Western Zhou people, writing 600+ years after Erlitou, already held the Xia/Yu tradition as historical fact. The tradition was ancient and not a Han-dynasty invention.

The most honest position: Erlitou represents a state-level society in the right time and place for Xia, and was almost certainly a factor in the cultural memory that produced the Xia tradition. But whether the Erlitou people called themselves "Xia" — a name that may have been given to them by later enemies — is unknown. See [[concept-erlitou]] for the full discussion.

## The political stakes of "Xia"

Keay makes a sharp observation: the word "Xia" had two uses. As a dynasty name, it referred to this early ruling house. As an ethnic label, it was used by people in the Central Plain during the 4th–3rd centuries BC (when the textual tradition was being compiled) to distinguish themselves — the civilized, literate "Xia people" — from surrounding "barbarians" (Di, Rong, Man, Yi).

This dual use raises a question: did later scholars *talk up* the Xia dynasty partly to legitimize the ethnic identity of those who called themselves its heirs? The name "Han" would later make the same transition — from dynasty to ethnic label — and is now the official term for China's dominant ethnic group.

## What came before them

The Xia emerged from a world of competing Neolithic cultures scattered across China — not just in the north. Millet was grown in the Yellow River region from ~8000 BC; rice in the Yangzi valley at about the same time. By ~3000 BC, the "Longshan" culture (centered in Shandong but widespread) had developed large settlements, elaborate tombs with rich grave goods, and a highly stratified society.

One Longshan site, **Erlitou**, has yielded both burnt divination bones and some of China's earliest bronze-casting — connecting it unmistakably to the better-documented Shang dynasty that followed.

---

## Legacy

The Xia matter most as a conceptual anchor. Whether or not they existed exactly as described, the idea of the Xia — a virtuous founding dynasty on the Central Plain — gave subsequent rulers something to legitimate themselves against. Confucius invoked them. The First Emperor implicitly competed with them. Every dynasty that followed reached back to the Three Dynasties as moral bedrock.
