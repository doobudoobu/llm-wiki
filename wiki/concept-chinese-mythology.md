# Chinese Mythology: An Overview

*This page covers the nature of Chinese mythology as a body of material — its sources, why it's so fragmentary, and how to read it. Individual figures and myths have their own pages.*

---

## Why China is wrongly said to have no mythology

The standard Western view that China lacks a real mythology is a myth in itself. There is a huge body of material. The reasons it went unrecognized:

1. **No Homer.** Greek mythology survived because gifted poets (Homer, Hesiod) retold myths in polished literary form. China had no equivalent — myths first appeared piecemeal, in fragments, across dozens of unrelated texts, and were never collated into a systematic whole.
2. **The wrong kind of writing.** In the archaic period, Chinese writing was ideographic and unwieldy — not yet sophisticated enough to express a full literary mythology. Myths survived through oral tradition and in compressed, allusive written fragments.
3. **Confucian suppression.** The Confucian scholarly tradition that dominated Chinese intellectual life from the Han dynasty onward viewed myths with deep suspicion. They rationalized mythic figures into historical ones, demoted gods to ministers, and reframed supernatural narratives as allegories for proper governance. A god who controlled floods became a hydraulic engineer under a sage-ruler's orders.

The result is a mythology that is fragmentary, inconsistent, often riddling — and genuinely fascinating.

## The key sources

| Text | Date | Nature |
|------|------|--------|
| *Songs of Ch'u* — "Questions of Heaven" | c. 4th century BC | ~160 sacred riddles implying myths without stating them; single most valuable mythographic document |
| *Classic of Mountains and Seas* | 3rd century BC–1st century AD (compiled in stages) | A bizarre geography/mythology compendium — aims to amuse, horrify, delight; second most important source |
| *Huai-nan Tzu* | c. 139 BC | Eclectic Taoist compilation; major cosmogonic narratives |
| *Historical Records* (*Shiji*), Sima Qian | c. 100 BC | Rationalizes myths into history; useful but strips supernatural content |
| *Explanations of Social Customs*, Ying Shao | c. AD 175 | Late Han; preserves myths in euhemerized (humanized) form |

## The problem of rationalization

The Confucian reworking of myth follows a consistent pattern: a primeval god becomes a sage-king; a divine act becomes a ministerial appointment; a cosmic event becomes a policy decision. Hou Chi, the god of millet, becomes a "minister of agriculture" under the sage-ruler Shun. Yü the flood hero becomes a hydraulic engineer. This makes it hard to recover the original mythic layer.

Sima Qian's *Historical Records* (c. 100 BC) is both indispensable and treacherous for this reason. He is the main source for many myths — but he systematically removes the supernatural content and links every figure to the respectable genealogy of the sage-rulers Yao and Shun.

The texts that preserve the most authentic mythic material are the ones *outside* the Confucian canon: *Songs of Ch'u*, *Classic of Mountains and Seas*, and *Huai-nan Tzu*.

## The cosmological picture

Chinese cosmogony describes creation not *ex nihilo* but from pre-existing matter: a primeval vapor (*qi*) that differentiates into Yin and Yang, heavy and light, male and female, earth and sky. The world is a square earth under a round domed sky, held up by pillars or mountains. It is a finite, comprehensible world — not part of a vast limitless universe. There are nine layers of sky, nine celestial gates, and the sky has "corners and edges" that are observable.

**Five distinct cosmological traditions** emerge from the early texts:
1. "Questions of Heaven" — the world picture from primordial vapor
2. The *Tao yuan* tomb text — pre-creation as "all was one, moist-wet and murky-dim" (strikingly similar to modern cosmological singularity)
3. *Huai-nan Tzu* — two gods born out of chaos, dividing into Yin and Yang
4. *Historical Records of Three Sovereign Divinities* — P'an Ku, the cosmic giant (3rd century AD; probably imported)
5. *Explanations of Social Customs* — Nü Kua, the goddess creatrix

There is no single authorized account. The P'an Ku narrative only became "canonical" in the late Han period because it served the patriarchal, male-dominant creation narrative that was gaining ideological ground. Before that, Nü Kua was the creation goddess.

## What Confucianism did to the gods

The most vivid illustration of Confucian mythic suppression is what happened to **Nü Kua**. In the earliest texts she is an independent primeval creatrix — she made the world, made humans, mended the sky when it broke. By the Han era she had been paired with Fu Hsi as his wife, demoted to an exemplar of the institution of marriage, and her independence systematically erased. A Tang-era version makes her a mortal woman married to her brother, subservient to the male deity "God in Heaven," ashamed of the incest that was originally presented as divinely sanctioned.

The same process applies to almost every mythic figure: the older the text, the more divine and independent the figure; the later the text, the more human and subordinate.
