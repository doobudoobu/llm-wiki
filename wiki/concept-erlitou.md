# Erlitou and the First Chinese State

**Period:** c. 1900–1600 BC (Erlitou); c. 1600–1400 BC (Erligang expansion)  
**Location:** Western Henan / Southern Shanxi (Erlitou); expanded to most of northern China (Erligang)  
**Significance:** First state-level society in China; origin of Chinese bronze-casting tradition; possible archaeological candidate for the Xia Dynasty

---

## The discovery

In autumn 1959, a team from the Institute of Archaeology (Beijing) was sent to western Henan to look for material remains of the Xia Dynasty — which historical texts placed in this region, a few centuries before the Shang. Near modern Luoyang, they found Erlitou: a site covering 500 ha, with palace foundations, bronze workshops, and levels of social complexity far exceeding anything previously known for that period. It became one of the most important archaeological sites in the world for understanding early state formation.

---

## The Erlitou state (c. 1900–1600 BC)

### Scale
- Site area: 500 ha (compared to ~280 ha for Taosi, the largest Longshan site)
- Estimated population at central site: 18,000–30,000
- Distribution of Erlitou cultural sites: across eastern Henan, Fen River valley (Shanxi), and upper Huai River — a settlement system with Erlitou clearly at the top

### The palace compound
At the center of the site, a walled rectangular compound containing at least eleven earth platforms. The two outstanding structures:
- **Foundation 1** (excavated 1960): 108 m E–W × 100 m N–S, raised 1–2 m above ground; surrounded by roofed hallways; central second platform with rectangular building. Probably the main ceremonial hall.
- **Foundation 2** (east wall of compound): slightly smaller but structurally similar; a large tomb (5.2×4.25 m) was excavated in its corridor — completely looted

Outside the palatial zone, three sections of road were found forming the central road system. In the southern gateway: **wheel tracks**, providing the earliest evidence for wheeled wagons in China.

### Bronze
Erlitou is the first Bronze Age culture in China. Key features:
- Vessel types: *jue*-cups (three legs, pouring spout), *jia*-heating vessels, *ding*-cauldrons — all central to the later Shang bronze repertoire
- Casting method: **sectional mold casting** — a clay model is made, soft clay is pressed against it and cut into sections, sections are baked into molds, molds are reassembled around a core, molten bronze is poured. This Chinese-specific technique was the mainstream Bronze Age technology in East Asia; lost-wax casting (predominant in the West) was comparatively underdeveloped in China
- Bronze workshop located at center-south of site; copper ore almost certainly from **Zhongtiao Mountains** (southern Shanxi, ~200 km away) — also the probable salt source
- Turquoise: a large workshop producing turquoise objects (including a 70.2-cm dragon-shaped object decorated with turquoise) was found just outside the south palace wall

The Luoyang Plain has no natural copper deposits. Moving ore hundreds of kilometers requires a level of organizational power characteristic of state-level, not chiefdom-level, societies.

### Orientation
Erlitou buildings were oriented **6–10 degrees west of north**. This is cosmologically significant: the orientation corresponds to a different stellar alignment than the one used at Yanshi, Zhengzhou, and Anyang, which are all oriented **7 degrees east of north**. The shift almost certainly marks a fundamental change in the cosmological-political culture — the cosmology that placed Erlitou in the center of the sky was replaced when the Shang arrived.

## The Xia debate

The Erlitou culture's dates (carbon-14: c. 1900–1600 BC) fall precisely in the period Chinese historical tradition assigns to the **Xia Dynasty** — the dynasty that preceded Shang. The same region (western Henan and southern Shanxi) is where texts place the activities of the Xia kings. No competing Bronze Age society of comparable power has been found in this region for this period.

**The case for identifying Erlitou as Xia:**
- Same time period
- Same geographical area
- Same scale of political power that texts attribute to Xia
- Erlitou pottery sequence continues unbroken from the Longshan cultures, then gives way to Shang — exactly as the historical tradition describes Xia giving way to Shang

**The case against:**
- Zero written evidence from any Erlitou site connects it to "Xia"
- The Shang oracle bones never mention Xia (though this is weakened: oracle bones were divinatory records focused on current affairs; they show no curiosity about past enemies)
- The identification cannot be confirmed with current evidence

**Li Feng's position:** The question must remain open. We cannot prove Erlitou was Xia, nor prove it was not. The absence of contemporaneous written evidence is not proof of non-existence. The most intellectually honest position is: Erlitou represents a state-level society that existed at the right time and place that historical tradition assigns to Xia, and Erlitou was — in all probability — a major factor in the cultural memory that produced the Xia tradition. But "pending the name Xia."

**The Bingong xu bronze** (mid Western Zhou, c. 950–850 BC): acquired in Hong Kong antique market, cleaned in Beijing in 2003. Carries a lengthy inscription praising Great Yu (legendary Xia founder) in terms that closely parallel the received tradition — indicating that Western Zhou people already regarded the Xia/Yu tradition as historical fact. The bronze cannot prove the Xia Dynasty existed, but it shows the tradition was ancient and not a Han invention.

## The founding of Shang and the Erlitou-Shang transition

Erlitou fell around 1530 BC. Two large urban centers then appeared near the Erlitou site:

### Yanshi (c. 1605–1365 BC)
- Located 5 km from Erlitou on the north bank of the Luo River
- Inner walled enclosure: 1100×740 m (Period I), later expanded to 1200 E–W × 1700 m N–S
- Central palace zone, warehouses, bronze casting foundry
- Buildings oriented **7 degrees east of north** — the Shang alignment
- Likely a Shang stronghold keeping watch over the Luoyang plain (formerly Erlitou/Xia territory)

### Zhengzhou (Erligang phase, c. 1580–1415 BC)
- Inner city enclosure: 317.9 ha — nearly ten times Yanshi
- The famous site "Erligang" (2 km south of the inner city) gave the phase its name
- Bronze hoards from Duling (1974) and Nanshuncheng Street (1996) show technically more advanced bronzes than at Erlitou — heavier, technically complex square vessels now possible
- Probable identity: capital Bo (Tang's founding capital) or Ao (ninth king Zhong Ding's capital)

## Erligang expansion: the first geo-cultural unity

During the Erligang period (~1600–1400 BC), something unprecedented happened: standard Erligang pottery types appear simultaneously across an enormous territory — Wei River valley in the west, central and southern Hebei in the north, middle Yangzi in the south. This is the first geo-cultural unity in China larger than any previous Neolithic interaction sphere, and it was not maintained after the Erligang period.

Whether this was political expansion or cultural diffusion cannot be determined from material culture alone — but the key outpost evidence is clear:

**Panlongcheng** (Hubei Province, 500 km south of Zhengzhou, on north bank of Yangzi): a walled Shang-style city (290×260 m) with bronzes and pottery types identical to Zhengzhou. Located at the south end of the ancient road through the Nanyang Basin, crossing the Yangzi River at the ancient Yunmeng Marsh — the key transit point for copper moving north from Jiangxi and Hunan mines.

**Copper mining:**
- **Tonglushan** (Daye City, Hubei, discovered 1973–5): five large tunnel clusters, wooden structures in horizontal tunnels with vertical shafts, bronze tools, pottery; open-surface quarrying and underground mining; contemporary with middle Shang through Western Zhou
- **Tongling** (Ruichang, Jiangxi, excavated 1989–92): similar tunnel system; pottery matches Panlongcheng (Upper Erligang/middle Shang); active from middle Shang through Warring States period
- Both sites almost certainly supplied copper for the Zhengzhou/Anyang bronze industry

## Legacy

Erlitou matters because it established the template: bronze vessels as ritual/political objects, sectional mold casting as the production method, palatial compounds as the architectural form. All of these continued through Shang and into Zhou. The Shang did not invent Chinese bronze civilization — they inherited it, massively expanded it, and put their name on it.
