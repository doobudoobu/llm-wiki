# Li Feng, *Early China: A Social and Cultural History* — Chapters 6–7

**Chapters:** 6 — "The inscribed history: the Western Zhou state and its bronze vessels" (pp. 112–138); 7 — "The creation of paradigm: Zhou bureaucracy and social institutions" (pp. 139–160)
**File:** `raw/early_china_li_feng.pdf`

---

## What these chapters cover

Two of Li Feng's most substantial chapters, covering the Western Zhou dynasty (1045–771 BCE) from two angles: the archaeological record (bronze vessels as historical documents, the *fengjian* state network, military expansion, the fall) and the social/institutional record (clan and lineage structure, the invention of bureaucracy, the Mandate of Heaven ideology, the Confucian Classics as Western Zhou products).

---

## Most interesting new material

**The five-planet conjunction of 1059 BCE.** In the fifth month of 1059 BCE, the five major planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Mars, Venus, Mercury) gathered in a narrow 30° arc of the northern sky, visible from the Zhou capital. This conjunction occurs roughly every 516 years. Modern astronomy has confirmed the date. The Zhou took it as Heaven's sign — it triggered King Wen's declaration of kingship. The [[concept-mandate-of-heaven]] was born in a real astronomical event, not just a political invention.

**The pre-dynastic Zhou worshipped Shang ancestors.** Oracle bones from the Zhou capital Qiyi show that before the conquest, the Zhou divined to deceased Shang kings including Wu Ding. They accepted that Shang was the superpower and honored its dead accordingly.

**"Fengjian" is not feudalism.** Li Feng published a 2003 *Harvard Journal of Asiatic Studies* article arguing that translating the Zhou political system as "feudalism" is a fundamental error. The Zhou system was based on kinship obligations, not contractual lord-vassal reciprocity. See [[concept-fengjian]].

**The Li *gui* inscription.** Discovered in Shaanxi in 1978, a bronze vessel records the Battle of Muye in present tense: "King Wu campaigned against Shang. It was the morning of the *jiazi* day (#1). Jupiter was upright, and we defeated them at the dusk of the day." A same-day report of the conquest, cast in bronze.

**Caucasoid shamans at Zhouyuan.** Two small bone sculptures with unmistakably non-East-Asian (Caucasoid) facial features were excavated from the Zhou capital. One has the character *wu* 巫 ("shaman") inscribed on top of the figure's head. Carnelian beads and faience items from India and West Asia appear regularly in mid-to-late Western Zhou tombs. The Zhou world reached into Central Asia.

**King Zhao drowned with his entire army in the Han River.** Launching a campaign to conquer the middle Yangzi, Zhao suffered total defeat — he lost the entire royal Six Armies and his own life in the river. Li Feng notes dryly: "Certainly no individual had ever cast a bronze to celebrate this disastrous end of the reign of King Zhao." A trauma so complete it produced no inscriptions.

**The Zhou invented bureaucracy.** Unlike Shang government (diviners doubling as officials, no formal administrative structure), the Zhou created real administrative offices — Supervisor of Land, Supervisor of Construction, Supervisor of Horses — organized into a formal Ministry (*gingshiliao* 卿事寮). About 100 surviving "appointment inscriptions" describe a regularized investiture ceremony. This was likely a genuinely new institution, invented to govern a territory far larger than Shang's.

**Heaven's Mandate as a source of anxiety, not just pride.** Since the doctrine said Heaven had passed from Xia to Shang to Zhou, it implied Zhou could lose it too. Only King Wen was the actual *recipient*; subsequent kings could only maintain it through virtue. Late Western Zhou poetry is haunted by the fear that they are failing to do so. See [[concept-mandate-of-heaven]].

**The Bao Si story is fictional.** Li Feng argues the famous account — King You lights beacon fires to make his silent concubine smile; regional lords stop responding; real barbarians kill him — is "essentially fictional." The real fall (771 BCE) originated in a political split in 777 BCE between senior officials loyal to King Xuan and the new King You, culminating in King You attacking the rival state Western Shen, which called in the Xianyuan barbarians for help.

**The Confucian Classics predate Confucius by 300 years.** The *Book of Changes* (Yijing 易经), *Book of Documents* (Shangshu 尚书), and *Book of Poetry* (Shijing 诗经) were created during the Western Zhou. The earliest authentic sections of the *Book of Documents* are government records from scribes associated with the Duke of Zhou — produced roughly 250 years before Confucius was born.
