# Qin Dynasty

**Period:** 221–206 BCE (as empire; ~15 years)
**Capital:** Xianyang (near modern Xi'an)
**Founder:** Qin Shi Huangdi (King Zheng of Qin, r. 247–210 BC as king; 221–210 BC as emperor)
**Preceded by:** [[dynasty-zhou]] (the Warring States period)
**Followed by:** [[dynasty-han]] (rose from the civil war after Qin's collapse)

---

## The paradox of Qin

Qin lasted barely fifteen years as an empire. Its first emperor died in 210 BC; the dynasty collapsed in 206 BC, four years later. And yet it is among the most consequential regimes in human history. The standardizations Qin imposed — of script, currency, weights, measures, law, road gauges, administrative structures — survived the dynasty itself and gave China its durable institutional skeleton. The Han dynasty that followed essentially kept the Qin system while ditching the Qin name and some of its harshest excesses.

As one scholar noted: Qin's seizure of power was violent, but "its accomplishments were great."

## Qin before the empire: the rise of a peripheral state

Qin started as a horse-breeding fief on the steppe-land borders of Gansu province, established by the Zhou a century after their own founding. It was peripheral to the "central states" of the Yellow River basin and was habitually looked down upon as semi-barbarian. This outsider status would later make Qin uniquely dangerous.

Three factors drove Qin's rise:

**1. The conquest of Sichuan (316 BC).** Qin lured the king of Shu (Sichuan) into requesting a road — the "Stone Cattle Road" — by staging fake gold-dunging stone cows for his ambassadors to admire. Once the road was built, Qin's armies used it to invade. Sichuan doubled Qin's territory, provided vast cereals and mineral wealth, and gave Qin strategic leverage over the southern state of Chu. Li Bing, a later Qin governor of Shu, built the **Dujiangyan irrigation system** (~270 BC), which still exists today as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and converted Sichuan into the great rice-bowl of inland China.

**2. The reforms of Shang Yang (from 356 BC).** A minister named Shang Yang imposed a radical "legalist" framework on the Qin state: universal conscription, collective household responsibility (neighbors reported on each other or shared punishment), a merit-based ranking system (battle promotions for enemy heads taken), standardized weights and measures, heavy taxation of trade, and a legal code of ferocious precision. The goal was total mobilization of state resources. It worked. Shang Yang was later "torn apart by carriages" under his own penal code after his patron died.

**3. Military supremacy.** By the 3rd century BC Qin had abandoned alliance politics and adopted a strategy of annihilation: defeat enemy forces and obliterate their capacity to fight back. Mass slaughters of 100,000–240,000 are recorded (with some exaggeration). By 246 BC, when thirteen-year-old King Zheng inherited the throne, Qin already controlled over half the territory it would eventually unify.

## Qin Shi Huangdi and the unification (246–221 BC)

See [[figure-qin-shi-huangdi]] for the emperor's life in full.

Between 238 BC (when he took personal power) and 221 BC, King Zheng's armies absorbed the remaining six warring states in rapid succession: Han, Zhao, Wei, Chu, Yan, Qi. "Thanks to the ancestral spirits, these six kings have all acknowledged their guilt and the world is now in profound order."

Having unified "all under Heaven," Zheng declined the proposed title "Greatly August One" and coined a new one: **Huangdi** (August Emperor), combining the characters for the mythical Five Emperors and the legendary Three Kings. He called himself *Shi Huangdi* — First Emperor — and announced that his successors would be Second Emperor, Third Emperor, and so on for "1,000 or 10,000 generations." The sequence stopped at Second Emperor.

## The standardization project

The First Emperor's administrators imposed a single framework across a territory spanning everything from the steppes of Mongolia to what is now northern Vietnam:

- **Script:** The "Small Seal" script replaced the diverse regional variants that had evolved during the Warring States. One written language for all literate people, regardless of spoken dialect. "Without this standardisation, China's bureaucrats would today need as many interpreters as the European Union."
- **Currency:** Round copper coins with a square hole — easily strung together — replaced the knife-money and spade-money of the various states. This design lasted more than 2,000 years.
- **Weights and measures:** Standardized throughout the empire with heavy penalties for variation.
- **Road gauges:** All vehicles were required to have axles exactly six feet wide, ensuring a tram-like ride in the deeply scored ruts of Qin's highways.
- **Law:** The detailed Qin legal code — recovered in the 1970s from a tomb in Hubei on bamboo strips — turns out to be less arbitrarily brutal than reputation suggests. It specified the exact nature of offenses, degrees of intent, and bureaucratic procedures. The emphasis was on "efficiency, precision, and fixed routine."
- **Administration:** Former ruling families were relocated to Xianyang under surveillance. All conquered territory was organized into 36 "commanderies," each divided into counties under centrally appointed officials. No more hereditary fiefs.

The First Emperor also **aligned all calendars**, moving New Year to the tenth month (onset of winter), and decreed that Qin "ruled by the power of water" according to the Five Phases/Elements theory. His court therefore wore black, flew black flags, and measured everything in multiples of six.

## The Great Wall

General Meng Tian was sent north with somewhere between 100,000 and 300,000 men (probably 221 BC) to push the Rong and Di peoples out of the Ordos desert loop and build what the Chinese called *changcheng* — "long wall." The reported length was 10,000 *li* (~5,000 km / 3,000 miles), from Lintao in Gansu to Liaodong east of Beijing.

Sima Qian visited the site a century later and was more impressed by Meng Tian's 850-km road through the Ordos badlands than by the wall itself. Modern scholarship thinks the "wall" was probably not a single continuous barrier but a chain of garrison outposts with their own encircling walls — more about consolidating Qin's northern advance than defending against invasion. Almost none of it survives; hangtu (tamped earth) above ground doesn't last two millennia of sandstorms and frost. The famous stone-and-brick Great Wall was built by the Ming dynasty, 1,700 years later.

## The book burning and scholar purge (213 BC)

In 213 BC, after a Confucian scholar suggested reviving the Zhou-era practice of granting fiefs to loyal kinsmen, the First Emperor's chancellor Li Si responded with a memorial: burn all books except those on medicine, divination, agriculture, and forestry. Anyone failing to burn within thirty days: tattooed and sentenced to hard labor.

The emperor concurred. The following year, 460 scholars were reportedly executed or buried alive.

The impact was probably exaggerated — books were few, bamboo lasts well in hiding, and the imperial archive retained copies of most texts. The Han dynasty, which had every reason to demonize Qin, compiled most of what we know about the burning. In one of history's great ironies, the attempt to restrict access to the past made Han scholars scrutinize surviving texts even more intently. "Its practical effect was to strengthen the tendency decried by Li Si of looking backward rather than toward the present."

## The terracotta army and the tomb

Planning for the First Emperor's tomb began when he was thirteen, on his accession. By his death, a mountain had been constructed over the burial chamber.

Sima Qian's description of the chamber's interior: floors of mercury flowing in the pattern of China's rivers and seas, a ceiling with constellations rendered in pearls, palaces and towers in replica, crossbows set to fire on intruders, whale-oil lamps designed to burn without going out. Surveys in 2008 detected elevated mercury concentrations in the soil above the unopened chamber, roughly following the shape of China's river system.

Sima Qian says nothing about the terracotta warriors. In 1974, well-diggers accidentally broke through into pits outside the main tomb complex and found an entire army — thousands of life-size clay soldiers, each with individualized features, arranged in battle formation. More pits have been opened since: bronze chariots, bronze birds, more warriors. The main burial chamber remains sealed.

## Collapse

The First Emperor died in 210 BC while on his fifth imperial tour, after shooting a sea monster with a crossbow (days before his death). His senior officials, terrified of instability, concealed the death and forged documents to eliminate the capable heir in favor of a more tractable second son, Huhai. Li Si was complicit. Within months, the cover-up unraveled; within two years, massive rebellions had broken out across the empire. Li Si was executed. Huhai was assassinated. The Qin dynasty ended in 206 BC — barely four years after the First Emperor's death.

---

## What Qin left behind

Qin is the most reviled dynasty in traditional Chinese historiography — the Han who replaced it had every reason to paint it black — and also one of the most influential. The unified empire it created, the administrative machinery it built, the standard script it imposed: all these outlasted the dynasty by two millennia.

It also gave China its name. "Qin" (pronounced "chin") passed into Sanskrit as "Cina," into Greek and Latin as "Sinai/Thinai," and eventually into English as "China."
