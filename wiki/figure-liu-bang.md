# Liu Bang — Han Gaozu

**Born:** ~256 BC, Pei district (northern Chu)
**Died:** 195 BC
**Title:** Han Gaozu — the Han "Great Progenitor"
**Significance:** First commoner to become emperor in Chinese history; the last for 1,500 years

---

## The unlikely emperor

Liu Bang was a provincial nobody. He had a limited education, a taste for bawdy company, and an indifferent military record. He'd fallen foul of Qin authorities and turned to brigandage. What he had going for him was Heaven — which showered him, according to Sima Qian, with auspicious portents from birth. His mother had conceived him by a dragon. Another dragon hovered over him when he slept. His features were sufficiently dragon-like to excite physiognomists, one of whom was so impressed he gave Liu Bang his daughter in marriage. "Readers are not to be misled by Liu Bang's limited education, his boorish behaviour and indifferent military record. Heaven's favour would more than compensate."

---

## Against Xiang Yu

For seven years (209–202 BC) Liu Bang fought Xiang Yu of Chu for control of China. By almost every conventional measure, Xiang Yu should have won:

- He was from a military aristocratic family; Liu Bang was a nobody
- He was a military genius; Liu Bang was a mediocre commander
- He had the larger army; Liu Bang's was usually outnumbered
- He won most of their battles

But Liu Bang had Sichuan — the vast, rich region that had once powered Qin — as an inexhaustible resource base. He had shrewd advisers. And he had luck: when Xiang Yu had him surrounded at Pengcheng and annihilation seemed certain, "a dust storm turned day to night" and Liu Bang escaped in the confusion.

Xiang Yu made the decisive political mistakes: burning Xianyang (and probably its archives), massacring the surrendered population, sending Liu Bang to what he thought was permanent exile in Sichuan, failing to build durable alliances. Liu Bang spent the years rebuilding, sending forces to harass Chu's rear, and waiting for Xiang Yu's military brilliance to run out of friends.

The final battle at Gaixia (202 BC) was overwhelming — Han forces outnumbered Xiang Yu three to one. See [[dynasty-han]] for Xiang Yu's last stand. After his death, Liu Bang assumed the emperorship on 28 February 202 BC.

## The imperial style problem

Han Gaozu was not a natural emperor. His idea of a good time was "a bacchanalia of brimming cups, earthy jokes and clumsy horseplay in the company of cronies from Pei." He was so contemptuous of formal erudition that he used scholars' hats as chamber pots. People didn't come back from his toilet visits, got slandered in their absence, or were "favored" without warning.

A Confucian courtier named Shusun Tong pointed out that some decorum was needed. Gaozu was doubtful: "See what you can do, but make it easy to learn... it must be the sort of thing I can manage." After a month of rehearsals in a specially built pavilion, the new ceremonial was introduced at the New Year assembly. Every noble and official from across the empire came to pay court. Everyone "trembled with awe and reverence." Gaozu sighed with satisfaction. He had at last "understood how exalted a thing it is to be an emperor."

## The real compromises

The empire Liu Bang built was held together by concessions. To win the civil war he had promised his generals kingdoms. He delivered — then spent most of his remaining years clawing those kingdoms back, replacing dangerous military men with his own (less dangerous) family members. He kept the Qin administrative machinery — registrations, conscription, group liability, the tariff of punishments — while publicly disavowing Qin's harshness. He paid tribute to the Xiongnu in silk and grain and an imperial bride rather than fight them, after his army was surrounded and nearly captured at Pingcheng in 200 BC.

"The tactics and behaviour appropriate to winning an empire being unsuited to ruling it" — Liu Bang had to change with the times. He did, at least partially.

---

## Legacy

He made the Han dynasty possible. That dynasty ran for 400 years and gave China its durable institutional identity. What's remarkable isn't that he succeeded — the chaos of post-Qin civil war offered openings to many contenders — but that the dynasty he founded lasted so long and left so deep a mark. Sima Qian, writing a century later under Gaozu's descendants, acknowledged that the man was something extraordinary even if he was hard to explain: the first true parvenu emperor in Chinese history, elevated not by birth or learning but by Heaven's evident and persistent favor.
