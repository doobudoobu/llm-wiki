# Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677)

**Full name:** Baruch (Hebrew) / Benedictus (Latin) de Spinoza  
**Born:** Amsterdam, to a Portuguese-Jewish merchant family  
**Died:** The Hague, aged 44, of lung disease (likely from glass-grinding dust)  
**Main work:** *Ethics Demonstrated in Geometrical Order* (*Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata*), published posthumously 1677  
**Position:** Rationalist, pantheist, determinist

Spinoza is one of the most radical thinkers in the Western philosophical tradition. His core claim — that God and Nature are one and the same thing, and that everything in the universe follows necessarily from the nature of that one thing — got him excommunicated at 23 and branded an atheist for two centuries. His own century called him dangerous. A later century called him "the God-intoxicated man."

---

## Life and context

Spinoza's family were *conversos* — Sephardic Jews who had converted to Christianity under the Portuguese Inquisition and then fled to Amsterdam, where they could practice Judaism openly. Amsterdam's Jewish community was prosperous but anxious, having recently escaped persecution; they policed themselves carefully to avoid drawing hostile attention from the Christian majority.

In **1656**, at age 23, Spinoza was issued a *cherem* — an excommunication decree — by the Amsterdam Jewish community. It is one of the harshest cherem documents on record, condemning him for "abominable heresies" and "monstrous deeds," but without specifying what they were. He never joined the Christian church either. He spent the rest of his life philosophically stateless, earning money as a lens grinder (a precision craft for scientific instruments and microscopes) and writing.

The *Ethics* was complete by around 1675, but Spinoza suppressed publication after learning that a theological treatise he'd published anonymously in 1670 had caused an outcry. He arranged for the *Ethics* to be published immediately after his death. He died in 1677 of lung disease — the glass-grinding dust had damaged his lungs over twenty years.

**Leibniz** visited him in 1676, a year before his death. They had been corresponding; Leibniz reportedly spent several days in private discussion with Spinoza and took extensive notes, which he later downplayed, apparently not wanting to be associated with so controversial a thinker.

## Intellectual context

Spinoza was working in the shadow of **Descartes** (1596–1650), whose *Meditations on First Philosophy* and *Principles of Philosophy* had dominated European metaphysics for a generation. Descartes had argued for a world of many independent substances — minds and bodies — sustained by a necessarily existing God who stood apart from creation and created it by free will.

Spinoza accepted Descartes' definitions of the key terms (substance, attribute, mode) but showed that if you think them through consistently, you don't arrive at Descartes' picture. You arrive at a single substance, which is everything, which is God, which acts by necessity — with no free will, no creation ex nihilo, no interventionist deity.

This was alarming enough that the *Ethics* was placed on the Catholic Index of Forbidden Books. In Protestant circles it was not much better received.

## Why he matters

Spinoza's influence runs through almost every major philosophical current that followed him:

- **The Enlightenment**: his biblical criticism and arguments for freedom of thought (in the *Theological-Political Treatise*) laid groundwork for secular liberal politics
- **German Idealism**: Hegel and Schelling considered him the unavoidable starting point for any serious metaphysics
- **Romanticism**: the poet Novalis called him "the God-intoxicated man"; Goethe read him throughout his life
- **20th-century philosophy**: Deleuze wrote two books on Spinoza; the concept of **immanence** (everything within the world, nothing transcendent) is fundamentally Spinozist

He is also, in a quiet way, one of the first thinkers to argue that human beings are part of nature, not exempt from it — a position that now seems obvious and then seemed outrageous.
