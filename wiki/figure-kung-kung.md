# Kung Kung (共工)

**Role:** Primeval water titan; flood-bringer; cosmic marplot  
**Defining act:** Charged into Pu-chou Mountain, shattering the pillar of Heaven  
**Name meaning:** 共 (gong) = quarrelsome, flooding waters; 工 = craft/work

Kung Kung is one of Chinese mythology's most vivid destroyers — not a demon, not a criminal, but a titan whose martial rage tips the cosmic order sideways. Literally.

---

## He broke the sky

In the most famous myth, Kung Kung fights the god Chuan Hsu for the title of God (*Ti* — supreme divine authority). He loses. In his fury he charges headfirst into Pu-chou Mountain, one of the eight pillars holding up the canopy of the sky.

The pillar snaps. The cord of earth breaks.

> Heaven tilted toward the northwest, and that is why the sun, moon, and stars move in that direction. Earth had a gap missing in the southeast, and that is why the rivers overflowed and silt and soil came to rest there. (*Huai-nan Tzu*)

Two permanent features of the Chinese cosmos explained by one tantrum. The sun rises in the east and sets in the west because the sky framework was permanently warped. Every river runs southeast because that's where the earth gaped open. Kung Kung didn't just fail — he restructured reality in his failure.

---

## Multiple roles across texts

The mythological tradition can't decide what to do with Kung Kung:

- **As flood-king:** *Kuan Tzu* (3rd century BC) says Kung Kung ruled the world when "water covered seven-tenths of the land and dry land consisted of three-tenths." He managed the world within those narrow confines — flood control as governance. In this version he's a primeval ruler, not a criminal.
- **As rebel:** *Discourses of the States* says he "abandoned this Way... wanted to dam the hundred rivers, reduce the highest ground, and block up the low-lying ground." His hydraulic program was cosmically destructive rather than beneficial. He was "destroyed" for it.
- **As sky-breaker:** *Huai-nan Tzu*'s version (above) — pure mythic violence, no moral framing.

The scholar William Boltz reads Kung Kung as a "personification of the Flood itself" — the chaos that Yü's hydraulic work overcame. In Lévi-Straussian terms: Kung Kung = chaos, Yü = order. The flood myth is Order defeating Chaos.

This also means the Pu-chou Mountain myth and the Yü flood myth may be **parallel versions of the same underlying story**, told in different registers. Both end with the cosmic order restored after a water-related catastrophe. See [[figure-yu-the-great]].

---

## Connection to Nü Kua

Elsewhere in *Huai-nan Tzu*, it is Kung Kung's destruction of Pu-chou Mountain that motivates [[figure-nu-kua]] to mend the sky with five-colored stones. The two myths are linked: Kung Kung makes the disaster, Nü Kua fixes it. The great goddess and the great marplot are cosmological counterparts.

---

## The name problem

The name Kung Kung is written in several ways, all suggesting water, noise, and conflict. The phonetic correlatives of his first name mean "quarrelsome" and "flooding waters." Whether this is mythological naming (his character in his name) or reverse etymology (the name came first, the meaning attached later) is debated. But it fits: he is flooding water, personified as a god who rages.
