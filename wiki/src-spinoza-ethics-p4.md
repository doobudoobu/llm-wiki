# Spinoza, *Ethics* — Part IV: Of Human Bondage, or the Strength of the Emotions

**Source:** *Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata* (1677), Part IV  
**Secondary guide:** Beth Lord, *Spinoza's Ethics: An Edinburgh Philosophical Guide* (2010)  
**Translation used:** R. H. M. Elwes (Gutenberg)

---

## The problem stated

Part III ended with a wave on the sea. Part IV asks what we can do about that.

The title is deliberate. "Human bondage" is slavery to our own affects — being dragged around by love, hatred, ambition, fear, and envy, without the power to check them. The *epigraph* might as well be Paul's lament from Romans: "the evil that I would not, that I do." Spinoza knows this situation precisely:

> "Human infirmity in moderating and checking the emotions I name bondage: for, when a man is a prey to his emotions, he is not his own master, but lies at the mercy of fortune: so much so, that he is often compelled, while seeing that which is better for him, to follow that which is worse."

Part IV is Spinoza's ethics: what is good, what is virtue, how to live. It is also, quietly, one of the most subversive documents in the Western philosophical tradition.

---

## Redefining good and evil

The Preface makes a move that everything else depends on.

From Part I, we know that nature contains no good or evil — everything that exists is a mode of God, and from God's perspective things simply *are*. So "good" and "evil" are not properties of things in themselves. They are modes of thinking — relations:

> "Music is good for one who is melancholy, bad for one who is mourning, and neither good nor bad to one who is deaf."

This is not relativism. It is precision. The definitions that follow are exact:

- **Good** (D1): what we *certainly know* to be useful to us — i.e., what rational understanding confirms increases our flourishing
- **Evil** (D2): what we *certainly know* prevents us from mastering some good

The key phrase is "certainly know." Mere belief or feeling doesn't count. The things most people call good — money, fame, approval, revenge — are imagined goods, based on affects and associations, not rational understanding. Henry VIII imagined it was good to execute Anne Boleyn. He was wrong. If he had truly understood his own nature, he would have known that destroying another person is contrary to his own flourishing.

This distinction — between imagined goods (based on affects) and true goods (based on adequate knowledge) — is the engine of Spinoza's entire ethical system.

## Virtue = Power

**Definition 8** is the pivot of Part IV:

> "By virtue and power I understand the same thing; that is, virtue, insofar as it is related to man, is the very essence, or nature, of man, insofar as he has the power of bringing about certain things, which can be understood through the laws of his nature alone."

Virtue is not obedience to God. It is not piety, meekness, or the suppression of desire. It is *power* — the power of a being to act from its own nature rather than being pushed around by external things.

From Part III's conatus: every thing's essence is its striving to persist. When that striving is fully expressed — when a being acts from its own nature, as adequate cause — that is virtue. So virtue is dynamic, not static. It rises and falls as activity rises and falls.

**P22, Corollary**: "The striving to preserve oneself is the first and only foundation of virtue. For no other principle can be conceived prior to this one, and no virtue can be conceived without it."

This is the scandalous claim. Moralists have always taught that self-interest must be subordinated to some higher principle — God's commands, duty, the common good. Spinoza says no: self-preservation is the root, and nothing can be grafted onto it from outside. What you have instead is a proper understanding of what self-preservation actually requires — and that, as we will see, leads directly to caring about others.

On **suicide**: it cannot come from one's own essence — essence is striving, which cannot include striving against itself. Suicide is what happens when a person is so completely overpowered by external forces — despair, sadness, external violence — that the self ceases to strive at all. The person who kills himself is not self-determining; he is the most fully enslaved of all, his essence ground down to zero by forces from outside.

## The social turn: nothing more useful than man

**P18, Scholium** is one of the most important passages in the *Ethics*, and one of the most counterintuitive:

> "To man, there is nothing more useful than man."

The argument is simple and beautiful. Our bodies agree with other human bodies more than with anything else in nature. That means that in encountering other humans, we acquire more common notions — adequate ideas of our shared nature — than in any other encounter. Common notions build rational knowledge. Rational knowledge increases our activity and virtue. Therefore: other rational people are the single greatest aid to our flourishing.

Even more: two individuals of the same nature joined together "compose an individual twice as powerful as each one." A community of rational people is an individual more capable — physically and mentally — than the sum of its parts.

**P35 and its corollary** are where this becomes startling:

> "When each man most seeks his own advantage, then men are most useful to one another."

The apparent paradox resolves once you understand "seeking your own advantage" correctly. If a person truly seeks his advantage — rationally, not through affects and imagined goods — he does what is genuinely good for his nature. And since human nature is shared, doing what is genuinely good for yourself *is* doing what is good for humanity. The person who harms another is necessarily acting from passion (anger, hatred, envy), not from rational self-interest. Genuine self-interest and genuine altruism are the same thing.

"Selfish Spinoza" is not a coherent reading of Part IV. The most self-interested man, on Spinoza's account, is the one who most promotes human flourishing.

## The ethics of joy

P45 contains Spinoza's broadside against religious morality:

> "A man who is guided by reason is more free in a State, where he lives under a general system of law, than in solitude, where he is independent... Laughter and merriment are simply pleasure."

The fuller scholium is more explicit. Spinoza is targeting Calvinism — the doctrine that sees bodily pleasure as morally suspect, that treats melancholy and self-denial as signs of piety. His counter:

> "It is the part of a wise man to refresh and restore himself in moderation with pleasant food and drink, with scents, with the beauty of green plants, with decoration, music, sports, the theatre, and other things of this kind, which anyone can use without injury to another."

**Joy is ethically good.** Not just pleasantly neutral — positively good. Joy increases the body's and mind's power to act, and greater power is greater virtue. To forbid yourself pleasure is a "savage and sad superstition." God does not take pleasure in human suffering. On the contrary: as we feel more joy, we participate more in the divine nature, because our being increases. Any religion that claims God is pleased by despondency "does violence to human beings and diminishes their virtue."

Two centuries later, Nietzsche called this "slave morality": the morality that valorizes weakness, meekness, suffering, and passivity as virtue. He explicitly credits Spinoza as a predecessor. Both agree that Christian ethics has ethics backwards — associating virtue with self-denial and sin with self-affirmation, inverting the actual relationship between joy and power.

**Hope and fear** (P47) are *not* ethically good. Nor are pity, humility, or repentance (P53, P54). All are sad passions. Spinoza accepts their practical utility — a person who acts rightly from pity rather than understanding is better than one who doesn't act at all — but they are not virtue. The rational person does not help others out of pity; he helps because he truly understands that helping is good for himself and for humanity. He does not feel guilt because he understands that all actions have causes. He does not feel pride because he understands the same.

**Hatred** in all its forms — envy, mockery, disdain, anger, vengeance — is necessarily evil (P45). Not because hatred violates a moral law, but because striving to harm another person is contrary to your own rational interest (see P37).

## Good and evil actions — the most disturbing claim

**P59** pushes to what sounds like moral nihilism:

> "To every action to which we are determined from an affect which is a passion, we can be determined by reason, without that affect."

The corollary: no action is evil in itself. Murder, considered purely as a physical event — trigger pulled, arm swung — is morally neutral or positive, a capability of the human body. It becomes murder, and evil, when it is *combined* with the image of another person and the affect of hatred. If that image and affect are removed, what remains is just an action, and it is good.

Is Spinoza saying murder could be virtuous? No — because reason could never determine a person to harm another (that is contrary to rational self-interest). If the hatred were truly removed, the action would not be murder; it would be something done on a shooting range or in surgery. Evil actions are confusions of good activities. There is no "force for evil" in anyone's nature. "Knowledge of evil is inadequate knowledge" (P64) — evil has no positive being; it is the privation of good, just as falsity is the privation of truth.

Beth Lord acknowledges this is extremely hard to accept in the face of the great evil human beings have actually done. It is where Spinoza reads most coldly.

## Politics: the state as managed affect

**P37, Scholium 2** applies this ethics to politics.

In a world of fully rational people, no laws would be needed — everyone would act from their own nature in ways that harm no one. In a world of fully irrational people, it would be Hobbes's state of nature: "poor, nasty, brutish, and short." The real world is in between.

The state exists to manage irrational people through stronger affects. Since the only way to restrain an affect is with a stronger contrary affect (P7), the state uses *fear* — fear of punishment — to override the affects of anger, greed, and hatred that would otherwise lead people to harm each other. Laws are "imaginary" in Spinoza's technical sense: not grounded in true knowledge of human nature, but in what the community imagines to be good and bad. At best, civil laws align with true human interests (the law against murder). At worst, they enforce religious doctrine.

Free will is a legal fiction. The state requires the concept to punish people — we hold people responsible only for what they "freely intended." Spinoza knows free will doesn't exist, but acknowledges this fiction is necessary. The murderer is no less dangerous for being necessitated by external causes. He must be stopped by stronger affects, not absolved.

The best state: one that promotes liberty, tolerance, and the pursuit of knowledge, reducing the need for coercive control as people become more rational. Spinoza's political conclusion is democracy.

## The free man

Part IV closes with a portrait of the free person:

**P67**: "A free man thinks of nothing less than of death, and his wisdom is a meditation not of death but of life."

This is a direct counter to the Stoic *meditatio mortis* — the practice of contemplating death to free yourself from fear of it. Spinoza says the Stoics have it backwards. The free person is not free because she has neutralized her fear of death through contemplation of it. She is free because she is too busy living — acting, understanding, building — to have death in view at all. The "meditation on life" is not a refusal to acknowledge mortality; it is a full commitment to the actual project of living well.

The free person, in summary:
- Determines herself by her own nature alone, not by affects
- Seeks the greater of two goods, the lesser of two evils
- Acts honestly and in good faith, without fear
- Understands that no one, including herself, acts from free choice — and therefore feels neither guilt, pride, nor resentment
- Knows that what seems evil follows from the necessity of the divine nature, not from any positive force of evil
- Prefers to live in a state and abide by its laws rather than in isolation
- Strives to increase the rationality of others

The last remark of Part IV is both a warning and a self-description:

> "Those who carp at mankind, and are more skilled in railing at vice than in instilling virtue, and who break rather than strengthen men's dispositions, are hurtful both to themselves and others."

Spinoza knew the type. He had been expelled by one kind and tolerated by another.

## Cross-domain connections

**Nietzsche and the revaluation of values**: Nietzsche explicitly acknowledged Spinoza as a predecessor on joy-ethics, self-preservation as virtue-foundation, and the critique of slave morality. His *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*'s teaching of "self-overcoming" is related, though Nietzsche pushes further into will-to-power territory Spinoza would have found excessive.

**Confucian self-cultivation vs. Spinoza**: Confucian *xiū shēn* (修身 — self-cultivation) and Spinoza's becoming more rational both aim at greater alignment between one's life and one's nature. But Confucianism grounds the project in ritual learning and social harmony; Spinoza in purely rational self-understanding.

**Hobbes and the social contract**: Spinoza agrees with Hobbes that the state exists to manage violent impulses through fear. But unlike Hobbes, Spinoza does not think the transfer of sovereignty to a Leviathan is permanent or ideal. The best state needs less and less coercion as people become more rational. Hobbes' pessimism about reason as a practical political force is something Spinoza quietly rejects.
