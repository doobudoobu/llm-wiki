# Wang Mang — The Confucian Reformer Who Briefly Replaced the Han

**Born:** 45 BC
**Died:** AD 23 (killed by a mob)
**Reigned:** AD 9–23 as emperor of the Xin 新 ("New") dynasty
**Context:** Interregnum between Western and Eastern [[dynasty-han]]

---

## Who he was

Wang Mang was a nephew of the dowager empress, a dedicated Confucian scholar, and one of the most genuinely interesting figures in Chinese history — a man who actually tried to implement Confucian ideals in government, watched it all collapse through no entirely fault of his own, and has been maligned by historians ever since because the dynasty he briefly replaced needed him to be a villain.

He rose through court politics during the last decades of the Western Han, serving as regent during the minority of Emperor Pingdi (AD 1–6). When Pingdi died at fourteen — leaving no heir — and the pool of male Han descendants was exhausted, Wang Mang made his move. On 10 January AD 9, at the tomb of Han Gaozu, he "accepted the resignation" of the Han dynasty and proclaimed himself emperor of the Xin 新 ("New") dynasty. Little Emperor Ruzi (aged one) was returned to the nursery.

---

## The reforms

Wang Mang's program was ambitious and idealistic:

- **Abolished slavery** — one of the few rulers in the ancient world to do so
- **Forbade the sale of land** — to prevent concentration of estates
- **Attempted land redistribution** — breaking up large holdings for the peasantry
- **Attempted to restore the Zhou "well-field" system** — equal plots for eight families sharing common land
- **Reformed the coinage** — including reintroducing archaic "spade money" and cowrie shells (disastrous)
- **Established "Five Equalisations"** — a system to stabilize prices and provide rural credit
- **Promoted Confucian scholarship** — opened the imperial academy, accorded the *Zhouli* 周礼 (Duke of Zhou's supposed text) classic status, began formally honoring Confucius himself
- **Rectified names** obsessively — renaming every province, commandery, and post to conform with Zhou-era precedents

The historian Ch'en Ch'i-yün called his reign "the climax of Han Confucian idealism." He wasn't wrong.

---

## The failure of the names

Wang Mang believed, with complete sincerity, in the Confucian doctrine of "rectification of names" — that if things are called by their correct, anciently-ordained names, cosmic order is restored and Heaven's favor secured. When his troops fared badly, it must be because their generals were operating under incorrect titles. When a province revolted, it obviously needed its name revised. When his reforms caused confusion, he pondered deeply and "the blame lies in titles not being correct."

He renamed his two sons to royal status because "without [the empire], the barbarians of the four quarters will be driven away and, within, *zhongguo* will be pacified." He redrew China's geography to conform with an ancient text's description of "all within the four seas" — which required declaring a lake in Qinghai to be the "Western Sea."

This was sincere. It was also, from any practical standpoint, catastrophic.

---

## The Yellow River and the end

In AD 11 the Yellow River broke its banks catastrophically — one of the greatest floods in Chinese history. The entire river shifted course, carving a new delta hundreds of kilometers north of the Shandong peninsula. Millions were displaced. Farmland was obliterated. The registered householders who formed the tax base were no longer at home. Revenue collapsed. Corvée labor couldn't be extracted from people who had become refugees.

The resulting mass migrations generated the Red Eyebrows — a peasant army of starving people moving west toward Chang'an, growing as it went. "Unrest sprang up along the migration routes, where starving peasants banded together to take food by force."

No emperor could have controlled this situation. Wang Mang tried everything: bounties for dead locusts, appetite-suppressing pills as military rations, and most memorably, a man who claimed he could fly. The aviator "constructed his wings from the pinions of a large bird, connected them by pivots, and having covered his body with more plumage, flew several hundred double-paces, then fell." The modern editor notes drily that "this is perhaps the earliest authentic account of human flight."

---

## The end

Chang'an fell in AD 23. Wang Mang was carried to a moated tower — too weak to walk — as fire consumed the Weiyang Palace. His last initiatives included a "General-Causing-Great-Waters-to-Run-So-Extinguishing-Any-Fire-that-has-Arisen" (a title, not a person) and a thousand-word personal appeal to Heaven accompanied by "heart-rending lamentations."

A thousand loyal supporters held the tower until their arrows ran out. The mob forded the moat, scaled the tower, and killed Wang Mang in the crepuscular slaughter. His head was kept as a souvenir for generations.

---

## The historical verdict

The *Hanshu* — compiled by the Ban family under the restored Han dynasty — caricatures Wang Mang as a unctuous hypocrite whose vanity and indecision were personally responsible for all disasters. Modern scholars have mostly rejected this. The floods would have destroyed any government. Many of his policies were well-intentioned. His Confucian scholarship was genuine. The Ban family had personal reasons to hate him (he'd dismissed one of them from office) and dynastic reasons to delegitimize his reign.

Wang Mang's Xin dynasty is excluded from China's official dynastic succession to this day. He is the only emperor in Chinese history who transferred the Mandate peacefully — through portents and political maneuvering rather than military conquest — and the only one whose dynasty was subsequently treated as if it had never legitimately existed.

What's most interesting about him is the gap between his intentions and the outcome: a man who genuinely believed that getting the names right would fix everything, undone by a river that didn't care about names at all.
