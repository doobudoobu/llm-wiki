# Yü the Great (禹)

**Role:** Flood hero; founder of the Xia dynasty; greatest savior in the Chinese mythological tradition  
**Father:** Kun (failed flood hero, killed by the fire god)  
**Birth:** Born from Kun's undecaying corpse  
**Walk:** The "Yü walk" — a lumbering side-shuffle caused by a body broken by ten years of flood labor

Yü is the greatest single mythological figure in the Chinese tradition — not in terms of cosmic power (he isn't a creator god), but in terms of narrative importance. Nearly every major classical text praises him. He never did anything wrong. He is the ideal Chinese hero: selfless, tireless, and successful.

---

## His father's failure and his birth

The flood myth is told across two generations, and the contrast between them is one of the great structural binaries in Chinese mythology.

**Kun** — Yü's father — was the first to attempt flood control. He stole God's "self-renewing soil" (*hsi-jang*) to dam the waters without divine permission. God ordered Chu Yung, the fire god, to execute Kun at Feather Mountain. But Kun's corpse didn't decompose for three years. When shamans finally cut it open, Yü burst from his belly.

The cosmological reading: Kun had to fail and die for Yü to be born. Kun is the necessary sacrifice — the broken egg.

> If Kun was not fit to control the flood, why was he entrusted with this task? . . . Lord Yü issued from Kun's belly. How did he metamorphose? (*Ch'u Tz'u*, "Questions of Heaven")

(The questions don't answer themselves — they're sacred riddles. But the implied answer to the last is: he transformed because he was divine.)

---

## Ten years without going home

Yü succeeded where his father failed, not by force but by method. Rather than damming the floodwaters (Kun's approach), he channeled them — digging, dredging, building drainage networks across the Nine Provinces. The labor was total:

> For ten years he did not visit his home, and no nails grew on his hands, no hair grew on his shanks. He caught an illness that made his body shrivel in half, so that when he walked he could not lift one leg past the other, and people called it "the Yü walk." (*Shih Tzu*)

He surveyed the known world: Bird Valley, Green Mound, the Land of Black-Teeth, the Lands of Crossed Toes, the Feathered Men, Naked People, the Land of Never Die. He was anxious for the black-haired people. His face became pitch black. He sought out wise men everywhere. "There was nowhere he neglected to travel."

The **Yü walk** — a lumbering gait caused by his shriveled half-body — became a ritual shamanic dance. Participants enacted his exhaustion as a form of sacred imitation.

---

## What he accomplished

The Nine Provinces were standardized. The Nine Mountains were deforested and put down for arable land. The Nine Rivers were dredged. The Nine Marshes were banked up. The Four Seas had their courses opened freely. All soils were compared and classified. Their land values and revenues were carefully controlled.

This is the mythological origin of the Chinese concept of the state as hydraulic manager. The ruler's legitimacy is tied to flood control — the one who controls water controls civilization. This idea runs from Yü through every dynasty.

## His strange birth and dragon-taming

Yü's mother was the T'u-shan girl. In the myth, Yü had transformed into a bear while working on a mountain (bears were associated with earth-moving) and ordered his wife not to look. She did, was horrified, and fled. She turned to stone on the mountainside, carrying his son in her womb. Yü caught up and ordered the stone to give him his son. The stone split open on the north side, and K'ai was born.

K'ai — whose name means "to open" — became the first lord of the Hsia and stole the music of Heaven (or received it, depending on the version). The flaw in K'ai's birth became his gift to the world: music.

## Binary opposites: Kun and Yü

The Kun–Yü myth cycle is ideal for Lévi-Straussian structural analysis (Birrell invokes this explicitly). The oppositions:

| Kun | Yü |
|-----|----|
| Fails | Succeeds |
| Disobeys God | Has divine authorization |
| Dies | Lives |
| Steals | Is given |
| Father | Son |
| Beginning | Completion |

Kun must fail for Yü to succeed. The two are inseparable: one story, told across two generations.

## The Yü tradition and the Xia dynasty

Yü's flood success made him the founder of the Xia dynasty — the first dynasty in Chinese historical tradition. See [[dynasty-xia]] for the archaeological debate over whether the Xia existed and whether Erlitou represents Yü's civilization.
