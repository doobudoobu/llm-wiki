# Timeline of Chinese Dynastic History

*Updated as sources are ingested. Dates are approximate — ancient Chinese chronology is contested for early periods.*

---

## Pre-imperial period

| Date | Event / Dynasty | Notes |
|------|----------------|-------|
| ~10,000 BC | Rice domestication begins (Yangzi basin) | Evidence from Hunan; earlier than previously thought by 2,000+ years |
| ~6500–5000 BC | Cishan–Peiligang culture | Earliest well-documented Yellow River farming; millet staple; small settlements 1–6 ha |
| ~6500 BC | Jiahu site (southern Henan) | Millet farmers with rice grains + earliest divination tortoiseshell (2 graphs); Neolithic precursor to oracle bones |
| ~5000–3000 BC | Yangshao culture | Painted red pottery; Yellow River basin; Jiangzhai village (~500 people, circular moat, 5 clan clusters) |
| ~3000–2000 BC | Longshan culture | Rammed-earth walled towns; 3-class stratification; Taosi largest site (280 ha, solar observatory) |
| ~2600–2100 BC | **Taosi** (Shanxi) | Largest Longshan town; solar observatory (one of world's oldest confirmed); copper bell cast in clay molds |
| ~2000 BC | Liangzhu culture vanishes | Advanced jade culture of Yangzi Delta disappears suddenly; cause unknown |
| ~2000 BC | Tarim Mummies | Caucasoid people (proto-Tocharian) in Xinjiang; possibly transmitted horses and chariots to "core" China |
| **c. 2100–1600 BC** | **Xia dynasty (traditional)** | Possibly historical; founder Great Yu (flood-tamer); archaeological candidate: Erlitou; debate unresolved |
| **c. 1900–1600 BC** | **Erlitou culture** | First state-level society in China; 500 ha; palace foundations; bronze casting; wheeled vehicle tracks (earliest in China) |
| **c. 1600–1415 BC** | **Erligang expansion** | Shang bronzes and pottery spread across China; Panlongcheng outpost (Hubei); first geo-cultural unity |
| **c. 1554 BC** | **Shang Dynasty founded** | Tang (Da Yi) overthrows Xia; capital at Bo; subsequent capital moves include Zhengzhou, then Anyang |
| **c. 1750–1040 BC** | **Shang dynasty** | Oracle bones; bronze industry; Anyang capital (final phase c. 1250–1046 BC); human sacrifice |
| c. 1250 BC | Huanbei Shang City (Anyang) | Discovered 1999; pre-oracle-bone Anyang center; establishes "Middle Shang" period |
| c. 1200 BC | Lady Hao active (Wu Ding's reign) | Senior consort, military commander; leads army of 10,000+; tomb discovered 1975, unlooted; 468 bronzes, 755 jades |
| c. 1200 BC | Mother Wu cauldron cast | 875 kg, 1.33 m high — largest bronze vessel in the world; required 1,000+ simultaneous artisans |
| c. 1250–1046 BC | Anyang period (late Shang) | Royal cemetery; 30,000+ human sacrifice victims; oracle bone archive; 100,000+ bones recovered |
| **c. 1045 BC** | **Battle of Muye — Zhou overthrow Shang** | King Wu of Zhou defeats Shang; last Shang king commits suicide |
| **c. 1045–771 BC** | **Western Zhou** | Feudal expansion; Mandate of Heaven articulated by Duke of Zhou; early kings idealized |
| 841 BC | First absolute date in Chinese history | A solar eclipse recorded in texts and astronomically confirmed |
| 771 BC | Sack of Zhou capital; Western Zhou ends | King You ignores vassal calls for help; killed by Xianyun attackers; court flees east to Luoyang |
| **770–481 BC** | **Spring and Autumn period** | ~148 semi-sovereign entities compete; Zhou kings ceremonial; Confucius |
| 636 BC | Jin hegemon recognized | Duke Wen of Jin (Chonger) recognized as *ba* (overlord) by Zhou king after nineteen years of exile |
| **551–479 BC** | **Confucius** | From Lu state; travels the central states seeking employment; dies a failure; eventually the most influential thinker in Chinese history |
| **481–221 BC** | **Warring States period** | 256 recorded wars; seven major states; standing armies; crossbow; Legalism; Qin rises |
| 433 BC | Tomb of Marquis Yi of Zeng | Sixty-five-bell bronze musical ensemble; 10 tonnes of bronze grave goods; excavated 1978 |
| ~400s–300s BC | Qin adopts Legalism | Shang Yang's reforms: universal conscription, collective punishment, merit-based promotion, standardized weights |
| 356 BC | Shang Yang's reforms in Qin | Administrative overhaul that makes Qin the dominant military power |
| 316 BC | Qin conquers Sichuan | Stone Cattle Road ruse; doubles Qin's resources; strategic leverage over Chu |
| ~270 BC | Li Bing builds Dujiangyan irrigation works | Diverts the Min River; still in use; UNESCO World Heritage Site |
| 256 BC | Last Zhou king submits to Qin | End of the Zhou dynasty; nine sacred cauldrons pass to Qin |

## Qin dynasty (221–206 BC)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| 247 BC | King Zheng inherits Qin throne (age 13) | Minority period; officials manage state |
| 238 BC | Zheng takes personal power | Eliminates rivals; Lao Ai rebellion crushed |
| 221 BC | **Qin unifies China** | Six remaining warring states absorbed; King Zheng becomes Qin Shi Huangdi, "First Emperor" |
| 221 BC | Administrative reforms | 36 commanderies; standardized script, currency, weights, axle widths, law |
| 221 BC | Meng Tian sent north | Builds northern "long walls" and roads across the Ordos; forerunner of the Great Wall |
| 219 BC | Li Bing's Hunan canal begun | Links Yangzi to Pearl River system; still exists |
| 219 BC | First expedition to "Islands of Paradise" | Several hundred boys and girls dispatched; never return; legend says they reached Japan |
| 213 BC | **Book burning** | Li Si's memorial; all books except medicine, divination, agriculture destroyed; 460 scholars executed |
| 210 BC | **Qin Shi Huangdi dies** | Dies on fifth imperial tour; death concealed; forged succession |
| 206 BC | **Qin dynasty collapses** | Civil war; Chu rebel Liu Bang (future Han founder) takes Xianyang |
| 1974 | Terracotta Army discovered | Well-diggers find thousands of clay soldiers in pits outside the emperor's tomb; Sima Qian never mentioned them |

## Han dynasty (206 BC – AD 220)

### Western Han (206 BC – AD 9)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| 202 BC | **Battle of Gaixia — Liu Bang defeats Xiang Yu** | Han forces 3:1; Xiang Yu surrounded, sings farewell, rides to his death; Liu Bang becomes emperor |
| 202 BC | **Han dynasty founded; capital Chang'an** | Liu Bang = Han Gaozu; first commoner emperor in Chinese history |
| 200 BC | **Battle of Pingcheng — Han humiliation** | Gaozu surrounded by Xiongnu in winter; escapes; first *heqin* treaty pays annual tribute to Maodun |
| 192 BC | Maodun writes to Dowager Empress Lü | Proposes marriage "as two lonely rulers"; Lü deflects with diplomatic humility |
| 180–157 BC | Reigns of Wendi and Jingdi | "Golden age" — low taxes, reduced punishments, population recovery; Han population nearly doubles |
| 141 BC | **Han Wudi takes the throne** | 54-year reign (to 87 BC); will transform the dynasty's character |
| 138 BC | **Zhang Qian sent west to find the Yuezhi** | Captured by Xiongnu for a decade; escapes; reaches Bactria; returns 126 BC to report other great civilizations |
| 136 BC | Confucian imperial academy established | 50 erudites appointed to interpret five canonical texts; earliest form of civil service examination |
| 129 BC | **Wudi ends *heqin* — wars against Xiongnu begin** | First of many campaigns; up to 90% casualties per expedition |
| 127 BC | General Wei Qing reclaims the Ordos | First major Han military victory over Xiongnu since dynasty's founding |
| 119 BC | **Han armies cross the Gobi into Outer Mongolia** | Peak anti-Xiongnu expansion; Silk Road corridor secured to Dunhuang |
| 119 BC | Zhang Qian sent west again | 300 men, gifts worth "100 billion cash"; Silk Road opens; Central Asian horses, grapes, alfalfa arrive in China |
| 99 BC | **Li Ling's surrender; Sima Qian castrated** | Li Ling fights 5,000 vs. impossible odds; surrenders; Sima Qian intercedes; charged with deceiving emperor; chooses castration to finish the *Shiji* |
| 87 BC | Wudi dies | Leaves exhausted treasury, depleted population, 7-year-old heir; regent Huo Guang holds real power for decades |
| 51 BC | **Southern Xiongnu submit to Han** | Succession dispute fractures confederacy; one claimant becomes subsidized Han client; annual subsidy eventually reaches 100 million cash |

### Xin dynasty / interregnum (AD 9–23)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| AD 9 | **Wang Mang founds Xin ("New") dynasty** | Takes throne on 10 January at Han Gaozu's tomb; "accepts resignation" of Han dynasty |
| AD 9–23 | Wang Mang's reforms | Abolishes slavery; forbids land sale; attempts Zhou "well-field" redistribution; obsessive name-rectification |
| AD 9–23 | Currency chaos | Reintroduces archaic spade money and cowrie shells alongside cash; markets seize up |
| AD 11 | **Yellow River catastrophic flood** | River shifts course by hundreds of kilometers; millions displaced; tax base collapses |
| AD 18 | Red Eyebrows rebellion begins | Starving refugees banding together; grows as it moves toward Chang'an |
| AD 23 | **Wang Mang killed; Xin dynasty ends** | Chang'an falls; Wang Mang carried to tower; writes thousand-word letter to Heaven; mob kills him; head kept as souvenir |

### Eastern Han (AD 25–220)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| AD 25 | **Eastern Han re-founded; capital Luoyang** | Han Guangwudi restores Han rule; dynasty moves east |
| AD 91 | Northern Xiongnu dispersed by Xianbei | Former Xiongnu subjects become the new steppe threat; Southern Xiongnu settled within frontier |
| AD 166 | First Roman embassy (or merchants) reaches Han | Identified as envoys from "Da Qin" (Rome); Parthians had long blocked direct contact |
| AD 184 | **Yellow Turban rebellion** | Daoist-millenarian mass movement; hundreds of thousands rise simultaneously across multiple provinces |
| AD 189 | **Dong Zhuo seizes the capital** | Warlord takes Chang'an; installs puppet emperor; Han imperial authority effectively ends |
| AD 220 | Last Han emperor abdicates | Three Kingdoms period begins (Wei, Shu, Wu) |

## Three Kingdoms, Jin, and Period of Disunion (189–581 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| AD 189 | Dong Zhuo seizes Chang'an | Han imperial authority ends; warlord fragmentation begins |
| **208–209 CE** | **[[event-battle-red-cliffs\|Battle of Red Cliffs]]** | Cao Cao's northern fleet burned by fireships; south-east wind turns the tide; three-way division locked in |
| **220 CE** | **Three Kingdoms begins** | Han Xiandi abdicates to Cao Pi (Wei); Liu Bei declares Shu Han; Sun Quan rules Wu |
| 263 CE | Wei conquers Shu Han | Liu Bei's kingdom falls first |
| **265 CE** | **Jin dynasty founded** | Sima Yan overthrows Wei; [[dynasty-jin]] |
| **280 CE** | **Jin conquers Wu; China briefly reunified** | Three Kingdoms ends |
| 290–311 CE | War of the Eight Princes | Jin princes fight over succession; devastates the empire; creates opening for non-Han invasion |
| **311 CE** | **Fall of Luoyang to Xiongnu** | Liu Cong sacks the city; Jin court flees south; Nanai Vandak writes his letter to Samarkand |
| 317 CE | Eastern Jin founded at Jiankang (Nanjing) | Million-person exodus south; northern China enters Sixteen Kingdoms chaos |
| 304–439 CE | Sixteen Kingdoms period | Non-Han states (Xiongnu, Xianbei, Di, Qiang, Jie) fragment the north |
| 386–534 CE | Northern Wei controls north China | Tabgach/Tuoba Xianbei unify north by 439; [[dynasty-northern-southern]] |
| **472–499 CE** | **Equal-field system introduced** | Northern Wei land reform by Dowager Empress Feng; used by Sui and Tang; lasts to mid-8th century |
| **493 CE** | **Northern Wei capital moved to Luoyang** | Aggressive Sinicization program; city of 1,300 monasteries and 500,000+ people |
| 534 CE | Northern Wei splits | Xianbei frontier troops rebel; Eastern Wei and Western Wei; both soon replaced |
| 557–581 CE | Northern Zhou rules from Chang'an | Forges the fubing system and institutional framework Sui and Tang will inherit |
| **589 CE** | **Sui reunifies China** | End of 370 years of fragmentation |

## Sui and Tang (581–907 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **581 CE** | **Sui dynasty founded** | Yang Jian (Sui Wendi) overthrows Northern Zhou; rebuilds Chang'an |
| 589 CE | Sui conquers the south | Chen dynasty falls; China reunified for first time since Han |
| **605–611 CE** | **Grand Canal constructed** | 2,500 km connecting Yangzi to Yellow River and Beijing; still in use today |
| 612–614 CE | Three Korean invasions all fail | Koguryo defeats Sui armies; mutinies; hundreds of thousands dead |
| **618 CE** | **Tang dynasty founded** | Sui Yangdi assassinated; Li Yuan (Tang Gaozu) takes the Mandate |
| **626 CE** | **Tang Taizong seizes throne** | Li Shimin kills brothers, deposes father; begins 23-year model reign |
| 629–645 CE | Xuanzang's journey to India | Monk travels to collect Buddhist texts; 15,000 km; writes *Record of the Western Regions* |
| 630 CE | Eastern Turk qaghanate destroyed | Taizong earns title "Heavenly Qaghan"; Tang empire extends to Mongolia |
| 645 CE | Taizong invades Koguryo — fails | The thing the Qin couldn't do |
| **649 CE** | **Tang Gaozong inherits; Wu Zetian ascends** | The weak emperor and the brilliant consort |
| 655–705 CE | Wu Zetian dominates Tang government | Effectively rules for fifty years; see [[figure-wu-zetian]] |
| 668 CE | Koguryo finally conquered | What Taizong twice failed; done by Wu Zetian's generals |
| 670 CE | Tibet seizes western Xinjiang | Four Garrisons (Tokmak, Kuqa, Kashgar, Khotan) fall |
| 690–705 CE | Wu Zetian rules as emperor | Renames dynasty Zhou; only woman emperor in Chinese history |
| 692 CE | Wu Zetian recovers the Four Garrisons | 30,000 permanent troops stationed in western China |
| **712 CE** | **Tang Xuanzong begins 45-year reign** | High Tang begins; "China century" at its peak |
| c. 736 CE | Chang'an becomes permanent capital | Population approaches 2 million; most cosmopolitan city in the world |
| 745 CE | An Lushan takes northeastern commands | Half-Sogdian general accumulates power from Manchuria to Ordos |
| **755 CE** | **An Lushan rebellion begins** | Luoyang falls; Chang'an falls; Xuanzong flees; see [[event-an-lushan-rebellion]] |
| 756 CE | Yang Guifei strangled at Mawei | Imperial escort mutinies; Xuanzong watches in silence |
| 757 CE | Chang'an recovered (with Uighur help) | An Lushan murdered by his own son |
| **763 CE** | **Rebellion ends; Tang transformed** | Regional autonomy permanently established; western empire lost to Tibet |
| 843–848 CE | Buddhist proscription | Thousands of monasteries destroyed; 250,000 monks defrocked; copper statues melted for currency |
| 874–884 CE | Huang Chao rebellion | Salt smuggler turned warlord captures both capitals |
| **907 CE** | **Tang dynasty ends** | Warlord Zhu Wen forces last emperor's abdication; Five Dynasties begins |

## Five Dynasties, Ten Kingdoms, and Song (907–1279 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **874–884 CE** | **Huang Chao rebellion** | Salt smuggler turns general; 4,800 km march through south China; sacks Chang'an 881; operatic final exit north into the dawn |
| **907 CE** | **Tang dynasty ends; Five Dynasties begin** | Zhu Wen forces abdication; five dynasties in 53 years to follow |
| 907–960 CE | [[dynasty-five-dynasties\|Five Dynasties & Ten Kingdoms]] | Five northern dynasties average ~10 years each; ten regional kingdoms south |
| 936 CE | Sixteen Prefectures ceded to Liao | Later Jin's founder Shi Jingtang trades the strategic northern passes (including modern Beijing) for Liao military backing — a gift that haunted China for two centuries |
| **953 CE** | **First printed Confucian classics** | Completed under Feng Dao's supervision; woodblock printing at state scale; books become cheap |
| **960 CE** | **Song dynasty founded; Zhao Kuangyin** | Later Zhou general acclaimed emperor by troops at Chenqiao; reunifies most of China; [[dynasty-song]] |
| **1005 CE** | **Shanyuan treaty (Song–Liao)** | First modern-style international frontier: fixed border, equal sovereignty; Song pays 200,000 silk + 100,000 oz silver annually |
| 1023 CE | World's first state paper currency | Sichuan's private *jiaozi* receipts nationalized; 1,256 years before the Fed |
| **1038–1227 CE** | **Xi Xia — "Great State of White and High"** | Tangut Buddhist empire controls Gansu horse corridor; [[dynasty-xi-xia]]; annihilated by Chinggis 1226–27 |
| **1043–44 CE** | **Fan Zhongyan's Minor Reforms** | Merit-based promotion, reduced redundancy; reversed in a year; his essay "Be first to bear the world's burdens" becomes canonical |
| **1069–76 CE** | **Wang Anshi's New Policies** | Low-interest state loans, price-smoothing granaries, corvée commutation — "most ambitious reorganization between First Emperor and Communist Party" (Keay); reversed on Shenzong's death 1085; [[figure-wang-anshi]] |
| 1086 CE | Foot-binding becomes near-universal | Rare before this date, apparently general among Han women by 1100; persists 800 years; non-Han dynasties shun it |
| **1115–1234 CE** | **Jurchen Jin dynasty** | Forest people from Manchuria overthrow Liao; sweep into northern China |
| **1127 CE** | **Fall of Kaifeng; Southern Song begins** | Jin armies sack capital; capture Song Huizong and Qinzong; court flees south to Hangzhou; [[dynasty-song]] |
| **1130–1200 CE** | **Zhu Xi** | Synthesizes Neo-Confucianism; Four Books; *lixue*; dominant intellectual framework for next 700 years; [[concept-neo-confucianism]] |
| 1141 CE | Yue Fei executed | Southern Song's greatest general; paddle-wheel warships; murdered by peace faction; "serve the country with utter loyalty" |
| **1206 CE** | **Chinggis Khan proclaimed** | Mongolian tribes united; world conquest begins |
| 1226–27 CE | Xi Xia destroyed | Chinggis's war of extermination; Tangut civilization erased so completely their script became unreadable |
| **1234 CE** | **Jin dynasty eliminated by Mongols** | Northern China falls; Song is next |

## Yuan dynasty and later (1271–1912 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **1267–73 CE** | **Siege of Xiangfan** | Six-year siege that opened the Yangzi basin; Muslim trebuchets from Persia finally break it; [[event-mongol-conquest-song]] |
| **1271 CE** | **Yuan dynasty declared** | Khubilai Khan takes "Yuan" (from *Yi-jing*, "the primal creative force") as dynasty name; capital Dadu (Beijing) |
| 1276 CE | Hangzhou surrenders | Southern Song capital falls with Khubilai's clemency; boy emperor taken north |
| **1279 CE** | **Battle of Yamen — Song ends** | Lu Xiufu straps seven-year-old emperor to his back and jumps into the sea; 800-ship fleet follows; [[event-mongol-conquest-song]] |
| 1271–1295 CE | Marco Polo in China | Describes Hangzhou as "the greatest city in the world"; not believed in Europe |
| **1368 CE** | **Yuan expelled; Ming founded** | Beggar-orphan Zhu Yuanzhang's peasant rebellion drives last Yuan emperor to Mongolia; [[dynasty-ming]] |

## Ming dynasty (1368–1644 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **1368 CE** | **Ming dynasty founded; Nanjing capital** | Zhu Yuanzhang (Hongwu Emperor) — orphaned beggar, Buddhist monk, peasant rebel — becomes emperor |
| **1403–1424 CE** | **Yongle Emperor** | Overthrows nephew; moves capital to Beijing; builds Forbidden City (1406–1420); commissions Zheng He voyages |
| **1405–1433 CE** | **Zheng He's seven voyages** | Treasure ships 130m long; 27,000 men; India, Arabia, East Africa; abandoned after Yongle's death; [[figure-zheng-he]] |
| **1406–1420 CE** | **Forbidden City built** | 980 buildings, 72 hectares; still standing |
| 1415 CE | Zheng He reaches the Persian Gulf | 4th voyage reaches Hormuz; 5th and 6th voyages reach Arabia and East Africa |
| **1449 CE** | **Tumu Incident — emperor captured by Mongols** | Zhengtong Emperor's incompetent campaign; 30,000 cavalry destroyed; emperor sits on the ground and is taken captive; Beijing defended without him |
| 1471s–1560s CE | Great Wall construction | Brick-and-stone reconstruction; Matteo Ricci calls it "unbroken line of defence" |
| **1521–24 CE** | **Great Rites Controversy** | Jiajing Emperor refuses posthumous adoption; backed by Wang Yangming's followers; has 200 protesting officials flogged; 19 die |
| 1557 CE | Portuguese established at Macao | First permanent European base on China coast |
| **1572–1620 CE** | **Wanli Emperor** | Longest reign, least effective; stopped reading memorials; ceased observing ancestral rites; drained by Korean war and fiscal collapse |
| **1644 CE** | **Ming falls; last emperor hangs himself** | Li Zicheng's rebels take Beijing; Chongzhen Emperor climbs Coal Hill at midnight and hangs himself from the Hat and Girdle Department crossbeam |

## Qing dynasty (1644–1912 CE)

| Date | Event | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **25 April 1644** | **Li Zicheng takes Beijing; Ming ends** | Gates found unlocked; Forbidden City falls; six-week interregnum |
| **5 June 1644** | **Manchu Qing enters Beijing** | Regent Dorgon's Banners occupy the Forbidden City; Mandate declared transferred |
| 1644–45 CE | Queue order issued | Han men must shave forehead and wear Manchu braid or face death; resistance prolongs conquest by decades |
| 1645 CE | **Yangzhou massacre** | Loyal Ming commander Shi Kefa; walls too narrow for cannon; ten-day sack; most notorious Qing atrocity |
| **1661–1683 CE** | **Zheng Chenggong (Coxinga)** | Half-Japanese Ming loyalist controls entire China coast; expels Dutch from Taiwan (1661); dies of grief on news of last Ming pretender strangled; [[dynasty-qing]] |
| **1661–1722 CE** | **Kangxi Emperor** | 61-year reign; military pacification of south; Treaty of Nerchinsk with Russia (1689); begins Zunghar wars |
| 1683 CE | Taiwan incorporated into Qing | Zheng Chenggong's son's navy defeated; Taiwan becomes prefecture of Fujian |
| **1689 CE** | **Treaty of Nerchinsk** | First Qing international treaty; Latin as common language; secures northern border; Russia gets China trade |
| 1690–1759 CE | **Zunghar wars and genocide** | Three Emperors pursue Zunghar Mongols; Qianlong Emperor's "final solution" — 40% killed by smallpox, 30% by armies, 20% fled; Xinjiang becomes "blank social space" |
| **1720 CE** | **Qing Banners enter Lhasa** | Establish Qing protection over Tibet; appoint ambans; detach Qinghai and Kham from Lhasa |
| **1736–1795 CE** | **Qianlong Emperor** | Greatest territorial extent; Zunghar genocide; Macartney mission (1793) |
| **1793 CE** | **Macartney Mission fails** | Britain's first embassy dismissed; emperor's letter to George III: "we have no need of your manufactures"; Macartney predicts empire's collapse |
| 1839–42, 1856–60 CE | Opium Wars | Britain forces legalization of opium; Hong Kong ceded |
| 1850–64 CE | Taiping Rebellion | Christian millenarian movement; 20–30 million dead; deadliest civil war in history |
| **12 February 1912** | **Qing dynasty ends; last emperor abdicates** | Puyi steps down; 2,000+ years of imperial rule officially ends |
