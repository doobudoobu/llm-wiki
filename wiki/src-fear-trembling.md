# Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling* (1843)

**Translated by Alexander Jech (Hackett, 2024)**  
**With: *Kierkegaard's Fear and Trembling: A Critical Guide*, ed. Daniel Conway (Cambridge, 2015)**  
**Pseudonymous author: Johannes de Silentio**

---

## What it is

A meditation on Abraham — on what it would mean to actually believe what Christians say they believe about him. The subtitle is *Dialectical Lyric*: it's philosophy written as poetry, rigorously argued but felt. The pseudonym is significant: Johannes de Silentio, "John of Silence," openly admits at the outset that he *cannot make the movement of faith*. He can describe it with precision; he cannot do it. The book is written from the outside of its own subject.

Published in 1843, the same year as *Either/Or*, two years after Kierkegaard broke off his engagement to Regine Olsen without explanation.

---

## The architecture

### Foreword

A sharp attack on the philosophical fashion of "going further" — of treating faith as a preliminary step, already achieved, from which properly serious thinkers advance. Johannes is contemptuous: the ancient Greeks treated doubt as a lifelong task; his contemporaries treat faith, which is far harder, as a weekend exercise. **"It seems to be supposed that they have found it, since they need not have worked at it."** His target is Hegel's system and its Danish heirs, who have absorbed everything — including faith — into mediated, universal thought.

### Prelude (Attunement)

Four variations on the Akedah (the binding of Isaac, Genesis 22). The story God gave is told; then four versions of how it might have gone wrong:

- **Version I**: Abraham, seeing Isaac's terror, pretends to be a monster — willing to kill his son for his own sake, not for God's — to protect Isaac from losing faith in God. He achieves this at the cost of becoming unrecognizable to his son.
- **Version II**: Abraham goes through with it, but his faith fails. He gets Isaac back but can no longer pray with joy; something has broken.
- **Version III**: Abraham's hand trembles at the knife. The act is accomplished, but he is never the same.
- **Version IV**: When they return home, Sarah notices something strange in Abraham's eyes. He forgets what he has done; she remembers.

None of these versions is the Abraham we honor. The whole argument that follows depends on you having felt *why* — on what precisely distinguishes the actual story from each of these near-misses.

### Tribute to Abraham (Eulogy)

A lyrical meditation on what it would mean to praise Abraham adequately. Johannes notes that any serious preacher who actually conveyed Abraham's act — conveyed it honestly, without softening the fact that **"the ethical expression for what Abraham did is that he intended to murder Isaac; the religious expression is that he intended to sacrifice Isaac"** — would terrify his congregation. Any listener who went home and tried to do the same would be condemned as a murderer. The minister who preached it would call the police. This contradiction is the book's engine.

### Preliminary Expectoration

The conceptual heart: the distinction between the **knight of infinite resignation** and the **knight of faith**.

#### The knight of infinite resignation

He loves something finite — a person, a project, a life — with his whole soul. Then he makes the infinite movement: he renounces it utterly, gives it up, accepts the loss as final. He does not whine or bargain; he closes the transaction completely. He lives on after this loss in the purity of his renunciation, which becomes a kind of eternal possession: he can never lose what he has already surrendered. This is tragic heroism. Noble, painful, final.

Johannes says he can make this movement. He calls it his "gigantic resignation." It's hard but it's *comprehensible* — it's what great tragic heroes do. Agamemnon sacrificing Iphigenia. Jephthah fulfilling his vow. These figures can explain themselves; their sacrifice serves the universal.

#### The knight of faith

Does everything the knight of infinite resignation does — and then believes, **by virtue of the absurd**, that he will receive it back, here in this world, in time. Not in the afterlife. Here. He expects the best roast beef for dinner because God will provide it. He looks like a tax collector; he walks with a spring in his step; there's nothing visibly extraordinary about him. You would never know.

What makes this impossible: he holds two contradictory things simultaneously. He has made the movement of infinity — he has truly given it up, the loss is real and complete — and he *also* believes in the finite return. Not as a consolation, not as wishful thinking. As a conviction that defies every rational calculation.

> "He believed in virtue of the absurd; for human calculation there could not be discussion of."

Abraham rides slowly to Moriah. He does not rush to get it over with. He does not delay to stall. He *believes*, at every moment, that God will not require it — and remains fully prepared to do it. He gets Isaac back "with more joy than the first time" because he needed no period of readjustment; he had never fully resigned himself to losing him.

Johannes: **"Abraham I cannot understand; I can in a certain sense learn nothing from him except to be astonished."**

## The Three Problemata

Three questions that all sharpen the same blade: Is Abraham a hero, or a murderer?

### Problema I: Is there a teleological suspension of the ethical?

**The Hegelian position (Hegel's implicit target):** The ethical — the social universal, Sittlichkeit — is the highest expression of the human. The individual is never justified in subordinating it to a private purpose. The ethical has no "outside." See [[concept-sittlichkeit]].

**The tragic hero:** Agamemnon sacrifices Iphigenia to save the Greek fleet. Jephthah sacrifices his daughter to fulfill his vow. Their acts are terrible, but they are intelligible: they sacrifice the particular for the universal. They remain within the ethical and can speak about what they're doing.

**Abraham:** He is not sacrificing the particular for the universal. God is not asking him to sacrifice Isaac for the good of Israel, or for any universal purpose. God is asking him for Isaac — *for God's sake, for Abraham's sake alone*. There is no universal being served. Abraham cannot explain himself because there is nothing to explain in terms that any ethical framework could receive. He either stands in absolute relation to the Absolute — higher than the ethical — or he is a murderer.

If the ethical is the highest, Abraham is a murderer. If faith is real, there is a teleological suspension of the ethical.

**Johannes's answer:** Yes. There is such a suspension. But it is a paradox, not a principle. The religious sphere is not a higher ethical rule; it is a different mode of existence entirely, accessible only to the *single individual* in his singularity.

### Problema II: Is there an absolute duty to God?

If there is, the individual stands higher than the universal — at least in the moment of faith. This is the direct inversion of Hegel: not the social institution but the individual person is the absolute term.

**The corollary:** If someone claims an absolute duty to God, they cannot express it in universal language. The moment it is universalized, it ceases to be absolute. It becomes just another ethical norm. Abraham's silence is not a choice; it is constitutive of what he is doing.

**Johannes's answer:** Yes. But notice how terrifying this is. The absolute duty to God, which stands above the ethical, *looks exactly like* the act of a madman who hears voices. Johannes does not resolve this. The knight of faith and the monster are externally indistinguishable. The difference is entirely interior — and unverifiable from the outside.

### Problema III: Was it ethically justifiable for Abraham to conceal his purpose from Sarah, from Eleazar, and from Isaac?

This is the most literary of the three. Johannes explores the aesthetics of concealment and disclosure, with examples from Greek tragedy, Shakespeare, and folktale. The tragic hero's concealment is always temporary — he reveals himself at the crucial moment (*anagnorisis*, recognition). The revelation transforms and redeems.

Abraham cannot have a recognition scene. If he speaks, he either:
1. Is understood — in which case the act becomes a universal principle (which it cannot be, see Problema I)
2. Is not understood — in which case his speech is vanity

His silence is not a psychological failure; it is the structural form of his situation. The religious stage requires that the individual *not* be able to communicate his absolute relation. The moment you can say it, it's something else.

**The biographical subtext**: Kierkegaard broke his engagement to Regine Olsen in 1841. He never explained why. He told her it was for her sake; he told others conflicting things. Fear and Trembling is widely read as his private meditation on whether he too had received a divine command that could not be communicated — and whether this could justify the silence that must look like, from the outside, mere cowardice or cruelty.

## The Epilogue

Short, sharp, and brilliant. Johannes returns to his Foreword theme: each generation must begin again at faith. Not at doubt — faith. You cannot inherit it. You cannot "go further" from it. It is not a preliminary. It is the highest passion available to a human being, and every generation that claims to have already passed through it is lying.

> "Faith is the highest passion in a person. There are perhaps in every generation many who do not even come to it, but no one goes further."

He ends with Heraclitus: "One cannot pass through the same river twice." His disciple improved this to "One cannot even do it once" — and thought he was going further. He had destroyed the insight by pushing it to its logical extreme. The warning is the same as in the Foreword: "going further" than the truth is not an advance; it is the loss of the truth.

## The Critical Guide's key debates

**Is Johannes reliable?** He is a pseudonym, and Kierkegaard insisted he could not be identified with his pseudonyms. Johannes says repeatedly that he *cannot* make the movement of faith. Some scholars read this as ironic distance — Kierkegaard using Johannes to say what he means; others read it as structural: the narrator's limitation *is* the point. The book cannot be written from inside faith; it can only approach from outside.

**Does the text endorse the teleological suspension?** Or does it expose its horror? Some scholars (Levinas most prominently) read Fear and Trembling as a warning: Abraham may be a monster, and the "teleological suspension of the ethical" is exactly as dangerous as it sounds. This is a serious reading. The book does not foreclose it.

**The midrashic quality.** Jacob Howland argues the Prelude is a form of midrash — Jewish interpretive retelling — and that Kierkegaard was deeply aware of Jewish interpretive traditions around the Akedah. The four variations are not failures; they are ways of approaching an inexhaustible text.

## Why this book matters

**It defines a sphere beyond Hegel.** Hegel's system claimed to absorb everything — including faith — into universal, mediated thought. Kierkegaard says: no. There is a mode of existence — the religious — in which the individual stands *alone* before the Absolute, without any mediating institution or universal principle. This mode cannot be philosophically justified; it can only be inhabited. The existentialist tradition begins here. See [[compare-moral-philosophy]] for the Hegel confrontation.

**It refuses to make faith safe.** Every domesticated reading of Abraham — he was really just expressing total trust, or performing a kind of radical obedience — misses Kierkegaard's point. If Abraham can be made comfortable, he is no longer Abraham. The terror is the thing.

**The pseudonym is load-bearing.** Johannes cannot make the movement of faith. He describes it, admires it, reaches toward it, and stops. This is not coy; it is honest. The book is written by someone at the limit of what philosophical prose can do. What lies beyond that limit — faith itself — can only be pointed at.
