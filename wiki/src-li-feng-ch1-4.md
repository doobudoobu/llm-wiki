# Li Feng, *Early China: A Social and Cultural History* — Chapters 1–4

**Author:** Li Feng (Columbia University)  
**Publisher:** Cambridge University Press, 2013  
**Chapters covered:** 1 (Introduction), 2 (Neolithic complex society), 3 (Erlitou and Erligang), 4 (Anyang and Bronze Age cultures)  
**Pages:** 1–89

---

## What this source adds

Li Feng is a historian-archaeologist who specializes in Bronze Age China. This book is far more archaeological than Keay's survey — it draws heavily on excavation reports and current Chinese scholarship. Chapters 1–4 cover the period from the Neolithic through the fall of the Shang.

---

## Chapter 1: The field and its politics

Three discoveries at the turn of the 20th century opened Early China as a modern scholarly field:
- **1899**: Oracle bones discovered in Beijing pharmacy → led to excavation of Anyang
- **1900**: 50,000 Buddhist manuscripts found in a sealed Dunhuang cave
- **1909**: Ming/Qing imperial archives recovered

Chinese historiography was reshaped by the **"Doubting Antiquity"** movement (Gu Jiegang, 1920s): argues that received texts about early China were layered fabrications, growing more elaborate over time. This had enormous influence on North American sinology. Li Feng's view: the movement was a useful corrective to uncritical traditionalism but is now mostly sidelined, because absence-of-evidence arguments keep being refuted by new excavations.

Japanese sinology had a distinctive path — Guo Moruo (1892–1978) did his best work in exile in Japan — and maintained high standards through the 1970s by escaping ideological Marxist periodization.

## Chapter 2: Neolithic China (~7000–2000 BC)

The standard story — Chinese civilization radiates outward from the Yellow River's Central Plain — was already outdated by the 1980s. The current model: multiple regional systems developing semi-independently, with the Central Plain eventually gaining an advantage whose cause is still debated.

**Key cultures:**
- **Cishan–Peiligang** (6500–5000 BC): earliest well-documented Neolithic in Yellow River valley; millet staple; small villages 1–6 ha
- **Jiahu** (southern Henan, ~6500–5500 BC): contemporary with Cishan–Peiligang; ten grains of *rice* found — earliest rice evidence in north China; also the earliest tortoiseshell with divination marks (two isolated graphs)
- **Yangshao** (5000–3000 BC): painted pottery; distributed across Shaanxi/Shanxi/Henan; village of Jiangzhai (200,000 m², circular moat, 500 people, 5 kin clusters) is the most completely excavated Neolithic village in China; debate continues over whether the society was matrilineal or organized as segmentary lineages
- **Longshan** (~3000–2000 BC): "rammed earth wall" towns; black/gray eggshell pottery; social stratification; Taosi site (Shanxi, 2,800,000 m²) shows 3-class burial system where top 10% held ~90% of wealth

**Three outstanding finds:**
1. **Taosi solar observatory** (discovered 2003): 13 pillars, 12 slits, all sightlines converging on one spot; used to track solstitial extremes and calibrate the lunar-solar calendar; among the oldest confirmed solar observatories in the world
2. **Lajia earthquake site** (~4000 BP, Qinghai): village frozen mid-destruction; mothers died protecting children; **earliest noodle ever found in China** (millet flour or wheat, overturned bowl) 
3. **Liangzhu culture** (Yangzi Delta, contemporaneous with Longshan): remarkable jade production; city centering on Mojiaoshan (larger than Taosi, near Hangzhou); mysteriously vanished ~2000 BC

## Chapter 3: Erlitou and Erligang (~1900–1400 BC)

### Erlitou (c. 1900–1600 BC)
The first true state-level society in China. Site covers 500 ha (twice Taosi), population estimated 18,000–30,000. Located in western Henan, south of the Luo River.

Key features:
- Palace compound with 11+ earth platforms; roofed hallways; two enclosed palace foundations (F1: 108×100 m; F2 slightly smaller)
- **Wheeled vehicle tracks** found in southern gateway — earliest evidence of wheeled wagons in China
- Bronze vessels (jue-cups, jia-heating vessels, ding-cauldrons) cast by sectional mold method, a Chinese-specific technology not used in the West
- Copper ore likely from Zhongtiao Mountains (Shanxi, ~200 km away); turquoise workshop identified inside palace
- No written evidence links Erlitou to the Xia Dynasty — but the same time period and region; Li Feng's position: the identification cannot be proven *or* disproven; both remain live possibilities

### The Xia debate
The Shang oracle bones never mention Xia — but oracle bones were divinatory records concerned only with current affairs; they show no curiosity about past enemies either. The argument from silence is weaker than it looks. The **Bingong xu** bronze vessel (mid Western Zhou, c. 950–850 BC) carries a long inscription praising Great Yu (legendary Xia founder) — evidence that Western Zhou people already held the Xia/Yu tradition as historical fact.

### Early Shang and the building orientation shift
After Erlitou's fall (~1530 BC), two major cities appear:
- **Yanshi** (1605–1490 BC, 5 km from Erlitou): inner city 1100×740 m; likely a Shang outpost controlling the Luoyang plain
- **Zhengzhou** (Lower Erligang, 1580–1415 BC): inner city 317 ha — nearly 10× larger than Yanshi; Shang capital Bo or Ao?

Critical detail: Erlitou buildings were oriented **6–10 degrees west of north**. Yanshi, Zhengzhou, and Anyang buildings were all oriented **7 degrees east of north**. The astronomical orientation shift almost certainly marks a fundamental change in cosmological-political culture — the Shang brought a different sky alignment.

### Erligang expansion
The Erligang period saw the first true Bronze Age geo-cultural unity in China — standard Erligang pottery found from Wei River valley (west) to Hebei (north) to middle Yangzi (south). **Panlongcheng** (Hubei, 500 km south of Zhengzhou) was likely a Shang military outpost controlling copper supply from the Yangzi region. **Tongling** (Jiangxi) and **Tonglushan** (Hubei) copper mines confirmed archaeologically.

## Chapter 4: Anyang and the contemporary Bronze Age (~1250–1046 BC)

### The oracle bone discovery
Wang Yirong (Beijing, autumn 1899) noticed archaic script on "dragon bones" prescribed as malaria medicine. His servant bought up remaining pieces from the pharmacy. After Wang's suicide during the Boxer Uprising, his ~300 bones passed to Liu E, who published them in 1903 — the first oracle bone publication. Luo Zhenyu tracked provenance to Anyang by 1908 and published 2,000 more inscribed pieces by 1911. **Wang Guowei** then reconstructed the complete Shang king list from sacrificial tables on the bones, matching Sima Qian's list closely and largely confirming its reliability.

### Anyang
Total site: 24 km² (2,400 ha) — at least 45× larger than any contemporary settlement, 200× larger than small villages.

**Palace zone** (south of Huan River): 53 building foundations in 3 clusters:
- **Cluster B** (center): 21 foundations; probable ritual/divination zone; numerous small burial pits
- **Cluster C** (southwest): temples; large sacrificial pits
- **Cluster A** (north): 15 buildings, few sacrificial pits = the king's living quarters

**Royal cemetery at Xibeigang** (north of Huan River, across river from palace): 15 tombs (8 with 4 ramps = royal), thousands of surrounding pits. **At least 30,000 human sacrifice victims** documented; 2,500 sacrificial pits identified, ~1,500 excavated, each with 10–15 skeletons (heads sometimes separated from bodies).

**Huanbei Shang City** (discovered 1999, north of Huan River): 2150×2200 m, oriented 13 degrees east of north; pre-dates the known Anyang palaces; dates to before Wu Ding (Period I) — the "missing" first three kings of Anyang were likely here. Establishes the "Middle Shang" period.

**Industrial scale:**
- **Bronze foundry** (discovered 2003–4): 380×100 m area, 70,000 mold pieces, 100 clay models — largest Shang-era foundry known
- **Bone workshop** (2006): 34,000 kg of processed bones, 17,600 m² total area; estimated output 77 artifacts/day, 4 million items over 150 years; possibly exported beyond Anyang

**Lady Hao** (wife of King Wu Ding, died c. 1200 BC): see [[figure-lady-hao]].

**Mother Wu ding-cauldron**: 1.33 m high, 875 kg — largest bronze vessel ever found in the world. Required over 1,000 simultaneous artisans. Now on display in the Anyang onsite museum.

### The regional network
Anyang was not isolated. Regional centers include:
- **Taixi** (southern Hebei, 200 km north): pottery jars with dry yeast residue = alcohol production center, likely supplying Anyang
- **Daxinzhuang** (Shandong, 240 km east): 14 inscribed oracle shells found — evidence of literacy outside Anyang

### Independent bronze cultures
By late Shang, several independent Bronze Age civilizations had risen:
- **Wucheng culture** (Jiangxi): responded to Erligang/Shang copper-extraction activities; local style
- **Sanxingdui** (Sichuan): walled city ~Zhengzhou-size; 1989 pits yielded 1,900 artifacts including life-size bronze human statue (172 cm), bronze heads with exaggerated eyes, 396 cm bronze tree; **completely alien religious system** from the Shang; yet imported Shang bronze vessel types
- **Lower Ordos** (northern Shanxi/Shaanxi): hu-jars and gui-tureens with animal-shaped hilts; close to Shang but possibly at war with it

## Most interesting new details

- The Taosi solar observatory (c. 2500–2100 BC): one of the oldest in the world; possibly the context in which a writing system was first needed to record calendar sections
- The building-orientation shift from Erlitou to Yanshi/Shang: stars change, cosmology changes, dynasty changes
- Wang Yirong bought oracle bones from a Beijing pharmacy in 1899 while sick — and committed suicide a year later defending the city
- The Huanbei Shang City (found 1999): an entire pre-oracle-bone Shang city hidden under the known Anyang complex, solving the mystery of where the first three Anyang kings were buried
- Sanxingdui's life-size bronze human statue (172 cm, probably held an elephant tusk) — the only complete life-size Bronze Age bronze human figure in China; its religion is otherwise unidentifiable from Chinese tradition
- At Lajia, archaeologists turning over a fallen bowl found the oldest noodle on record
