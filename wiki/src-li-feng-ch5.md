# Li Feng, *Early China: A Social and Cultural History* — Chapter 5

**Full title:** *Early China: A Social and Cultural History*

## What this chapter covers

A detailed scholarly treatment of the oracle-bone inscriptions as windows into late Shang literacy, religion, political structure, and royal succession. Li Feng is both a historian and archaeologist, and the chapter draws heavily on the work of David Keightley (the leading Western oracle-bone scholar) and K.C. Chang, while incorporating discoveries unavailable to earlier syntheses.

---

## Most interesting new material

**The divination mechanics in precise detail.** A full oracle-bone inscription had four parts: preface (date, diviner's name), charge (the matter divined), prognostication (the king's reading), and verification (what happened). The crack itself was produced by a heated metal stick touching pre-cut oval and round holes in the bone's back — producing a crack in the shape of the character *bu* 卜 ("to divine"). The king, not the diviner, examined the crack. Verifications appear in only 1.2% of inscriptions — the king may have preferred oral prognostication to avoid a written record of error.

**The non-royal oracle bones (discovered 1991, published 2003).** 1,583 inscribed shells and bones found at Huayuanzhuang-east, just 300 meters south of the Anyang palace zone, belonged to a prince — probably Wu Ding's brother. These non-royal bones had entirely different conventions: time-of-day markers, irregular layouts, and the prince himself divined (something the king never did). The discovery proved that divination was "a highly secret proceeding" — diviners 300m from the palace apparently had no access to royal standards and vocabulary.

**The High God (*di*) as the celestial Northern Pole.** Two independent recent scholars (Pankenier 2004, Allan 2007) have identified the Shang supreme deity with the Northern Pole — the hollow center around which all constellations revolve — based on the oracle-bone character's derivation from the surrounding star formation. By the end of the dynasty, two kings took this title for themselves: Di Yi (King 28) and Di Xin (King 29).

**The sacrificial system.** Five types of sacrifice (*yi, ji, zai, xie, yong*) rotated through the entire royal king list on a scheduled cycle; completing all five types for all ancestors took one year — called a "sacrificial circle." Human sacrifice victims (primarily Qiang prisoners from western Shaanxi/Gansu) were offered in large numbers: one record offers 100 Qiang prisoners, 300 cattle, 300 sheep, and 300 pigs to three ancestors simultaneously. 2,000+ human sacrifice pits found in Xibeigang.

**Shang succession revealed by the bones.** The oracle-bone king list allows scholars to trace how the dynastic succession rule evolved. The original rule (brothers before sons) held for ~12 generations before powerful kings violated it to keep the throne in their own family line. Primogeniture — the father-to-son rule followed by most later dynasties — was established by repeated violation, not decree.

**The Shang as a hegemonic rather than legitimate state.** The Shang state had no fixed perimeter and no permanent administration beyond the royal court. The Archer-Lords (*hou*) who recognized Shang supremacy did so because the king maintained continuous military display through hunting expeditions and punitive campaigns. When royal power diminished, the network shrank. There was no permanent membership in the Shang state.
