# Lu Yu (陸羽)

**Born:** 733 CE, Hubei province  
**Died:** 804 CE  
**Active:** Tang dynasty  
**Famous for:** Writing the *Classic of Tea* (*Chajing* 茶经); deified posthumously as the God of Tea (*chashen* 茶神)

---

## The abandoned child

Lu Yu was left near a river in Hubei as an infant. A Buddhist monk named Zhiji found him among a flock of wild geese in late autumn — a scene strikingly like the story of Moses, though without any evidence of deliberate echo — and brought him back to Longgai Monastery in Tianmen county.

When the time came for Lu Yu to take his monastic vows, he refused. Zhiji's response was characteristic: he assigned Lu Yu the worst jobs in the monastery — tending cattle, cleaning latrines, plastering walls — in hopes that punishment would produce compliance. It didn't. Lu Yu taught himself to read by tracing characters on the backs of cattle with a bamboo stylus. At thirteen he ran away and joined a troupe of traveling entertainers, working as a clown and sometime playwright.

His name came from divination. A monk cast the *I Ching* on his behalf and drew hexagram 53 — "Wild Goose Gradually Ascending Over Wood." The text reads: "The wild goose gradually advances toward the tree, perhaps it will find a flat branch — no blame." From this the characters for his surname (*Lu* 陸) and given name (*Yu* 羽, "feathers") were derived.

## The exile that made him

The [[event-an-lushan-rebellion]] of 755 CE scattered Lu Yu south, out of the war-torn north and into the Jiangnan region — the lush tea country south of the Yangzi, where the cultivation traditions were deepest. He would spend the next forty-eight years there.

In 757 he met the monk-poet **Jiaoran** (730–799), who would become his closest friend and intellectual companion. Jiaoran was already writing the first serious verse about tea as a spiritual practice. Between them they built the cultural infrastructure — poetry, aesthetics, a vocabulary of attention — that would make tea a major category of Tang literary culture.

## The *Classic of Tea* (*Chajing* 茶经)

Published around 780 CE, the *Chajing* is a small book — roughly 7,000 characters in three fascicles — but it transformed a minor regional drink into a civilizational art form.

It is a **practical manual**, not a mystical text. Later admirers projected religious content onto it; the book itself is precise, empirical, and opinionated:

**Equipment:** Twenty-four specific items are required. Nothing more, nothing less. Lu Yu lists them by name and explains their purpose. Some items can be left at home for rough outdoor tea-making; others are non-negotiable.

**Fuel:** Charcoal is best. Wood is acceptable if it has not been used for cooking (absorbed odors ruin the water). Never use wood from trees that exude oils or gums.

**The boiling sequence:** This is the heart of the *Chajing*. Water must pass through three stages:
1. **First boil:** Fish-eye bubbles appear along the bottom and edges. Add a pinch of salt.
2. **Second boil:** Strung-pearl bubbles rise continuously. Ladle out one dipperful of water and set aside.
3. **Third boil:** The water rolls. Add the powdered tea in a swirling motion with a bamboo whisk. Return the reserved ladle of water to calm the boil. The froth that forms — white, fine-bubbled, clinging to the cup — is what makes good tea.

**Water quality:** Lu Yu rated specific water sources. Mountain spring water is best. River water is second. Well water is last.

The *Chajing* also provides the first systematic history of tea: its cultivation regions, its processing methods, its legendary past (including the Shennong origin story Lu Yu invented — see [[concept-tea-culture]]). By giving tea a history, Lu Yu gave it dignity.

## The God of Tea

Lu Yu was not deified during his life. But within a generation of his death, tea merchants had placed clay effigies of him behind their shop counters and were making offerings. The practice was specific: pour tea over the figurine when business is good; pour water over it when business is bad. The rebuke was gentle but clear: you are failing us, and we are reminding you.

Song dynasty Chan Buddhists tried to claim Lu Yu as one of their own — a devout lay practitioner who had imbued tea with Buddhist meaning. Benn's judgment: there is no evidence for this. It was institutional appropriation of a celebrated figure, not historical recovery. The Lu Yu of the *Chajing* cared about charcoal and fish-eye bubbles, not enlightenment.

---

## Why he matters

The cultural category "refined tea" did not exist before Lu Yu. What he did for tea is what Toklas did for American cooking, or what Johnson did for the English language: took something practiced informally and gave it a history, a vocabulary, a correct method, and a set of values. After the *Chajing*, you could be *right or wrong* about tea. That is Lu Yu's real invention.

His closest peer in Tang intellectual culture was his friend Jiaoran — the monk who first put tea into serious poetry — but it is Lu Yu who was deified, because the *Chajing* was teachable, quotable, and reproducible. Jiaoran wrote about the experience of tea; Lu Yu wrote about the practice of it.
