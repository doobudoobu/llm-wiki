# Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE)

By almost any measure, the Tang is China's most celebrated dynasty — the apogee of Chinese imperial civilization. The largest empire in the world at its peak, the most cosmopolitan city in history at its capital, the golden age of Chinese poetry, and the period when tea became a mass cultural phenomenon. It ended in fragmentation, but its legacy shaped everything after it.

| Phase | Period | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Foundation | 618–626 | Li Yuan (Tang Gaozu); quelling rebellions |
| High Tang I: Taizong | 626–649 | Territorial expansion; institutional consolidation; model emperor |
| High Tang II: Wu Zetian and Xuanzong | 649–755 | Greatest empire extent; Chang'an cosmopolitanism; Li Bo and Du Fu |
| An Lushan Rebellion | 755–763 | The pivot; Tang never fully recovered |
| Late Tang decline | 763–907 | Regional warlords; eunuch power; peasant rebellions |

## The founding: Li Yuan and his son

The Li family, dukes of Tang, were Sui military aristocracy — mixed Han/Xianbei descent, horse-loving, cosmopolitan, intensely capable. When Sui Yangdi's empire disintegrated in 616–17, Li Yuan finally endorsed a popular prophecy about a Li succession, marched on Chang'an, installed a Sui puppet emperor, then received the Mandate himself in 618.

Most of the real work of founding the dynasty was done by his second son, Li Shimin, who massacred rival brothers in a palace coup in 626, deposed his father, and became **Tang Taizong** (r. 626–649). He then rewrote the histories of his accession. Taizong was one of China's genuinely great emperors — expansive, curious, responsive to remonstrance, acutely conscious of history's judgment. His chief remonstrator, the cantankerous Wei Zheng, badgered him about fiscal restraint and the avoidance of war for seventeen years; Taizong ignored him on the big things but kept him in office and publicly revered him after his death as "a sturdy bamboo touched by frost."

### The model reign

Under Taizong: harvests were good, granaries filled, bandits declined, and travel became safe. "Merchants travelling in the wilderness were never again robbed by bandits. The prisons were usually empty. Horses and cows roamed the open country. Gates went unlocked." The Standard History may flatter, but the economy genuinely recovered.

He destroyed the Eastern Turk qaghanate in 630 — picking off their leadership during an internal crisis — and earned the title "Heavenly Qaghan" from the steppe peoples. His monk, **Xuanzang**, set off in 629 on a sixteen-year journey to India to collect Buddhist texts, returning in 645 with five hundred trunks of statuary and manuscripts. Xuanzang's *Record of the Western Regions* remains the primary source for seventh-century India; his fictional counterpart is the hero of *Journey to the West* (also known as *Monkey*), one of the great works of Chinese literature.

Taizong's one notable failure was Korea — he launched a massive assault on Koguryo in 645, got bogged down in Manchurian fortified cities, and retreated before the winter. He tried again in 647. He was planning a third invasion when he died in 649.

## Wu Zetian: the only empress

**Wu Zetian** (r. effectively 655–705, as emperor in her own right 690–705) is the most contested figure in Tang history. She was a concubine of Tang Taizong who became the consort of his heir, Tang Gaozong — technically incest under Tang law. Through a combination of intelligence, ruthlessness, and an uncanny gift for reading political situations, she became the dominant power by 655 and the sole emperor by 690. She is the only woman in Chinese history to have ruled in her own name.

The Tang Standard Histories, written largely by the Confucian bureaucrats she had clashed with, portray her as a monster: she supposedly smothered her own infant daughter to frame the existing empress (this is probably true), kept male concubines of scandalous behavior, and executed opponents with theatrical cruelty. She also, as even the histories concede, ran an extremely effective government for fifty years.

Her genuine achievements include:
- Holding the empire together through the Tibetan assault on western China (they captured Kuqa and the Western Regions in 670, but she recovered them in 692)
- Defeating the Khitan rebellion of 695 "with a calm and decision that were wholly admirable"
- Expanding the civil service examination system and establishing the Hanlin Academy
- Completing the conquest of Koguryo in 668 (the thing Taizong had failed at)

Her fall came when she was eighty-something, bedridden, and had taken two feckless young half-brothers (both named Zhang) as favorites. Their orgies scandalized everyone, including her own supporters. In 705, senior ministers rallied troops, dragged the heir apparent from his chambers, entered the palace, beheaded the Zhangs on the spot — and found a disheveled old empress barring their path. "Rapidly comprehending the situation, she addressed her trembling son and the other plotters in terms of contempt. Then, her half century of power at an end, she returned to bed." She died later that year.

## Tang Xuanzong and the golden age

**Tang Xuanzong** (r. 712–756, the *long* reign; not to be confused with the pilgrim Xuanzang) presided over what the Chinese call "High Tang" — the greatest flowering of Tang civilization and the closest thing to a "China century" in pre-modern history.

For the first decade of his reign he was an exemplary ruler: austere, responsive to remonstrance, careful in appointments, diligent in administration. His chief minister Yao Chong had practically dictated his terms of employment, including bans on military adventurism, eunuch influence, imperial favorites, and expenditure on Buddhist temples. Xuanzong largely complied. The economy recovered, registered households soared from under 3 million to 8.5 million (42+ million people), and the canal system was overhauled.

As of 736, the court settled permanently in **Chang'an** — possibly the most extraordinary city that has ever existed. At nearly 2 million inhabitants it was the largest city in the world. Its markets sold Siberian furs, Indian pepper, Indonesian spices, Arabian frankincense, and Mediterranean coral. Persian and Sogdian merchants financed the overland trade; a "poor Persian" was listed as the number-one anomaly in a contemporary "list of contradictions in terms." Nestorian Christian churches, Zoroastrian temples, Manichaean meeting halls, and Buddhist monasteries coexisted within the walls. Dancing girls from central Asia performed in "a dress of gauze" and beguiled poets "with laughter like a breath of spring." The Tang polo-playing aristocracy imported central Asian ponies trained to drink wine from goblets held in their soft noses.

### The poets

No dynasty was graced by so many poets, or such famous ones. Around 50,000 Tang poems survive. At least two — **Li Bo** (701–762) and **Du Fu** (712–770) — are generally considered the greatest poets in Chinese history.

Li Bo, "a Mozart of words" and "the Chinese Byron": versatile, playful, cosmopolitan. His poem "Fighting South of the Ramparts" captures a veteran soldier describing endless campaigns from the Tian Shan to the northern frontier:
> *Crows peck at their guts and festoon the withered trees with their entrails. Captains and soldiers are smeared on the bushes and grass. The General schemed in vain. Know therefore that the sword is a cursed thing which the wise man uses only if he must.*

Du Fu, "a Robert Burns to Li Bo's Byron": compassionate, attentive to ordinary suffering, formally daring. His poems about the An Lushan rebellion are among the most powerful in any language. In one, he flees Chang'an with his family through the night:
> *Night deepened on Pengya Road, the moon shone over White-water Hills. A whole family endlessly trudging, begging without shame from the people we met: valley birds sang, a jangle of soft voices; we didn't see a single traveller returning...*

## The An Lushan Rebellion (755–763): the pivot

In 755, one of the Tang's most powerful frontier generals launched a rebellion that captured both capitals, forced the emperor to flee, and killed millions. The dynasty survived but was never the same. See [[event-an-lushan-rebellion]] for the full story.

The short version: **An Lushan** was a half-Sogdian, half-Turk general who commanded the entire northeastern border from Manchuria to the Ordos — including the horse-breeding pastures of Gansu. He and the court favorite Yang Guozhong had been maneuvering against each other for years; when Yang Guozhong moved to eliminate him, An Lushan marched. Luoyang fell in 755. Chang'an fell in 756 after a disastrous imperial counter-attack. The emperor fled to Sichuan; on the road, his bodyguard mutinied and demanded the execution of his beloved consort **Yang Guifei**, whom they blamed (not unreasonably) for the disaster. The emperor, powerless, watched as she was strangled with a length of silk.

The Tang recovered Chang'an in 757 with Uighur cavalry. An Lushan's own son murdered him in 757. The rebellion formally ended in 763 when the last rebel general's head was presented in Chang'an. But the dynasty that emerged was fundamentally different: regional military governors (*jiedushi*) were now effectively autonomous; the northeastern provinces would remain outside direct imperial control for the next four and a half centuries; frontier defenses had collapsed in the west, allowing the Tibetans to seize the Gansu corridor and the Western Regions.

## Late Tang: the long decline

The second half of the dynasty (763–907) was, in Keay's framing, a century of imperial dynasty managing the devolution of its own power. Some emperors were capable; none could reverse the structural changes the An Lushan rebellion had created.

The **salt monopoly** (from the 760s) became the backbone of state revenue — at its peak providing half of all tax income. Tea and liquor were added. A new commercial class of licensed merchant-distributors emerged, running parallel to and often more powerful than the regular bureaucracy.

A vicious proscription of Buddhism in 843–848 under Emperor Wuzong destroyed thousands of monasteries and defrocked up to 250,000 monks and nuns — less out of theological conviction than economic desperation, to seize monastic copper statues for currency and monastic land for tax revenue.

The final collapse came through the **Huang Chao rebellion** (874–884): a salt smuggler who turned warlord, captured both Chang'an and Luoyang, and massacred tens of thousands. The Tang nominally survived until 907, when a warlord named Zhu Wen forced the last emperor's abdication and founded the Later Liang — beginning the Five Dynasties period. See [[dynasty-five-dynasties]].

## The Tang legacy

What the Tang left behind:
- The **examination system** as the primary pathway to government office — the model for all subsequent dynasties
- The legal codes (*Tang Code*) that became the template for law throughout East Asia, including Japan, Korea, and Vietnam
- **Chang'an** as the model city, studied and imitated from Japan to Central Asia
- Tea as a Chinese cultural practice (see [[concept-tea-culture]])
- The standard of cosmopolitanism — openness to foreign culture, religion, and personnel — that no later dynasty quite matched
- Li Bo and Du Fu as the permanent touchstones of what Chinese poetry could be
