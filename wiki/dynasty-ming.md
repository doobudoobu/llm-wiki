# Ming Dynasty (1368–1644 CE)

The last ethnic Han Chinese dynasty. Founded by a beggar who had survived plague, starved as a monk, and joined a peasant rebellion. It lasted 276 years, built the Forbidden City, sent the largest naval expeditions in pre-modern history, and then turned inward as Europe turned outward. It ended with a peasant rebel seizing Beijing while the Manchus waited at the gate.

---

## The founding (1368)

**Zhu Yuanzhang** — the Hongwu Emperor — had the most extreme rags-to-throne story in Chinese history. His entire family died in a plague when he was sixteen. He begged at a Buddhist monastery, spent years as a wandering monk, then joined a Red Turban millenarian rebellion in 1352 and rose through sheer military talent.

He took Nanjing in 1356, gradually conquered the Yangzi basin, and in 1368 proclaimed the Ming dynasty — "Brilliant," "Effulgent" — declaring the Yuan's Mandate forfeit. Zhu was the second commoner emperor in Chinese history; the first was Han Gaozu, a comparison he actively encouraged.

The Great Wall of Nanjing — 33 km of brick, largely still standing — was his stronghold and capital. He would be buried there on Purple Mountain.

His reign was defined by terror. Obsessed with controlling every aspect of government, he abolished the chancellery and handled executive functions himself. Mass purges were the instrument of discipline: one case in 1380 produced an estimated 30,000–40,000 executions of the supposed associates of a disgraced minister. Courtiers were beaten in the imperial presence, sometimes to death. No reign of terror in Chinese history compares with it. When he died in 1398, the empire heaved a sigh of relief.

## The Yongle Emperor and Zheng He (1403–1424)

Zhu Yuanzhang's designated heir died before him; his grandson the Jianwen Emperor inherited, only to be overthrown in a civil war (1399–1402) by his uncle — the Prince of Yan, who renamed himself the **Yongle Emperor**.

Yongle moved the capital north to **Beijing** — partly to secure the northern frontier against the Mongols, partly, tradition says, because the floor of the Nanjing palace wouldn't clean after so much blood was spilled during his seizure of the city. He built the **Forbidden City** (construction 1406–1420; 980 buildings, 72 hectares; still standing) and extended the Grand Canal to reach the new capital.

Then he commissioned the **Zheng He voyages**. See [[figure-zheng-he]] for a full account. The key points:

- Seven voyages between 1405 and 1433, six ordered by Yongle
- Flagship treasure ships over 130 m long — five times the size of Columbus's *Santa María*
- Fleets of 100–300 ships, 27,000 men; reached India, Arabia, East Africa
- Purpose: not exploration or trade but *tributary ritual* — demonstrating imperial centrality and receiving submission from distant rulers. Exotic animals (giraffes, rhinos) were trophies of Heaven's favor.

The voyages were abandoned after Yongle's death. Vietnam had drained their strategic justification; Confucian officials had always opposed the expense; the eunuchs who championed them fell from favor. Replacement construction was forbidden. The ships rotted.

Keay's reading: the voyages failed not through bureaucratic sabotage but structural mismatch — they were designed as ritual, not as the commercial-imperial enterprise Europeans would later run. "More money, power, and ceremony applied in the same old way was simply bound to fail."

## The Tumu Incident (1449)

The Oyirat Mongol leader **Esen** united the western steppe and began raiding from Xinjiang to Hebei. The Zhengtong Emperor — great-grandson of Yongle, twenty-one years old, led by his eunuch tutor Wang Zhen — marched out to engage him personally with a supposed half-million men.

What followed was twenty-eight days of strategic incompetence: constant rain, missed intelligence, a minister who kept falling off his horse, ominous clouds materializing from nowhere. The army reached Datong, saw no enemy, turned back. At a post-station called **Tumu**, the wells were dry, rations exhausted, the army surrounded. The Mongols attacked. Half the imperial forces died or defected.

The emperor dismounted, sat on the ground, watched his guards die around him, and was discovered alive by a Mongol prince who suspected he was "someone special." He was right: the Son of Heaven was now a Mongol hostage.

In Beijing the court elected the emperor's brother as the Jingtai Emperor and defended the city. Esen, unable to ransom his prize, eventually returned the Zhengtong Emperor free of charge. There were then two emperors. In 1457 a palace coup ("the forcing of the gate") reinstalled the Zhengtong Emperor, making him uniquely the only Ming emperor with two reign periods.

Tumu shattered Ming military prestige and became the hinge of the dynasty's history. The emperors after it were never as forceful as those before.

## The Great Wall

Most of what tourists visit as "the Great Wall" is Ming. The Qin and Han walls were rammed earth (*hangtu*); the Ming rebuilt and extended in brick and stone, with crenellated battlements and observation towers, from the 1470s through the 1560s and beyond.

The strategic driver was the Mongol threat — particularly after Tumu, and especially after **Altan Khan** rode around the eastern end of the wall in 1550 and burned Beijing's suburbs in plain sight of the emperor. The wall was extended east to the sea at **Shanhaiguan**, the massive gateway that would prove so consequential in 1644.

The Jesuit **Matteo Ricci** (who visited in 1598) called it "an unbroken line of defence with a tremendous wall 405 miles long." His Belgian successor **Ferdinand Verbiest** pronounced it exceeded the seven wonders of the world combined. Neither had seen the full length.

## The Great Rites Controversy (1521–1524)

A constitutional crisis concealed within a liturgical dispute.

The Zhengde Emperor died without heirs in 1521. His collateral kinsman, the fifteen-year-old **Jiajing Emperor**, was summoned from the provinces. The Confucian bureaucracy insisted he undergo posthumous adoption by the Zhengde Emperor — making the dead emperor his father, his own real father an "uncle." Ritual continuity required it.

Jiajing refused. Filial piety, he argued, was the foundation of Confucian morality; no imperial ritual could compel a son to demote his living father. He was backed by a faction of officials influenced by **Wang Yangming**'s philosophy — that moral knowledge is innate, not derived from precedent and scholarship.

The confrontation peaked in 1524: two hundred senior officials knelt at the throne-room gate chanting the names of great Ming emperors in protest. The Jiajing Emperor ordered his Embroidered Guard to beat them. Of the thirty-seven flogged with rods, nineteen died.

The emperor won. His real parents were honored as imperial ancestors. But the dynasty's moral authority — its claim to ritual legitimacy — was damaged in ways that proved lasting. Historians have dated the rot of the late Ming to this moment.

## Wang Yangming (1472–1529)

The greatest Ming philosopher, whose ideas echoed through the Great Rites Controversy and beyond.

Where Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucianism required extensive scholarship to "investigate the principle of things," Wang Yangming argued that moral knowledge (*liangzhi*) is already fully present in every person's mind. You don't need to study your way to virtue — you need to act. "Knowledge and action are one."

He was also a distinguished general and administrator, practicing what he taught. His school — *xinxue*, "the learning of the mind" — spread throughout East Asia and would eventually be seen as proto-egalitarian: if moral capacity is universal, the hierarchy of scholar over laborer is less absolute.

See [[concept-neo-confucianism]] for his relationship to Zhu Xi.

## Population and culture

The Ming period saw an extraordinary demographic surge: from roughly 85–100 million in 1368 to around 275 million by 1600 — nearly a tripling. New World crops (maize, sweet potato, groundnuts) from the 1570s onward made marginal upland soils productive and fed the growth in the south and southwest.

Ming cultural life was less court-concentrated than earlier dynasties. Suzhou was a center of painting and poetry; Hangzhou a literary hub; Nanjing nearly as culturally active as Beijing. Publishing boomed, literacy spread, a reading public emerged.

The **vernacular novel** arrived: *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*, *Water Margin*, *Journey to the West* (1592), and the domestic-erotic *Plum in a Golden Vase* are the "four masterworks of Ming fiction." Drama continued the Yuan tradition and evolved into new forms.

## The fall (1644)

A perfect storm: the **Little Ice Age** brought cold summers, crop failures, and famine from the 1620s onward; plague swept through; the silver supply from the Americas was interrupted; eunuch tax-collectors ("mine supervisors") extorted the gentry. The northern frontier garrisons went unpaid; deserters formed "bandit" armies that snowballed into rebellion.

**Li Zicheng**, a former postal worker from Shaanxi, emerged as the most powerful rebel by 1641. He captured Xi'an (renaming it Chang'an), swept east, and entered Beijing on 25 April 1644.

The **Chongzhen Emperor**, alone, climbed Coal Hill sometime after midnight. He looked at the fires in the suburbs and the unmanned walls. Then he walked to the nearby pavilion — the headquarters of the Imperial Hat and Girdle Department — and hanged himself from a crossbeam. He left a note in his own blood on his robe: "Though a minister die, yet may the dynasty be restored."

Li Zicheng found the city gates unlocked. He held Beijing for six weeks. Then he marched to Shanhaiguan to confront the loyal Ming general **Wu Sangui** — and Wu Sangui invited the Manchus in.
