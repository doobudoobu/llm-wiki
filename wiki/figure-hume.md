# David Hume (1711–1776)

**Full name:** David Hume  
**Born:** Edinburgh, Scotland  
**Died:** Edinburgh, aged 65  
**Main works:** *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40); *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (1748); *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751); *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion* (published posthumously, 1779)  
**Position:** Empiricist, skeptic, naturalist; founder of moral sentimentalism

Hume is the philosopher who most completely follows the empiricist program to its conclusions — and finds that those conclusions are deeply unsettling. Cause and effect? A habit of expectation, not a logical necessity. The self? A bundle of perceptions, not a substance. The foundations of morality? Sentiment, not reason. Religion? Arguments that dissolve under examination. He said he wanted to apply Newton's experimental method to the study of human nature; he found that human nature, examined without prejudice, is stranger and less secure than anyone had admitted.

## Life and context

Hume grew up in a minor Scottish noble family, studied at Edinburgh, and tried law before abandoning it for philosophy. His first and greatest work, *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739), was published when he was 28 and, in his own words, "fell dead-born from the press." He spent the rest of his life rewriting its central arguments into more accessible form.

He was twice denied university chairs on account of his reputation for atheism or near-atheism — at Edinburgh and then at Glasgow. He worked instead as a tutor, a librarian, and a diplomat's secretary, eventually serving as chargé d'affaires in Paris (1763–65), where he was lionized by the *philosophes* of the French Enlightenment.

**Rousseau**: Hume brought Jean-Jacques Rousseau to England in 1766 as a refugee from European persecution — and Rousseau repaid him by publicly accusing him of conspiracy and betrayal. The rupture became a European scandal. Hume was baffled; Rousseau was, by this point, mentally unwell. It is one of the stranger episodes in the history of philosophy.

**Adam Smith** was a close friend. The two were the twin pillars of the Scottish Enlightenment. Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) is deeply indebted to Hume's moral philosophy, as is his *Wealth of Nations*.

**His death**: Hume died of bowel cancer in 1776, and his death became a philosophical event in itself. He faced it with serenity and even good humor. James Boswell, desperate for evidence of deathbed panic, visited him and found none. Adam Smith wrote a letter describing Hume's composure as approaching "perfectly wise and virtuous." The Edinburgh clergy were horrified. For many, Hume's peaceful death was more disturbing than anything he had written.

## Intellectual context

Hume was working in the wake of **Locke** and **Berkeley**, who had taken Descartes' rationalism and replaced it with empiricism — the view that all knowledge derives from experience. Hume pushed this further. If knowledge comes from experience, then everything we confidently believe but cannot directly experience is, at best, a useful habit of mind rather than a known truth.

**Against Descartes and the rationalists**: Descartes had argued that reason alone could establish the foundations of knowledge and morality. Hume replies that reason is "cool and disengaged" — it can discover facts and relations, but it cannot, by itself, motivate or evaluate. You cannot derive an "ought" from an "is" by pure logical inference (the famous is/ought gap, buried as a footnote in the *Treatise* but enormously influential).

**Against Hutcheson**: Hume's moral predecessor, Francis Hutcheson, had argued for an innate "moral sense" — a special faculty that directly perceives moral qualities. Hume's view is both more modest and more deflationary: there is no special faculty. The sentiment of moral approbation is the same human sympathy that makes us feel pleasure at another's happiness and pain at their suffering, operating under favorable conditions.

**Against Hobbes and Mandeville**: Both had argued that human nature is fundamentally self-interested, and that apparently benevolent acts reduce to disguised self-love. Hume's reply: benevolence is real, primary, and irreducible — and even if it weren't, a tiny residue of genuine sympathy would be enough to found morality.

## The core positions

**Empiricism about knowledge**: All meaningful ideas derive from impressions (immediate experience). Abstract ideas that cannot be traced to any impression are meaningless. This is Hume's "fork": a statement is either a relation of ideas (logical/mathematical — certain but empty) or a matter of fact (empirical — informative but uncertain). Anything that falls into neither category — much of metaphysics and theology — is "sophistry and illusion."

**Skepticism about causation**: We never perceive causal necessity — only constant conjunction. The sun rises after night, billiard balls move after collision. We call this "cause and effect," but we are reporting a habit of expectation, not a logical connection. Causation is projected onto the world by the mind, not read off it.

**Moral sentimentalism**: Morality is grounded in sentiment — specifically, in the feeling of approbation or disapprobation that arises when we contemplate actions and characters from a general point of view. Reason cannot motivate; it can only inform. The "final sentence" on moral matters depends on a feeling.

**Utility and sympathy**: What reliably produces moral approbation is usefulness — qualities that benefit people, promote society, make life better. The mechanism is sympathy: we imaginatively enter others' perspectives, and what benefits them pleases us by a kind of contagion. Hume's is an empirical finding, not an a priori principle.

## Why he matters

Hume's influence on subsequent philosophy is enormous and runs in several directions:

**Kant**: Hume famously "awakened Kant from his dogmatic slumber." Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* is, in large part, a response to Hume's skepticism about causation. If we cannot read causal necessity off experience, Kant argues, it must be built into the structure of experience itself — it is a form of human cognition, not an external fact. The whole of Kantian philosophy pivots on this response.

**Utilitarianism**: Hume's identification of utility as the foundation of moral approval is the direct ancestor of Bentham and Mill. He does not himself reduce ethics to utility — he uses utility as a *diagnostic* for what produces approbation, not as a maximization principle — but the intellectual lineage is clear.

**Naturalistic ethics**: The 20th century revival of naturalism in ethics — ethics as continuous with the natural and social sciences rather than deriving from a priori principles — owes a great deal to Hume.

**Metaethics**: The is/ought gap is perhaps the most cited single observation in metaethics. Whether or not moral facts can be derived from non-moral facts is still a live debate, and Hume opened it.
