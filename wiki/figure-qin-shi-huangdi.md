# Qin Shi Huangdi — First Emperor of China

**Born:** 259 BC
**Died:** 210 BC (age ~49)
**Reigned as King of Qin:** 247–221 BC (from age 13)
**Reigned as Emperor:** 221–210 BC
**Personal name:** Ying Zheng (or King Zheng of Qin)

---

## The man in Sima Qian's portrait

Sima Qian, China's greatest historian, writing a century after the First Emperor's death, gave us the only physical description we have: "arched nose and long eyes, the puffed-out chest of a hawk, the voice of a jackal, and the heart of a tiger or a wolf."

This portrait is hostile — Sima Qian was writing under the Han dynasty, which had overthrown Qin and had every incentive to demonize its predecessor. But Sima Qian is also careful and honest enough to note elsewhere that Qin's "accomplishments were great." The picture that emerges is of a ruler who was simultaneously brilliant, meticulous, paranoid, and possessed by a sense of his own transcendence that grew stranger as he aged.

---

## Legitimacy problems from birth

Zheng's birth was shadowed by a story — possibly true, possibly a later fabrication — that he was not actually the son of the Qin crown prince but of a merchant named Lu Buwei, who had planted his pregnant concubine in the royal household. If true, the "First Emperor" was an impostor. If false, someone planted the story deliberately to undermine him.

What is clearly true is that after Zheng's accession at age 13, his mother (now Dowager Queen) resumed an affair with Lu Buwei, who eventually tired of her attentions and replaced himself with a man named Lao Ai — "a man with an unusually large penis" whom he disguised as a eunuch and installed in the royal apartments. The Dowager Queen bore two sons by Lao Ai.

When the young king came of age in 238 BC and the facts "were all brought to light," the response was characteristically total. Lao Ai raised a rebellion; it was crushed; "several hundred heads were cut off in Xianyang"; 4,000 families were transported to Sichuan; and Lao Ai himself was torn apart by carriages. Lu Buwei was exiled and, fearing worse, drank poison. Zheng had established his style.

## The unification campaigns (238–221 BC)

Qin was already dominant when Zheng took personal power. The six remaining "warring states" — Han, Zhao, Wei, Chu, Yan, Qi — were absorbed in just seventeen years. The campaigns were characterized less by the mass slaughter of earlier decades than by overwhelming force and strategic inevitability. Zheng described his actions as corrective: "to punish violence and rebellion."

By 221 BC it was done. The victory proclamation: "Thanks to the ancestral spirits, these six kings have all acknowledged their guilt and the world is now in profound order."

He chose his own title. "Huangdi" — August Emperor — combined the characters for the mythical Five Emperors and the legendary Three Kings. He would be *Shi* (First) Huangdi. His successors would be numbered Second, Third, and so on for "1,000 or 10,000 generations." The line ended after one generation.

---

## The standardization obsession

What followed unification was one of the most ambitious administrative projects in ancient history. In every conquered territory, the same standards were imposed:

- A single script (Small Seal), eliminating regional writing variants
- A single currency: round copper coins with a square hole
- Standard weights, measures, and axle widths (enforced by heavy penalties)
- 36 "commanderies" divided into counties under centrally appointed officials
- All noble families of defeated states relocated to Xianyang

The First Emperor also declared Qin ruled by "the power of water" (per Five Phases philosophy), and decorated his state accordingly: black uniforms, black flags, everything measured in multiples of six, New Year moved to the tenth month. This was not mere ceremony — it was a claim that the emperor's rule conformed to the cosmic order.

## The book burning (213 BC)

When a Confucian scholar suggested reviving Zhou-era feudal grants to loyal kinsmen, chancellor Li Si responded with a memorial proposing the destruction of all books except those on medicine, divination, agriculture, and forestry. The emperor agreed. Thirty days to burn; failure meant tattooing and hard labor.

The following year, reportedly 460 scholars were executed or buried alive.

Traditional historiography calls this an atrocity. Modern scholars are more cautious: books were few, copies survived in concealment and in the imperial archive, and the losses were possibly exaggerated by Han scholars who wanted to cast Qin in the worst possible light. But the symbolic violence was real and unforgettable.

## The obsession with immortality

As the First Emperor aged, he became increasingly preoccupied with cheating death — the one thing his dominion over "All-under-Heaven" couldn't give him.

- Sorcerers and magicians were summoned to provide life-prolonging elixirs (which may actually have poisoned him with mercury).
- The emperor dispatched expeditions to find the "Islands of Paradise" in the Yellow Sea, where immortality was rumored to be commonplace. One expedition consisted of "several hundred boys and girls" — and never returned. Legend says they reached Japan and stayed.
- He made death a taboo subject at court, punishable by death.
- He had a tornado on a hilltop near Changsha treated as *lèse-majesté*: 3,000 convicts stripped the hill of trees and painted it red (the color of convicted criminals), as punishment.
- In 210 BC, he was carrying a crossbow along the coast, looking for the sea monster he'd dreamed was destroying his fleet. He found one and shot it. Then he died.

## The tomb

Construction began when he was thirteen. By his death, a mountain stood over the burial chamber. Sima Qian's description:

> "Mercury was used to fashion imitations of the hundred rivers, the Yellow River and the Yangzi, and the seas, constructed in such a way that they seemed to flow. Above were representations of all the heavenly bodies, below, the features of the earth. Crossbows were set so that they would immediately shoot down anyone attempting to break in."

Modern surveys have detected elevated mercury concentrations in the soil above the unopened chamber, roughly mapping China's river system. The tomb remains sealed (as of 2026).

Sima Qian *never mentioned* the terracotta warriors. In 1974, well-diggers accidentally broke into pits outside the main complex and found thousands of life-size clay soldiers, each with individualized faces, in full battle formation. More pits have been found since. What else surrounds the unexcavated mountain is still being discovered.

## Death, succession, and collapse

The First Emperor died in 210 BC on his fifth imperial tour, far from the capital. His senior officials — including chancellor Li Si and the eunuch Zhao Gao — concealed the death, forged orders eliminating the capable heir Fusu (who obediently committed suicide), and installed a pliable younger son, Huhai, as Second Emperor. The smell from the imperial carriage, Sima Qian notes, was masked by ordering a cart of salted fish to follow it.

The cover-up lasted months. Within two years, the empire was ablaze with rebellion. Li Si was executed. Huhai was assassinated. By 206 BC the dynasty was gone.

---

## The historical verdict

The First Emperor is the most demonized figure in traditional Chinese historiography. He burned books. He buried scholars. He worked hundreds of thousands to death on walls and roads and palaces. He was an "oriental despot" running a totalitarian state.

He is also the man who unified China, created the administrative template that lasted for two thousand years, standardized the writing system that made a single civilization possible across a continent, and gave China its name. The Han dynasty that replaced him kept almost everything he built while disowning everything he stood for.

Keay suggests the comparison to Ashoka of India is instructive. Ashoka is universally revered because only his own inscriptions survive — and he wrote well of himself. The First Emperor wrote similarly, but Sima Qian's fuller account undercuts him. If the *Shiji* had been lost and only the emperor's stelae survived, history might have been as kind to him as it has been to Ashoka.
