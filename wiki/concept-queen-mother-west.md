# The Queen Mother of the West (西王母 Xī Wáng Mǔ)

**Role:** Goddess of plague, death, and immortality; ruler of K'un-lun Mountain paradise  
**Early form:** Wild beast-goddess — tiger's fangs, panther's tail, matted hair; associated with plague and execution  
**Late form:** Elegant hostess at the Jade Pool, growing the peaches of immortality in her orchard

No single figure in Chinese mythology better illustrates the systematic domestication of divine women than the Queen Mother of the West. She begins as one of the most terrifying beings in the tradition and ends as a genteel aristocrat who pours wine for kings beside her jade pond.

---

## The wild form: tiger fangs and plague

The earliest description comes from the *Classic of Mountains and Seas* (c. 4th–1st century BC):

> "The Queen Mother of the West rests on a small table and wears a victory headdress. She has leopard's tails and tiger's fangs and is good at whistling. In her wildness she controls the constellations of the Five Calamities and the Sevenfold Execution, for the Sky God."

She is an agent of divine punishment — the Five Calamities and Sevenfold Execution are plagues, execution schedules, disasters. She doesn't rule territory so much as she administers catastrophe on behalf of heaven. Her "good at whistling" is part of this: shamanic whistling was associated with summoning spirits.

In this early form she is genuinely terrifying — one of the most powerful divine figures in the pantheon, not an object of romantic devotion.

---

## K'un-lun Mountain: her western paradise

The Queen Mother presides over K'un-lun Mountain (崑崙), God's earthly capital in the far northwest. K'un-lun's features:

- 800 leagues square, 80,000 feet high
- The Tree Grain — a sky-ladder connecting earth to heaven
- Nine jade wells, with jade fences
- The K'ai-ming beast: nine-headed, tiger-bodied guardian who never sleeps
- Pearl trees, jade trees, the "Never-Die" tree
- Male and female phoenixes
- Six shamans holding branches of the drug of immortality over the undecaying corpse of a murder victim, preserving him forever

The Queen Mother's specific location is described as the "K'un-lun void" or the Jade Mountain (玉山) — she inhabits a hollow or cave within this mountain system, not the mountain's peak.

## The peaches of immortality: a late invention

The image most people associate with the Queen Mother — her orchard of peaches that take 3,000 years to blossom and another 3,000 to ripen, conferring immortality on whoever eats them — is a **late elaboration**, not an ancient feature of her mythology.

In the oldest strata, she **controls** immortality (and its denial), but her mechanism is the elixir of immortality — the drug that Chang'e stole from Yi the Archer, which Yi had obtained from the Queen Mother. The peach orchard and the famous banquet appear in later Han and post-Han texts, codified in the Tang dynasty, and reach their most elaborate form in *Journey to the West*.

What persists from early to late is the association with immortality and the west. What changes is her character: from wild plague-bringer to gardening hostess.

## King Mu of the Zhou and the first romantic encounter

The first time the Queen Mother appears in a literary meeting with a human ruler, the encounter is strange and melancholy rather than celestial and sweet. King Mu of the Zhou (穆王, c. 10th century BC) reaches her domain after a fantastical western journey. They feast together at the Jade Pool, compose songs to each other, and the King of Zhou promises to return. She sings back:

> "When will you come back?  
> For now I will govern my herds and flocks  
> at these sweet springs."

He delays. He never returns. The song is as much about loss as it is about immortality — the divine queen waiting by the jade pool for a king who promised to come back and didn't.

This meeting is recorded in the *Mu Son Tzu Chuan* (5th–4th century BC), one of the earliest surviving romantic narratives in Chinese literature. But note what the Queen Mother is here: she's still independent, she still has her own territory and her own will, she sings back to him as an equal.

## The Jade Pool banquet: complete domestication

By the Han dynasty, the Queen Mother hosts periodic banquets at the Jade Pool attended by the gods and blessed humans. The peaches ripen on her trees. She sends blue birds as messengers. She receives the emperors, pours wine, gives gifts.

Her tiger fangs and plague administration have vanished entirely. She has become the divine equivalent of an impeccable hostess — powerful, yes, but her power is now about presiding over a celestial garden party rather than executing divine punishment.

Edward Schafer's diagnosis (cited by Birrell): this domestication reflects "the contempt of some eminent and educated men for animalian gods, and partly the increasing domination of masculinity in the elite social doctrine."

## Connection to Chang'e and the immortality myth

The link between the Queen Mother and the moon is through **Yi the Archer and Chang'e**:

1. Yi, after his great deeds, obtained an immortality elixir from the Queen Mother of the West (enough for two people)
2. Chang'e (Yi's wife) stole it and drank it all
3. She fled to the moon to escape Yi's anger
4. She became the moon goddess — beautiful, lonely, sometimes a toad

The Queen Mother is the source of immortality's raw material. She is the gatekeeper who decides who gets access — and when the elixir is stolen and misused, the result is not transcendence but isolation on the moon. See [[figure-yi-archer]].

## The wild and the tame: two versions in one tradition

The Queen Mother exists in the textual record as essentially two different goddesses separated by several centuries:

| Early QMW | Late QMW |
|-----------|----------|
| Tiger fangs, panther tail, matted hair | Elegant, wearing a victory headdress with jade |
| Controls the Five Calamities and executions | Grows peaches, hosts garden parties |
| Wild and shamanic | Cultivated and aristocratic |
| The west as wilderness | The west as paradise |
| Agent of divine punishment | Dispenser of divine rewards |

Both versions are "canon" — they coexist in the tradition because no one systematically purged the old texts. Chinese mythology is an archaeology, not a revision.
