# Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)

**Born:** Copenhagen, Denmark  
**Died:** Copenhagen, aged 42  
**Main works (pseudonymous):** *Either/Or* (1843); *Fear and Trembling* (1843); *Repetition* (1843); *The Concept of Anxiety* (1844); *Philosophical Fragments* (1844); *Concluding Unscientific Postscript* (1846); *The Sickness unto Death* (1849)  
**Main works (under own name):** *Upbuilding Discourses* (1843–46); *Works of Love* (1847)  
**Position:** The first existentialist; anti-Hegelian; philosopher of the single individual, of subjectivity, of faith as the highest passion

Kierkegaard wrote in the shadow of Hegel's enormous system and spent his career puncturing it from the inside. Where Hegel absorbed everything — including faith, art, and history — into universal, mediated thought, Kierkegaard kept insisting: there is something the system cannot reach. The existing individual. The person who must choose. The one who stands alone before God, or before death, or before the choice that defines a life.

He invented existentialism without naming it. He influenced Heidegger, Sartre, Camus, and virtually every 20th-century thinker who made the individual's concrete existence the starting point of philosophy.

## Life and context

Kierkegaard grew up in Copenhagen in a household shaped by his father's severe, guilt-ridden pietism. His father — a prosperous wool merchant who had once cursed God in a moment of cold and despair as a child — believed he had damned his family, and several of his children died young. Kierkegaard absorbed this atmosphere of guilt, dread, and morbid religiosity, and it shaped everything.

He studied theology at the University of Copenhagen but spent most of his time in cafés, theaters, and the lecture halls where Hegel's system dominated Danish intellectual life. His dissertation (*On the Concept of Irony*, 1841) was on Socrates — already a sign: he was interested in the thinker who *didn't* write, who worked through dialogue and disruption rather than systematic exposition.

**Regine Olsen.** In 1837 Kierkegaard fell in love with Regine Olsen. They became engaged in 1840. He broke off the engagement in 1841, without full explanation, despite Regine's distress and his own obvious anguish. He told conflicting stories about why. The relationship haunted him for the rest of his life; Regine eventually married someone else. *Fear and Trembling* (1843) is widely read as his meditation on this — the "divine command" he received that he could not explain to anyone, the silence that looks from the outside like cruelty.

**The pseudonyms.** From 1843 onward, Kierkegaard published a series of works under elaborate pseudonyms — Victor Eremita, Johannes de Silentio, Johannes Climacus, Anti-Climacus — who disagree with each other, explore different life-views, and cannot simply be identified with Kierkegaard himself. This is his theory of "indirect communication": the truths he cares about (about faith, choice, subjectivity) cannot be directly stated and transmitted like information. They have to be encountered, felt, struggled with. The pseudonyms create the conditions for that encounter without predetermining the outcome.

**Attack on the Church.** In his final years, Kierkegaard launched a public campaign against the Danish National Church — particularly against the funeral sermon given for its bishop, in which the late churchman was called a "witness to the truth." Kierkegaard found this obscene: the comfortable, institutionalized Christianity of Copenhagen was not Christianity at all but a kind of social convention that had stolen the name. His pamphlets became increasingly raw and combative. He collapsed in the street in October 1855 and died six weeks later, aged 42, in the hospital.

## The three stages (spheres of existence)

Kierkegaard's central framework — most fully developed in *Either/Or* and the *Concluding Unscientific Postscript* — is the idea that human existence unfolds through three distinct spheres, which are not developmental stages you pass through automatically but modes of existence requiring a *leap* to enter:

**The aesthetic sphere:** Life organized around pleasure, beauty, and immediate sensation. The aesthete seeks interesting experiences, avoids boredom, lives in the moment. This sounds enviable but ends in despair: the aesthete's self is constituted entirely by external stimulation, which is always contingent and always failing. Don Juan. The seducer. The brilliant ironist who keeps all commitments at arm's length.

**The ethical sphere:** Life organized around duty, universal principles, social responsibility. The ethical person commits — to a marriage, a vocation, a moral code — and finds continuity and identity through those commitments. Judge Wilhelm, one of the *Either/Or* voices, is the type: reliable, upright, convinced that choosing the ethical life is the highest human achievement. Hegel's *Sittlichkeit* is the philosophical articulation of this sphere. See [[concept-sittlichkeit]].

**The religious sphere:** Life organized around an absolute relation to the Absolute — to God — which cannot be mediated through any universal principle or social institution. The religious individual does not simply follow ethical norms more devotedly; he may be required to *suspend* them. This is Abraham's sphere. It cannot be explained from within the ethical; it can only be entered by a leap.

The key point: **each transition requires a leap, not a logical step.** You cannot argue your way from aesthetic to ethical, or from ethical to religious. The system does not carry you there. You have to choose — and the choice defines what you are.

## Against Hegel: the single individual

Hegel's system absorbed the individual into larger social wholes — Spirit actualizing itself through institutions, history, and the rational state. The individual is a "phase" of Spirit's development, preserved and transcended (aufgehoben) in the totality.

Kierkegaard's objection: the existing individual cannot be sublated. He exists *now*, in time, with *this* anxiety, *this* guilt, *this* choice before him. No amount of philosophical perspective from the outside — no view of his situation as a "phase" of Spirit — makes his situation easier or his choice less necessary. The system looks at the individual from a standpoint the individual never occupies.

> "The System begins with the immediate and ends with the concrete synthesis. This is objective thought, and subjectivity must be got rid of. But subjectivity is precisely the difficulty." (*Concluding Unscientific Postscript*)

The battle cry: **subjectivity is the truth** — not relativism, but the claim that the truths that matter most (how to live, whether to believe, what to commit to) are ones that have to be appropriated from the inside. They cannot be grasped as objective facts. You have to stake yourself on them.

## The concept of anxiety (*Angest*)

*The Concept of Anxiety* (1844), by the pseudonym Vigilius Haufniensis, develops what may be Kierkegaard's most original philosophical contribution.

Anxiety (*Angest* — also translated dread or angst) is not fear of a specific threat. It is the dizzying awareness of freedom — the recognition that you *must* choose, that nothing determines your choice from outside, and that you could choose otherwise. It is, as Kierkegaard memorably puts it, "the dizziness of freedom" — like looking down from a height and feeling not just fear of falling but the terrifying awareness that *you could jump*.

Original sin, on this reading, is not a historical event but a structural feature of human existence: the moment consciousness becomes aware of freedom, anxiety enters. Adam before the command is anxious not because he knows what sin is but because he *doesn't* — and could find out. The prohibition creates the possibility.

This analysis was absorbed by Heidegger (who read Kierkegaard carefully, rarely citing him) as the basis for his account of Angst in *Being and Time*.

## Key terms

**Leap of faith**: The transition between spheres cannot be accomplished by argument; it requires a non-rational act of will. The leap is not irrational (arbitrary) but trans-rational (beyond what reason can reach).

**The absurd**: What faith requires believing. Not logical contradictions but claims that *defy every reasonable expectation* — that the finite individual matters infinitely, that God became man, that Isaac will be returned after the knife. The absurd is precisely what cannot be mediated by philosophical thought.

**The single individual** (*den Enkelte*): Kierkegaard's central concern. Not the individual as an instance of a universal type (Hegel's individual-as-phase), but the irreducibly particular person who exists here and now, must choose, and cannot be represented by any category.

**Indirect communication**: The truth about subjectivity, faith, and existence cannot be directly stated and transmitted. It has to be provoked, created in the recipient through the structure of the encounter. Hence the pseudonyms, the literary forms, the irony.

**Despair** (*The Sickness unto Death*): The fundamental human failure — not living as what one is. Despair has multiple forms: not knowing you have a self; knowing it but refusing to be it; willing to be what you are not. The cure is to be transparent to God — to will to be the self God has made you.

## Influence

**Heidegger**: Drew heavily on Kierkegaard's accounts of anxiety, authenticity, and the structure of individual existence — rarely with acknowledgment. *Being and Time* is in many ways a secularized Kierkegaard.

**Sartre**: "Existence precedes essence" is Kierkegaardian structure applied without God. Sartre's radical freedom, his insistence that you are what you choose, and his account of bad faith all derive from Kierkegaard's framework, pushed to atheist conclusions.

**Barth and 20th-century theology**: Karl Barth's "dialectical theology" and neo-orthodoxy are directly Kierkegaardian — the infinite qualitative difference between God and man, the critique of cultural Christianity, the insistence on revelation over reason.

**Existentialism broadly**: Camus, Merleau-Ponty, and the whole tradition that makes the concrete, existing human person the philosophical starting point are post-Kierkegaardian.

## The post-Hegelian fault line

Kierkegaard and Marx are the two most influential post-Hegelian thinkers, and they go in opposite directions. **Marx** inverts Hegel: instead of Spirit actualizing itself through history, material/economic forces drive history and consciousness is their product. The individual is still absorbed into larger forces, now economic rather than spiritual.

**Kierkegaard** *individualizes* Hegel: the error is not Hegel's direction but his starting point — universal, objective thought. The truth is not out there in the system; it is in here, in the existing subject who must choose. The individual is not a phase of anything. He is the starting point and the ending point.

This divide maps onto the 20th century almost exactly: Continental philosophy after Hegel goes either Marxist/materialist or existentialist/phenomenological, and these lines run through Kierkegaard's opposition to the system.
