# Mongol Conquest of Song (1234–1279)

The longest and hardest of the Mongol conquests. It took forty-five years from the elimination of the Jin dynasty in 1234 to the final defeat of the last Song loyalists at sea in 1279. The Southern Song resisted the most powerful military force in the world for longer than most states have existed.

---

## Why Song was hard to conquer

The Mongols were cavalry specialists. Their tactics — mobility, encirclement, psychological terror, scorched earth — worked brilliantly on open steppe and against walled cities in dry terrain. The Southern Song posed different problems:

- The **Yangzi River** was an enormous natural barrier against cavalry
- Song had a **sophisticated navy** — paddle-wheel warships, fire weapons, ramming vessels — that Mongol cavalry couldn't answer
- Song held dozens of **fortified cities** deep in the river network
- The humid south was miserable for Mongolian horses and men alike

The Mongols tried direct assault early and failed. Möngke Khan died at the siege of Diaoyucheng in Sichuan (1259), probably from dysentery. What finally worked was attrition, engineering, and naval power adopted from Chinese defectors.

---

## The hinge: siege of Xiangfan (1267–1273)

**Xiangfan** controlled the Han River — the main corridor connecting north China to the Yangzi basin. It was twin cities, facing each other across the river: Xiangyang on the south bank, Fandeng on the north. Both were heavily fortified.

Khubilai besieged them in 1267 and didn't take them until 1273. Six years.

The Song mounted repeated relief efforts. The most dramatic was a night river-assault led by **Colonel Chang** — a hundred paddle-wheel warships carrying a thousand men and critical supplies, running the Mongol river blockade under fire arrows and catapult shot. He broke through once. The second attempt failed.

The breakthrough came when Khubilai's commanders requested reinforcements from Persia. The Il-Khanate sent Muslim engineers who constructed **counterweight trebuchets** (*huihui pao*, "Muslim catapults") capable of hurling 150 kg stones with accuracy and destructive force that Chinese catapults couldn't match. At the first bombardment of Fandeng, the noise alone — unfamiliar, catastrophically loud — reportedly caused the garrison to surrender. Xiangyang negotiated shortly after.

The road south was open.

---

## The end of Northern Song resistance (1273–1276)

With Xiangfan gone, Mongol armies moved down the Han River to the Yangzi, then east toward Hangzhou. The Song court — riven by factional paralysis, unable to field a coordinated response — watched city after city fall or surrender.

**Hangzhou** itself negotiated surrender in 1276. Khubilai's generals entered without pillaging; he wanted Song's extraordinary civilization intact. The boy emperor (Song Gongdi, age 6) and his mother the Empress Dowager were taken north with formal courtesy.

It was a careful, deliberate clemency — Khubilai understood what Hangzhou was.

---

## The last stand (1276–1279)

Two Song loyalists refused surrender: the minister **Lu Xiufu** and the general **Zhang Shijie**. They took a rump court south by ship, proclaiming successive new emperors as each claimant died. Through 1277 and 1278 they conducted a floating resistance down the southern coast, hunted by the Mongol admiral **Zhang Hongfan** — himself a northern Chinese commander serving the Yuan.

The end came at the **Battle of Yamen**, off the Pearl River Delta near modern Hong Kong, on 19 March 1279.

The Song fleet — 800 ships, perhaps 100,000 people — was cornered. The Mongol attack began at dawn. Zhang Shijie escaped with a few ships; most of the fleet was destroyed or captured.

**Lu Xiufu** strapped the seven-year-old emperor to his back and jumped into the sea. The empress, other court ladies, and most of the remaining loyalists followed. The sea closed over them.

Song was over.

---

## What it meant

The fall of Song was not experienced at the time as the end of "Chinese civilization" — the Yuan maintained Chinese institutions, Confucian examinations (eventually), Buddhist monasteries, and the bureaucratic apparatus. Life continued.

But the memory did not fade. The image of Lu Xiufu and the child emperor entering the sea became one of the defining tragic images of Chinese history — loyalty carried to the point of death, civilization refusing to be simply surrendered.

The recovery of the south (and specifically the establishment of a Han Chinese dynasty after Mongol rule) became the explicit goal of the Ming founders a century later. The Mongol conquest shaped Chinese political identity for centuries after.
