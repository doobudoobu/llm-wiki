# Han Dynasty

**Period:** 206 BCE – 220 CE (~400 years, divided into two phases)
**Capitals:** Chang'an (Western Han); Luoyang (Eastern Han)
**Founder:** Liu Bang (Han Gaozu), the first commoner to become emperor — and the last for 1,500 years
**Preceded by:** [[dynasty-qin]]
**Followed by:** Three Kingdoms period (220–280 CE)

---

## Why it matters

The Han is the dynasty that made China. Qin unified the empire; Han made it durable. The administrative machinery, the civil examination tradition, Confucianism as state ideology, the Silk Road, the standard of living for ordinary people — all of these were shaped under the Han. When Chinese people today call themselves "Han Chinese," they are invoking this dynasty. When the *Shiji* 史记 and *Hanshu* 汉书 were written — the first two of China's 24 Standard Histories — they were written under the Han. The dynasty is the lens through which everything earlier was interpreted.

## The two Hans

| | Western (Former) Han | Eastern (Later) Han |
|--|--|--|
| **Period** | 206 BC – AD 9 | AD 25 – 220 |
| **Capital** | Chang'an (near Xi'an) | Luoyang |
| **Separated by** | Wang Mang's Xin interregnum (AD 9–23) | |
| **Character** | Vigorous expansion; Silk Road; bureaucratic consolidation | Reconstruction; palace factionalism; gradual unraveling |

The Western Han is what people mean when they celebrate the "golden age of Han." The Eastern Han is a long, sad story of palace coups, eunuch power, dowager empresses, infant emperors, and nomadic pressure — until the Yellow Turbans revolt of AD 184 effectively ended the dynasty in everything but name.

## The founding: Liu Bang vs. Xiang Yu (210–202 BC)

Qin collapsed within four years of the First Emperor's death. What followed was seven years of civil war between, essentially, two men: **Xiang Yu** of Chu (aristocratic, militarily brilliant, volatile) and **Liu Bang** of Han (a provincial nobody, barely literate, saved repeatedly by luck and Heaven's evident favor).

Xiang Yu was the better commander by far. He shattered Liu Bang's forces multiple times, surrounded him on multiple occasions, and on at least one occasion Liu Bang escaped only because a dust storm turned day to night. But Xiang Yu was politically clumsy — he burned Xianyang, massacred its people, sent Liu Bang to Sichuan thinking it was exile, and failed to build alliances. Liu Bang, drawing on Sichuan's vast resources, built up his forces, lured Xiongnu allies, and gradually hemmed in Xiang Yu.

The end came at Gaixia in northern Anhui. Surrounded, his 800 men reduced to 28, Xiang Yu heard the Han encampments singing the songs of Chu. He wept in his tent with Lady Yu, sang a song of farewell, then rode into the night for a final stand of three impossible victories against impossible odds. He reached the Yangzi — where a boat waited and safety beckoned — but could not bring himself to cross. "How could I not feel shame in my heart?" He turned back, fought on foot until faint with wounds, then cut his own throat rather than be taken prisoner. Chinese chess still pits Han against Chu.

On 28 February 202 BC, Liu Bang assumed the title of Supreme Emperor. He was Han Gaozu — the Han "Great Progenitor." See [[figure-liu-bang]].

## The early Han problem: keeping the empire

Gaozu lived only seven more years. The empire he'd won was held together by compromises: he'd given his generals kingdoms to secure their loyalty. Those kingdoms were now a problem. The entire eastern half of the First Emperor's domain had been parceled out as hereditary fiefs. Han Gaozu began reclaiming them; his successors continued the process.

After his death, power passed briefly through a timid teenager, two infant emperors, and then the Dowager Empress Lü — who effectively ran the empire from 195–180 BC and is treated by Sima Qian as a de facto ruler. She was both formidable and ruthless. When her husband's favorite concubine gave birth to a rival heir, the dowager empress had her mutilated ("the human pig" — blinded, deafened, limbless — and displayed in a privy). Her own son, the emperor, was so horrified that he "gave himself up each day to drink" and abdicated governance.

After the Empress Lü's death, the scholarly emperors Wendi (r. 180–157 BC) and Jingdi (r. 157–141 BC) presided over a genuine golden age: good harvests, light taxes, many amnesties, the first signs of Confucian cultural influence. The 154 BC revolt of six kingdoms was crushed. The Han were consolidating.

## Han Wudi: the Martial Emperor (141–87 BC)

Fifty-four years on the throne. The longest and most eventful Han reign. See [[figure-han-wudi]].

Under Wudi, the Han:
- Went to war decisively against the **Xiongnu** (see [[concept-xiongnu]]), reclaiming the Ordos and pushing into Mongolia
- Opened the **Silk Road** through Central Asia via Zhang Qian's explorations
- Conquered **Nanyue** (Guangdong/northern Vietnam) in 111 BC
- Established commanderies in **Manchuria and Korea**
- Made **Confucianism the state ideology**, with an imperial academy and a syllabus of five classical texts
- Began the **examination system** for selecting officials

The cost was enormous. "All within the four seas were exhausted, the population having been reduced by half," says the *Hanshu* 汉书. Taxes soared, state monopolies on salt, iron, and liquor were introduced, and war consumed men at rates that sometimes reached 90% casualty. But the empire's extent at its peak (~90 BC) would not be matched until the Tang dynasty, eight centuries later.

---

## Confucianism as state ideology

Wudi didn't invent this — the early Han had cultivated Confucian ritual from the start, partly to distinguish themselves from the despised Qin. But Wudi institutionalized it. In 136 BC he established an academy of scholars (eventually 50 erudites) to select and interpret a canon of five classical texts, all associated with Confucius. Qualification by examination for government posts — the embryo of the civil service exam system that would define Chinese governance for 2,000 years — began here.

The Confucian bargain: emperors rule by moral example and ritual, not by arbitrary decree; officials cultivate virtue and expertise; the people follow. In practice, the Legalist administrative machinery Qin had built remained intact under the Han (registrations, conscription, group responsibility, tariff of punishments). Confucian ideology was the lubricant; Legalist structure was the engine.

## The Han bureaucracy

At its height under the Former Han, the empire was administered by ~130,000 officials governing a registered population of 57.6 million in ~12 million family households. Officials were posted from outside their home areas (to prevent local corruption), salaried, graded in an elaborate hierarchy, and subject to surveillance by a separate censorate. Even the emperor could be formally remonstrated by senior officials. Even the emperor's concubines had bureaucratic rankings: a "Brilliant Companion" ranked equivalent to Chancellor; a "Maid for All Purposes" ranked at 100 bushels.

## Wang Mang's Xin interregnum (AD 9–23)

The last decades of Western Han saw three successive emperors fail to produce heirs, ominous portents multiplying, and Wang Mang — a Confucian scholar and relative of the dowager empress — maneuvering into power as regent, then acting emperor, then actual emperor. In AD 9 he "accepted the resignation" of the Han at the tomb of Han Gaozu and proclaimed the Xin ("New") dynasty.

Wang Mang was a genuine Confucian idealist who attempted to implement the Duke of Zhou's vision of a just agrarian society: he abolished slavery, tried to cap and redistribute land, reformed the currency, rectified every name and title, and modeled his edicts on 1,000-year-old Zhou precedents. Then the Yellow River flooded catastrophically in AD 11, displacing millions, destroying the tax base, and igniting the Red Eyebrows rebellion. No government could have survived the chaos; Wang Mang's rectified names and archaic currencies made it worse. He died in AD 23, decapitated by a mob in a burning tower, clutching a thousand-word letter he'd written personally to Heaven. See [[figure-wang-mang]].

## Eastern Han: the long unraveling (AD 25–220)

The Han were restored by Liu Xiu (Han Guang Wudi, r. AD 25–57), who rebuilt Luoyang into a capital of half a million, reconquered the empire, and held it together through thirty years of ceaseless campaigning. But the structural problems never went away:

**Succession crises.** Not one of the last nine Later Han emperors was an adult at accession. Infants, invalids, and imbeciles followed one another while power was exercised by dowager empresses, regent uncles, eunuch factions, and landed clans.

**Factional warfare.** Each empress brought her family to court; each dowager empress protected her faction's interests; each eunuch cohort that put an emperor on the throne collected marquisates and monopolized palace appointments. Purges were routine. "Several hundred" might be executed when a faction fell. Confucian scholars began refusing government service altogether, preferring the countryside and Daoist meditation.

**Nomadic pressure.** The Xiongnu fractured into Southern (who submitted) and Northern (who didn't). The Northern were eventually driven off by the Xianbei, who then built their own confederacy and threatened the Han more effectively than the Xiongnu had. Meanwhile the Qiang (proto-Tibetan) peoples caused constant trouble in the Gansu corridor, threatening the Silk Road access.

**The Yellow Turbans (AD 184).** A mass peasant uprising organized around a charismatic healer who promised a new age of peace and plenty, and who explicitly targeted the overthrow of the Han. In "a few weeks the whole empire joined in rebellion." The suppression required giving military command to provincial governors, which created independent warlords. By AD 189, when the last effective Han emperor died, warlords were already deciding the succession. Dong Zhuo, a Gansu general, brought his army to Luoyang. The eunuchs were massacred. The last Han princes "slipped over the historical horizon into the kinder realms of fiction" — into the *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*.

## What the Han left behind

- The idea of China as a unitary empire, with one emperor, one bureaucracy, one Confucian culture
- The civil service examination tradition (embryonic under Han, fully developed under later dynasties)
- The Silk Road as a permanent feature of Eurasian commerce
- The *Shiji* 史记 and *Hanshu* 汉书 — the template for all subsequent Chinese historical writing
- "Han Chinese" as an ethnic identity — still the official term for China's dominant group
