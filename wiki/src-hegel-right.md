# Hegel, *Elements of the Philosophy of Right* (1820)

**Translated by S.W. Dyde (1896), Batoche Books edition**  
**Full work — Introduction + Parts I–III + World-History**

---

## What it is

Hegel's attempt to show how freedom becomes *real* — not abstract, not inner, not merely private — in the world. The book moves through three stages, each one exposing the inadequacy of the previous:

1. **Abstract Right**: Individual will as bare personality; property, contract, crime
2. **Morality** (*Moralität*): Individual will as conscience; intention, responsibility, the Good
3. **Ethical Life** (*Sittlichkeit*): Freedom actualized in concrete institutions — family, civil society, the state

Each stage is a *correction* rather than a simple addition. Abstract right turns out to be unstable without morality; morality without ethical life is empty formalism that can justify anything; only in *Sittlichkeit* does freedom find a solid home.

The thesis in one sentence (§4): **"The idea of right is freedom."** Not freedom as absence of constraint, but freedom as self-determination — the will that gives itself its own law through rational institutions it can reflectively endorse.

---

## The Preface — and the scandal

The preface is more famous than most of the book. Two things made it immediately notorious:

**The attack on Fries.** Hegel singles out Jakob Fries — a liberal philosopher and popular professor — by name, mocking his "superficial" appeal to feeling over reason. Since Fries had been suspended from his chair after participating in a liberal student rally (the Wartburg Festival, 1817), the attack reads as political denunciation during a period of state repression. Hegel's defenders say he was defending rigor; his critics say he was covering his flanks.

**"The actual is rational; the rational is actual."** The most contested sentence Hegel ever wrote. Does it mean: existing reality is rational and should be accepted? Or: only that which is truly rational is truly real, and contingent existing institutions are merely *apparent* rather than actual? The sentence immediately split his followers into Left (radical) and Right (conservative) Hegelians — a split that produced Marx on one side and Prussian statism on the other. Hegel himself seems to have meant something like: the rational is not some ideal hovering above reality but must work itself out into concrete institutions, and what is truly actual is never merely given but always a rational achievement. But the ambiguity is real, and he knew it.

## Part I: Abstract Right

The will, taken in its most abstract form, is just a bare individual — a **person** in the legal sense. It has rights simply by virtue of being a rational will. These rights are realized first in **property**: taking possession of an external object is the will's first way of making itself concrete in the world.

Property is not about need or use. It is about **self-expression**: "I put my will into this thing." This is why personality requires property — not because you need things to survive, but because without any external embodiment the will is nowhere. (Hegel's implicit critique of slavery: you cannot recognize a person as a person while refusing them any property in themselves.)

From property: **Contract** — mutual recognition of property through exchange. Now there are two wills involved, a "common will" that both share. But this common will is still merely contingent — both parties happen to agree. It does not have universal validity.

From contract: **Wrong** — when one party violates the agreement. Wrong comes in three forms: unpremeditated wrong (mistake about rights), fraud (appearance of right concealing its violation), and violence and crime (explicit negation of right). **Punishment** is not revenge or deterrence — it is the *annulment of crime*. The criminal's act claimed that his particular will could override universal right; punishment demonstrates that this claim was wrong by negating the negation.

The transition to Morality: enforcing right against a criminal requires a will that identifies itself with universal right against the particular criminal will. This self-identifying will — a will that takes its own universality as its object — is Morality.

## Part II: Morality

Morality is Kant's domain: the individual subject who acts from duty, whose inner intention determines the moral worth of acts. Hegel takes Kant seriously here but the whole section is building toward a critique.

**Intention and Well-being** (§§119–128): Actions have both an inner and outer aspect. Morality requires that the agent take responsibility for the foreseeable consequences of action (not just the narrow intention), while also insisting that genuine moral worth lies in the inner intention and not merely the external result.

**The Good and Conscience** (§§129–141): The Good is the unity of the abstract right and individual well-being — what is both universally right and genuinely beneficial. Conscience is the individual's subjective certainty that it knows what is good.

Here the critique lands. Conscience can go wrong in two ways:
- **Hypocrisy**: knowing the good but acting otherwise, then dressing the action in moral language
- **Moral fanaticism** (what Hegel calls "the beautiful soul" or "irony"): retreating into pure inner conviction, refusing to commit to any external action because external action always involves compromise. This is the person who is too morally pure to ever actually do anything. The Romantics, in Hegel's view, fetishized this posture.

Most dangerously: a subjective conscience with no external touchstone can justify *anything*. Hegel notes that the worst historical atrocities have been committed by people fully convinced of their own moral righteousness. The Reign of Terror. Fanaticism. Sincere conviction is not enough — and may be the most dangerous state of all.

The conclusion of Morality is thus its own impossibility: subjective conscience demands an objective content it cannot generate from within itself. That content is Ethical Life.

## Part III: The Ethical System (*Sittlichkeit*)

This is Hegel's great positive contribution. See [[concept-sittlichkeit]] for full treatment.

*Sittlichkeit* is the ethical life of a community, lived in its concrete institutions. It is distinguished from *Moralität* in that its norms are not abstract duties that the individual derives from pure reason, but the actually existing customs, laws, and practices of a particular society — which the individual inhabits the way they inhabit a language: mostly unreflectively, as a second nature.

Antigone appears here as the paradigm figure: she does not deliberate about whether to bury her brother; she simply knows that it must be done, the way she knows the grammar of Greek. That is what living inside genuine ethical life looks like. Moral anguish is a symptom of ethical breakdown.

### The Family

The first moment of ethical life. Its principle is **love** — immediate, felt, unreflective unity with another. Members of a family do not relate as independent individuals pursuing separate interests; they are a *unity*, a single person with shared property and shared care.

**Marriage** is an ethical relationship, not a contract. To treat it as a contract is to misunderstand it — the parties do not merely exchange services but surrender their independent personalities into a new unity. (Hegel is conservative on marriage; he finds no place for divorce except where the unity has already broken down beyond repair.)

**Property**: The family's property is held in common; its management belongs to the man as the external, active side of the family's unity. Hegel is unselfconsciously patriarchal here.

**Children and dissolution**: The family's purpose includes producing and educating children — who eventually become independent, at which point the family dissolves into multiple independent individuals. This dissolution transitions to Civil Society.

### Civil Society (*Bürgerliche Gesellschaft*)

Here is where the *Philosophy of Right* becomes genuinely strange and powerful. Civil society is not the state; it is the market economy — the system of individuals pursuing their private interests, connected by exchange and need. Hegel's analysis is remarkably sociological:

**The system of needs**: The economy works through the mutual dependence of private interests. I produce for others in order to earn what I need from others. The market coordinates this without any individual directing it. Hegel sees Adam Smith's "invisible hand" logic clearly.

**But**: The market also generates **poverty**, and poverty generates a **rabble** (*Pöbel*). The poor do not just lack material goods; they lose their sense of honor, their feeling of right, their capacity for self-respect. They feel entitled to subsistence from society without obligation to it. And crucially, wealth does the same thing at the other end — the very rich also become ungoverned by ethical constraint.

**Hegel's crisis**: He cannot solve this. He floats the idea of *Corporations* (professional guilds that give members identity and dignity, mediating between the individual and the state) as a partial answer. He floats colonization — exporting the surplus rabble — which he immediately recognizes as not solving the problem, only relocating it. The problem is that civil society contains a structural tendency to generate more poverty than its own resources can absorb. Hegel ends the section without a resolution. **This is the crack that Marx walked through.**

**Administration of justice**: Civil society also includes courts and the rule of law, which protect individuals' abstract rights. This is an advance over mere custom — rights are made explicit and enforceable.

**Corporations**: Voluntary associations of people sharing an occupation, providing social belonging, dignity, and purpose for their members. A kind of intermediate institution between the private individual and the state. Hegel valued these as bulwarks against the atomized individual of liberal society.

### The State

The state is the highest moment of ethical life — the point at which the universal and the particular are genuinely reconciled. **"The state is the march of God in the world"** (§258 addition). This is Hegel's most scandalous statement. He does not mean crude statism; he means that the rational state — one whose institutions actually embody universal freedom — is the concrete form in which Spirit knows itself as free.

The state Hegel has in mind is a **constitutional monarchy**:

- A hereditary monarch as the single individual decision-point — not because the monarch is wise, but because decisions must eventually be made by *someone particular*, and making that someone hereditary removes the dangerous ambition that would attend an elected sovereign. The monarch signs laws with "I will" but does not author them.
- An executive that administers the universal interest through professional civil servants (an educated class who identify with the state's rationality, not with private interest)
- A legislature representing both the aristocracy (the landed estates, stable and rooted) and civil society's corporations (the bourgeois element, dynamic and particular)

**Why not democracy?** Hegel's critique of direct democracy: the atomized individual of civil society, left to vote their private interest, does not produce rational political outcomes. The political institutions must be structured so that the universal interest is genuinely represented, not just the aggregate of private interests. Universal suffrage assumes that individuals naturally embody the universal; Hegel thinks this is naive. You need *institutions* that do the work of making private persons into political actors.

**The sovereign individual**: The state is the "actuality of concrete freedom" (§260) — not freedom as individual caprice but freedom as rational self-determination within a rationally organized community.

## International Law and World-History

Between states there is no judge — international law rests on the "ought" without any enforcement mechanism. Treaties are binding only as long as states will them to be. Hegel is genuinely realist here: Kant's eternal peace proposal is admirable but naive, because peace requires an authority over states that would negate their sovereignty.

**War** is not simply evil. It is the moment in which a nation's particular interests are subordinated to its existence as a whole — the moment that demonstrates the state is not merely a service provider for private satisfactions. Nations need war (or its possibility) to avoid the stagnation and ossification that Hegel sees as the alternative: "in peace the civic life becomes more and more extended; each separate sphere walls itself in... and at last there is a stagnation of mankind." This is Hegel at his most uncomfortable.

**World-History**: Nations are the instruments of the World-Spirit. Each rises to embody a particular principle of Spirit's self-development, exhausts it, and falls. The sequence Hegel identifies:

1. **Oriental world**: One is free (the despot) — the rest are natural subjects. Spirit has not yet distinguished the universal from a single individual.
2. **Greek and Roman world**: Some are free (citizens, not slaves) — freedom exists but is particular, depending on who happens to be born free.
3. **Germanic (Christian) world**: The principle that *all* are free is recognized. Freedom is now universal in principle — working itself out in institutions.

Hegel writes in 1820 that the Germanic world, shaped by the Reformation and the Revolution, is the endpoint of this sequence. He does not quite say history is finished — the actualization of the rational state in concrete institutions is ongoing — but the *principle* has been discovered.

States and individuals are "unconscious tools and organs of the world-spirit, through whose inner activity the lower forms pass away" (§344). Nations do not choose their historical role; they embody it.

## Why this book matters

**It changes the problem.** Before Hegel, political philosophy asked: how do we justify the state to individuals who were, in some sense, prior to it? (Social contract theory from Hobbes through Rousseau.) Hegel dissolves this question by denying the premise. There is no individual prior to ethical community; the very structure of self-consciousness requires recognition, and recognition requires social institutions. The question is not "why should I obey the state?" but "what kind of state makes genuine freedom real?"

**The poverty/rabble problem is still live.** Hegel sees that market economies structurally produce poverty, and that poverty structurally degrades the conditions of political life. He cannot solve it. This is the most honest admission in the book — and one of the most important.

**The method eats its own conclusion.** The dialectical method implies that every achieved form will eventually generate its own negation. If the modern state is the answer, the dialectic should eventually produce a contradiction within it. Marx saw this. Hegel's students immediately split along exactly this fault line.
