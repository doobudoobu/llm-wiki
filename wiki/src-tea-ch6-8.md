## Chapter 6: Song Dynasty Tea

### The Northern Park and imperial tribute tea

Song tea culture was dominated, at the elite end, by Fujian — specifically by the **Northern Park** (*Beiyuan*) in Jian'an county, which supplied tribute tea (*gongcha* 贡茶) directly to the imperial court. The Park itself was tiny — a strip of hills about twenty *li* long — but its prestige was enormous. Picking was done with fingernails, not fingers, lest the minute precious buds be contaminated by human sweat.

The production of top-grade **cake tea** (*piancha* 片茶) was grotesquely labor-intensive:

1. Only the smallest single-leaf buds were picked
2. Rinsed four times
3. Steamed precisely (over- or under-steaming ruined the taste)
4. Pressed overnight in a heavy press to extract all juice
5. Pounded with a mortar and pestle into a paste
6. Pressed into a mold embossed with dragons or phoenixes
7. Heated, roasted, blanched in boiling water
8. Cured over low heat for six to fifteen days
9. Slightly re-steamed and cooled rapidly with fans for the final "wax" sheen

Cakes were branded like luxury goods: "Golden Coin," "Inch of Gold," "Peerless Buds of Longevity," "Dragon Buds of Ten Thousand Year Longevity." Counterfeiting was rife — a whole manual, *Essentials of Tea Connoisseurship*, was devoted to detecting fakes.

By the Yuan dynasty the Northern Park was abolished as a state operation. In 1391, the first Ming emperor ordered its unique wax teas discontinued because they "overtaxed the people's strength." The technology had never spread beyond Jian'an, so the wax teas disappeared forever.

### Emperor Huizong on tea

**Emperor Huizong** (r. 1101–1126) wrote *Essays on Tea from the Daguan Reign Period* — a surprisingly practical manual, full of concrete detail on cultivation, processing, and quality assessment. He preferred **white tea**: grown wild among cliff forests, impossible to cultivate, produced by only four or five families with one or two shrubs each. Extremely difficult to steam and dry. "If its steaming and drying are done right, it has no equal."

Huizong was a connoisseur of real authority. He was also, two decades after writing this manual, captured by the Jurchen at the fall of Kaifeng in 1127 and died in captivity in the north. The most refined tea emperor in Chinese history ended his life as a prisoner. See [[dynasty-song]].

### Diancha — "pointing tea"

The standard Song method of making tea was **diancha** 点茶 ("pointing tea"): pour a thin stream of hot water from a narrow-spouted ewer (*ping*) into powdered tea in a shallow bowl (*jian*). Whisk vigorously with a bamboo whisk. The resulting froth — white, creamy, clinging to the bowl — was the point. The darker the bowl, the better the froth showed up. Jian-ware bowls from Fujian, dark and slightly rough on the inside to grip the froth, became the preferred vessel.

Cai Xiang (1012–1068) coined the term *diancha* and was the first to systematize the aesthetics of tea contests: whose tea frothed better? Whose froth lasted longer? Tea contests became a social performance, an index of cultivation and taste.

This method was what Japanese monk Eisai brought back from Song China — which is why Japanese *matcha* ceremony (whisking powdered tea in a bowl) still looks like it does. China itself abandoned this method in the Ming.

### "Go drink tea"

The famous Chan kōan is recorded in *Essentials of the Five Records of the Transmission of the Flame* (compiled 1179–1253):

> The Master (Zhaozhou) asked a monk who had just arrived, "Have you been here before?" The monk said, "Yes, I have." The Master said, "Go drink tea." He then asked another monk the same question. "No, I have not." "Go drink tea." Afterwards, the abbot asked him: "The monk had been here before, and you said 'Go drink tea.' The second monk had not been here before, and you also said 'Go drink tea.' Why is that?" The Master beckoned the abbot to come closer. Then the Master said, "Go drink tea."

The phrase was understood as Zhaozhou's way of saying that awakening is inseparable from the routines of daily life. It became one of the key catch-phrases of Song Chan literature — and also a marker of the simple fact that tea was a daily necessity in every Chan monastery.

### Song monastic tea ceremonies

Tea ceremonies in Song Chan monasteries were among the most elaborate rituals in the monastic calendar, modeled closely on imperial court banquet etiquette. The protocols were written on silk **tea posters** (*chabang* 茶榜) — sometimes calligraphed by famous literati — and posted publicly. The most famous *chabang* was by the monk Puguang; it was later engraved in stone at Jietian Temple on Mount Song.

Formal nodal-day ceremonies (four times yearly, at the seasonal nodes) involved:
- Evening before: decoction ceremony
- Three further days of tea and decoction ceremonies
- Seating plans modeled on imperial audience halls
- Monks divided into east and west ranks, like civil and military officials
- Medicinal pills distributed alongside tea

The protocol for a traveling monk (*guada*, "one who hangs up his possessions") arriving at a monastery was precise: "tea first, decoction later" (*xiancha houtang*). Tea on arrival; decoction when leaving (to relieve weariness from conversation). No deviation permitted.

## Chapter 8: Ming and Late Imperial Tea

### The Ming tea revolution

The most consequential moment in the history of Chinese tea came in **1391**, when the first Ming emperor, Zhu Yuanzheng (Taizu, r. 1368–1398) — a man of peasant origin with no patience for aristocratic excess — issued an edict abolishing cake tea as tribute. Henceforth, four types of whole-leaf tea would be sent to court instead.

The early Ming writer Ye Ziqi had already noted the shift: "The people have stopped using powdered tea from Jiangxi; leaf tea is everywhere." By 1487, powdered cake tea survived only in Fujian and Guangdong; everywhere else, leaf tea had won.

**Ming brewing method** — what we would recognize as making a pot of tea:
1. Boil water in a metal or ceramic pot on a charcoal brazier
2. Rinse the leaves in a colander with warm water
3. Transfer warm leaves to a stoneware teapot; fill with boiling water
4. Pour the entire first infusion into small cups
5. Add more water for subsequent infusions, increasing steeping time each round

Simple, repeatable, improvable. You didn't need twenty-four pieces of specialized equipment. You didn't need to be an expert. Tea became portable and democratic. Roadside tea stalls proliferated. Merchants in Nanjing, Suzhou, and Yangzhou made fortunes.

### Yixing ware

The famous **Yixing** (*zisha* 紫砂, "purple clay") teapots emerged in this context. More porous than regular stoneware, they preserved the delicate fragrance of fine teas that would be lost in less absorbent vessels. Tea connoisseurs — particularly those drinking Luojie and Huqiu teas — favored them. They are still made in Yixing county today in essentially the same form.

### The five famous Ming teas

Ming connoisseurs recognized five supremely prestigious teas, all from Buddhist monastic areas:

**Longjing** (Dragon Well 龙井, Hangzhou): Green, "sweet and delicious," fragrance of soybean flower. The true product came from an area no larger than ten *mu* (under two acres); everything else claiming the Dragon Well name was a fake. Still China's most famous tea.

**Songluo** (Anhui): Invented by the monk **Dafang** from Huqiu monastery in Suzhou, who applied Huqiu's pan-firing techniques to tea he picked on Mount Songluo in Anhui. Color of pear blossom; fragrance of bean stamens. So famous that neighboring counties started calling their tea "Songluo," and eventually even Fujian monks requested Dafang's methods.

**Luojie** (Yixing): Grown in a cleft where a hermit named Luo once lived. Genuine tea produced only at five of eighty-eight sites, at no more than eleven kilograms per year. Leaf flavor like "jade dew." Coarse veins; required careful steaming to be palatable. A catty of the real article cost more than 2,000 cash.

**Huqiu** (Tiger Hill, Suzhou): Called "king among teas" — color "moonlight white," flavor of bean flowers, extremely mild. Hard to store; flavor dissipated rapidly. Best drunk from Yixing teapots. Monks at Tiger Hill eventually found the tea business too intrusive and let the plantation go uncultivated.

**Wuyi** (Fujian): Cliff tea (*yancha*), grown on the mountain itself rather than the riverbank; ninety-nine recognized varieties, distinguished by amounts of sun, rain, wind, and dew. Required three steepings before becoming insipid (compared to two for other teas).

### Xu Cishu's list of appropriate occasions for tea

The author of *Commentary on Tea* (*Cha shu*, 1549–1604), Xu Cishu, compiled a list of when tea was appropriate. It is the most quotable document in Ming tea literature:

> When mind or hands are idle / When weary of declaiming verse / When one's state of mind is confused / When listening to songs and melodies / When songs and melodies end / When closing one's gate and avoiding responsibilities / When playing the zither or looking at paintings / When chatting together deep into the night / At a clear desk by a bright window / In the bridal chamber, or pavilion / When entertaining honored guests / Hosting visitors and concubines / Visiting friends who have just returned / When the weather is warm and fine / In light cloud and drizzle / On small bridges or gaily painted pleasure boats / In thick forests of trees and tall bamboo / When flowers blossom and birds sing / On hot days at lotus pavilions / Burning incense in the lesser courtyard / As drunken guests disperse / When the children have left the building / At pure and secluded temples / At famous springs and scenic rocks.

### Water connoisseurship

Ming connoisseurs were as obsessive about water as about tea. The "number two spring under heaven" was on Mount Hui; pilgrimages to draw its water were a genre of social activity. Yuan Hongdao once ordered a servant to carry home thirty jars of Hui springwater. The servants, resenting the labor, secretly replaced the Hui water with water from a closer mountain spring. Yuan's friends tasted it and praised it. Yuan later discovered the substitution — and was "truly angry."

### The aesthetics of seclusion

Ming tea culture was, at its core, an aesthetics of escape. Tea was the companion of the retired scholar, the solitary contemplative, the man who had withdrawn from the vicious factional politics of the Ming court. Li Rihua's description:

> A simple room with a couch and a table, burning incense and a cup of tea, empty and uncluttered with other things. Just sitting alone in contemplation, naturally a pure and numinous energy comes to my body. The foul vapors of the world are thus gradually dissipated.

Tea became not just a beverage but an *idea* — a repository for all Ming literati fantasies about the contemplative life. To drink tea correctly was to be the kind of person who could drink tea correctly: a scholar, a connoisseur, a man of refinement withdrawn from official noise.

## What's most interesting here

1. **Huizong** — the most knowledgeable tea emperor in Chinese history, captured by the Jurchen and dying in captivity
2. **The Ming tea revolution**: one peasant emperor's practical edict accidentally created modern tea
3. **Xu Cishu's list**: the most human document in tea literature — "as drunken guests disperse," "when the children have left the building"
4. **The water substitution**: servants swap famous springwater for ordinary water; everyone praises it anyway
5. **Monk Dafang** inventing Songluo tea by transplanting one monastery's techniques to another mountain — monastic knowledge transfer as the engine of tea innovation
