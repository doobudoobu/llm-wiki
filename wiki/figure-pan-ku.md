# P'an Ku (盤古)

**Role:** Cosmic giant; firstborn semi-divine human; his body becomes the universe  
**First attested:** 3rd century AD (late!)  
**Origin:** Possibly Central Asian or Tibetan; not natively Chinese

P'an Ku is the most famous creation figure in Chinese mythology and almost certainly not originally Chinese. He is what happened when the Han-era trend toward male-dominant creation narratives needed a canonical cosmic male creator — and found one in a late text that scholars now think was imported.

---

## The myth

Before Heaven and Earth separated, all was chaos like a chicken's egg. P'an Ku was born in the middle of it. For 18,000 years Heaven and Earth slowly separated — the light Yang rose to become sky, the heavy Yin settled into earth — and P'an Ku grew with them. Every day the heavens rose ten feet higher, the earth grew ten feet thicker, and P'an Ku grew ten feet taller. After 18,000 years everything reached its fullest extent — and P'an Ku began to die.

His death created the world:

> His breath became the wind and clouds; his voice became peals of thunder. His left eye became the sun; his right eye became the moon. His four limbs and five extremities became the four cardinal points and the five peaks. His blood and semen became water and rivers. His muscles and veins became the earth's arteries; his flesh became fields and land. His hair and beard became the stars; his bodily hair became plants and trees. His teeth and bones became metal and rock; his vital marrow became pearls and jade. His sweat and bodily fluids became streaming rain. All the mites on his body were touched by the wind and were turned into the black-haired people.

(*Wu yun li-nien chi*, 3rd century AD)

The black-haired people — that is, the Chinese — are literally P'an Ku's mites.

---

## Why he is probably not Chinese

Three reasons to be skeptical of P'an Ku's Chinese origin:

1. **He appears only in late texts.** The myth first surfaces in works attributed to Hsu Cheng of the Three Kingdoms period (3rd century AD). It is absent from the entire corpus of early Chinese mythography — "Questions of Heaven," *Classic of Mountains and Seas*, *Huai-nan Tzu*, and others. This is a 600-year silence.
2. **It shares too many features with Indo-European cosmologies.** The motif of the cosmos built from a giant's body — flesh becomes earth, blood becomes rivers, hair becomes plants — is the defining feature of Norse creation (Ymir), Vedic creation (Purusha), and ancient Near Eastern creation (*Enuma Elish*). These "homologic sets" (Bruce Lincoln's term) don't appear independently: they constitute a cross-cultural family. The specialist Jaan Puhvel suggests the P'an Ku myth is traceable to the ancient Near East via Central Asian intermediaries.
3. **The text style betrays literary composition.** The chicken's egg simile for primordial chaos, the elegance of the body-to-world transformation — these are literary touches, not authentic mythological fragments. The mythographer Rémi Mathieu suggests the myth may derive from Tibetan peoples of the southwestern region where Hsu Cheng lived.

---

## What he displaced

P'an Ku's rise to "canonical" creation myth status came at the expense of **Nü Kua** — the actual native Chinese creatrix, six centuries older in the texts. Nü Kua's creation myth is female-centered; P'an Ku's is male-centered. The Han-era trend toward patriarchal systematization of myth preferred P'an Ku, and so he became the creation story most Westerners encounter when they look up Chinese mythology.
