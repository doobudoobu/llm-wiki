# Keay, *China: A History* — Chapters 4–6

**Author:** John Keay | **Chapters covered:** 4 ("Han Ascendant," 210–141 BC), 5 ("Within and Beyond," 141 BC–AD 1), 6 ("Wang Mang and the Han Reprise," AD 1–189)

---

## What these chapters cover

- **Chapter 4:** Qin's collapse; Liu Bang vs. Xiang Yu; Han dynasty founding; jade burial suits; the Lady Dai mummy; early Han diplomacy with the Xiongnu; the kingdom of Nanyue in the south.
- **Chapter 5:** Han Wudi's long reign; war with the Xiongnu; Zhang Qian's explorations opening the Silk Road; Sima Qian's castration and the writing of the *Shiji*; Han bureaucracy; the decline into palace faction warfare.
- **Chapter 6:** Wang Mang's "Xin" interregnum (AD 9–23); the Yellow River flood of AD 11; the Red Eyebrows rebellion; restoration of the Eastern Han at Luoyang; the Later Han's long decline; Yellow Turbans revolt of AD 184; the dynasty's effective end in AD 189.

---

## Most interesting details

**Xiang Yu's last night.** The greatest general of his generation, surrounded at Gaixia with his 800 men reduced to 28, heard the Han encampments singing the songs of his home state of Chu. He wept in his tent with Lady Yu, sang of how the times were against him, then rode into the night for a final heroic stand. When a Han officer offered to take him prisoner for the reward money, Xiang Yu cut his own throat: "So I will do you a favour." Chinese chessboards to this day mark one side "Han" and the other "Chu."

**Liu Bang's toilet problem.** The future founding emperor of the Han was a hard-drinking boor from the provinces who, when drunk, used scholars' hats as chamber pots. He had to be taught by a Confucian courtier that there were rules of protocol. After the first rehearsal he sighed, "I can do that all right." At the New Year ceremony with nobles from across the empire, "everyone trembled with awe." Liu Bang at last "understood how exalted a thing it is to be an emperor."

**The Lady Dai.** A noblewoman buried at Mawangdui in Hunan in ~186 BC. When excavated in 1972, after 2,100 years her flesh was still responsive to a gentle poke, her joints could be moved, and a post-mortem revealed 138 and a half melon seeds still in her digestive tract. Her tomb also contained the earliest scale maps yet found in China — one still recognizable if you flip it upside down (Chinese maps put south at the top).

**Sima Qian chose castration over suicide.** When the Grand Historian interceded on behalf of a defeated general and was accused of deceiving the emperor, he faced a capital charge. Officials were expected to commit suicide; instead Sima Qian chose "the punishment of rottenness" — judicial castration — so he could finish the *Shiji*. "When I have truly completed this work, I shall deposit it in the Famous Mountain. If it may be handed down to men who will appreciate it... though I should suffer a thousand mutilations, what regret should I have?"

**Zhang Qian's shock discovery.** Sent west to find anti-Xiongnu allies, explorer Zhang spent 13 years captured by the Xiongnu, escaped, traveled to Bactria and Ferghana, got captured again, escaped again, and returned to report: there are other great states out there. Their people live in cities, keep records by writing, make their living in much the same way as the Chinese — and shockingly, they are quite unaware that *zhongguo*, the "Middle Kingdom," is anywhere near the middle.

**Wang Mang's attempted Confucian utopia.** The one-man Xin dynasty (AD 9–23) abolished slavery, redistributed land, rectified every name and title, redesigned the coinage, and appealed to the Duke of Zhou's precedent for everything. Then the Yellow River flooded in AD 11, displacing millions, creating the Red Eyebrows rebellion, and bringing the dynasty down. Wang Mang died in a tower, having written Heaven a thousand-word personal appeal. He was decapitated and dismembered by the mob.
