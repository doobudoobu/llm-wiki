# Neolithic China

**Period:** c. 7000–2000 BC  
**What it is:** The 5,000 years before any recognizable dynasty — farming villages evolving into walled towns evolving into stratified proto-states

---

## The end of the simple story

For most of Chinese history, the standard account held that civilization began in the Yellow River's Central Plain (roughly modern Henan province) and spread outward. By the 1980s, archaeology had demolished this story. China had dozens of distinct Neolithic cultures across its entire territory — north and south, coast and interior — each developing semi-independently. The new model is sometimes called the **"Chinese Interaction Sphere"**: regional cultures stimulating and exchanging with each other without any single dominant center.

The question of *why* the Central Plain eventually produced the first recognizable states remains unsettled.

---

## The early farmers

Agriculture in China did not begin in one place. Two domestication centers developed in parallel:
- **Millet** in the Yellow River region (north) from at least ~6500 BC
- **Rice** in the Yangzi River region (south) from at least ~7000 BC — possibly as early as 10,000 BC based on Hunan finds

**Cishan–Peiligang culture** (6500–5000 BC) represents the earliest well-documented Neolithic phase in the Yellow River valley. Small settlements of 1–6 ha; millet stored in ash pits; simple three-legged bowls and storage jars; domesticated millet confirmed by charcoal analysis at over twenty sites.

The **Jiahu site** (southern Henan, ~6500–5500 BC) is a notable outlier: a Cishan–Peiligang community that also had ten grains of *rice* (the earliest rice evidence in north China) and, most remarkably, the **earliest tortoiseshell with inscription marks** — two isolated graphs on a shell, almost certainly used for divination. The connection between early divination shells and the later oracle bones is suggestive.

## Yangshao (5000–3000 BC)

The Yangshao culture is defined by its distinctive painted red-brown pottery — fish motifs giving way over time to abstract geometric patterns. Sites are concentrated across Shaanxi, Shanxi, and Henan, with the core area at the territorial junction of all three modern provinces.

**The Jiangzhai village** (Shaanxi, 20 km east of Xi'an) is the most completely excavated Yangshao site. At its earliest occupation phase:
- 200,000 m² settlement surrounded by a circular moat
- Five residential clusters, each centered on a large house with smaller satellite houses and storage pits
- Three cemeteries, each apparently belonging to one cluster's kin group
- Estimated population ~500

The society was organized — at minimum — as a segmentary lineage system: lineages sharing an ancestor, internally divided into segments of roughly equal standing. Whether it was matrilineal (the old Marxist interpretation) is now mostly rejected.

Late Yangshao shows signs of change: at the **Xipo site** (western Henan), an enormous house measuring 240 m² was found — eight times a normal house — alongside unusually rich tombs nearby. Wealth was beginning to concentrate.

## Longshan (c. 3000–2000 BC)

Around 3000 BC, the Longshan cultures mark a step change in social complexity. The defining feature is the **rammed earth wall**: more than ten Longshan towns have been found in Henan, Shandong, and Shanxi with wall enclosures protecting an elite core area while ordinary dwellings remained outside. Social stratification visible in burial goods is now dramatic.

The largest Longshan site is **Taosi** in southern Shanxi:

### Taosi (2600–2000 BC)
- Site area: 2,800,000 m² (280 ha)
- Three burial classes: great tombs (3×2 m+, wooden coffins, 100+ items), medium tombs (coffin, 20–30 items), small tombs (0.5 m wide pits, no coffin, no goods)
- Small tombs = 90% of all burials; their occupants held ~10% of the wealth. More concentrated inequality than the modern United States (2007 data)

**The Taosi solar observatory** (discovered 2003): a circular rammed-earth building in a separate enclosure attached to the city's southeast wall. Thirteen solid pillars support twelve narrow slits; all twelve sightlines converge on a single observation point at the platform center. Used to mark the solar year's extremes — the summer and winter solstices. One of the oldest confirmed solar observatories in the world, in use from roughly 2500 to 2100 BC. The astronomer David Pankenier suggests this structure provided the context in which a writing system was first needed — to record which slit corresponded to which date and festival. The connection is speculative but not trivial, given that Shang oracle bones record a mature lunar-solar combined calendar by the thirteenth century BC.

**Taosi metallurgy**: a copper bell found in a mid-size tomb (Taosi, 1983) — cast in clay molds using nearly pure copper (97.86% Cu, 1.5% Pb, 0.16% Zn). The sectional mold casting method used here became the mainstream Chinese bronze technology.

Taosi may have ended violently: evidence of destruction, possibly by people from a competing site to the south.

## Liangzhu (c. 3300–2000 BC)

Contemporaneous with the Longshan cultures, the Liangzhu culture in the Yangzi Delta (Jiangsu/Zhejiang) represents an independent pathway to social complexity, defined by extraordinary jade production. Large mound tombs yielded dozens of beautifully crafted jade objects — cong cylinders, bi discs — clearly elite luxury goods. A city centering on Mojiaoshan near Hangzhou (discovered 2007) was protected by walls even larger than Taosi.

Then, around 2000 BC, the Liangzhu culture **vanished**. No gradual decline — it simply disappears from the archaeological record. Climate change, flooding, or conquest: unknown.

## The Lajia earthquake site

In summer 2000, archaeologists excavating the residential site at Lajia (Qinghai, near the upper Yellow River) found a village frozen in disaster. A catastrophic earthquake (the dwelling pits were the base of collapsed loess caves) and probable subsequent flooding had killed the inhabitants instantly. In cave pit F4:
- A young woman (28–30) kneeling, leaning against the east wall
- Beneath her chest: remains of a 1–2-year-old infant, its arms around her waist
- Nearby: another mother surrounded by three teenagers and a child under 10
- To the north: four children, the youngest ~3, holding each other

When archaeologists turned over a fallen pottery bowl left on the floor, they found the **oldest noodle in China** — ~50 cm of preserved millet or wheat noodle. The site dates to ~4000 BP (roughly contemporaneous with the late Taosi period).

## What the Neolithic left to the Bronze Age

The Neolithic period established patterns that shaped everything that followed:
- Millet farming → population density → stratification → rammed-earth defended centers → Erlitou state
- Divination on tortoiseshell (Jiahu) → oracle bone divination (Shang)
- Sectional mold copper casting (Taosi) → sectional mold bronze casting (Erlitou, Shang)
- The "Chinese Interaction Sphere" → competing regional Bronze Age cultures (Sanxingdui, Wucheng, Lower Ordos) that persisted alongside and independent of Shang
