# Zheng He (c. 1371–1433)

A Yunnanese Muslim eunuch who commanded the largest naval expeditions in pre-modern history. His story begins with a ten-year-old captured in a provincial military campaign and ends with a fleet that put every other navy in the world to shame — and was then allowed to rot.

---

## Origins

Born **Ma He** in Yunnan, the son of a Muslim official who had made the pilgrimage to Mecca. When Qing forces conquered Yunnan in 1381/82, the eleven-year-old Ma He was captured, castrated, and dispatched to serve in the household of the Prince of Yan — the man who would become the Yongle Emperor.

He became the prince's most trusted confidant. When the prince overthrew his nephew the Jianwen Emperor in 1402 and seized the throne, Ma He was rewarded with the surname Zheng and a command: he would lead China's new world armada.

---

## The treasure ships

The largest vessels of the Zheng He fleet — the "treasure ships" — were the largest wooden sailing ships ever built. The most considered scholarly estimate puts the largest at over 130 meters long with a beam of around 50 meters — capable of displacing 20,000–30,000 tonnes. For comparison: Columbus's *Santa María* was about 20 meters long.

The ships had:
- Six to nine masts, each up to 60 meters tall, with collapsible rattan sails
- Watertight bulkheads
- Retractable sternpost rudders and centreboards
- Rectangular cross-section for shallow estuarial draught

"In the language of a later era of navalism, China had the ships, had the men, and had the money, too." Each fleet comprised 100–300 ships carrying up to 27,000 men — soldiers, sailors, doctors, translators, merchants, and craftsmen.

## The seven voyages (1405–1433)

All seven voyages followed roughly the same monsoon-dictated course: south to Vietnam, Java, Malacca, Sumatra, Sri Lanka, and then Calicut on the Kerala coast of India — "the great country of the Western Ocean." From Calicut some voyages continued:

- **4th voyage** (1413–15): first visit to Hormuz in the Persian Gulf
- **5th voyage** (1417–19): Djofar and Aden on the Arabian coast
- **5th and 6th voyages**: ports on the Somali coast of East Africa; possibly Malindi (modern Kenya)
- Chinese envoys also reached Mecca, though not in Chinese ships

Seventy years before Vasco da Gama rounded the Cape of Good Hope, Zheng He's armadas had demonstrated maritime mastery of the entire Indian Ocean.

---

## The purpose: ritual, not trade

The voyages were not exploratory expeditions and not primarily commercial enterprises. Their purpose was tributary ritual: the demonstration of imperial centrality and the collection of submissions.

Foreign rulers who received Zheng He's fleet were expected to send tribute missions to Beijing — acknowledging their place in the cosmic order under the Son of Heaven. The emperor wanted to display China's wealth and power "in strange lands in order to make manifest the wealth and power of the Middle Kingdom." Zheng He's stele at Changle records that he was commissioned "to go to the foreigners' countries and confer presents on them so as to transform them by displaying our power while treating distant peoples with kindness."

The exotic animals that came back — giraffes (resembling the auspicious mythical *qilin*), rhinos, ostriches, lions — were trophies of Heaven's favor, not scientific specimens. Military force was used on three occasions in seven voyages, always in service of existing client rulers.

No colonies were established. No territory was claimed. The contrast with European expansion — which moved from exploration to trade to empire — could hardly be sharper.

---

## The abandonment

Zheng He's sixth voyage returned in 1423; a moratorium was announced. The Yongle Emperor died that year, drained by the Vietnam campaign that had ended disastrously. His Confucian bureaucracy had always opposed the voyages as wasteful extravagance run by eunuchs. With the eunuchs out of favor, there was no constituency to defend the enterprise.

A seventh voyage was commissioned by an admiring successor emperor in 1431; Zheng He commanded it. He died at sea, probably on the return voyage, in 1433. He was buried at Nanjing.

The ships were allowed to rot. Replacement construction was specifically forbidden. The shipbuilding records were allegedly destroyed by officials eager to ensure the voyages could not be revived. Within a generation the knowledge of how to build treasure ships was lost.

## The "what if"

The Zheng He voyages are the most discussed "what if" in Chinese history. Had China continued and expanded its maritime program:
- The spice routes and trade lanes of the Indian Ocean would have been under Chinese control before Portuguese arrival
- European maritime expansion in Asia might have been impossible, or at least much harder
- The global equilibrium that tilted toward Europe from the 16th century might have tilted differently

Keay's structural explanation for why this didn't happen: the voyages were never designed as commercial or imperial enterprise in the European sense. They were tributary ritual — the same old system, more expensively applied. "More money, power, and ceremony applied in the same old way was simply bound to fail." The moment their specific ritual-political function was exhausted, they had no other justification.

---

## The name

Zheng He is also remembered as the man whose existence the Yongle Emperor used as an official justification for the voyages: the alleged purpose was to search for the Jianwen Emperor (whom Yongle had deposed and probably killed) in case he had somehow escaped to India or Africa. It was a fabrication, but it allowed Confucian officials to appear zealous about eliminating threats to Yongle's legitimacy while actually opposing the voyages' real purpose.
