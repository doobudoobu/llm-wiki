# Hume, *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751)

**Source:** *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals* (1751), David Hume  
**Text used:** Project Gutenberg edition (public domain)  
**Hume's own verdict:** He called it "incomparably the best" of all his writings.

---

## The question

Section I opens with the fundamental question of moral philosophy: is morality founded on **reason** or **sentiment**? Can our moral distinctions be derived by argument the way geometric truths can, or does the final judgment depend on an internal feeling?

Hume's preliminary answer is that *both concur*, but in different ways. Reason maps the territory — it tells you the facts and tendencies of actions, who gains and who is harmed, what the likely consequences are. But when all the facts are assembled, the final verdict — "this is good," "this is vicious" — depends on a **sentiment**, not a further inference. Reason informs; sentiment approves or condemns.

His method is deliberately Newtonian: no metaphysical axioms, no systems built in advance. Follow the phenomena. Look at what we actually praise and blame, find the common thread, and work upward toward general principles.

---

## The four-fold taxonomy

The organizing principle of the book is that personal merit divides into four kinds:

1. **Qualities useful to others** — justice, fidelity, honesty, beneficence
2. **Qualities useful to the person themselves** — prudence, industry, frugality, fortitude
3. **Qualities immediately agreeable to others** — good manners, wit, modesty, decency
4. **Qualities immediately agreeable to the person themselves** — cheerfulness, magnanimity, dignity of mind

Hume's "Cleanthes dialogue" (Section IX) presents this taxonomy in action: four friends describe the same ideal man, each praising a different dimension. Together they sketch, Hume says, "a model of perfect virtue." The form is as elegant as the argument: the taxonomy is proved by example rather than definition.

## Justice as artificial virtue (Section III)

The most philosophically dense section. Hume's thesis: justice is not a natural impulse but a **convention** — an artificial virtue grounded entirely in **utility**.

Three thought experiments that prove it:

**Universal abundance**: If nature automatically satisfied every desire, there would be no need to establish rules of property. Justice arises only because goods are scarce.

**Universal benevolence**: If every person were perfectly benevolent toward every other, rules assigning *mine* and *thine* would be unnecessary — resources would be shared spontaneously.

**Extreme scarcity** (a gang of robbers, a besieged city): When survival is at stake, justice is suspended. "The strict laws of justice are suspended, in such a pressing emergence, and give place to the stronger motives of necessity and self-preservation."

Conclusion: justice exists because of the *middle condition of mankind* — moderate scarcity + moderate (not universal) benevolence. It solves a problem that doesn't arise at the extremes.

**The "inferior species" corollary**: If we had the same advantage over some group that we do over animals, we would have no duties of justice toward them — whatever acts of kindness we might show would be "generosity, not justice." Hume notes this with disturbing calm: it implies our duties toward others depend on rough equality of power. This is one of the most uncomfortable passages in the book.

## Why utility pleases: sympathy (Section V)

The key question: if utility grounds morality, why does utility *please* us even when we have no personal stake in it? Hume praises actions in remote centuries, in foreign nations, where no self-interest is involved. Why?

His answer: **sympathy** (not self-love). We imaginatively enter into the perspective of the people affected. We are pleased when we conceive of others' happiness; we are pained when we conceive of their suffering. The human countenance "borrows smiles or tears from the human countenance" (Horace).

This demolishes the Hobbesian and Mandevillean self-love account via what Hume calls an *experimentum crucis*: if morality rested on self-love, we would be indifferent to events that cannot possibly affect us. But we are not indifferent. We praise virtue in Spartan heroes, we condemn tyranny in distant ages, not because it advances our interest, but because sympathy bridges the gap.

Sympathy is also the mechanism of emotional contagion: we cannot remain indifferent spectators of mutual wrangling and reproaches, "we suffer by contagion and sympathy," even when no harm comes to us.

## Benevolence as genuine (Section II; Appendix II)

Against Hobbes and Mandeville, Hume insists that benevolence is real — not a disguised form of self-love. He develops this most fully in Appendix II.

The argument from parsimony: in physics, complex hypotheses are often correct. But in the study of the passions, the simplest, most obvious explanation is almost always right. If benevolence looks like a direct, primary impulse — it is one. The attempt to resolve it into self-love by "philosophical chymistry" requires elaborate and implausible refinements.

The argument from animals: animals display genuine affection — for their offspring, for their companions. We cannot explain this away as self-interest. By analogy, we have no reason to deny genuine benevolence in humans.

The argument from structure: for self-love to operate, there must be some prior object that self-love is *about* — some pleasure or good that gives the self a stake. Benevolence must precede self-love; it cannot be derived from it.

A concession: Hume doesn't need to win this argument. Even if 99% of human nature is selfish, the tiny residue of genuine sympathy — "some particle of the dove kneaded into our frame, along with the elements of the wolf and serpent" — is sufficient to found morality. That one particle, because it is *common* to all and *universal* in scope, can serve as a moral standard in a way that private passions cannot.

## The conclusion and the monkish virtues (Section IX)

The Conclusion is where Hume becomes polemical.

He enumerates the four kinds of merit, then — in the most overtly anti-religious passage in the book — places *monkish virtues* explicitly in the **vice** column:

> "Celibacy, fasting, penance, mortification, self-denial, humility, silence, solitude, and the whole train of monkish virtues; for what reason are they everywhere rejected by men of sense, but because they serve to no manner of purpose; neither advance a man's fortune in the world, nor render him a more valuable member of society; neither qualify him for the entertainment of company, nor increase his power of self-enjoyment? We observe, on the contrary, that they cross all these desirable ends; stupify the understanding and harden the heart, obscure the fancy and sour the temper."

A gloomy enthusiast may get into the calendar after death, but "will scarcely ever be admitted, when alive, into intimacy and society." Hume's framework is built on what promotes flourishing — and self-mortification fails this test completely.

Virtue, in Hume's final picture, "talks not of useless austerities and rigours, suffering and self-denial. She declares that her sole purpose is to make her votaries and all mankind, during every instant of their existence, if possible, cheerful and happy."

## The sensible knave problem

Hume's most candid moment comes in Section IX Part II. Can he show that virtue is always in the self-interest of the virtuous?

He answers yes — with one exception: the **sensible knave**. A clever person who violates justice whenever they can do so secretly and profitably might appear to have a rational case. Hume concedes this directly:

> "If his heart rebel not against such pernicious maxims, if he feel no reluctance to the thoughts of villainy or baseness, he has indeed lost a considerable motive to virtue."

His response: such a person sacrifices "the invaluable enjoyment of a character, with themselves at least, for the acquisition of worthless toys and gewgaws." Knaves, however cunning, eventually betray themselves when temptation proves too strong. The honest person retains inward peace of mind and the consciousness of integrity.

This answer is notably incomplete and Hume knows it. It does not refute the knave on rational grounds — it simply points out what the knave forfeits. Hume does not pretend otherwise.

## Appendix I: The definitive case for moral sentiment

The most rigorous section. Hume offers five arguments that morality cannot rest on reason alone.

**1. The intelligibility argument**: Take any specific moral case — ingratitude, say. Anatomize all the facts. Reason them through. "You never will come to any issue or conclusion." There is no discoverable *fact* or *relation* in the world that constitutes the moral wrong; the wrongness arises from a sentiment in the spectator.

**2. The geometric analogy fails**: In mathematics, we derive unknown relations from known ones. In moral deliberation, all the facts must be *already known* before we can render moral judgment. After all facts are assembled, "the understanding has no further room to operate." Judgment is not an inference; it is a *feeling* that arises when the whole is presented.

**3. Oedipus and Nero**: Oedipus killed his father in ignorance of the relation between them — a moral mistake of *fact*, involuntary. Nero killed his mother knowing everything — a moral demerit of *sentiment*, felt as detestation. Since both stood in formally similar relations to their victims, morality cannot consist in relations. "In these sentiments then, not in a discovery of relations of any kind, do all moral determinations consist."

**4. The young tree**: A tree that overshadows and destroys its parent stands in all the same relations as Nero to Agrippina. If morality were relations, the tree would be as guilty as Nero.

**5. The regress argument**: Why do you exercise? For health. Why do you want health? Because sickness is painful. Why do you hate pain? *Impossible to answer*. Ultimate ends "recommend themselves entirely to the sentiments and affections of mankind, without any dependance on the intellectual faculties." Something must be desirable in itself, and that desirability can only be grounded in sentiment, not in a further reason.

**The final division**: "Reason being cool and disengaged, is no motive to action... Taste, as it gives pleasure or pain, and thereby constitutes happiness or misery, becomes a motive to action, and is the first spring or impulse to desire and volition."

## Appendix III: Benevolence vs. justice — the vault and the wall

The most elegant formulation in the entire book, distinguishing the two main types of virtue:

**Benevolence** builds like a **wall**: each stone of kindness adds to the total. A single act of generosity increases the total good in the world; a lone benefactor increases rather than decreases our esteem, since rarity adds value.

**Justice** builds like a **vault**: "each individual stone would, of itself, fall to the ground; nor is the whole fabric supported but by the mutual assistance and combination of its corresponding parts." A single act of justice, considered in isolation, may harm the people most immediately involved. Its benefit arises only from the whole system operating together — a miser who legally inherits wealth from a good man is a wrong that justice permits, because the alternative (case-by-case judgment) would collapse the system entirely.

This is why benevolence can be particular and spontaneous, while justice must be general and rule-governed.

## Cross-domain connections

**Spinoza's conatus vs. Hume's sympathy**: Spinoza grounds morality in the drive to persevere in one's own being; utility is what promotes that. Hume's sympathy extends naturally beyond the individual — it is structurally similar to what Spinoza calls emotional contagion (P27 of Part III: imagining a similar being feeling an emotion causes us to feel the same). Both reject moral rationalism; both locate the engine of ethics in affect rather than reason. But Spinoza's starting point is metaphysical necessity; Hume's is empirical observation. See [[src-spinoza-ethics-p3]].

**Against Stoic rationalism**: Hume shares Spinoza's rejection of Stoic attempts to suppress the passions through reason. For Hume, reason is "cool and disengaged" — it cannot move us. The Stoic sage who achieves indifference through pure reason is a phantom. See [[src-spinoza-ethics-p5]].

**Confucian ritual and moral habituation**: The Confucian *li* (禮) system trains responses through repeated ritual practice — building associations that eventually produce automatic moral feeling without deliberation. This is structurally continuous with Hume's account: sympathy and social sentiment are the moral foundation, and ritual is the mechanism for cultivating and standardizing them. See [[concept-neo-confucianism]].
