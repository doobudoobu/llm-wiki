# Qing Dynasty (1644–1912 CE)

The last imperial dynasty of China — and the largest, controlling more territory than any previous dynasty. Founded by the Manchu (formerly Jurchen) people of northeast China, who had prepared their conquest for thirty years before riding through the gates in 1644. It lasted 268 years and doubled the empire's territory before collapsing under internal rebellion and Western pressure.

---

## Origins: from Jurchen to Manchu

The Jurchen forest people of Manchuria had already ruled northern China once, as the Jin dynasty (1115–1234), before being destroyed by the Mongols. By the early 17th century, under the brilliant leader **Nurhaci** (r. 1616–26), they were reassembling.

Nurhaci organized his people into **Banner** units — military-social formations identified by colored flags. Originally four banners, expanded to eight, then including Mongol and Han Chinese recruits, the Banners were the Manchu striking force and the backbone of Qing society for over a century. In 1616 Nurhaci declared a new Jin dynasty; his son **Hong Taiji** renamed it "Qing" ("Pure") in 1636 and renamed the people "Manchu," shedding the contentious historical baggage of "Jurchen."

Hong Taiji died in 1643, leaving a five-year-old heir. Regent Prince **Dorgon** led the Banners into China proper.

## The conquest of the Ming (1644–1683)

**How it happened**: the peasant rebel Li Zicheng took Beijing on 25 April 1644; the last Ming emperor hanged himself on Coal Hill. When Li Zicheng marched to Shanhaiguan to confront the loyal Ming general **Wu Sangui**, Wu opened the gate and invited the Manchus in. On 5 June Dorgon's Banners entered the empty Forbidden City and declared the Mandate of Heaven had passed to the Qing.

This was, as Keay notes, the least amount of China any dynasty had previously conquered before gaining the throne.

**The queue order**: Regent Dorgon required all Han Chinese men to shave the front of their heads and wear the remaining hair in the Manchu-style braid. "Leave your hair, lose your head; keep your hair, lose your head." Resistance meant death. This gratuitously provocative measure prolonged resistance for decades. It was also designed as a genuine act of cultural inclusion — Han men were being invited to *join* the Manchu enterprise, to become Bannermen. Neither framing fully worked.

**The Yangzhou massacre** (1645): the loyal Ming commander Shi Kefa held Yangzhou against the advancing Banners. The walls were too narrow for the cannon; improvised gun platforms collapsed under the defenders. When the city fell, it was sacked for ten days — the most notorious atrocity of the conquest, carefully chronicled and long remembered.

**Southern Ming resistance**: four successive "Southern Ming" regimes held out south of the Yangzi. The last was chased across Burma; its pretender was eventually strangled at Wu Sangui's order in 1662. The last serious loyalist at sea — the extraordinary **Zheng Chenggong** (Coxinga) — controlled the coast from Guangdong to Zhejiang for years, expelled the Dutch from Taiwan in 1661, and died there in his thirties. When the Qing finally took Taiwan in 1683, the conquest was complete.

## The Three Emperors (1661–1799)

Three long-reigning emperors gave the Qing its period of peak stability and expansion:

| Emperor | Reign | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| **Kangxi** | 1661–1722 | 61 years; military campaigns; Treaty of Nerchinsk; began Zunghar wars |
| **Yongzheng** | 1723–1735 | Administrative consolidation; ruthless anti-corruption |
| **Qianlong** | 1736–1795 | 60-year reign (abdicated to avoid outlasting grandfather Kangxi); territorial peak; Zunghar genocide; Macartney mission |

The Kangxi Emperor is one of the most fully documented rulers in Chinese history. His letters survive in large numbers — spirited, decisive, passionate about hunting, genuinely concerned for his troops. He was also an enthusiastic practitioner of Tibetan Buddhist ritual. He turned scholarly holdouts nostalgic for the Ming into collaborators by commissioning enormous encyclopedias and literary anthologies.

The Qianlong Emperor presided over the greatest territorial expansion and the most disturbing act of deliberate mass murder in Chinese imperial history.

## The Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689)

Russia had been pushing east through Siberia since the 1580s, reaching the Pacific in 1647. By the 1650s Russian traders and Cossacks were appearing in the Amur River basin — Manchu ancestral territory. After decades of skirmishes, the two empires met at Nerchinsk in 1689.

The Qing delegation included two Jesuits; the Russian delegation included a classically educated Pole. **Latin** served as the common language — neutral enough that Russian insistence on equal status and Qing insistence on tributary hierarchy could both be buried in the ablative absolute.

The treaty fixed the Russo-Chinese border along the Argun River, gave Russia commercial access to the Qing empire, and provided for a Russian Orthodox church and language school in Beijing. Both sides got what they needed. Relations remained functional for 150 years — strikingly unlike the eventual catastrophe of Qing-Western maritime relations.

## The Zunghar genocide (1690–1759)

The **Zunghar Mongols** of western Mongolia and Xinjiang represented the last serious attempt to recreate the empire of Chinggis Khan. Their leader Galdan (r. 1671–97) had studied as a Buddhist novice in Lhasa and combined pan-Mongol claims with Dalai Lama legitimacy.

The Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong emperors pursued the Zunghars across seventy years of intermittent war. The Kangxi Emperor took a personal interest bordering on obsession — Galdan was his prize quarry, to be hunted "to the ends of the earth." Smallpox was deployed as a weapon against Inner Asian peoples with low resistance. Livestock was sequestered; the steppe burned.

When in 1759 the Qianlong Emperor finally declared victory, Zungharia was almost empty. Of an estimated 600,000 Zunghars:
- 40% had died of smallpox
- 30% had been killed by Qing armies
- 20% had fled across Russian, Kazakh, and Kyrgyz frontiers

"Zungharia was left as a blank social space," which the Qing then filled with state-sponsored Han, Manchu, Hui Muslim, and Turkestani settlers. One authority does not hesitate to call the Qianlong Emperor's campaign "the final solution."

The territory became **Xinjiang** ("New Frontier") — and remains one of the most contested regions of China today.

## Tibet

The Qing relationship with Tibet was multidimensional in ways that later centuries' simplifications obscure. Manchu emperors were sincere devotees of Tibetan Buddhism and sought Dalai Lama endorsement as a source of legitimacy — not unlike the Mongol khans before them. In 1652 the Kangxi Emperor feted the "Great Fifth" Dalai Lama in Beijing.

Qing military intervention in Lhasa came in 1720, when the Kangxi Emperor sent Banners to expel Zunghar forces that had occupied the city and install a new Dalai Lama. Two Beijing-appointed *ambans* (commissioners) and a garrison remained as advisors. Qinghai and eastern Tibet (Kham) were detached from Lhasa's authority and brought under more direct Qing administration — a division that shaped all subsequent Tibet policy.

The Qing approach to Tibet — supporting the religious establishment, sponsoring translation of Tibetan texts, building dozens of Tibetan temples in Beijing — was patronage, not suppression. By the 1750s over a third of all Mongols had become monks. The post-1959 situation is fundamentally different in character.

## The fall (19th–20th century)

The Qianlong Emperor received the **Macartney Mission** from Britain in 1793 — the first formal British embassy — and dismissed it with a letter to George III congratulating him on his "sincere humility and obedience" and noting that China had no need of British manufactures. Macartney accurately predicted in his journal that the empire was "an old, crazy, First-rate man-of-war" drifting toward catastrophe.

What followed confirmed his analysis:
- **Opium Wars** (1839–42, 1856–60): Britain forced China to legalize opium imports and cede Hong Kong
- **Taiping Rebellion** (1850–64): a Christian millenarian movement killed an estimated 20–30 million people
- **Boxer Rebellion** (1900): international occupation of Beijing
- **Xinhai Revolution** (1911–12): the child emperor Puyi abdicated on 12 February 1912

The dynasty did not end with conquest but with abdication — and the logic of the Mandate of Heaven, which had governed Chinese political life for 2,500 years, officially ended with it.

**The last emperor's afterlife**: Puyi was briefly restored in 1917 (eleven days), installed as the Japanese puppet emperor of Manchukuo in 1934, captured by Soviets in 1945, underwent communist re-education, and ended his days as a gardener in Beijing, dying in 1967.
