# Wang Anshi (1021–1086)

The greatest reformer in Chinese history before the modern era — and perhaps the greatest example of what happens when you win every argument and still lose. Wang Anshi came closer than anyone between the First Emperor and the Communist Party to fundamentally reorganizing how the Chinese state worked. He failed, and his failure shaped Chinese political culture for centuries.

---

## The problem he was trying to solve

By the mid-11th century, Northern Song faced a structural crisis. Military spending — maintaining a large professional army and paying tribute to Liao and Xi Xia — consumed most of the state's revenue. Peasant smallholders were being crushed by private usurers who charged interest rates of 50–100% per year on spring planting loans. The tax system bore most heavily on the poorest. The professional army was expensive, unreliable, and unable to defeat nomad cavalry. The bureaucracy was overstaffed with underemployed officials.

Emperor Song Shenzong, who took the throne in 1067, wanted change. He found his reformer in Wang Anshi, then prefect of Jiangnan East, and made him chancellor.

---

## The New Policies (1069–1076)

Wang Anshi's program — the *xinfa* ("new methods") or "New Policies" — attacked the fiscal crisis from multiple directions simultaneously:

**The *qingmiao* (green sprouts) loans**: the state would lend grain or money to farmers at planting time at 20% annual interest — higher than state loans today but a quarter of what private usurers charged. This would break the usurers' grip, fund itself, and generate modest state revenue. Wang estimated it could raise several million strings of cash annually.

**The *baojiaf* (mutual security) system**: households were grouped in units of ten, then fifty, then five hundred, responsible for mutual surveillance and local militia service. This would reduce the need for expensive professional soldiers and rebuild local social order.

**The granary (*changpingcang*) reform**: state granaries would buy grain when prices were low (stabilizing farmers' income) and sell when prices rose (stabilizing consumers' costs). A classic price-smoothing intervention.

**Corvée commutation (*muyifa*)**: instead of requiring peasants to perform labor service for the state, tax them in cash and hire workers. This was more efficient and less disruptive to agricultural cycles.

**Military reorganization**: reduce the professional army's size while expanding local militia, cutting costs.

---

## The arguments

Wang Anshi won the intellectual debates. He argued from Confucian classics, from historical precedent, from practical evidence. His opponents — led by **Sima Guang**, the great historian — argued from principle: the *qingmiao* loans would burden local officials with collection duties and create new opportunities for corruption; mutual security units would disturb established social relationships; disrupting the natural order of things was itself a form of harm.

The conservative argument was not stupid. Wang's policies did create implementation problems: some officials forced loans on households that didn't want them to meet quotas; the mutual security system created new forms of local oppression. The reforms were real interventions in a complex system, and interventions have unintended consequences.

But Wang's diagnosis was also real. The usurers were crushing the peasantry. The professional army was bankrupting the state. Something had to change.

---

## The fall

Song Shenzong died in 1085. His mother the **Empress Dowager Gao** took the regency for the child emperor. She was conservative and personally disliked Wang Anshi's methods. She recalled Sima Guang, who reversed nearly all the New Policies within a year.

Wang Anshi died in retirement in 1086, the year after the reversal — possibly, accounts suggest, of a broken heart.

The reversal was not permanent. Under later emperors the "New Policies" faction returned, the conservatives were expelled, the policies reinstated. Under Song Huizong (the emperor who was later captured by the Jin) the reform faction had the conservatives' names inscribed on stone tablets as enemies of the state. Politics in the late Northern Song was essentially factional warfare over Wang Anshi's legacy.

---

## Legacy

Wang Anshi was condemned by later Neo-Confucian orthodoxy, primarily through Sima Guang's influence and Zhu Xi's endorsement of Sima's historical judgment. The *Zizhi Tongjian* (Sima Guang's great chronicle) treats him harshly; Neo-Confucian educators reinforced this.

20th-century reassessments were more positive. Liang Qichao (1873–1929), the reformist journalist, wrote a biography praising Wang Anshi as China's answer to Western modernizers. During the Socialist era, Chinese historians tended to view him favorably as an opponent of the landlord class. He has never stopped being controversial.

Keay's observation stands: the scale of what Wang Anshi attempted puts him in a category almost alone in premodern Chinese history.
