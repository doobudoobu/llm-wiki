# Tea Culture in China

*Currently covers Benn, Chapters 2–6 and 8: early history, mythology, Buddhism, Tang poetry, Lu Yu, Song dynasty, and Ming dynasty.*

---

## The word didn't exist until the 8th century

The Chinese character *cha* (茶) — the word for tea — was invented in the mid-Tang dynasty by removing one stroke from the older character *tu*. A 17th-century scholar examined stone inscriptions at Mount Tai and found *tu* in inscriptions from 779 and 798 CE, and *cha* in inscriptions from 841 and 855 CE. Before that, what we now call tea was referred to by several different names — *tu*, *ming*, *jia*, *chuan*, and in Sichuan, *kutu* (bitter *tu*). This terminological chaos makes the early history of tea nearly impossible to reconstruct with confidence, because you can never be quite sure if a given ancient text is referring to tea or some other bitter plant.

## Shennong didn't discover tea

The standard origin story — the mythological emperor Shennong (the "Divine Husbandman") discovered tea when leaves accidentally fell into his boiling water — was **invented by Lu Yu** in his *Classic of Tea* (c. 760 CE). Shennong barely appears in classical texts before the late Warring States period, and his connection to tea is found nowhere before Lu Yu.

Who was Shennong? One of the "Three August Sovereigns" of mythical antiquity — a heroic figure credited with teaching humanity to farm and personally taste-testing hundreds of plants to determine which were safe to eat, reportedly being poisoned seventy times a day in the process. By the Tang dynasty he had evolved into the patron saint of both agriculture and medicine. Lu Yu's genius was hitching tea to this enormously prestigious culture hero — making tea not just a regional beverage but a substance with divine sanction going back to the dawn of civilization.

A competing Tang account placed tea's discovery much later: only after "Qin captured Shu" (316 BCE), making it a discovery of the Warring States period. This version lost.

## The earliest reliable reference to tea

The most famous "early evidence" for tea — a Han dynasty text called the *Slave's Contract* (c. 58 BCE) listing among a slave's duties "brewing tea" and "buying tea from Wuyang" — is probably unreliable. The oldest surviving version of the text (found in a Tang encyclopedia compiled c. 640 CE) contains no mention of tea at all. Later versions differ dramatically in which characters are used. Scholars now consider it too textually unstable to serve as reliable evidence.

The **earliest credible reference** to tea comes from the *History of the Three Kingdoms* (c. 290 CE): the emperor Sun Hao of Wu (r. 264–280) was a legendary drinker who expected everyone at his court to keep pace with him at banquets. One courtier, Wei Zhao, couldn't manage it — so he quietly had tea substituted for his wine. An early (and quietly subversive) use of tea: help your boss think you're drinking when you're not.

## The weird supernatural world of early tea

Before Lu Yu refined it into a cultured aesthetic practice, the world of tea in early medieval Chinese texts was genuinely strange. In the anomaly literature Lu Yu draws on for his *Classic of Tea*, tea appears as something connected to the uncanny:

**The ghost who still wants tea.** A man named Xiahou Kai dies. His ghost returns — wearing the same flat-topped hat, sitting on the same bed by the west wall — and pesters members of his household for tea to drink. Even death didn't break the habit.

**The hairy mountain demon.** A man goes into the mountains to pick tea and encounters a creature more than ten feet tall, completely covered in hair. The creature grabs him, leads him to a clump of wild tea plants, then produces tangerines from its chest as a gift. In the version Lu Yu quotes, the man is alarmed; in the fuller version, he's convinced he's going to die. The wild tea plant, growing in mountain wilderness, was guarded by something not quite human.

**The immortal tea seller.** In the time of Emperor Yuandi of Jin (317–323), an old woman sold tea in the marketplace every day. Her container never ran dry, no matter how many customers she served. She gave all the cash to orphans and beggars. Officials had her arrested. That night, she picked up her tea container and flew out of the prison window.

**The Taoist tea master.** A man named Yu Hong enters the mountains to pick tea and meets a Taoist immortal riding three green oxen. The immortal — "Master of Cinnabar Mound" — tells him: "I've heard you're a skilled cook. In these mountains are great tea plants. Please offer me the leftovers from your dipper." Later poets would write of tea making drinkers grow feathers.

What does all this add up to? Tea, in the pre-Tang imagination, was a substance at the edge of the ordinary world — found in wild mountains, guarded by strange beings, connected to ghosts, immortals, and the boundaries between the living and the dead. Lu Yu was working with this raw material when he built his refined cultural edifice around it.

## Tea as offering before it was a beverage

The earliest ritual use of tea appears not in temples but in family religion. In one story, a widow and her sons live over an ancient burial mound and make tea offerings to whoever is inside. Her sons want to dig it up; the ghost appears in a dream to thank her for the tea and scatters hundreds of thousands of cash coins in the courtyard by morning.

More formally: Emperor Shizu Wu of Southern Qi (r. 483–494 CE) issued a deathbed edict commanding that his altar use only "cakes, fruit, tea, dry foodstuffs, wine and dried meat" — explicitly no animal sacrifice. Tea was thus suitable for imperial ancestral offerings centuries before it became a mass cultural phenomenon.

## Tea was a Tang invention

Benn's conclusion: the most sensible reading of all this evidence is that tea as a *cultural practice* — a refined beverage with aesthetic, religious, and social meaning — was essentially a Tang dynasty invention. Before the mid-Tang it was a minor regional specialty of south China, known primarily as medicine. After Lu Yu's *Classic of Tea* (c. 760 CE) and Buddhist monks spreading tea drinking as an alternative to alcohol, it spread across the empire within decades. By the 780s there was already a government tax on it.

The contrast with coffee in Europe is apt: both had a similar lag between initial discovery and mass adoption, and both spread rapidly once an intellectual and religious culture embraced them.

## Buddhism and tea: a complicated relationship

Buddhist monastic rules formally prohibited alcohol, but Tang monastery account books tell a different story: Dunhuang records from the 8th–9th centuries show roughly 9% of expenditures going to alcohol. The monks were drinking. Tea served as a socially acceptable substitute — something that let literati and monks mix at gatherings where wine would otherwise have been obligatory.

The clearest snapshot of this world is the *Chajiu lun* ("Mr. Tea versus Mr. Alcohol"), a debate text discovered at Dunhuang in which Tea and Alcohol argue about superiority. Neither wins. A third party interrupts: Mr. Water. Water underlies both of them. The debate is over.

Buddhism wasn't the only vector. Taoist painters used tea in a different register: **Wu Daozi**, Tang's most celebrated muralist, needed to be drunk to enter the flow-state that produced his best demons. His rival Gu Kuang made the opposite argument — tea gives you the same access to the *dao* without the subsequent blur.

The **[[event-an-lushan-rebellion]]** (755–763 CE) — which killed tens of millions and collapsed Tang confidence — may have done more to reduce Chinese alcohol consumption than any religious preaching. When half the country is in ruins, excess becomes harder to justify.

## The 1987 Famen si discovery

Workers repairing a collapsing Tang pagoda at Famen si broke through to an underground crypt containing a finger-bone relic of the Buddha — and the most complete imperial Tang tea service ever found: gilt silver, exquisite, catalogued in a contemporaneous inventory. Scholars keen to link imperial tea culture to esoteric Buddhist ritual pointed to the proximity of the relics and the teaware. Benn's reading is cooler: the inventory clearly describes the tea items as imperial gifts, not ritual equipment. The exotic explanation is probably wrong. It was a gift set that happened to be stored near holy relics.

## Tea poetry: how a vocabulary was invented from nothing

Tea is **entirely absent from pre-Tang poetry** — the Shijing, the Chuci, the Six Dynasties corpus, nothing. Then in the mid-Tang it erupts as a major poetic subject, almost entirely through two men: [[figure-lu-yu]] and the monk-poet **Jiaoran** (730–799).

Jiaoran was Lu Yu's closest friend; they met in 757 CE, both displaced southward by the An Lushan rebellion. Jiaoran established the foundational poetic vocabulary: tea as spiritual purifier, as conduit to the transcendent, as the substance that makes contemplation possible.

**Lu Tong's "Seven Cups"** poem became the canonical statement of this tradition — the most quoted tea poem in Chinese history:

> The first cup moistens my lips and throat. The second cup banishes my loneliness and boredom. The third cup searches my withered entrails... The seventh cup — ah, but I could take no more! I only feel the pure wind rising beneath my arms.

**Li Bai** used tea to frame transcendence: the monks at Transcendent's Palm peak who cultivated their tea plants eighty years and grew rosy-cheeked; the silver-furred bats who had drunk stalactite water long enough to glow.

**Bai Juyi** is the useful counterexample. He wrote frequently about tea and enjoyed it plainly, without any religious framing — just *this is good, I drink it with friends*. His ordinariness reveals that everyone else's spiritual vocabulary was a deliberate choice, not a default.

## Lu Yu and the *Classic of Tea*

[[figure-lu-yu]] (733–804 CE) is the person who turned tea from a regional beverage into a civilizational art form. His *Classic of Tea* (*Chajing* 茶经, c. 780 CE) — roughly 7,000 characters — is a precise, practical manual covering equipment, fuel, water quality, and the critical three-stage boiling sequence:

1. **First boil** (fish-eye bubbles): add salt
2. **Second boil** (strung pearls): ladle out a dipperful of water
3. **Third boil** (rolling): add powdered tea in a swirl; return the reserved water; froth is the point

After the *Chajing*, you could be *right or wrong* about tea. That is Lu Yu's real invention: the category of correctness.

He was deified after his death. Tea merchants placed clay effigies of him behind their counters and poured tea over the figurine when business was good — and water when it was bad. The gentle rebuke was specific: you are failing us.

## Song dynasty: cake tea and the competitive cup

Song tea culture was dominated by **cake tea** (*piancha* 片茶) — compressed bricks of powdered tea molded with dragon and phoenix insignia. Production was grotesque in its precision: only single-leaf buds, rinsed four times, steamed to exact timing, pressed overnight, pounded, molded, cured for up to fifteen days. The prestige product came from the **Northern Park** (*Beiyuan*) in Fujian's Jian'an county, an imperial plantation that supplied tribute tea directly to the court.

The standard preparation was **diancha** 点茶 ("pointing tea"): pour a thin stream of boiling water from a narrow-spouted ewer into a bowl of powdered tea, then whisk vigorously with a bamboo whisk until a white, creamy froth formed. Dark Fujian *jian*-ware bowls showed off the pale froth to best effect. Competitive tea contests — whose froth was whiter? whose lasted longer? — became a literati performance art.

**Emperor Huizong** (r. 1101–1126) wrote the most refined Song tea manual, full of precise technical detail; he personally preferred almost-unobtainable wild white tea. He was captured by the Jurchen in 1127 and died in captivity in the north. The most knowledgeable tea emperor in Chinese history ended his life as a prisoner. See [[dynasty-song]].

The Chan phrase **"Go drink tea"** (*chicha qu* 吃茶去) is attributed to Chan Master Zhaozhou Congshen (778–897): asked to two monks — one who had been to the monastery before, one who had not — the answer is always "go drink tea." The abbot asks why the same answer for both; Zhaozhou tells him to come closer, then says it again. Awakening is inseparable from the daily routine. Tea was a necessity in every Song Chan monastery; formal monastic tea ceremonies were among the most elaborate rituals in the monastic calendar, modeled on imperial court banquet etiquette.

## Ming dynasty: the revolution nobody planned

The most consequential moment in Chinese tea history was a single bureaucratic edict. In **1391**, the first Ming emperor Zhu Yuanzheng — a man of peasant origin — abolished cake tea as tribute on the grounds that its production "overtaxed the people's strength." Whole-leaf tea would be sent to court instead.

The result was unintentional: **leaf tea** replaced powdered tea across China within a generation. The Ming brewing method — rinse leaves, steep in a stoneware teapot, pour and re-steep — is essentially what most of the world does today. Tea became portable, democratic, and commercially explosive. Roadside tea stalls appeared everywhere.

**Yixing** (*zisha* 紫砂, "purple clay") teapots emerged in this context: slightly porous stoneware that preserved delicate fragrances that other vessels lost. They remain the definitive teapot form.

The five great Ming teas — **Longjing** (Dragon Well, Hangzhou), **Songluo** (Anhui), **Luojie** (Yixing), **Huqiu** (Tiger Hill, Suzhou, the "king among teas"), and **Wuyi** (Fujian cliff tea) — were all grown at Buddhist monastic sites. The monk Dafang invented Songluo tea by applying Huqiu monastery's pan-firing techniques to tea he picked on a different mountain; neighboring counties immediately started branding their tea "Songluo."

Ming connoisseurship reached heights of refined excess. Water obsession was as intense as tea obsession: Yuan Hongdao once ordered thirty jars of the famous "number two spring under heaven" from Mount Hui. His servants secretly replaced the water with ordinary mountain spring water. His friends tasted it and praised it effusively. Yuan discovered the substitution and was furious.

The best statement of the Ming aesthetic is Xu Cishu's list (1549–1604) of appropriate occasions for tea: "when mind or hands are idle / when weary of declaiming verse / in light cloud and drizzle / on small bridges or gaily painted pleasure boats / as drunken guests disperse / when the children have left the building / at pure and secluded temples."

Tea in the Ming had become an *idea* as much as a beverage — the essential companion of the scholar in retreat from vicious court politics, the prop of all literati fantasies about the contemplative life.

## The great split: why Japanese tea looks like Song tea

When the Japanese monk Eisai visited Song China (1168–1191), he brought back the *diancha* method — whisking powdered tea in a bowl. Japan preserved this as *matcha*. China moved on: the Ming leaf-tea revolution happened in 1391, and powdered/whisked tea vanished from China within decades. This is why Japanese tea ceremony still looks like 12th-century Chinese tea.
