# Spinoza, *Ethics* — Part I: Concerning God

**Source:** *Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata* (1677), Part I  
**Secondary guide:** Beth Lord, *Spinoza's Ethics: An Edinburgh Philosophical Guide* (2010)  
**Translation used:** R. H. M. Elwes (Gutenberg)

---

## What this part is and why it's hard

Part I of the *Ethics* is ontology — the study of being itself. Before Spinoza can tell us anything about minds, emotions, ethics, or freedom, he needs to establish what kind of thing exists and what kind of thing we are. The answer he arrives at is radical enough that he has to prove it from first principles.

The format is modelled on Euclid's geometry: Definitions → Axioms → Propositions → Proofs. Each proposition is proved from the definitions, axioms, and earlier propositions. The style feels cold and mechanical, but this is deliberate — Spinoza wants mathematical certainty, not persuasion.

Beth Lord's advice to new readers: the definitions feel arbitrary at first. Trust them provisionally. Their meaning and truth will only become fully apparent when the whole argument is finished.

---

## The problem Spinoza is solving

To understand what Spinoza is doing, you need to know what he's arguing against.

**The Aristotelian picture**: The world is made up of many independent substances (things). Each substance has properties (attributes) that can change. My body is a substance; its weight is an attribute. The attribute depends on the substance but the substance depends on nothing else.

**The Cartesian revision** (Descartes, 1644): Every substance has one *fundamental* attribute that constitutes what kind of thing it is. Bodies have **extension** (they take up space). Minds have **thinking**. These two kinds of substance are completely different and don't interact directly. A third substance — God — is infinite, necessarily existing, and created and sustains all the others.

The 17th-century reader came to Spinoza with this picture already in their head: a world of many independent substances (minds and bodies), all sustained by a God who stood apart from creation and could have made things differently.

Spinoza is going to dismantle this. Not by rejecting the definitions, but by following them more carefully than Descartes did.

## The definitions: where everything begins

Spinoza opens with eight definitions. The crucial ones:

**Definition I — Cause of itself (***causa sui***)**:
> "That of which the essence involves existence, or that of which the nature is only conceivable as existent."

Something is self-caused if its very nature requires it to exist. It cannot not exist. There was no "before" it started existing, because to start existing it would already have to exist — so it must be eternal. A self-caused thing is the eternal activity of bringing itself into being.

**Definition III — Substance**:
> "That which is in itself, and is conceived through itself: that of which a conception can be formed independently of any other conception."

Two conditions: a substance (1) *exists in itself* — its being depends on nothing else; (2) *is conceived through itself* — understanding it requires no other concept. Spinoza's key word is **"in"**: not spatial but logical. To be "in itself" means to depend logically on itself alone.

This matches the Cartesian definition — Descartes would agree. The trouble starts when Spinoza applies it rigorously.

**Definition IV — Attribute**:
> "That which the intellect perceives as constituting the essence of substance."

This is the most subtle definition. An attribute is NOT a property OF substance, like weight is a property of a body. An attribute is the substance ITSELF, as perceived in a certain way. Extension and thinking are not things substance *has*; they are ways substance *is*.

The distinction matters because it means you can't separate a substance from its attribute to find the "real" substance underneath. Substance without its attribute is just pure, indeterminate being.

**Definition V — Mode**:
> "The modifications of substance, or that which exists in, and is conceived through, something other than itself."

Modes are the opposite of substances. A mode depends on something else for its existence and can only be understood through that something else. Rain is a mode of water: it depends on water, and you can't truly understand what rain is without knowing what water is.

**Definition VI — God**:
> "A being absolutely infinite — that is, a substance consisting in infinite attributes, of which each expresses eternal and infinite essentiality."

God is defined as a substance with **infinite** attributes. Not two (extension and thinking, as Descartes had it) — infinite. Every way that being can be expressed, to an infinite extent.

## The argument: from definitions to one God

### Props 1–5: only one substance per attribute

The first five propositions build on each other quietly. The big moment is **Proposition V**:

> *There cannot exist in the universe two or more substances having the same nature or attribute.*

This breaks from Descartes. Descartes believed there could be many thinking substances (human minds) and many extended substances (bodies). Spinoza shows this is incoherent.

The proof: how would you distinguish two substances that share the same attribute? Either by a difference in their attributes (but they share an attribute, so that fails) or by a difference in their modes. But modes are just the surface manifestations of substance. To compare the *substances*, you must set the modes aside — and when you do, there is nothing left to distinguish them. They're the same substance. Therefore: there can be at most **one** substance with any given attribute.

(A natural objection: couldn't there be two substances with the same attribute sitting side by side in space? No — because space itself is a mode of substance, not a container substances exist inside. You can't use space to distinguish substances because substances are prior to space.)

### Props 6–8: substance is necessary and infinite

Since no substance can be produced by another substance of a different attribute (they share nothing, so can't cause each other — Prop. III, V), each substance must be **self-caused** (Prop. VII). And if a substance's essence involves its own existence, it exists **necessarily** — it cannot fail to exist.

Furthermore, every substance is **necessarily infinite** (Prop. VIII). For a thing to be finite, it must be limited by another thing of the same nature. But there's only one substance of each attribute (Prop. V), so nothing of the same nature exists to limit it. Therefore substance is infinite.

This is where everyday objects quietly disappear from the picture. A rock is finite. A human body is finite. These finite things cannot be substances — substances are necessary and infinite. Finite things must be *modes*.

### Props 9–11: God exists necessarily

A substance can have more than one attribute (Prop. IX–X). The more reality a thing has, the more attributes it has. A substance of infinite attributes has infinite reality — every way that being can be expressed.

God is defined as this substance of infinite attributes. Since every substance necessarily exists (Prop. VII), God necessarily exists (Prop. XI).

Beth Lord's clarification on why this isn't a circular argument: Spinoza doesn't argue "God is defined as necessarily existing, therefore God necessarily exists" — that's the Anselmian/Cartesian ontological proof, which doesn't work. Spinoza argues: **being itself cannot not exist**. God is being as such — the totality of what it is for anything to be. You cannot coherently imagine being as a whole not existing, because that very imagining would require being. If you find yourself doubting the existence of God, Spinoza would say: you cannot doubt being. You are being. What you're really doubting is the anthropomorphic God of religion, which is something else.

"If you prefer, call it being, substance, power, or nature. You cannot not believe in being; so you cannot not believe in Spinoza's God." — Beth Lord

### Props 12–14: one substance only

Substance cannot be divided (Props. XII–XIII). And since God has infinite attributes, any other putative substance would need at least one attribute — but that attribute is already God's. By Prop. V, two substances can't share an attribute. Therefore there is no other substance (Prop. XIV).

**There is exactly one substance. It is God. Everything else — every mind, body, thought, stone, and storm — is a mode.**

The two corollaries to Prop. XIV:
1. God is the only substance in all of nature.
2. Extension and thinking are attributes of God.

This is the famous identification: **Deus sive Natura** — "God, or Nature." Not God *created* Nature. God *is* Nature. The phrase appears explicitly in Part IV's Preface: "that eternal and infinite being we call God, or Nature."

## What comes out of Props 15–36

With the monist conclusion established, Spinoza works through the implications:

**P15**: Everything is *in* God. Nothing can be or be conceived without God. God is not somewhere; God is the ontological ground of everything.

**P16**: From the necessity of God's nature, infinitely many things follow in infinite ways. God doesn't decide to create — things follow from God's nature as necessarily as the properties of a triangle follow from the definition of a triangle.

**P17**: God acts solely by the necessity of its own nature, constrained by nothing external. This is what "free" means for God: acting from one's own nature alone, not compelled by anything outside.

**P18**: God is the **immanent** cause of all things, not the **transient** cause. A transient cause produces effects external to itself (a craftsman makes a pot). An immanent cause produces effects that remain within itself. God's effects — all the modes of the world — are *in* God. God doesn't stand outside creation making it; God is what it all is.

**P29**: Nothing in the universe is contingent. Everything that happens follows necessarily from the nature of God. "Contingency" is not a feature of reality — it's a symptom of our ignorance of causes. When we say something is "contingent" we mean we don't know the cause that determined it.

**P29, Note** — *Natura naturans* and *Natura naturata*:

> *Natura naturans* ("nature naturing") = God as active — substance itself, the attributes, the ground of everything. Nature as cause.

> *Natura naturata* ("nature natured") = all the modes, everything that follows from God's nature. The world as it appears to us.

Both are "Nature." The distinction is between Nature as the active source (the naturing) and Nature as the result (the natured). There is no meaningful separation between them — but the distinction helps us see that what we ordinarily call "the world" is the expressed face of something that cannot be reduced to any of its expressions.

**P32**: Will is a mode of thinking, not a special power above the causal chain. Even infinite will requires causes. Therefore God has no "free will" in the sense of arbitrary choice. God no more acts by free will than a triangle "chooses" to have interior angles summing to 180°.

**P33**: Things could not have been brought into being in any order different from what has obtained. The world is not one option among many that God selected. It is the only expression possible of God's nature.

## The Appendix: the teleology problem

The Appendix is the most polemical section of Part I, and possibly the most important for understanding what Spinoza is really doing.

He diagnoses a near-universal human error: **teleological thinking** — the assumption that things exist *for a purpose*, that nature acts with ends in view, that God made eyes for seeing and the sun for light.

His explanation for why this error persists is sharp:

1. Humans are ignorant of the causes of things.
2. But humans act for purposes — we always do things *for* something.
3. We assume everything else does too.
4. We find things in nature that are useful to us (eyes, light, food).
5. We can't have made them, so something else must have made them *for us*.
6. That something is God, who must therefore be a being with purposes (like us, but bigger).

This whole chain of reasoning collapses once you see that things don't happen *in order to* achieve purposes — they happen because they are *necessitated* by prior causes. The person who insists on teleology, when pressed for the cause behind the cause, eventually retreats to "God's will" — which Spinoza calls **the sanctuary of ignorance**: the place you run when you've run out of explanations.

The stone-on-the-head example: if a stone falls from a roof and kills someone, the teleologist says the stone fell *in order to kill the man*. Ask why the wind was blowing — God's will. Ask why the man was walking there — God's will. Every "why" gets answered with "purpose" and every "purpose" eventually bottoms out in God's will, which is just the name for "I don't know."

The consequences of this error:

- **"Good" and "bad"** are not features of nature — they're descriptions of how things affect human beings. What helps us = good; what hinders us = bad. These are relative to us, not properties of reality.
- **"Beautiful" and "ugly"** are how our senses respond to things, not qualities objects possess.
- **"Order" and "chaos"** are descriptions of how easy things are for us to imagine, not structures inherent in nature. "Order is what's easy to picture; confusion is what's hard." When we say God made the world "in order," we are attributing our imagination to God.

The punchline: all the "explanations commonly given of nature are mere modes of imagining, and do not indicate the true nature of anything, but only the constitution of the imagination."

Mathematics, Spinoza notes, almost alone escaped this prejudice — because mathematicians consider only the essence and properties of figures, without asking what they're *for*.

## The upshot: what this means for everything else

Part I establishes the ground for the rest of the *Ethics*. If the world is one substance, then:

- Minds and bodies are not two kinds of thing — they are two *attributes* of one thing (addressed in Part II)
- Human emotions and actions follow necessarily from our nature, just as everything follows necessarily from God's nature (Parts III–IV)
- Freedom can't mean "free will" (escaping the causal chain) — it has to mean something else (Part V)

The whole ethical project — how to live well, how to be free — rests on this foundation. You can't understand Spinoza's ethics without Part I. Which is why he starts here, even though it's the hardest part to read.

## A note on the "God or Nature" problem

Many readers come to Spinoza as atheists and find themselves unsettled: does he actually believe in God? Is this really theology in disguise?

The honest answer is that the question might not have the right shape. Spinoza's God has none of the properties that make God theologically interesting: no personality, no intentions, no free will, no particular concern for human beings, no ability to intervene in nature, no relationship with any religious tradition. What God *is* is being itself, the totality of what exists, the ground from which everything necessarily follows.

Is that "God" or "Nature"? Spinoza says: same thing.

His contemporaries called him an atheist. Novalis called him "the God-intoxicated man." Both were responding to the same text. The question of which label fits might depend more on the labeller than on Spinoza.

## Cross-domain connections

**Daoism** ([[src-tao-liu-ch1-2]]): The Daoist concept of the natural world as numinous, as "things before humans have got at them," resonates with Spinoza's *Natura naturans* — Nature as active, prior to human categorization, not reducible to human purposes. Both traditions resist anthropomorphism; both insist that human concepts of "good" and "bad" are not features of reality as such.

**Confucian teleology**: The Confucian habit of historicizing myths — turning gods into sage-emperors who acted for good governance purposes — is a version of exactly the teleological projection Spinoza diagnoses in the Appendix. Both impose human purposiveness (in this case, moral-political purpose) onto processes that Spinoza would say follow by necessity.
