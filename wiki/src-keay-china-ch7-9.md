# Keay, *China: A History* — Chapters 7–9

**Source**: John Keay, *China: A History* (2008)  
**Chapters**: 7 ("Four Hundred Years of Vicissitude," 189–550), 8 ("Sui, Tang and the Second Empire," 550–650), 9 ("High Tang," 650–755)  
**Coverage**: Three Kingdoms through the An Lushan rebellion

---

## What this source covers

Chapter 7 covers the dissolution of Han rule through the Three Kingdoms period, the Red Cliffs battle, the collapse of the Jin dynasty, the Sixteen Kingdoms chaos in the north, Buddhism's dramatic spread under non-Han patrons like Fotudeng and Shi Hu, the crystallization of Daoism as an institutional religion, and the Northern Wei's great experiment in Sinicization under Dowager Empress Feng and Xiao-wendi.

Chapter 8 covers the Northern Zhou's institutional innovations, the Sui dynasty's reunification, Sui Wendi's administrative reforms and Buddhist patronage, Sui Yangdi's Grand Canal and Korean disasters, and the Tang founding through Tang Taizong's expansion — including the story of the monk Xuanzang and his sixteen-year journey to India.

Chapter 9 covers the fifty-year dominance of Wu Zetian, the High Tang under Tang Xuanzong (the cosmopolitan peak of Chang'an, Li Bo and Du Fu, the exotic culture of the silk routes), and the An Lushan rebellion that shattered the dynasty's golden age — including Yang Guifei's death and the beginning of the Tang's structural transformation.

## What's most interesting

**The Nanai Vandak letter** — a Sogdian merchant's 313 CE letter home to Samarkand, found 1,600 years later in an abandoned postbag at Dunhuang, saying "Luoyang is no more, Yeh is no more! . . . You will have no wealth from here." One of the most vivid primary sources in Chinese history.

**Fotudeng and the lotus** — a Buddhist missionary securing the patronage of a murderous Xiongnu general by conjuring a blue lotus from his begging bowl. Shi Hu's frank explanation for his Buddhism: "Buddha, being a foreign god, is the very one we should worship." The unlikely origins of Buddhism's mass adoption in northern China.

**The intercalary dynasty concept** — Keay's extended meditation on the Sui as a necessary, traumatic correction between the Period of Disunion and the Tang, consciously parallel to Qin between Warring States and Han. A genuine interpretive framework rather than just a narrative device.

**The Grand Canal** — Keay's comparison to the first transcontinental railroads in North America. The canal made China's economic integration feasible; some sections are still the busiest waterways in the world; 21st-century plans envisage converting it to pipe Yangzi floodwater to Beijing's aquifers.

**Wu Zetian's fall** — at eighty-something, bedridden, having pardoned her scandalous favorites one too many times, she was confronted by ministers and an heir apparent in the palace. Her last act was to address them "in terms of contempt." Then she returned to bed.

**Yang Guifei** — strangled with silk at the emperor's silent consent, on the road to Sichuan, as the army mutinied. Xuanzong's helplessness and guilt became the raw material of one of the great tragic love stories in Chinese literature.

**"A poor Persian"** — listed as the number-one "anomaly or contradiction in terms" in a Tang literary parlor game. Chang'an's Persian merchants were always rich. They financed the silk trade and dealt in silver coin (preferred over debased Tang copper). Context for the extraordinary cosmopolitanism of High Tang.

## New information vs. existing wiki

- Confirms and greatly expands Three Kingdoms material — particularly the Red Cliffs battle mechanics, the literary renaissance, and the historiographical debate over Wei vs. Shu legitimacy
- Adds the Sixteen Kingdoms period, previously not covered in detail
- Substantially enriches Northern Wei: Dowager Empress Feng, the equal-field system, the 493 capital move to Luoyang
- Explains the Northern Zhou's institutional role in creating the Sui-Tang framework
- Provides the first detailed account of Buddhism's transmission to China (Fotudeng, Kumarajiva, Xuanzang)
- First full coverage of the Sui: Grand Canal, the intercalary concept, Yangdi's character
- First detailed Tang coverage: Taizong, Wu Zetian, Xuanzong and High Tang cosmopolitanism, An Lushan
