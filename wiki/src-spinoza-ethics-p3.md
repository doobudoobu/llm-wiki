# Spinoza, *Ethics* — Part III: On the Origin and Nature of the Emotions

**Source:** *Ethica Ordine Geometrico Demonstrata* (1677), Part III  
**Secondary guide:** Beth Lord, *Spinoza's Ethics: An Edinburgh Philosophical Guide* (2010)  
**Translation used:** R. H. M. Elwes (Gutenberg)

---

## The manifesto

Part III opens with a provocation. Spinoza has been reading the philosophical literature on the passions, and he is annoyed.

Most writers treat the emotions as flaws in human nature — things to be bemoaned, derided, despised, or abused. The best of them (Descartes included) argue that a strong will, guided by reason, can achieve mastery over the passions — as though reason and emotion were two armies, reason to win, passion to be conquered.

Spinoza rejects all of this. His alternative is bracing:

> "Nothing comes to pass in nature, which can be set down to a flaw therein ... the passions of hatred, anger, envy, and so on, considered in themselves, follow from this same necessity and efficacy of nature ... and possess certain properties as worthy of being known as the properties of anything else."

Human beings are not **a kingdom within a kingdom** (his phrase). We are not exempt from nature's laws, able to determine ourselves regardless of outside causes. We are part of nature. Our feelings follow from our nature and from our interactions with other things, by the same necessity as everything else. Treating emotions as moral failures produces bad philosophy and worse psychology. Spinoza will treat "human actions and desires in exactly the same manner, as though I were concerned with lines, planes, and solids."

This is not coldness. It is the refusal to despise ourselves for being finite.

## The definitions: activity, passivity, affect

**Definition I — Adequate and inadequate cause**: A cause is adequate when its effect can be clearly and distinctly understood through that cause alone. A cause is inadequate (partial) when the effect follows from it *and* from other things.

**Definition II — Acting and being acted upon**: We *act* when something follows from our nature through us as adequate cause. We are *passive* when we are only a partial cause — when external things partly determine what happens.

**Definition III — Affect**: An affect is a modification of the body by which its power of activity is increased or diminished, together with the idea of that modification. If we are the adequate cause of the modification, it is an *active* affect. If external things are partly responsible, it is a *passive* affect — a **passion**.

This framework is crucial. An emotion is not primarily a feeling in the head. It is a change in the body's power to act, and simultaneously (by parallelism) a change in the mind's power to think. Joy is a real increase in the body-mind's capacity for action and thought. Sadness is a real diminishment of that capacity. Emotions are not decorative accompaniments to action; they *are* changes in power that determine what we can do.

## Props 1–3: Active and passive; the body's unknown capabilities

**P1**: The mind is active insofar as it has adequate ideas; passive insofar as it has inadequate ideas.

**P2** (parallelism reminder): The body cannot determine the mind; the mind cannot determine the body.

Spinoza expects incredulity. His challenge:

> "No one has hitherto laid down the limits to the powers of the body — no one has as yet been taught by experience what the body can accomplish solely by the laws of nature... Somnambulists do many things in their sleep, which they would not venture to do when awake: these instances are enough to show, that the body can by the sole laws of its nature do many things which the mind wonders at."

When people say "the mind determined the body to do X," they are simply confessing ignorance of the physical cause of X. It is the same move as saying "God willed it" — a name for not knowing the cause.

The famous images: "An infant believes that of its own free will it desires milk, an angry child believes that it freely desires vengeance, a timid child believes that it freely desires to run away; further, a drunken man believes that he utters from the free decision of his mind words which, when he is sober, he would willingly have withheld."

Those who believe they act from free mental decisions **dream with their eyes open**.

## Props 4–9: *Conatus* — the foundation of Spinoza's psychology

**P4**: Nothing can be destroyed except by an external cause.

**P6**: Everything, insofar as it is in itself, *endeavours to persist in its own being*.

**P7**: This endeavour is nothing else but the actual essence of the thing.

These two propositions are the foundation of everything that follows. Every thing — every mode of God — expresses God's power in a finite, limited way. To be that thing is to be that particular expression of power. There is no separate "will to live" added on to a thing's nature; the striving to persist just *is* what it is to be that thing.

The Latin word is **conatus** — endeavour, striving, effort. For Hobbes (a major influence on Spinoza), conatus was a mechanical notion: the basic force underlying motion. For Spinoza it is at once metaphysical, psychological, and ethical. It is what makes each particular thing the thing it is.

**P8**: Conatus involves no finite time — it tends toward indefinite persistence. Nothing contains a built-in expiration date. Spinoza would have found the Freudian "death drive" literally incoherent: a being's essence cannot include striving toward its own destruction. Death always comes from outside.

**P9**: The mind is conscious of its own conatus. When considered in relation to the mind alone, we call it *will*. In relation to mind and body together, *appetite*. Appetite with consciousness of itself is *desire*.

And then — one of Spinoza's most striking inversions:

> "In no case do we strive for, wish for, long for, or desire anything, because we deem it to be good; but on the other hand, we deem a thing to be good because we strive for it."

Value does not precede desire. There is no independent list of good things that we then desire. What we desire *is* what we call good. "Good" and "bad" are not properties of things in themselves — they are descriptions of how things affect our conatus. This demolishes any ethics based on conforming desire to a pre-given standard. But it also opens the possibility of a more honest ethics built on understanding what actually promotes flourishing.

## P11: Joy, sadness, desire — the three primary affects

All emotions are combinations and variations of three primitives:

- **Desire** (*cupiditas*): appetite with consciousness — conatus at the level of striving
- **Joy** (*laetitia*): a transition to *greater* perfection — an increase of the body-mind's power to act
- **Sadness** (*tristitia*): a transition to *lesser* perfection — a decrease of that power

"Perfection" here is not moral. It means: the degree of being, the extent of a thing's power to act and exist. When joy increases, *more* of you is actualized. When sadness diminishes, *less* of you is actualized.

From these three, Spinoza derives the entire catalogue of human emotion — doing for psychology something like what Newton did with force: establishing a small set of primitives from which everything complex emerges by analysis.

## P12–18: Love, hate, and the mechanics of conditioned emotion

**Love** = joy accompanied by the idea of an external cause.  
**Hate** = sadness accompanied by the idea of an external cause.

We strive to keep present what we love; we strive to remove or destroy what we hate.

**P15**: *Anything* can accidentally become a cause of joy, sadness, or desire — simply by being present when we were feeling one of these emotions. No causal connection is required. This is Spinoza's account of classical conditioning, three hundred years before Pavlov.

**P16**: A thing that merely resembles something that once brought us joy — we will love it. A thing that resembles something painful — we will hate it. A single bad experience with a dog leads someone to hate dogs. We are drawn to people who resemble our first love.

**P17 — Vacillation**: When the same thing triggers both joy and sadness — through conflicting associations — we simultaneously love and hate it. This state of vacillation is to emotion what doubt is to imagination.

**P18 — Hope and Fear**: We are affected as strongly by imagined past or future things as by present ones (because the image is present in us regardless of when the thing occurs). Therefore:
- **Hope** = inconstant joy about a future outcome that is uncertain
- **Fear** = inconstant sadness about a future outcome that is uncertain
- Uncertainty removed: hope → confidence; fear → despair

## P21–35: The social emotions — how we infect each other

**Emotional contagion (P27)**: By conceiving a thing like ourselves as feeling an emotion, we are ourselves affected with the same emotion. Because our bodies are structurally similar, imagining another person's experience involves our own body in a similar modification. We feel what we imagine others feel. This is the foundation of **compassion** (imitation of pain) and **emulation** (imitation of desire).

This has alarming consequences:
- We love what others love; we hate what others hate; we desire what others desire (P31-32)
- If two people desire the same unique thing, conflict is inevitable
- "Everyone is ambitious to be loved and praised by all and wants other people to live according to his temperament"

**Praise, blame, esteem, repentance (P29-30)**: We strive to do whatever we imagine brings others' praise. We esteem ourselves when we imagine being praised; we feel repentance (self-hatred) when we imagine being blamed. Self-esteem is self-love; repentance is self-hate. The man who lives entirely for praise loves himself as an image reflected in others' eyes — not as anything that actually belongs to him.

Scholium to P30: "One who exults at being esteemed is proud and imagines himself to be pleasing to all, when he is burdensome to all." Presumably Spinoza knew this type personally.

**Jealousy (P35)**: When the person we love is with another, we feel simultaneously love and hatred for them, combined with envy of their new companion. The hatred is stronger in proportion to how much joy the person formerly brought us — and stronger still if they have gone to someone we already hate.

Spinoza provides an example that Lord notes "can only come from personal experience":

> "He who imagines that a woman he loves prostitutes herself to another not only will be saddened, because his own appetite is restrained, but also will be repelled by her, because he is forced to join the image of the thing he loves to the shameful parts and excretions of the other."

The analytical precision barely conceals raw feeling. Spinoza, like everyone else, was a finite mode.

## P40–49: Hatred, anger, ethnic prejudice

**Reciprocal hatred (P40)**: We hate those we imagine hate us unjustly. Since we almost always think others' hatred for us is unjust, "it seldom happens" that we feel shame — anger and resentment are far more natural responses.

**Hatred overcome by love (P43-44)**: Hatred can be diminished or destroyed by love. When we imagine an enemy loving us, we begin imagining ourselves as the cause of their love, and hatred gradually gives way. Love arising from prior hatred is *stronger* than love without that history — the transition starts from a lower point, and the rise is greater. This is the mechanism behind narratives of redemption.

**Ethnic hatred (P46)**: Spinoza's analysis of racism and nationalism, written by someone from a persecuted minority:

> If someone has once been affected with sadness by someone of a different class or nation, and he imagines that person under the universal name of the class or nation, he will hate everyone of that class or nation.

This is nothing but the accidental conditioning from P15-16, applied at scale. Race and nation are imaginary generalizations — linguistic conventions imposed on individuals, not grounded in any individual's essence. There is no rational basis for racism or nationalism.

**Love and imagined freedom (P49)**: We love or hate things more intensely when we imagine them to be free agents responsible for their actions. This is why interpersonal love and hatred are so much more powerful than our feelings toward rocks or storms. If we truly understood the causes that determined a person's behavior, our feelings would be more tempered.

## P53–57: Self-love, humility, and the radical particularity of emotion

When the mind considers its own power of thinking, it necessarily feels joy (P53). Everyone loves to show off: "men, for this reason, are troublesome to one another."

Humility is rare (P55). It is also easily faked. "Those who are believed to be most despondent and humble are usually most ambitious and envious" — the ostentatious self-deprecation of those who most desperately want admiration. Spinoza's examples from three hundred years later could be lifted from Jane Austen or Dickens, and Lord explicitly notes this.

**P56-57 — Affects are radically particular**: There are as many species of joy as there are objects that affect us. Each instance of love is specific to you + that specific person + that specific moment. "Love" in general is a linguistic convention. The reality is that your love for this person today is unlike any emotional encounter you have ever had or will have — shaped by your specific bodily history, your specific associations, the specific state your body is in right now.

This extends to animals: horses, dogs, and beetles all feel desire, joy, and sadness — but different from ours, because their essences differ. "There is no small difference between the gladness by which a drunk is led and the gladness a philosopher possesses."

---

## P58–59 and the closing image

**Active affects (P58)**: Beyond the passive affects driven by external things, there are active affects — joy and desire that arise from our own activity, from adequate ideas and adequate causation. The joy of genuine understanding is different in quality from the passive joy of pleasure. Both matter; the passive joys of experience prepare the ground for active joy.

**No active sadness (P59)**: Active sadness is a contradiction in terms. Sadness is necessarily the diminishment of power — it *is* passivity by definition. There is no such thing as the joy of self-destruction. Insofar as we are active, we can only experience joy.

The closing image of Part III, Spinoza's most memorable:

> "We are driven about in many ways by external causes, and, like waves on the sea, driven by contrary winds, we toss about, not knowing our outcome and our fate."

A finite mode is a wave: it rises from the infinite continuum of substance, is pushed and pulled by forces it cannot see or name, and eventually breaks, dissolving back into the sea. Part IV asks what, if anything, we can do about this.

## The Definitions of the Affects

Part III closes with 48 named emotions defined systematically. A sample:

| Affect | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| Love | Joy with the idea of an external cause |
| Hate | Sadness with the idea of an external cause |
| Hope | Inconstant joy about an uncertain future |
| Fear | Inconstant sadness about an uncertain future |
| Envy | Hatred causing us to feel sad at another's joy |
| Compassion | Sadness at another's misfortune |
| Pride | Joy from thinking too highly of oneself |
| Repentance | Sadness accompanied by the idea of oneself as cause |
| Shame | Sadness from imagining being blamed by others |

**General Definition of the Affects** (end of Part III): An affect is a confused idea by which the mind affirms a greater or lesser force of existing of its body than before — and insofar as this idea is confused, the mind cannot have adequate knowledge of the body's power to act.

## Cross-domain connections

**Stoic therapy vs. Spinoza**: The Stoics aimed to extirpate the passions through rational discipline; Spinoza says this is both impossible and misguided. We cannot simply choose not to feel things. The goal is not suppression but transformation — passive affects, understood adequately, can become active ones. The method is Part V's project.

**Buddhist *kleśa***: The Buddhist doctrine of emotional afflictions rooted in ignorance has structural resonance with Spinoza. Inadequate ideas drive passive affects; adequate understanding transforms them. Both traditions refuse to moralize about passion, preferring causal analysis. Both are interested in liberation from suffering rather than moral condemnation of those who suffer.

**Confucian ritual and emotional conditioning**: The Confucian use of *li* (禮 — rites, ritual) to train emotional responses through repeated practice is a version of the conditioning mechanism Spinoza describes in P15-17. The Confucian project deliberately constructs associations that Spinoza shows we inevitably form. Whether rites form better associations or simply more socially approved ones is a genuine question Spinoza's framework can sharpen.
