# Benn, *Tea in China: A Religious and Cultural History* — Chapters 3–5

**Source:** Benn, James A. *Tea in China: A Religious and Cultural History*. University of Hawaii Press, 2015.
**Chapters:** 3 ("Buddhism and Tea in the Tang Dynasty," pp. 42–71), 4 ("Tea Poetry in Tang China," pp. 72–95), 5 ("The Patron Saint of Tea," pp. 96–116)

## Chapter 3: Buddhism and Tea in the Tang

The chapter opens with the *Chajiu lun* — "Mr. Tea versus Mr. Alcohol" — a debate text found at Dunhuang in which tea and alcohol argue about which of them is superior. Neither wins. A third party interrupts: Mr. Water. Water is the foundation of both. Game over.

Buddhist monastic rules formally prohibited alcohol, but the reality was more complicated. Dunhuang monastery accounts from the 8th–9th centuries show roughly 9% of expenditures going toward alcohol — the monks were drinking. Tea served partly as a socially acceptable substitute, something that allowed literati and monks to mix freely at gatherings where wine would otherwise have been expected.

The **Famen si** discovery of 1987 is one of the more remarkable excavations of the 20th century: workers repairing a collapsing pagoda unearthed an underground crypt containing an official inventory and a complete imperial tea service in gilt silver — the finest Tang-era teaware ever found, along with a finger-bone relic of the Buddha himself. Benn deflates the fashionable "esoteric Buddhist ritual" reading: the imperial inventory clearly marks the tea items as gifts from the emperor, and nothing in the inventory text associates them with any specific ritual. The most exotic explanation is probably wrong.

The An Lushan rebellion (755–763) — which killed tens of millions and shattered Tang confidence — may have done more than Buddhist preaching to reduce alcohol consumption in China. When half the country is in ruins, the reasons to drink shift.

**Wu Daozi**, the most celebrated Tang muralist, reportedly had to be drunk to paint his demons and enter the brushwork's flow-state. His rival Gu Kuang painted tea's effects in religious terms: tea enables the Taoist painter to access the *dao* without the blur of intoxication.

## Chapter 4: Tea Poetry in Tang China

Tea is **completely absent from pre-Tang poetry**. The Shijing, the Chuci, the Six Dynasties verse corpus — nothing. Then in the mid-Tang it erupts as a major poetic subject, almost entirely through two figures: [[figure-lu-yu]] and the monk-poet **Jiaoran** (730–799), Lu Yu's closest friend.

Jiaoran was, according to Benn, the single most important architect of tea's poetic vocabulary. He wrote the first sophisticated verse on tea as a spiritual practice, and his poetry established the language every subsequent Tang tea poet would use.

His **"three cups" poem** is the locus classicus:

> The first cup moistens my lips and throat.  
> The second cup banishes my loneliness and boredom.  
> The third cup searches my withered entrails to find some five thousand scrolls of ideograms.  
> The fourth cup raises a light perspiration, scattering through my pores all the wrong things of life.  
> The fifth cup purifies my flesh and bones.  
> The sixth cup puts me in communication with the transcendents.  
> The seventh cup — ah, but I could take no more! I only feel the pure wind rising beneath my arms.

(This is actually **Lu Tong's** "Seven Cups" poem — the most quoted tea poem in Chinese history. Jiaoran wrote the precursor, but Lu Tong's version became canonical.)

**Li Bai** contributed a poem about tea from "Transcendent's Palm" — a ledge on Mt. Tianmen high enough that the monks who cultivated tea there were, according to local legend, 80 years old but rosy-cheeked, and the bats that lived in the nearby caves had drunk enough stalactite water to grow silver fur. Li Bai received the tea as a gift from one of these monks and wrote about it in typically hyperbolic register.

**Bai Juyi** stands apart: he wrote frequently about tea but without any religious or mystical framing — just "this is good, I enjoy it." His straightforwardness shows that the spiritual vocabulary surrounding tea was a deliberate *choice* by the poets who used it, not an unavoidable default.

## Chapter 5: Lu Yu — God of Tea

**Lu Yu** (733–804) is the man who invented the cultural category "tea." Before him, tea was a minor regional beverage with folkloric associations. After his *Classic of Tea* (*Chajing* 茶经, ~7,000 characters, c. 780 CE), it was a civilizational art form with its own history, aesthetics, and social meaning.

His origin story is extraordinary: abandoned as a baby near a river in Hubei; a monk named Zhiji found him among wild geese in late autumn and raised him at Longgai Monastery. When the time came to take his vows, Lu Yu refused. The monk punished him with the dirtiest jobs the monastery had — tending cattle, cleaning latrines, plastering walls. Lu Yu taught himself to read by tracing characters on cattle with a bamboo stylus. Eventually he ran away at 13 to join a troupe of entertainers, where he worked as a clown and writer. His name was taken from an *I Ching* divination: hexagram 53 ("Wild Goose Ascending Over Wood"), which generated the characters 鸿 (wild goose) and 渐 (gradual) — Lu Yü's given name, *Yu* (羽, "feathers"), and surname came from the encounter.

The An Lushan rebellion (755) scattered him south into the Jiangnan region, where he would spend the next 48 years. He met Jiaoran in 757. The *Chajing* was probably published around 780.

The *Chajing* is a practical manual, not mystical text. It covers: the correct equipment (twenty-four specific items, nothing more), the proper fuel (charcoal is best; never use wood that has absorbed cooking odors), and the critical question of boiling temperature. Lu Yu was precise about this: the water must show "fish-eye bubbles" at the first boil, "strung pearls" at the second. You add salt at the first boil. You ladle out a dipperful of water at the second boil. You add the powdered tea at the third boil by swirling it in with a bamboo whisk, and the resulting froth is what makes good tea: white, fine-bubbled, and clinging to the cup.

He was deified after his death. Tea merchants placed clay effigies of Lu Yu behind their counters and made offerings to them — pouring tea on the figurine when business was good, switching to water when it was bad (a gentle rebuke to the God). Song dynasty Buddhist scholars tried to claim him as a devout lay practitioner of Chan Buddhism. There is no evidence for this; it was institutional appropriation of a celebrity.

## What's most interesting here

1. **The Jiaoran-Lu Yu friendship** as the engine of the whole tea poetics movement — two men who met in exile during a civil war and together invented a cultural tradition
2. **Lu Yu's origin story**: the abandoned child who refused to become a monk and instead became the patron saint of tea
3. **The Famen si discovery**: state-of-the-art imperial teaware buried under a pagoda with Buddha's finger bone — then Benn's dry debunking of the mystical reading
4. **Mr. Water wins the debate**: neither tea nor alcohol; the substance that underlies both
5. **Bai Juyi's ordinariness** as a lens that shows everyone else's spiritual framing was a choice
