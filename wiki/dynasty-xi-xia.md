# Xi Xia — "The Great State of White and High" (1038–1227)

One of the most important states in medieval China, and one of the most completely forgotten. The Xi Xia ruled the Gansu corridor and Ordos plateau for nearly two centuries, controlled the crucial overland horse trade that the Song desperately needed, maintained a sophisticated Buddhist culture, and had a writing system not fully deciphered until the 20th century. They were so thoroughly annihilated by Chinggis Khan that almost nothing survived.

---

## Origins

The Tanguts were a Tibeto-Burman people who had settled the Ordos and upper Yellow River basin. Under the Tang they were nominally subordinate; in the fragmentation after 907 they became effectively autonomous. In 1038, the ruler **Yuanhao Weiming** (known to Chinese sources as Li Yuanhao) formally declared an independent empire, took an imperial title, and named his state in Chinese *Da Xia* ("Great Xia") — claiming the same ancient legitimacy as the Han Chinese dynastic lineage. In his own language, the state name translated as "the Great State of White and High."

---

## Strategic importance

Xi Xia occupied one of the most strategically critical positions in East Asia: the **Gansu corridor**, a narrow strip of irrigable land between the Tibetan plateau and the Mongolian steppe, connecting the Yellow River heartland to central Asia. Everything passing between China and the Silk Road west — caravans, diplomats, horses — passed through Gansu.

The Song desperately needed **horses** for cavalry but had lost the northern breeding grounds to the Liao and Jin. Xi Xia controlled the horse trade. This gave the Tanguts enormous leverage over their larger Chinese neighbor.

The Shanyuan treaty (1005) had stabilized the Song-Liao border; the Song now had a second external power demanding tribute. Xi Xia was formally recognized and paid substantial annual subsidies by the Song. The Tangut rulers alternately played Song against Liao, Song against Jin, and everyone against everyone to maintain independence.

---

## Culture and Buddhism

The Tanguts were devout Mahayana Buddhists. Their capital **Zhongxing** (modern Yinchuan) had enormous Buddhist temple complexes; the remains of several stupas survive. They produced Buddhist texts in the Tangut script — a complex logographic writing system that Yuanhao commissioned in deliberate imitation of Chinese characters, asserting cultural parity.

The **Liangzhou stele** (erected 1094) records the Tangut rulers' donations and Buddhist credentials in three scripts: Tangut, Chinese, and Tibetan. It is one of the primary documents for understanding the Tangut language and the state's self-image as a civilized Buddhist empire equal to its neighbors.

The Tangut script was so complex that after the state's destruction it was simply forgotten. Texts turned up at the Dunhuang cache and elsewhere; decipherment was not completed until Russian and Chinese scholars worked on it in the 20th century.

---

## Destruction by Chinggis Khan (1226–1227)

The Tanguts had previously submitted to Mongol authority but failed to provide troops for Chinggis's central Asian campaigns — a refusal the great khan regarded as a mortal insult. In 1226 he launched what was clearly intended to be a war of extermination.

Chinggis died during the campaign — accounts differ whether from a fall from a horse, an arrow wound, or illness. His dying instructions were reportedly to complete the destruction of Xi Xia before announcing his death.

The Tangut ruler surrendered. He was executed. The capital was sacked. The population was killed or dispersed. The annihilation was so complete that Chinese sources record almost no survivors; the Tangut language ceased to be spoken; the scripts became unreadable. A two-century Buddhist empire was simply erased.
