# Moral Philosophy: Comparative Overview

A living comparison of moral philosophers in the wiki. Grows as new thinkers are added. Currently covers: **Spinoza**, **Hume**, **Hegel**.

## The thinkers in depth

### Spinoza (1632–1677): virtue as power

Spinoza's ethics is inseparable from his metaphysics. The same logic that establishes one infinite substance, God or Nature, also establishes the nature of value. Since nature contains no purposes, "good" and "evil" cannot be objective features of the world — they are descriptions of how things affect us:

> "In no case do we strive for, wish for, long for, or desire anything, because we deem it to be good; but on the other hand, we deem a thing to be good **because we strive for it**."

Value follows desire, not the other way around. What is "good" is what genuinely increases our power to act and persist — what expresses our *conatus* (the essence of each thing: its striving to persist). Virtue and power are the same word.

The radical move: **self-preservation is the one and only foundation of virtue.** Not a starting point to be transcended by some higher principle — the root from which everything else grows. But properly understood (through rational knowledge rather than passion), self-preservation requires the flourishing of other rational people. "To man, there is nothing more useful than man." Two rational people joined together form an individual twice as capable. Genuine self-interest and genuine altruism converge.

Joy is ethically positive. Sadness is ethically negative. Any morality that valorizes suffering, self-denial, or humility — that treats despondency as piety — is not merely mistaken; it actively diminishes human power and is therefore evil. Spinoza's target is Calvinism and Christian asceticism generally.

The free person "thinks of nothing less than of death, and his wisdom is a meditation not of death but of life."

**What Spinoza cannot account for**: Critics note that the framework has no real place for moral luck, for the suffering of people whose *conatus* simply isn't powerful enough to flourish, or for why rational understanding should motivate at all (Hume's challenge). The determinism can feel cold when applied to genuine victims.

### Hume (1711–1776): virtue as what we approve

Hume's starting point is empirical: what actually happens when we make moral judgments? The answer is that we feel something — approval or disapproval. Reason can inform our judgments (telling us facts about consequences and character), but the "final sentence" is always a feeling. Reason is "cool and disengaged" — it can tell you how things are, but it cannot tell you what to care about.

The **is/ought gap**: no collection of factual statements logically entails a moral conclusion. You cannot derive "you ought not to harm the innocent" from any description of how things are. The conclusion requires a sentiment.

The mechanism is **sympathy**: we imaginatively enter others' perspectives, and what affects them affects us. This is not a principle we choose; it is a feature of human nature. Morality is grounded not in metaphysics but in our psychological constitution.

What reliably produces approbation? Two things: **utility** (qualities that benefit people, produce social goods) and **agreeableness** (qualities pleasing to oneself or to others). Hume maps this as a four-fold grid: useful-to-others (justice, beneficence), useful-to-self (prudence, industry), agreeable-to-others (wit, politeness), agreeable-to-self (cheerfulness, self-command).

**Justice** is special: it is not a "natural" virtue arising from immediate sentiment but an **artificial** virtue — it depends on established conventions, institutions, and social practices. There is no natural sentiment that attaches to property rights or contracts; these are human constructions, and without them the sentiment of justice would have no object. Hume's three thought experiments show justice collapsing under conditions of abundance (no need for rules), universal benevolence (love already distributes everything rightly), and extreme scarcity (survival overrides property rights).

**Against self-interest theories** (Hobbes, Mandeville): benevolence is real and irreducible. Hume simply does not find it plausible that all apparent generosity conceals disguised self-love. Even a small residue of genuine sympathy would be enough to found morality; the observed facts are easier to explain by simply taking benevolence at face value.

The **sensible knave** problem: Hume acknowledges he cannot prove to someone who asks "but why should I be virtuous?" that virtue pays. The argument bottoms out in the sentiment itself — the person who genuinely lacks moral sentiment is simply missing something. You can explain morality to someone who has it; you cannot install it in someone who doesn't.

**What Hume cannot account for**: Hume's ethics is historically thin — he cannot explain why moral standards change across time, why some historical norms are better than others, or why the sentiment itself is authoritative. Hegel's critique is precisely this: Hume's sympathy is a brute fact about human psychology, but it cannot explain why the norms it generates are *binding* rather than merely felt. The is/ought gap cuts against Hume too: why should sympathy ground what we *ought* to do?

### Hegel (1770–1831): virtue as recognition

Hegel's ethics is embedded in a larger theory of what it means to be a self-conscious being at all. The fundamental discovery of the *Phenomenology*'s Self-Consciousness section is that **selfhood requires recognition**: I cannot constitute my own self-understanding in isolation. I need others who recognize my validity as a rational subject — and whose validity I in turn recognize.

This means ethics is never merely a matter of individual psychology. The standards for what counts as good, the norms that govern recognition, are constituted in the **social practices and institutions** of a historical community. Ethics is not something that individual subjects bring to society; it is something that society makes possible for individuals.

Hegel distinguishes two levels of ethical life:

- ***Moralität*** (Morality): Abstract, individual duty — Kant's categorical imperative, the claim that what is right is what pure reason can universalize. Hegel's critique: this produces empty formalism. "Act only on universalizable maxims" can justify almost anything, or nothing at all. It ignores the actual historical and social context that gives actions their meaning.

- ***Sittlichkeit*** (Ethical Life): The concrete ethical life of a community — its family structures, civil society, and political institutions. These are not external constraints on freedom; they are what make self-realization possible. To be free is not to escape your community's norms but to participate in them as a fully self-conscious member who understands and endorses them.

**The master/slave lesson**: Power relationships that deny genuine recognition are self-undermining. The master who dominates without genuine recognition from the slave receives worthless recognition from a being he has reduced to nothing. The slave, through work, discovers genuine independence. The moral: coercive authority cannot generate the kind of recognition that makes ethical life possible.

**Freedom** for Hegel is not the absence of constraint (negative liberty) or even the inner freedom of the Stoic. It is **self-determination through rational institutions** — being part of a social order whose norms you can reflectively endorse as the actualization of your own freedom. The rational state does not limit freedom; it is freedom, made concrete.

**History matters morally**: Each historical epoch embodies a form of ethical life (*Sittlichkeit*) suited to the level of Spirit's self-knowledge at that point. Ancient Greek ethical life (beautiful, unselfconscious unity) was genuinely good — but its failure to recognize the individual subject meant it was inherently unstable. The modern world, with its emphasis on subjective freedom and individual rights, represents a genuine moral advance. We cannot simply choose to return to an earlier ethical form.

**What Hegel cannot account for** (or is at least challenged on): The claim that history is a story of moral progress is vulnerable to the obvious horrors. The system's tendency to absorb every particularity into the universal has been criticized as totalizing — Kierkegaard's challenge that the individual simply cannot be fully sublated into any system. And Hegel's equation of the rational with the actual ("the actual is rational, the rational is actual") has been read as conservative apologetics for existing institutions.

## Fault lines: where they actually disagree

### 1. Can reason motivate moral action?

**Spinoza**: Yes — adequate ideas not only reveal what is truly good, they transform passive affects into active ones. Understanding your anger as the product of ignorance is itself partially freeing.

**Hume**: No. Reason "is and ought only to be the slave of the passions." The most reason can do is inform desire; it cannot supply it. Moral motivation is always ultimately affective.

**Hegel**: The question is wrongly framed. "Reason" is not a faculty that exists independently of the social practices in which it is exercised. What motivates us is the desire for recognition, which is already structured by the rational norms of our community. Reason and motivation are not separate things that have to be bridged.

---

### 2. Is morality grounded in nature or history?

**Spinoza**: Nature. The structure of being — conatus, power, joy — grounds ethics independently of any particular social arrangement or historical epoch. A slave who understands her own nature can be as free as a king.

**Hume**: Human nature. The moral sentiments are a stable feature of our psychological constitution. Cross-cultural differences in moral belief are differences in information and custom, not in the underlying sentiment. Justice, prudence, and benevolence would be virtues in any human society.

**Hegel**: History. The forms of ethical life are historically developing. There is no view from outside history that lets us pronounce on what would be "naturally" good. The modern recognition of individual subjective freedom is a genuine moral achievement that no amount of natural-law reasoning could have derived in advance.

---

### 3. Is self-interest the foundation of ethics, or a problem for it?

**Spinoza**: Self-interest is the foundation — but rational self-interest, which requires other people's flourishing, is indistinguishable from genuine altruism. The selfish man of passion is not really serving his interests.

**Hume**: Self-interest is one motive among others, neither foundational nor especially privileged. Genuine benevolence exists alongside self-love; neither reduces to the other.

**Hegel**: The individual/universal opposition is a false dichotomy. In a well-ordered ethical community, serving the community *is* serving yourself, because your individual identity is partly constituted by your community membership. Neither pole is prior.

---

### 4. What is the relationship between morality and emotion?

**Spinoza**: Emotions are the material of ethical life, not obstacles to it. The goal is not to suppress passion but to understand it, transforming passive affects (driven by external things) into active ones (arising from our own nature). Joy is morally good because it increases power.

**Hume**: Emotions are foundational. Without moral sentiment, there is no moral judgment. But the right emotions need to be cultivated — moved from partial attachment (love of family, tribe) to the more impartial sympathy that genuine moral approbation requires.

**Hegel**: Emotions are shaped by social practices and the structures of recognition. The "right" feelings are not natural givens but achievements of ethical formation (*Bildung*). The early *Phenomenology* traces how raw desire and fear are slowly educated into recognizing others as subjects.

---

### 5. What is political freedom?

**Spinoza**: A state that uses law (fear of punishment) to restrain irrational passion, freeing people to pursue rational understanding. The best state promotes liberty, tolerance, and the pursuit of knowledge. Democracy.

**Hume**: Political society arises from the conventions that constitute justice. These conventions are not contracts but evolved practices — habits that prove stable because they benefit everyone. The government that honors these conventions is legitimate; one that systematically violates them is not.

**Hegel**: The state actualizes freedom — it is not a constraint on pre-political individuals but the institutional form in which genuine freedom first becomes real. The private individual who thinks the state limits her freedom has not yet understood what freedom is.

## Agreements worth noting

Despite their disagreements, these three thinkers share some important conclusions:

1. **Against free will**: All three deny libertarian free will (the ability to have done otherwise, holding all prior causes fixed). Spinoza from determinism; Hume from the regularity of character with circumstances; Hegel because the "free" isolated individual is an abstraction from a social whole that constitutes it.

2. **Against moralizing**: None of them are comfortable with moral condemnation as a primary ethical response. Spinoza says condemnation is just ignorance of causes; Hume says pity and blame both rely on the same imaginative mechanism as approbation; Hegel says moral judgment that is not grounded in recognition of the social formation behind the action is abstract and hollow.

3. **Against religious ethics of self-denial**: Spinoza is the most explicit (the Calvinist ethics is "savage superstition"), but Hume's "monkish virtues" passage and Hegel's critique of the Unhappy Consciousness as alienated religious self-negation both point the same direction.

4. **Social life is necessary for moral life**: Even Spinoza, the most individualist of the three, insists that the rational community is the greatest good for each of its members.
