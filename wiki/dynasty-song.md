# Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE)

The Song dynasty is the great paradox of Chinese history: the most culturally and economically sophisticated civilization on earth, perpetually on the defensive, losing territory by the generation. The dynasty that gave the world printing, gunpowder weapons, the compass, paper money, and restaurant culture — that supported thirty cities of 40,000–100,000 people at a time when Europe had six — could not hold its own capital against cavalry armies from the steppe.

|-------|--------|-------|
| Northern Song | 960–1127 | Full China; capital Kaifeng; Wang Anshi's reforms; intellectual peak |

## The Song achievement

By 1100, Song China had:

- A population of roughly 100 million, producing grain surpluses that fed a genuinely urban, non-agricultural class
- **Printing** in mass use — cheap books, spreading literacy, a publishing industry
- **The magnetic compass** — in use by Song sailors by the 11th century, enabling the maritime trade that overtook the Silk Road
- **Gunpowder weapons** — fire arrows, bombs, early guns and flamethrowers in military use
- **Paper money** — the world's first state paper currency, begun in Sichuan in 1023
- **Champa rice** — a drought-resistant fast-ripening Vietnamese strain imported under Song Zhenzong (r. 998–1022), allowing double-cropping and feeding the population surge. This was China's "green revolution," a thousand years before the term existed.

These weren't curiosities or court playthings. By the 11th century all three of the inventions Francis Bacon would later credit with transforming Western civilization — printing, compass, gunpowder — were already in everyday Chinese use.

## The political culture: reforms and their enemies

The Northern Song developed a sophisticated political culture centered on the scholar-official class (*shi dafu*), civil service examinations, and open factional debate. This was, for a while, genuinely remarkable: educated men argued policy in print, petitioned the throne, formed political groupings.

**Fan Zhongyan's Minor Reforms** (1043–44): the reformist chancellor Fan Zhongyan pushed for merit-based promotion, reduced official redundancy, and local agricultural development. The conservatives blocked him within a year. But his essay "On the Affairs of a Prefect" — with its famous opening "Be the first to bear the world's burdens, the last to enjoy its pleasures" — became a touchstone of Chinese political culture.

**Wang Anshi's New Policies** (1069–76): under Song Shenzong, the reformer Wang Anshi attempted what Keay calls "the most ambitious reorganization between the First Emperor and the Communist Party." The centerpiece was the *qingmiao* (green sprouts) loan — low-interest state loans to farmers at planting time, cutting out usurers who charged 100% interest. Other reforms: state granaries to stabilize grain prices, corvée commutation (cash taxes instead of labor), local militia to reduce reliance on the professional army.

Wang Anshi won every debate. He lost the political war. The conservative faction — led by Sima Guang, the historian — blocked the reforms on principle: disrupting established relationships, burdening officials with new administrative tasks, trusting the state over private merchants. On Song Shenzong's death in 1085, his mother the Empress Dowager took the regency, recalled Sima Guang, and reversed nearly everything. Wang Anshi died the following year.

**Ouyang Xiu** (1007–1072) stands apart: poet, historian, essayist, and pioneer of a political theory of legitimate factionalism. His essay "On Factions" argued that good ministers naturally formed groups united by principle — something earlier Chinese political thought had considered inherently corrupt. His framework for thinking about legitimacy in fragmented eras (orthodoxy enters "a state of limbo" during disunion) influenced later historians and was one of the most original political ideas of the Song.

## The Jurchen catastrophe (1127)

The **Jurchen** were a forest people from Manchuria who overthrew the Khitan Liao dynasty and founded the **Jin dynasty** (1115–1234) under the founder Aguda. Their military expansion was spectacular: within twenty years they had destroyed Liao, swept into northern China, and in 1127 sacked **Kaifeng**.

The captured emperors — **Song Huizong** (who had abdicated to his son the moment the crisis broke) and his son Song Qinzong — were carried north as prisoners. Huizong had been one of the finest calligraphers and painters in Chinese history; the Jin titled him "Muddled Virtue" and let him die in Manchuria. Qinzong died there too.

The surviving Song court reconstituted itself south of the Yangzi under **Song Gaozong** at Lin'an (modern Hangzhou) — the Southern Song. The shame of 1127 never left Chinese cultural memory; it would motivate nativist politics for the next 150 years.

## Southern Song: the halcyon age

Despite — or because of — its contracted territory, the Southern Song produced a concentrated brilliance. Hangzhou, the new capital, was extraordinary. Its population may have reached 1.5–2 million by the 13th century; it had layered streets, canal networks, restaurants specializing by regional cuisine, teahouses, literary clubs, opera performances, and fire brigades. The Venetian Marco Polo, arriving under Yuan rule decades later, called it "the greatest city in the world."

**Jingdezhen** (Jiangxi province) became the center of porcelain production so identified with China that the word *china* entered European languages from its product rather than its maker. Song porcelain — *celadon*, *qingbai*, the translucent white wares — reached the Middle East, Africa, Southeast Asia, and eventually Europe in vast quantities through the maritime trade.

**Tea culture** under the Song reached aesthetic extremes: powdered tea whisked to a froth, competitions judging the whiteness of the foam, paintings of the whisking process. This is the tradition that crossed to Japan and became the tea ceremony. See [[concept-tea-culture]].

## Yue Fei (1103–1141)

The Southern Song's greatest general was also its most celebrated martyr. **Yue Fei** developed paddle-wheel warships to fight the Jurchen cavalry, which could not cross rivers and had no answer to Song naval technology. He won significant victories and may have been close to recovering the north.

He was recalled by court order, then arrested on trumped-up charges of insubordination — probably at the instigation of the peace-faction chancellor Qin Gui, who feared that military success would empower the generals over the civilian government. Yue Fei was imprisoned and killed in 1141. His son was executed alongside him.

The phrase assigned to him — four characters tattooed on his back by his patriotic mother: *jingzhong baoguo* ("serve the country with utter loyalty") — became one of the most famous expressions in Chinese. His tomb in Hangzhou became a patriotic shrine; the effigies of Qin Gui and his wife kneel in iron before it, meant to be spat on by visitors.

## Foot-binding

Foot-binding began in the Tang era as a court fashion, possibly for dancing girls. Under the Song it became general: rare before 1086, apparently universal among Han women within a generation, persisting for 800 years until its eradication in the 20th century. Non-Han women — Jurchen, Mongol, Manchu — shunned it throughout their dynasties' rules, distinguishing themselves from their Han subjects.

The binding started in childhood, progressively breaking and folding the foot bones to produce the "three-inch golden lotus." It was performed by mothers on daughters. Its social function was as a marker of refinement and marriageability; unbound feet marked peasant or barbarian status. The pain was lifelong. Roughly half of all Han women for 800 years walked on ruined feet.

## Neo-Confucianism: Zhu Xi and the Four Books

The philosopher **Zhu Xi** (1130–1200) synthesized centuries of Confucian thought into a system — called *lixue* ("the learning of principle") or Neo-Confucianism — that became the dominant intellectual framework of China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam for the next 700 years.

Zhu Xi's core move was to elevate four texts to canonical status — the **Four Books**: the *Analects*, *Mencius*, *Great Learning*, and *Doctrine of the Mean* — and to interpret them through a metaphysical framework in which every thing has a *li* (principle or pattern) and a *qi* (vital force). Moral self-cultivation meant investigating the *li* of things until one's own principle was clear.

On history: Zhu Xi sided with Shu Han as the legitimate Three Kingdoms state (not Cao Wei), setting orthodoxy on this question for centuries. He argued that the examination of historical precedent was itself a form of moral cultivation.

Zhu Xi was politically conservative. His Neo-Confucianism reinforced hierarchical social structures and the subordination of women. The elevated social conservatism that Neo-Confucianism brought to the Song probably accelerated foot-binding's normalization.

## The fall (1271–1279)

The Mongols under Chinggis Khan had destroyed the Xi Xia (1227) and the Jin dynasty (1234) before turning to Song. Khubilai Khan completed the work. See [[event-mongol-conquest-song]] and [[dynasty-yuan]] for the full story.

The Song's last stand was extraordinary. The inland fortress of **Xiangfan** held out for six years (1267–73) before Mongol siege engineers from Persia finally broke it with counterweight trebuchets. After Xiangfan fell, Hangzhou surrendered with negotiations in 1276. The last emperor — a seven-year-old boy — was carried on the back of his last loyal official, Lu Xiufu, into the sea off the Pearl River Delta as the Mongol fleet closed in. 1279. The accompanying fleet of 800 Song ships followed them into the water.
