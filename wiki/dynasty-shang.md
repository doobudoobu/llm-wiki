# Shang Dynasty

**Period:** c. 1750–1040 BCE
**Location:** Lower Yellow River basin, centered on the Central Plain (modern Henan province); capital at Anyang in the final phase (c. 1240–1040 BC)
**Followed by:** [[dynasty-zhou]], overthrown at the Battle of Muye c. 1045 BC

---

## The founding myth: a swallowed egg

The Shang traced their divine descent through an ornithomorphous miracle. The founding myth (from the *Classic of Poetry*, c. 600 BC, and Ssu-ma Ch'ien's *Historical Records*):

The goddess **Chien Ti**, second consort of the god Ti K'u, went with two companions to bathe. A black bird dropped an egg. Chien Ti picked it up and swallowed it. She became pregnant and gave birth to **Ch'i** (also called Hsieh) — who grew up to help Yü control the great flood, and was rewarded with the Shang fiefdom by Emperor Shun.

An earlier variant has God ordering a swallow to visit two women in a nine-story tower; the swallow sang "yee-yee," the women fought over it, it laid two eggs and flew north and never returned. The women composed a song: "Swallow, swallow, you flew away!" — supposedly the first composition in the style of Northern Music.

The Ch'in people had an identical origin myth: their ancestress swallowed a black bird's egg and gave birth to the Ch'in ancestor. The egg + divine bird formula was clearly a Bronze Age legitimacy device. The founding people descend from a union between a woman and the sky.

## Why the Shang matter

The Shang are where Chinese history stops being archaeology and starts being records. They left behind:

1. **The oracle bones** — the world's oldest Chinese writing, scratched on cattle shoulder blades and turtle shells (see [[concept-oracle-bones]])
2. **Bronze casting** on an industrial scale — some vessels weigh nearly three-quarters of a tonne
3. **Anyang**, one of the most thoroughly excavated sites in China, revealing a royal necropolis and 50+ large building foundations

Everything before the Shang is inference and archaeology. The Shang let us hear something like a voice.

## What kind of state it was

Not an empire — more what historians call a "hegemonic state." The Shang king ruled directly over a small core; outlying subordinates recognized Shang supremacy through the title "Archer-Lord" (*hou*) or "Prince" (*zi*). The Shang conceived their territory as the **"Four Lands" (*situ* 四土)** — east, west, south, north — surrounding the Central Shang at Anyang. Beyond these were the "Fang" 方 polities: independent foreign enemies against whom the king campaigned.

The key word is *hegemonic* rather than *legitimate*. The Shang had no permanent administration beyond the royal court. Their hold over the Archer-Lords (*hou* 侯) depended entirely on continuous military display — royal hunting trips were political strategy as much as ritual: the king showed up, dominated, moved on. When a king's power weakened, the perimeter of the Shang state shrank accordingly. There was no permanent membership in the Shang state, and no permanent enemy of it.

The king was peripatetic — he moved his "capital" whenever it became inauspicious, usually under military pressure. Later texts name seven such removals. Anyang (also called "Yin") was probably the last.

Shang power depended heavily on the energy of the individual king. The oracle bones record him going out repeatedly — to hunt, to fight, to inspect his domain, to perform ritual. He was less a bureaucrat sitting in a palace than a ritual-warrior constantly in motion.

The Shang government was correspondingly **rudimentary**. The oracle bones show no differentiated administrative offices until the very end of Anyang, when "Document Maker" and "Superintendent" first appear. The king was assisted by diviners who were essentially personal attendants, occasionally handling administrative matters brought to court. Religious function and administrative function had not yet been separated.

## Bronze and what it reveals

Bronze in Shang China occupied a position similar to monumental stone in Egypt: it was the material of power, prestige, and divine connection. Enormous effort went into production — locating copper and tin deposits, mining them, transporting ore, maintaining furnaces hot enough to melt metal.

The vessels produced were not tableware. They were **ritual objects** — food containers, wine vessels, cooking pots — used in ceremonies honoring ancestors and gods, then buried in tombs with the elite. A tomb might contain hundreds of them.

The technology was breathtaking: large vessels were cast in sectional molds (no soldering), with decoration incised on the inner face of the mold so it emerged raised on the finished piece. Designs crawl with animal motifs — the *taotie* 饕餮 face, coiling serpents, birds — whose exact meaning is still debated.

The sheer scale is worth sitting with. A single fifth-century BC tomb (post-Shang, but in the same tradition) contained **10 tonnes of bronze**. Nothing remotely comparable exists anywhere else in the ancient world for this period.

## The Anyang metropolis

Anyang covers 24 km² (2,400 ha) — at least 45× larger than any contemporary settlement, roughly 200× larger than a normal village of the period. The site divides into two main zones separated by the Huan River:

**Palace zone** (south bank): 53 large building foundations in three clusters. Cluster B (center) was the probable ritual/divination zone; Cluster C (southwest) was temples; Cluster A (north, fewest sacrificial pits) was the king's residential quarters. All construction was on tamped-earth platforms and accompanied by ritual killings during the building process.

**Royal cemetery at Xibeigang** (north bank): fifteen tombs, eight with four access ramps — those are the royal tombs, belonging to Wu Ding (King 21) through Di Yi (King 28). Over **2,500 sacrificial pits** have been identified surrounding the large tombs, with roughly 1,500 excavated; each pit contains 10–15 skeletons (heads sometimes separated). **At least 30,000 human sacrifice victims** documented in the Xibeigang cemetery alone.

**Huanbei Shang City** (north of Huan River, discovered 1999): 2,150×2,200 m, oriented 13 degrees east of north — significantly larger than the known Anyang palaces, and *earlier* than them (before Wu Ding, Period I). This was the first Shang center at Anyang, used by the first three kings after Pan Geng's move. Establishes the "Middle Shang" period. The discovery solved the long-standing mystery of why oracle bones from the first three Anyang kings were absent from the known site.

**Industrial infrastructure:**
- Bronze foundry (discovered 2003–4): 380×100 m, 70,000 mold pieces, 100 clay models — largest Shang foundry known
- Bone workshop (2006): 34,000 kg of processed bones in a 17,600 m² factory; estimated output of 77 bone artifacts/day, ~4 million items over 150 years; possibly exported beyond Anyang

## Lady Hao and what a "lesser tomb" looked like

In 1975, archaeologists found a tomb at Anyang that had never been robbed. It belonged to **Lady Hao** (*Fu Hao*), the most powerful woman of the Anyang period — senior consort of King Wu Ding (King 21), military commander, and ritual officiant. She led armies of over 10,000 soldiers; the oracle bones document her more than any other woman in the corpus. See [[figure-lady-hao]] for her full profile.

Her tomb (no. 5) was 150 m west of the palace zone — close enough that Wu Ding apparently wanted her spirit nearby. A building foundation constructed above the pit after her burial inadvertently foiled all looters.

Contents:
- **468 bronze vessels and weapons** (the full technical achievement of late Shang casting)
- **755 jade objects** — largest Shang jade assemblage ever found
- ~564 bone artifacts; 16 sacrificed human retainers

Nearly 2,000 artifacts in total — the richest tomb excavated at Anyang, and this was not a royal tomb.

The **Mother Wu ding-cauldron** — probably cast by Lady Hao's son to honor her — measures 1.33 m high and weighs **875 kg**, making it the largest bronze vessel ever found in the world. Casting it required more than 1,000 artisans working simultaneously.

"If the bigger royal tombs were richer than this, their contents are beyond imagining."

## Human sacrifice

The Shang elite did not like going to the afterlife alone. Retainers, servants, guards, horses, and pets accompanied them as grave offerings. In the largest tombs, victims are counted in the hundreds. Skeletons show dismemberment, decapitation, and skulls sawn off — possibly for bone carving.

Some victims were prisoners of war. Others were almost certainly killed specifically for burial.

This was not a secret or a crime. It was ritual, performed publicly, part of how the elite displayed their connection to the ancestors and their command over human bodies.

## Royal succession

The oracle bones allow scholars to reconstruct Shang succession rules in detail. The dynasty began with what appears to have been simple father-to-son succession. After the founding king Da Yi, a new rule emerged: the throne passed among brothers of the same generation before returning to the next generation's oldest son. The rationale was probably practical — ensuring an adult always held the throne.

This "circulating" system held for roughly twelve generations, then broke down under powerful kings (Zu Yi, Wu Ding, Wu Yi) who simply refused to return the throne to their brother's family line. Each violation coincided with a major political shift — a capital move or a reconfiguration of Shang power. By the dynasty's end, **primogeniture** (father-to-son) was established not by decree but by repeated violation of the older rule.

Each king's name was drawn from the ten Heavenly Stems. One influential theory (K.C. Chang) holds that the royal lineage was actually organized into two exogamous groups — "Yi-group" and "Ding-group" — that alternated the kingship, explaining regularities in the name distribution across the king list.

## The Shang religion: ancestors as bureaucrats

The Shang king conceived of himself as the pivot between the living world and a celestial bureaucracy of ancestors and gods. The ancestors were not passive spirits; they were expected to actively intervene in earthly affairs — interceding with the supreme deity Di, influencing harvests and wars, protecting the lineage. They were consulted constantly via oracle bone divination.

The supreme deity Di 帝 was stern and not always cooperative. He might "incite the Shang's enemies" rather than help the Shang against them. When Lady Hao was pregnant, King Wu Ding lobbied Di for a male heir. The oracle gave a non-committal response. After 31 days, "she gave birth and it was not good; it was a girl."

The emphasis was on this-worldly practicality. Ancestors and gods had real jobs to do. They weren't transcendent creators; they were powerful officials in an invisible administration that ran parallel to the earthly one.

## King Kai and the cattle raid

Before the Anyang dynasty we know archaeologically, the mythological tradition records an earlier Shang king, **King Kai** (also written Hai), seventh in descent from the founder Ch'i. He was a herdsman-king who traveled to a neighboring tribe as a diplomatic guest, had an affair with his host's wife, and was killed in retribution — his herds stolen. His successor avenged him.

Birrell reads this as the Chinese instance of the universal "first cattle raid" myth — the pastoral culture origin story found across the world. The Shang began as herdsmen, and their mythological record preserves that memory. The story is embedded in *Questions of Heaven* (4th century BC), so garbled it took Yuan K'o's 20th-century reconstruction to make it legible.

## Fall of the Shang: King Chou (紂)

Around 1045 BC, the Zhou — a subordinate people from the Wei River valley to the west — overthrew the Shang at the **Battle of Muye**. The last Shang king, later portrayed as a monster of depravity (drunkenness, incest, sadism), committed suicide.

But Ssu-ma Ch'ien's portrait of **King Chou** is more interesting than the usual monster-tyrant. He describes a man who starts extraordinary:

> King Chou was a man with remarkable qualities, most discerning, besides being forceful and quick-witted... His natural strength exceeded that of other men... He was sufficiently clever to be able to oppose official censure, and with his eloquence he was able to gloss over his mistakes.

The fall is gradual, psychological: he became too fond of drink, too fond of praise, unable to accept criticism. He boasted to his courtiers, heaped praise on himself, made everyone resent him. His favorite concubine **Ta Chi** directed his excesses. He expanded the royal parks, massed naked entertainers, threw all-night drinking parties, and had the court musician compose "very effete" new dances.

King Wen of Chou (father of the Zhou founders) was imprisoned by King Chou on the word of a jealous rival. He ransomed himself — his ministers traveled the country and collected two beautiful girls, a large water-cowrie, and a white horse with a red mane. When Chou saw them he "looked up to Heaven and sighed, 'How lovely they are!'" and immediately released Wen. The future conqueror freed himself by exploiting his captor's appetites. The Zhou would subsequently claim that Heaven had transferred its "Mandate" from the Shang to themselves — though this doctrine seems to have been a Zhou invention after the fact. See [[concept-mandate-of-heaven]].

## What the Shang established

Despite being a local proto-state, not an empire, the Shang demonstrated cultural traits that became defining features of Chinese civilization:

- Ancestor worship and lineage-based legitimacy
- A ritual calendar and formal ceremonies
- The Chinese script (direct ancestor of modern Chinese writing)
- Bronze as the prestige medium of power
- A "this-worldly" orientation: gods and ancestors are practical actors, not transcendent creators

"By 1045 BC it had demonstrated many of the cultural traits that have come to be seen as typically, even peculiarly, Chinese."
