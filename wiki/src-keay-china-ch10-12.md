# Keay, *China: A History* — Chapters 10–12

**Source**: John Keay, *China: A History* (2008)  
**Chapters**: 10 ("Disorder Restored," 755–1005), 11 ("Civilization Gridlock," 1005–1235), 12 ("All Under Yuan," 1235–1405)  
**Coverage**: Late Tang disruption through Huang Chao, Five Dynasties, Song in full, Yuan conquest

---

## What this source covers

Chapter 10 covers the aftermath of the An Lushan rebellion, Huang Chao's extraordinary rebellion (4,800 km march through south China, sack of Chang'an 881), the Five Dynasties period, Feng Dao's notorious career serving all five dynasties, the invention of paper money in Sichuan, the printing revolution (first printed Confucian classics 953), the commercial revolution that shifted China's center of gravity south, the Song founding, the Khitan Liao empire's dual-administration system, and the Shanyuan treaty of 1005 — the first "genuine international frontier in the modern sense."

Chapter 11 covers the Xi Xia ("Great State of White and High") — the Tangut empire of Gansu and Ordos controlling the crucial Gansu horse corridor; Fan Zhongyan's Minor Reforms; Wang Anshi's sweeping New Policies (low-interest state loans, state granaries, corvée commutation) under Song Shenzong; Ouyang Xiu and the Song political culture; the full scope of Song's cultural achievements (thirty cities of 40–100k population, printing/compass/gunpowder in everyday use, Champa rice); foot-binding; and the Jurchen Jin dynasty — Aguda's conquest, the fall of Kaifeng 1127, Song Huizong titled "Muddled Virtue," Southern Song under Gaozong. Closes with Zhu Xi and Neo-Confucianism.

Chapter 12 covers Southern Song as China's cultural peak — Hangzhou's population, Jingdezhen porcelain (origin of the word "china"), Yue Fei's paddle-boat navies and judicial murder; the Mongol conquests from Chinggis to Khubilai — Xi Xia destroyed 1226–27, Jin eliminated 1234, the six-year siege of Xiangfan (1267–73), Hangzhou's surrender 1276, the last Song emperor drowned at sea 1279; and early Ming through Yongle's 1405 voyages.

---

## What's most interesting

**Huang Chao's march** — after the fall of Chang'an, the rebel commander had his concubines killed, then rode north alone into the dawn with only a pair of servants. An extraordinary, operatic exit that mirrors Xiang Yu at Gaixia five hundred years earlier.

**Feng Dao** — the only official to serve all five dynasties (and several Liao emperors) without apparent loss of dignity or rank. Later historians condemned him; his own explanation was that he served "the people," not the emperor. One of the strangest careers in Chinese history.

**Huang Chao's paper money** — copper cash was so heavy that merchants in Sichuan deposited it with private exchange shops and took paper receipts instead. The government nationalized this in 1023 as the world's first state-backed paper currency.

**Wang Anshi's reforms** — Keay calls them "the most ambitious reorganization attempted between the First Emperor and the Communist Party." Low-interest loans (targeting usurers), state granaries, corvée commutation into cash taxes. Wang Anshi won every argument but lost the political war.

**Foot-binding** — rare before 1086, apparently universal among Han women by 1100, persisting for 800 years. Keay notes it: non-Han women (Jurchen, Mongol, Manchu) shunned it throughout, distinguishing themselves from Han subjects.

**The siege of Xiangfan** — a six-year siege (1267–73) that became the hinge of the entire Mongol conquest of Song. Colonel Chang's paddle-boat relief attempt with a hundred ships and a thousand men. Ultimately surrendered after the Mongols brought in Muslim siege engineers from Persia with counterweight trebuchets that could hurl 150 kg projectiles.

**The last Song emperor** — Lu Xiufu, the last loyal official, strapped the seven-year-old emperor to his back and jumped into the sea off the Pearl River Delta as the Mongol fleet closed in. The accompanying fleet of 800 Song ships immediately followed. 1279.

**Khubilai Khan naming his dynasty** — took "Yuan" from the *Yi-jing*'s hexagram *qian* ("the creative"): "great is the creative, the primal *yuan*." Not just a dynastic name but a cosmological claim.
