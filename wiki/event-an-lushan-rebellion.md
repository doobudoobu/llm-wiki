# An Lushan Rebellion (755–763 CE)

The pivot point of the Tang dynasty — and one of the deadliest events in human history before the modern era. A single rebellion transformed China's most powerful empire into a patchwork of autonomous provinces, ended the cosmopolitan High Tang, and set the conditions for the dynasty's eventual fall.

---

## The man who broke the Tang

**An Lushan** was a half-Sogdian, half-Turk general who had risen through the frontier armies of the northeast by a combination of military talent, political savvy, and performed buffoonery. He was illiterate in Chinese, spoke several non-Han languages, and cultivated the image of a loyal, simple soldier who had no ambitions beyond pleasing his emperor. This image was at least partially genuine.

By 752, An Lushan commanded the three northeastern frontier provinces (Pinglu, Fanyang, Hedong) — stretching from Manchuria to the Ordos. His cousin controlled two more. "The whole northern border from the Ordos to Manchuria was controlled by the Ans." He also held the commission for the imperial horse-breeding stations in Gansu, controlling the cavalry mounts the army depended on. In terms of military resources, he was the most powerful man in the empire outside Chang'an.

At court, the aging Tang Xuanzong had in 745 fallen in love with **Yang Guifei**, a consort of extraordinary beauty who had previously belonged to one of his sons. Through her influence, her distant cousin **Yang Guozhong** had become the dominant figure at court. Yang Guifei reportedly "adopted" An Lushan as her son, going through a grotesque ceremony in which the portly fifty-something general was dressed in baby clothes and bathed. The emperor made An Lushan a duke, then a prince — a title normally reserved for imperial family.

---

## The break

An Lushan and Yang Guozhong each saw the other as an existential threat. When Yang Guozhong began purging An Lushan's agents at court and spreading rumors of rebellion, An Lushan retreated to his northeastern base and stopped coming to Chang'an. Requests for his attendance were rebuffed; when in late 755 he declined to perform obeisance before an imperial envoy, it was tantamount to a declaration of war.

He claimed he was marching to rid the empire of Yang Guozhong. His army — veteran professional soldiers, substantially non-Han, with Manchurian Khitan auxiliaries — swept south. Luoyang fell before the year was out. Hastily raised imperial armies were defeated.

In mid-756, the emperor and Yang Guozhong overruled their commanders to launch a massive counter-attack. It was ambushed and annihilated, leaving Chang'an undefended.

---

## The flight and Yang Guifei's death

The great Tang Xuanzong fled Chang'an toward Sichuan. Two weeks into the journey, at a posting station called Mawei, the imperial escort mutinied. They murdered Yang Guozhong and his family. Then they turned to the emperor and demanded the execution of Yang Guifei.

The emperor was powerless to protect her. He concurred. Yang Guifei asked only that, instead of execution, she be strangled with a length of silk — so that her body would remain intact. The emperor's trusted eunuch performed the deed.

The emperor continued to Chengdu, broken-hearted. He lived another five years, outlasting An Lushan, witnessing the Tang restoration. "In neither is he said to have taken the remotest interest."

The relationship between Xuanzong and Yang Guifei became one of the richest veins in all Chinese literature and art. The eighth-century poet Bai Juyi (Tang era) wrote the *Song of Everlasting Regret*, a long narrative poem about their love and her death, which became one of the most-read poems in Chinese history. Countless operas, dramas, and paintings followed. The basic question — why did he not save her, or die with her? — is left permanently open.

## The aftermath: a transformed empire

An Lushan was murdered by his own son in early 757. His "Great Yan" dynasty spluttered on through four claimants (three murdered by their successors) before the last one's head was presented to the Tang emperor in 763. The rebellion was over.

But the Tang that reassembled itself was fundamentally different:

**Military governors (*jiedushi*) were now autonomous.** To fight the rebellion, the Tang had appointed military governors throughout the heartland. These men and their successors would not give up their powers. The northeastern provinces — roughly modern Hebei, Shandong, parts of Shanxi — effectively became self-governing, remitting no taxes to Chang'an and selecting their own successors. They would remain outside direct imperial control for the next four and a half centuries.

**The western empire was lost.** While Tang armies had been recalled to fight An Lushan, the Tibetans seized the Gansu corridor, Xinjiang, and eventually Chang'an itself (briefly, in 763). "The aftermath of the An Lushan rebellion thus marked 'the end of Chinese administration in Eastern Turkestan [Xinjiang] for almost a thousand years.'"

**The Uighurs became indispensable — and expensive.** Tang Suzong recaptured Chang'an in 757 largely thanks to Uighur cavalry. The Uighurs charged accordingly: enormous trade subsidies, an imperial bride, and "a painful equality of status." When in 762 they sacked Luoyang — their own ally's capital — the fires burned for weeks and tens of thousands died. The Tang had no choice but to accept it.

**The population collapsed.** Census figures show registered households falling from around 9 million before the rebellion to perhaps 2 million in the immediate aftermath. The actual death toll — from battle, famine, disease, and displacement — may have been 30–35 million, making An Lushan's rebellion one of the deadliest events in human history before the 20th century.

## The poets

The rebellion produced some of the greatest Chinese poetry. Du Fu fled Chang'an with his family and spent years as a displaced refugee, writing about hunger, exhaustion, and the human cost of empire:

> *Night deepened on Pengya Road, the moon shone over White-water Hills. A whole family endlessly trudging, begging without shame from the people we met.*

Li Bo, more comfortable at court, wrote military laments:

> *Captains and soldiers are smeared on the bushes and grass. The General schemed in vain. Know therefore that the sword is a cursed thing which the wise man uses only if he must.*

Both poets were alive during the rebellion; both died shortly after it (762 and 770 respectively). Their bodies of work are inseparable from the catastrophe they witnessed.

---

## The long shadow

The An Lushan rebellion is the structural explanation for everything that follows in Tang and post-Tang history:
- Why the Tang spent a century unable to reassert central control over the northeast
- Why the late Tang relied increasingly on eunuchs (they were personally loyal to the emperor in ways bureaucrats and generals were not)
- Why the Five Dynasties period after 907 was so fragmented
- Why the Song dynasty, which reunified China in 960, adopted a systematically civilian-dominated military policy — to prevent another An Lushan

The pattern of a non-Han general commanding frontier forces going rogue would recur in Chinese history. The Tang had created the conditions for it by staffing the frontier commands with non-Han professionals and giving them enormous autonomous resources — a solution to the Confucian aversion to permanent professional armies that ultimately destroyed the dynasty it was meant to protect.
