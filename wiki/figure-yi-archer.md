# Yi the Archer (羿)

**Role:** Divine archer; solar hero; slayer of monsters  
**Consort:** Chang'e (Ch'ang O), who stole his immortality elixir and fled to the moon  
**Ambiguity:** Hero in the solar myth; villain in the political myth

Yi is one of the most vivid figures in Chinese mythology and one of its most tragic. He saved the world from solar destruction and was rewarded with heroic fame — and then, in other myths, he is a degenerate usurper and his wife abandons him. Chinese mythology rarely lets its heroes be simply heroic.

---

## He shot down nine suns

The central Yi myth: in the era of the sage-ruler Yao, all ten suns rose at once. The ten suns were children of Hsi-Ho (charioteer of the sun), normally taking turns at their daily journey. But they all rose together, and:

> The ten suns all rose at once, scorching the sheaves of grain and killing plants and trees, so that the people were without food. And the Cha-yü Dragon-Headed beast, the Chisel-Tusk beast, the Nine-Gullet beast, the Giant-Gale bird, the Feng-hsi wild boar, and the Giant-Head long-snake all plagued the people. (*Huai-nan Tzu*)

Yao ordered Yi to deal with it. Yi was given a vermilion bow and plain-colored arrows by the god Ti Chün. He shot down ten suns, killed the six monsters, and roads and signposts appeared in the world for the first time. For this, Yao was acclaimed Son of Heaven.

Most versions say Yi shot nine suns (leaving one to light the world); at least one says Yi himself shot all ten and it was Yao who stopped him.

---

## His divine weapons and their source

Ti Chün, the divine ruler who sent Yi to save the world, is a figure of particular interest: he is also the father of the ten suns (through his consort Hsi-Ho). He ordered Yi to shoot down his own children. This detail is passed over without comment in most texts, which tells you something about how Chinese mythology treats the internal logic of divine relationships.

---

## Chang'e and the moon

Yi obtained an elixir of immortality — enough for two — from the Queen Mother of the West. Chang'e (his wife) stole it and drank it all herself, then fled to the moon to escape his anger. In some versions she turned into a toad; in others she remained a beautiful goddess, lonely on the moon forever. The Mid-Autumn Festival (Moon Festival) is traditionally associated with Chang'e.

Birrell notes that Chang'e may be a trickster figure in early versions of the myth — she has the typical trickster trait of betraying her closest ally for personal gain, which gives her the typical trickster's ambiguous reception (sympathy and condemnation in equal measure).

---

## The political Yi: usurper and victim

A separate tradition (from *Tso Chuan* and *Classic of History*) casts Yi as the ruler who usurped the Hsia government — a degenerate who neglected governance to go hunting all day. His minister Feng Meng eventually turned on him and killed him. In this tradition Yi is responsible for his own downfall.

The ambiguity prevented Yi from attaining the unambiguous heroic status of Yü the Great — who, according to all the myths in the Yü cycle, never did anything wrong.

---

## The Torch Dragon connection

In the solar myth complex, Yi's archer function links him to the myth of the world-tree (Leaning Mulberry) where the ten suns rest, and to Hsi-Ho, the charioteer. The god Chu Lung (Torch Dragon) — whose open/closed eyes create day and night in the far north — may represent an earlier, separate solar myth that predates the Yi cycle.
