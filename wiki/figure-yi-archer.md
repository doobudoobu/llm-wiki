# Yi the Archer (羿)

**Role:** Divine archer; solar hero; slayer of monsters  
**Consort:** Chang'e (Ch'ang O), who stole his immortality elixir and fled to the moon  
**Ambiguity:** Hero in the solar myth; villain in the political myth

Yi is one of the most vivid figures in Chinese mythology and one of its most tragic. He saved the world from solar destruction and was rewarded with heroic fame — and then, in other myths, he is a degenerate usurper and his wife abandons him. Chinese mythology rarely lets its heroes be simply heroic.

---

## He shot down nine suns

The central Yi myth: in the era of the sage-ruler Yao, all ten suns rose at once. The ten suns were children of Hsi-Ho (charioteer of the sun), normally taking turns at their daily journey. But they all rose together, and:

> The ten suns all rose at once, scorching the sheaves of grain and killing plants and trees, so that the people were without food. And the Cha-yü Dragon-Headed beast, the Chisel-Tusk beast, the Nine-Gullet beast, the Giant-Gale bird, the Feng-hsi wild boar, and the Giant-Head long-snake all plagued the people. (*Huai-nan Tzu*)

Yao ordered Yi to deal with it. Yi was given a vermilion bow and plain-colored arrows by the god Ti Chün. He shot down ten suns, killed the six monsters, and roads and signposts appeared in the world for the first time. For this, Yao was acclaimed Son of Heaven.

Most versions say Yi shot nine suns (leaving one to light the world); at least one says Yi himself shot all ten and it was Yao who stopped him.

---

## His divine weapons and their source

Ti Chün, the divine ruler who sent Yi to save the world, is a figure of particular interest: he is also the father of the ten suns (through his consort Hsi-Ho). He ordered Yi to shoot down his own children. This detail is passed over without comment in most texts, which tells you something about how Chinese mythology treats the internal logic of divine relationships.

---

## Chang'e and the moon

Yi obtained an elixir of immortality — enough for two — from the Queen Mother of the West. Chang'e (his wife) stole it and drank it all herself, then fled to the moon to escape his anger. In some versions she turned into a toad; in others she remained a beautiful goddess, lonely on the moon forever. The Mid-Autumn Festival (Moon Festival) is traditionally associated with Chang'e.

Birrell notes that Chang'e may be a trickster figure in early versions of the myth — she has the typical trickster trait of betraying her closest ally for personal gain, which gives her the typical trickster's ambiguous reception (sympathy and condemnation in equal measure).

## The political Yi: usurper and victim

A separate tradition (from *Tso Chuan* and *Classic of History*) casts Yi as the ruler who usurped the Hsia government — a degenerate who neglected governance to go hunting all day. He dismissed competent ministers and installed a flatterer who "practiced flattery at court and bribery in society at large." His own clansmen assassinated him on his return from the hunt. They cooked his body and served it to his sons. His sons couldn't bring themselves to eat him. They were all put to death.

The ambiguity prevented Yi from attaining the unambiguous heroic status of Yü the Great — who, according to all the myths in the Yü cycle, never did anything wrong.

---

## He shot the river god and stole his wife

Chapter 4 of Birrell catalogs Yi's crimes in detail. Beyond the usurpation, the oldest sources charge him with:

1. **Shooting the Lord of the River (Lo River) in the left eye** — an unprovoked attack on a god who had done him no harm. Multiple commentators try to justify this: one says the river god "killed people by drowning them, so Yi shot him." But the earliest source (*Questions of Heaven*) simply asks: "God sent down Yi to drive away the evils besetting the Hsia people, so why did he shoot down the Lord of the River and take his wife, Lo-pin?"

2. **Stealing the river goddess Fu-fei (Lo-pin)** — her later name is Lo-shen, the famous Goddess of the Lo River. Tradition says she was originally a daughter of Fu Hsi who drowned in the Lo River and became its goddess. Yi had her.

When the Lord of the River complained to God, God's response was striking: *"Why were you shot by Yi?"* — *"I had transformed myself into a white dragon to play on the riverbank."* — *"If you had kept to the river depths as a god, how could Yi have committed this crime against you? Today you became a reptile, so you were bound to be shot."* The river god is dismissed. Yi goes unpunished — because the victim was careless.

## How he died: the peach-wood club

Yi's student **Feng Meng** learned archery from Yi and mastered everything Yi knew. He realized that only Yi in the whole world was better than he was. So he killed him — not with an arrow, but with a club carved from **peach wood**, the one thing Yi couldn't shoot aside.

> "Club" is the large stick that he made out of peach wood to batter Yi to death with. From that time on, demons are terrified of peach wood. (*Hsu Shen's commentary on Huai-nan Tzu*)

This is the origin of a real Chinese apotropaic tradition: peach wood was used to ward off demons and evil spirits throughout Chinese history. The greatest archer in the world was killed by a piece of wood, wielded by the person he trusted most. After Yi died, he became the god Tsung Pu.

Mencius's reaction to the Feng Meng story is interesting: he doesn't condemn Feng Meng simply as a murderer. The story implies Yi bore some responsibility for what happened to him — he created the student who surpassed him, and he failed to anticipate the danger.

## The moon is overcrowded

Chang'e/Heng O is not alone on the moon. Also present:
- A **striped toad** (Ch'an-ch'u) — possibly the transformed Chang'e herself, possibly her companion. The toad represents immortality through skin-shedding and rebirth.
- A **hare**
- A **mortar and pestle** (grinding the drug of immortality)
- **Wu Kang** with his axe

Wu Kang was an alchemist who made an unspecified "mistake in his quest for immortality" and was exiled to the moon. His punishment: chop down a cassia tree. But the tree always heals. He chops forever. The word *cassia* is *kuei* (桂), which puns on *kuei* (歸) = "to return." His punishment is built into the name of the tree he's chopping.

By the Han dynasty, the moon in Chinese mythology had accumulated this full cast of characters: the abandoned goddess, the toad, the hare, the alchemist, the eternally regrowing tree. A small, airless, lonely place, populated entirely by people who wanted to live forever and got stuck there.

## The Torch Dragon connection

In the solar myth complex, Yi's archer function links him to the myth of the world-tree (Leaning Mulberry) where the ten suns rest, and to Hsi-Ho, the charioteer. The god Chu Lung (Torch Dragon) — whose open/closed eyes create day and night in the far north — may represent an earlier, separate solar myth that predates the Yi cycle.
