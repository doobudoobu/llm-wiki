# Jin Dynasty (266–420 CE)

The Jin dynasty briefly reunified China after the Three Kingdoms — and then promptly came apart again, producing one of the longest and most chaotic periods of fragmentation in Chinese history.

| Phase | Period | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Western Jin | 266–316 | Reunified China; collapsed in civil war and nomadic invasion |
| Eastern Jin | 317–420 | Southern rump state; kept Han culture alive while the north burned |

---

## Brief triumph, swift catastrophe

The Jin's founder, Sima Yan, was the grandson of Sima Yi — the Wei general who had outmaneuvered Zhuge Liang. His family had been the effective rulers of Wei for a generation before he formalized the arrangement by receiving Wei's last emperor's abdication in 265. In 280 the Jin conquered Wu, ending the Three Kingdoms period. For the first time since Han's collapse, China was united under a single dynasty.

It lasted about a decade before things went wrong.

---

## The War of the Eight Princes (290–311)

When Sima Yan died in 290, he left a successor who was reportedly severely mentally disabled. Imperial kinsmen, each commanding semi-autonomous military districts — a structural mistake Sima Yan had made to reward his relatives — immediately began fighting for control. The resulting "War of the Eight Princes" lasted over twenty years and devastated the empire.

Whoever could conscript troops did. Farmers were dragged from fields, livestock commandeered, seed grain eaten, roads clogged with refugees and corpses. "The rivers filled with floating corpses, bleached bones covered the fields," says the Jin History. "There was much cannibalism. Famine and pestilence went hand in hand." The formulaic phrasing of Standard Histories may for once have been accurate.

The people who benefited were the non-Han groups who had been settled inside the empire as auxiliaries — Xiongnu, Xianbei, Di, Qiang, Jie — who now had the combination of weapons, horses, and motivation to act as independent powers. The Jin princes had invited them in as military support. They stayed as predators.

## The fall of Luoyang (311)

In 313, a Sogdian merchant named Nanai Vandak wrote a letter from somewhere in northern China back to his superior, probably in Samarkand. The letter was found sixteen centuries later in an abandoned postbag at Dunhuang. It is the most vivid eyewitness account of what was happening:

> *. . . And, Sir, the last Emperor — so they say — fled from [Luoyang] because of the famine. And his fortified residence burnt down, and the town was destroyed. So Luoyang is no more, Yeh is no more! . . . They pillaged up to N'ymn'ymh and up to Ngap, these Xiongnu who yesterday had been the Emperor's property! . . . And, Sir, if I wrote you all the details of how China fared, it would be a catalogue of debts and woe. You will have no wealth from here . . .*

The Xiongnu leader who took Luoyang was Liu Cong, descendant of a shanyu who had married a Han princess under the old "peace-through-kinship" treaties — he had literally inherited the right to use the Han surname Liu. His predecessor Liu Yuan had called himself "king of Han" in a bid to attract Chinese support. Ten thousand defenders were slaughtered in the final assault, and the world's second-largest city was left a ruin.

Chang'an fell twice in quick succession. The Jin emperor was captured and killed. His successor was captured and killed. A distant cousin, Sima Rui, was stationed on the Yangzi and managed to keep the dynasty alive in the south — as the Eastern Jin, reigning from Jiankang (modern Nanjing).

At least a million, possibly several million, people fled south in the exodus that followed — aristocratic émigrés shoulder-to-shoulder with destitute refugees, entire districts relocating together under their local lords, keeping their administrative designations intact like portable states. The parallels with later Chinese history (the Ming/Qing transition, the Nationalist retreat to Taiwan) are striking.

## The Sixteen Kingdoms (304–439): chaos in the north

The northern half of the former empire fractured into the so-called Sixteen Kingdoms — a mix of competing dynasties, most of them founded by non-Han peoples: Xiongnu, Xianbei, Di, Qiang, Jie. Almost none lasted more than a generation. A few examples convey the atmosphere:

**The Later Zhao dynasty** (319–351) was founded by Shi Le, a former Xiongnu slave and outlaw. His successor Shi Hu has been charitably described as a psychopath. On falling out with his heir apparent, he reportedly had the young man, his consort, their twenty-six children, and their two hundred retainers all killed and buried in the same coffin. (Minus the retainers.) During his reign, Buddhism made dramatic progress in northern China — not despite but because of Shi Hu's patronage. A Buddhist monk named **Fotudeng** had secured Shi Le's conversion by filling his begging bowl with water and conjuring a bright blue lotus from it. Shi Hu, impressed, declared, "Buddha, being a foreign god, is the very one we [as outsiders] should worship" — a remarkably clear-eyed assessment of Buddhism's utility for rulers who couldn't use Confucian orthodoxy to legitimate themselves.

Most of the Sixteen Kingdoms nominally deferred to Han traditions, adopted prestigious dynastic names (Wei, Zhao, Qin, Yan, Jin), experimented with bureaucratic government, and failed. The chaos ended only when the Xianbei confederation called the Tabgach (Tuoba) — known in Chinese as the Northern Wei — swept in from beyond the Mongolian steppe, eliminated all rivals by 439, and reunified the north for nearly a century. ## Eastern Jin in the south (317–420)

In the south, the Eastern Jin kept Chinese civilization intact at Jiankang. The great calligrapher **Wang Xizhi** worked here. The poet **Tao Yuanming** (Tao Qian), who retired from office in disgust and wrote pastoral poems about chrysanthemums and returning to farm life, was Eastern Jin.

But the southern court was riven by conflict between the powerful aristocratic clans who had come south (they monopolized senior offices and treated the emperor as their instrument) and the dynasts who wanted to reassert royal authority. When a general named Liu Yu finally managed to defeat the northern challengers in the early 400s and restore some territory, he was strong enough to force the last Eastern Jin emperor's abdication in 420 and found the Liu Song dynasty — beginning the period conventionally called the Northern and Southern Dynasties.

---

## Why the Jin matters

The Jin collapse established several patterns that haunted Chinese history:
- **Rewarding kinsmen with autonomous military power** creates structural fragility, as the War of the Eight Princes demonstrated conclusively.
- **Non-Han peoples settled inside the frontier** will act as independent powers when the center weakens — a lesson the Tang would partly forget.
- **The north-south division** introduced by the Jin collapse lasted nearly three centuries (311–589). The Southern courts preserved and transmitted Han culture while the north was transformed by non-Han rule. Both halves of this dual China would eventually merge into the cosmopolitan Tang.
