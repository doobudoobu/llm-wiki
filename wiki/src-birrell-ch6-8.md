# Birrell, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* — Chapters 6–8

**Chapters:** 6 ("Myths of the Yellow Emperor"), 7 ("Myths of Yi the Archer"), 8 ("Myths of Yü the Great")

---

## The punchline up front

These three chapters each trace how a major mythological figure was progressively rewritten by later traditions. The Yellow Emperor was a violent warrior-god who got sanitized into a Taoist philosopher-king. Yi the Archer was an ambiguous demigod who later commentators kept trying to rehabilitate no matter what he did. And Yü the Great — the only consistent hero in the tradition — accumulated an entire secondary mythology of deities helping him, including one who directly foreshadows Sun Wukong.

The chapters also raise a meta-question: what happens when myth gets turned into philosophy? Chuang Tzu used the Yellow Emperor as a sock puppet for Taoist arguments, making him prostrate himself before random sages. Ssu-ma Ch'ien turned Yü into a demythologized civil servant of Shun. The gods shrink as the philosophers expand.

## Chapter 6 — Myths of the Yellow Emperor (黄帝)

The early Yellow Emperor is almost entirely a warrior. His battles:

**vs. the Flame Emperor (Yen Ti 炎帝):** Brothers who each ruled half the universe. "The Flame Emperor had the same father and mother as the Yellow Emperor, and he was his younger brother. Each possessed one half of the universe. The Yellow Emperor followed the Way, but the Flame Emperor refused to obey." They fought on the Wastes of P'an-ch'üan and Cho-lu. His vanguard: bears, grizzly bears, wolves, panthers, cougars, tigers. His banners: eagles, fighting pheasants, falcons, kites. Three battles, then Yellow Emperor won.

**vs. Ch'ih Yü (蚩尤):** The war god attacks. Ch'ih Yü summons the Wind God and the Rain Master to release a cloudburst. The Yellow Emperor deploys his dragon Responding Dragon (Ying Lung 應龍) to store up all the water; then sends his daughter Drought Fury (Nü Pa 女魃) to stop the rain. Ch'ih Yü is killed. But Responding Dragon, having fought, can't ascend back to Heaven — so wherever he descends afterward, drought follows. Rainmaking ritual: someone impersonates the Responding Dragon and rain comes.

**vs. K'uei (夔):** A one-legged storm god from the eastern sea — looks like an ox with a blue body, one hoof, no horns. Its light is like sun and moon; its voice like thunder. The Yellow Emperor captures it, skins it, makes a war drum from its hide. He strikes it with a bone from the thunder beast. The sound travels 500 leagues. The world stands in awe. In *Classic of History* this same K'uei becomes Yao's master of music, a human court official who makes animals dance — one of the most blatant examples in the tradition of myth being historicized into administration.

**vs. the Four Emperors:** In a later (3rd century AD) moralizing version, the four directional gods gang up on the Yellow Emperor. He says: "If the ruler is in danger, the people beneath will be unstable." He defeats them. But the text already inserts the caveat: "he took no pleasure in war or aggression." By this point even the war myths are being apologized for.

**The Black Pearl (philosophical mythopoeia):** Chuang Tzu uses the Yellow Emperor to argue for Taoist instinct over learning. The Yellow Emperor loses his black pearl; sends Knowledge to find it — fails; sends Wrangling Debate — fails; sends Shapeless — finds it immediately. "That's amazing! How was Shapeless the one who was able to find it?" The myth is invented to make a point. Birrell calls this "mythopoeia" — new myths manufactured for philosophical purposes, not preserved from a living tradition.

**The Dark Lady and the Art of War:** After nine inconclusive battles with Ch'ih Yü, the Yellow Emperor retreats to T'ai Mountain. A woman with a human head and bird's body appears from the fog. He prostrates himself; she teaches him the art of war. Birrell identifies this as a "latter-day manipulative invention" — the Dark Lady is a post-Han Taoist goddess grafted onto the myth to give Taoist tradition precedence over all warfare.

## Chapter 7 — Myths of Yi the Archer

Chapter 7 recaps and extends what Chapter 4 covered. New material:

**The Rock of the Nine Suns (Wu Chiao):** When Yi shot down the nine suns, they fell onto Wu Chiao — a rock forty thousand leagues across in the middle of the great ocean that infinitely consumes all water that flows into it. It lies at "the tail end of all flowing rivers." This is the cosmological drain of the world. The suns fell into it and it consumed them.

**The Moon is overcrowded:** Chang'e/Heng O is not alone on the moon. Also present: a striped toad (the transformed Chang'e, or her companion — the toad = immortality, its skin-shedding = eternal rebirth), a hare, a mortar and pestle, and **Wu Kang** with his axe. Wu Kang was an alchemist who made a mistake in his quest for immortality and was exiled to chop down a cassia tree on the moon — but the tree always heals. He chops forever. The word "cassia" (*kuei* 桂) is a pun on *kuei* = "to return." His eternal chopping = eternal return. The moon is literally haunted by people who pursued immortality and got stuck there.

**Han commentators defending Yi:** Birrell notes that late Han commentators consistently tried to rehabilitate Yi no matter what he did. When Yi shot the river god in the eye for no reason, commentator Kao Yu explained: "the Lord of the River killed people by drowning them, so Yi shot him." Commentator Wang Yi had Yi only *dream* about an affair with Fu-fei. The tradition actively resisted leaving its hero as a criminal.

## Chapter 8 — Myths of Yü the Great

Birrell clarifies: there are **four separate flood myths** in the Chinese tradition, each with a different central figure:
1. Kung Kung — the marplot who caused a flood through his hydraulic experiments
2. Nü Kua — who mended the broken sky and stopped the floods
3. Kun — Yü's father, who stole God's self-renewing soil and failed
4. Yü — who succeeded by channeling rather than damming

They are not four versions of the same story. They are four separate mythological traditions, probably from different regional cultures, that were eventually all compiled into the same classical texts.

**Yu as warrior:** Yu isn't only a hydraulic engineer; he's a divine warlord who punishes gods:

- **Fang-feng:** A god who arrived late to Yü's assembly of the gods at K'uai-chi Mountain. Yü killed and beheaded him. When Confucius heard the story, he was asked: "Was Fang-feng really a giant?" Confucius confirmed it: one joint of Fang-feng's skeleton "filled up a whole cart."
- **Hsiang Liu (相柳):** Kung Kung's nine-headed official with a snake's body and human faces. He ate from nine mountains simultaneously. Everywhere he went turned to marsh or ravine. When Yü killed him, his blood was so poisonous that the five grains couldn't grow near it. Yü excavated the area and filled it three times — it leaked three times. He turned the location into a raised terrace for the gods.
- **Wu-chih-ch'i (無支祁):** The ancestor of Sun Wukong. A river monster shaped like an ape with an upturned snout and metallic eyes — "stronger than nine elephants." He spoke formal rhetoric, knew every depth and distance of the Yangtze and Huai rivers, and moved so fast "now you'd hear him, now you wouldn't; now you'd see him, now you wouldn't." Yü couldn't capture him alone. Three generals failed. Finally Keng-ch'en "chained his neck with a huge rope and threaded a metal bell through his nostril" — and banished him to the south of the Huai River. After that, the Huai River flowed peacefully to the sea. People made images of the monster and kept them to ward off storms.

Ming novelist Wu Cheng-en (ca. 1506-1582) directly converted Wu-chih-ch'i into Sun Wukong for *Journey to the West*. The ape, the strength, the supernatural speed, the river-god setting, the chains — all already present in the Yü myth.

**The Nine Cauldrons:** Yü collected metal from all nine provinces and cast nine bronze cauldrons — one per province, engraved with images of all spirits (malign and benign) so the people could tell them apart. The cauldrons embodied divine legitimacy: when the ruling house was virtuous, the cauldrons were heavy; when corrupt, they were light. Yü → Hsia → Shang → Zhou, each dynasty inheriting (or seizing) the cauldrons from the last. When the Chou were overthrown by Ch'in, "one cauldron flew into Ssu River and the other eight went into Ch'in territory." The Qin First Emperor famously dredged the Ssu River for it and found nothing.

The Nine Cauldrons became the Chinese equivalent of the Holy Grail — the sacred objects whose possession proved legitimate rulership.

**Yü the World Measurer:** In *Huai-nan Tzu*, Yü measures the entire earth by sending two officers to pace it out. Distance east-to-west: 233,500 leagues and 75 paces. North-to-south: the same. The earth was conceived as a perfect square. This is the mythological origin of Chinese cartography.

**From myth to literature:** In later narratives (4th century AD onward), Yü accumulates helper-goddesses. Most notably, **Jasper Lady** — the 23rd daughter of the Queen Mother of the West — becomes his primary divine helper. She transforms constantly (rock, cloud, dragon, crane, phoenix) and finally gives Yü a jade box containing a "priceless document in a distinguished script" that enables him to complete his work. Birrell reads this shift as the evolution from myth to Taoist literature: Yü goes from independent demigod to a hero dependent on (and subservient to) a Taoist pantheon of goddesses. The story didn't change; its frame did.
