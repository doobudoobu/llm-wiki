# Birrell, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* — Chapters 9–11

**Chapters:** 9 ("Goddesses"), 10 ("Immortality"), 11 ("Metamorphoses")

---

## The punchline up front

Chapter 9 documents how several major Chinese goddesses were systematically demoted over time — stripped of their independence, their wildness, their power — and made subordinate to male gods. The Queen Mother of the West is the clearest example: she begins as a plague-bringer with tiger's fangs and a panther's tail, and ends as a polite lady hosting kings for tea beside a jade pool.

Chapter 10 traces the Chinese obsession with immortality from its mythic roots to the Qin First Emperor's desperate island searches. Birrell's key insight: in early mythology, the line between mortality and immortality was genuinely blurred. Even the "immortal" gods die. Metamorphosis is the real Chinese afterlife.

Chapter 11 is the most technically beautiful chapter in the book — metamorphosis myths work like encoding devices, the transformed state expressing something essential about the figure who changed. Ch'ih Yü's fetters become a maple grove (his blood is red). A broken promise to a horse produces the first silkworm. A fish becomes a bird and a philosopher uses it to argue about the relativity of perspective.

## Chapter 9 — Goddesses

**The chapter's thesis:** Fewer goddesses than gods in the classical Chinese pantheon, but they are often more mythologically significant. Nü Kua made humans and repaired the sky. The Queen Mother of the West controls plagues, death, and immortality. Hsi-Ho drives the sun chariot. Ch'ang-hsi bore the twelve moons. But male scholarship systematically cut them down. Karlgren even argued Nü Kua "might be male." Edward Schafer's blunter diagnosis: "the contempt of some eminent and educated men for animalian gods, and partly due to the increasing domination of masculinity in the elite social doctrine."

**Nü Kua's demotion:** In Han iconography she becomes a consort entwined with Fu Hsi like Zeus and Hera — her ancient independence eliminated. The full *Huai-nan Tzu* passage on her acts of cosmological repair ends strangely: after mending the sky, supporting the poles, killing the black dragon, stopping the flood — she "holds the secret of the Way of the True Person and follows the eternal nature of Heaven and earth" without displaying her achievements. The greatest act in mythology done in silence.

**Weaver Maid (織女) and Draught Ox (牛郎):** The original *Classic of Poetry* (c. 600 BC) poem has *no romantic content*. Weaver Maid weaves all day but never finishes her cloth; Draught Ox is powerful but never yoked to a carriage. Both are images of futile labor. The romance — God's daughter who married too happily, neglected her weaving, was punished by separation, meets her husband once a year across the Sky River — comes from a 12th century AD text. The magpie bridge feature enters in the 6th century AD. Six hundred years of gradual romantic elaboration on top of a poem about uselessness. The stars are Vega (Weaver Maid) and Altair (Draught Ox). The festival is the seventh night of the seventh month.

**The Hsiang Queens:** Yao's two daughters became the wives of Shun. When Shun died on tour in Hunan, they drowned in the Hsiang River. The speckled bamboo of Hunan is still called the "Hsiang Queens' bamboo" — the spots are their tear stains. Long before this story existed, the Chu "Nine Songs" were already hymning river goddesses of the Hsiang. Earlier goddesses, absorbed into the Yao-Shun myth cycle.

**Woman Ch'ou (女丑 Nü Chóu):** A drought deity born a corpse. She counters drought by self-immolation and is reborn because she never truly dies. She hides her sun-scorched face with her sleeve. Wears green clothes (= vegetal renewal). "Where the ten suns are up above, Woman Ch'ou lived there on the top of the mountain." Birrell: commentators have argued this points to a real practice of sacrificing shamanesses to the sun in times of drought, "but the mythic motifs are so complex and profound that such a reading would seem to be too narrow."

**The Queen Mother of the West (西王母):** Her own page. See [[concept-queen-mother-west]].

**The Goddess of Salt River:** A local myth from the Pa tribe of Sichuan. The Lord of the Granary wins chieftainship by throwing a sword at a rock (only he hits it) and floating an earthenware boat (only his stays afloat). He sails to Yen-yang, where the Salt Goddess propositions him. He refuses. She sleeps with him at night, metamorphoses into a swarm of insects at dawn, blots out the sun for ten straight days. He tricks her by giving her a silk cord as a "girdle" — which is actually a hunting weapon. She accepts it out of vanity. He shoots her through the cord. The world clears.

The myth may encode: a patriarchal grain culture conquering a matriarchal salt-rich culture. Both protagonists have food in their names (Lin = granary, Yen = salt). In primitive economies, control of salt = enormous power.

## Chapter 10 — Immortality

**The early Chinese view:** In the oldest strata of myth, even gods die — the Flame Emperor, Ch'ih Yü, Kun, Fang-feng. What distinguishes the divine from the mortal is metamorphosis: when a god is killed, part of them lives on transformed. Kun → bear. Ch'ih Yü's blood → red maple trees. P'an Ku → the universe. This is the real Chinese answer to death: not heaven, but transformation.

The concept of *physical* immortality (deathlessness for a living body) emerges in the late Chou and Qin periods — and is immediately associated with the powerful and the paranoid. The First Emperor of Qin sent magicians to find the eastern islands. See [[dynasty-qin]].

**K'un-lun Mountain Paradise:** God's earthly capital in the northwest, 800 leagues square, 80,000 feet high. Its features:
- The Tree Grain (a sky-ladder)
- Nine jade wells
- The K'ai-ming beast: nine-headed, tiger-bodied guardian
- The "Never Die" tree, pearl trees, jade trees
- Male and female phoenixes
- **Six shamans** guarding the undecaying corpse of the murder victim Cha Yü, each holding a branch of the drug of immortality over him — keeping him from decomposing in perpetuity

Curiously, the Queen Mother of the West is absent from this description, though elsewhere in the same *Classic of Mountains and Seas* she's said to preside over Jade Mountain in the K'un-lun void.

**The Island Paradises in the East:** Five islands floating in a bottomless pool east of the Eastern Sea: Tai Yu, Yuan Chiao, Fang Hu, Ying-chou, P'eng-lai. Gold and jade terraces. White birds. Fruit trees whose blossoms are richly flavored — eat them and you never grow old. The immortals flit between the islands day and night.

Problem: the islands' bases were not anchored. They drifted with the tides. God assigned fifteen giant sea turtles to hold them up, alternating in groups of three every sixty thousand years. Then a giant in the Dragon Earl's kingdom fished six turtles in a single cast, bundled them onto his back, and left. Two islands — Tai Yu and Yuan Chiao — drifted to the North Pole and sank. The remaining three islands are the famous Three Immortal Isles: **P'eng-lai, Fang Hu, and Ying-chou** — the ones the Qin First Emperor was searching for.

**P'eng-tsu (彭祖):** The Chinese Methuselah. Great-great-grandson of the god Chuan Hsu. By the end of the Shang dynasty he was 767 years old but "never declined into old age." When an imperial concubine was sent to ask his secret, he began his answer with a catalog of loss: orphaned in the womb, lost his mother at three, exiled for a hundred years, buried forty-nine wives, lost fifty-four children. "My good humor in my guts has been broken... I am afraid I am not long for this world." He didn't give the secret. He simply disappeared. Seventy years later someone caught a glimpse of him in the kingdom of Flowing Sands, heading west.

The Chinese Methuselah's lesson about longevity is that it doesn't spare you from grief.

## Chapter 11 — Metamorphoses

Birrell opens by listing Chinese metamorphoses alongside Greco-Roman ones: Daphne / Yi Yin's mother (both into trees). Ganymede-Orion / Fu Yueh (star ascensions). Psamathe / Chang'e (toad). The chapter makes the point that in Chinese myth, as in Greek, metamorphosis is almost always either punishment or apotheosis — and often the transformed state symbolizes something about the figure that changed.

**P'an Ku → universe:** Already covered. His breath = wind, his eyes = sun and moon, his blood = rivers, his hair = stars, his sweat = rain, the mites on his body = "the black-haired people."

**K'un fish → P'eng bird (鯤 / 鵬):** From *Chuang Tzu*, citing an older text *Tall Stories from Ch'i*. In the North Gloom sea there is a fish called K'un — "countless thousand leagues big." It transforms into the P'eng bird, whose wings are "like clouds suspended in the sky." To migrate south, it dashes water three thousand leagues high and rises ninety thousand leagues on a whirlwind. It flies for six months before resting. Chuang Tzu uses this to argue about perspective: from up there, the blue sky must look to the P'eng just like what we see when we look up. The microcosm and macrocosm mirror each other. The metamorphosis is a philosophical proposition.

**Ch'ih Yü → maple grove:** The war god's wooden fetters, thrown on Sung Mountain after his execution, turned into a grove of red maple trees. His blood is red; his fetters become red-leaved trees. The red salt flats of Hsieh-chou are still called "Ch'ih Yü's Blood." His burial mound emits "scarlet vapor like the roll of blood-red silk" — "Ch'ih Yü's Banner." A god executed centuries ago whose blood still marks the landscape.

**Fu Yueh → star:** A sage found in a prison chain gang building at Fu Gorge, promoted to prime minister by King Wu Ding of the Shang (who found him in a dream). He governed so well he ascended to become a star — specifically Winnower Star and Tail Star. Later, Ssu-ma Ch'ien naturalizes this into a touching story: the king sent men to find the man who matched his dream, found him in a work camp, and elevated him.

**Tu Yü → nightjar:** An elaborate Sichuan myth: a king descended from Heaven, married a woman who emerged from a well, made a minister of a corpse that floated from another province and came back to life. That minister Pieh Ling controlled a flood while Emperor Wang had an affair with his wife. Wang abdicated in shame and went into exile. The nightjar (tzu-kuei bird) began to call — people of Shu still say it's Emperor Wang. Three metamorphoses in one myth: the Shu kings who became immortals, the resurrected corpse, and the exiled emperor-turned-bird.

**The Silkworm Horse:** A girl promises her horse that she'll marry it if it brings her father home from his travels. The horse does it. The father kills and skins the horse when he finds out. The girl mocks the dead skin: "You're just a domestic animal, yet you wanted a human as your wife..." Before she finishes, the horse skin rises up, wraps her in it, and vanishes. Days later she's found transformed into a giant silkworm spinning in a mulberry tree, cocoon thick and prolific. Everyone rushed to grow the tree. Origin of sericulture.

The word "mulberry" (*sang* 桑) is a pun on "mourning" (*sang* 喪). The tree of silk = the tree of grief. A broken promise + a taunted corpse + punishment = the entire Chinese silk industry.
