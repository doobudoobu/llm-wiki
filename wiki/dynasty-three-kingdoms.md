# Three Kingdoms (189–280 CE)

After the collapse of the Han, China fractured into three rival states that couldn't destroy each other for sixty years. The period produced Chinese history's most beloved military strategists and became the setting for *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* — still one of the most widely read novels in the world.

## How it started: warlords and orphaned emperors

When Emperor Lingdi died in 189, the Han court dissolved into factional violence. A brutal warlord named Dong Zhuo seized Chang'an, swapped one boy emperor for another, and triggered a cascade of armies marching against each other with the emperor as a pawn. The empire became a hunting ground for capable men of obscure origin — "Poison Yu and Big Eyes Li," as contemporaries listed them. Cao Cao's father had been the adopted son of a palace eunuch; his family origins were unknown. Liu Bei had sold sandals as a child.

For the next thirty years the last Han emperor, Han Xiandi, was passed from one warlord to another, "lodged among thorns and wattles, wandering without shelter." He ended up in the custody of Cao Cao, who parked him in the eastern province of Xu and used the imperial name as a source of legitimacy for his own expanding power.

## The Battle of Red Cliffs (208 CE)

Everything pivoted on a single winter night in early 209. Cao Cao, having swept north, moved south to conquer Wu and end the three-way standoff before it began. He chained together a bridge of boats on the north bank of the Yangzi near the Red Cliffs (modern Hubei) and prepared to float it across to land his army.

On the south shore, Liu Bei and Sun Quan's generals put out a small fleet of yawls heading north. Lookouts on Cao Cao's bank reported their approach; defectors were expected, so no alarm was raised. When the fleet closed to hailing distance, fuses were lit below decks, hulls packed with oil-soaked tinder ignited, and the incoming boats became a wall of fire sweeping into Cao Cao's anchored fleet.

The flames spread from the river to his camp. His army, already weakened by exhaustion and disease (possibly plague), tried to retreat but found every road cut off by pre-positioned forces. The northern army was practically annihilated. Cao Cao was captured, then released — his captor owed him a favor — but he never invaded the south again.

Keay calls the Red Cliffs battle "the key to the history of the period," the moment that locked in China's division for another seventy years.

## The three kingdoms in practice

**Wei** under Cao Pi (who forced Han Xiandi's abdication in 220) held the Yellow River heartland and the most direct claim to Han succession. Wei had the largest territory and the most orthodox institutional continuity.

**Wu** under Sun Quan controlled the Yangzi delta — the emerging economic engine of China, rich in silk and trade, with access to Vietnam and southeast Asia. Sun Quan eventually declared himself emperor. Wu became the ancestor of the later "southern dynasties" that would preserve Han culture during northern chaos.

**Shu Han** was the most romantic and least secure. Liu Bei — described as exceptionally tall, arms reaching to his knees, eyes that could see around to his ears — had a faint claim to Han royal descent (supposedly a very distant relative of Former Han emperors). He ruled Sichuan, a natural fortress but not enough of a resource base. His chancellor and strategist **Zhuge Liang** became the archetype of the supremely loyal, brilliant official, still revered almost as a saint. After Liu Bei's death, Zhuge Liang kept the kingdom alive through sheer competence until his own death in 234.

## The Romance and the historians

The *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*, probably written in the 14th century, makes Liu Bei its hero and Cao Cao its villain — cunning and treacherous rather than brilliant and effective as the histories suggest. This tilt reflects the novel's origins in southern readings of the period: "southern" narratives preferred Liu Bei's virtue and Han bloodline over Cao Pi's realpolitik.

The novel also reflects Ming dynasty politics: if written as the Mongol Yuan crumbled, Cao Cao and his Wei dynasty could be read as foreign usurpers (like the Mongols) and Liu Bei as the authentic Han resistance. The greatest national epic was conscripted as nationalist propaganda.

Chinese historians writing under Jin patronage favored Wei. Southern court historians favored Shu Han. The debate lasted centuries. It's one of the earliest examples of how the writing of history in China was always also a political act.

## Literature born from catastrophe

The Han collapse triggered one of China's first golden ages of literary expression. Court poetry (*fu*) had been bombastic, imperial, catalogic. But poets fleeing the violence discovered something sharper. Wang Can, escaping Chang'an after Dong Zhuo's murder, wrote "Seven Sadnesses":

> Once out the gate, nothing to see,  
> Just white bones covering the plain,  
> A starving woman on the road  
> Embraces a child and abandons it in the grass.

The Cao family were literary patrons as well as warlords. Cao Cao and Cao Pi wrote poetry themselves; Cao Pi's prose criticism introduced the concept of *qi* (innate individual inspiration) as a measure of literary worth distinct from mere skill. Their relative Cao Zhi is rated among China's finest poets. Keay notes: "without entirely renouncing didactic and dynastic responsibilities, writers looked on the past anew and began to convey a sense of their own troubled time."

## Daoism's emergence

The Three Kingdoms period is when Daoism first crystallized as a distinct movement rather than a loose philosophical tendency. The Celestial Masters — also called the "Five Pecks" movement — had established a quasi-theocracy in Hanzhong (Sichuan border) with organized welfare, gender equality, and a millenarian promise of *taiping* (heavenly peace). When Cao Cao defeated them in 215, their leaders were absorbed into Wei with titles and fiefs. They then helpfully produced revelatory texts prophesying that the next emperor would be "a princeling of Wei." Cao Pi, being exactly that, accepted the emperorship.

This was "the first in a long tradition of Daoist legitimation of emperors" — the beginning of a centuries-long entanglement between Daoist clergy and dynastic power. See [[concept-mandate-of-heaven]].

## End of the Three Kingdoms

In 263 Wei finally conquered Shu Han. Two years later the Sima family, who had been the power behind Wei for generations, overthrew the Cao dynasty and founded the Jin. In 280 the Jin extinguished Wu. China was briefly united again — though not for long. See [[dynasty-jin]].
