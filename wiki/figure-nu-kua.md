# Nü Kua (女媧)

**Role:** Primeval goddess; creatrix of humans; repairer of the broken cosmos  
**First attested:** c. 4th century BC ("Questions of Heaven")  
**Attribute:** Compass and measuring rope; serpentine lower body

Nü Kua is the oldest and most important goddess in the Chinese mythological tradition. She predates the male cosmic giant P'an Ku by six centuries in the textual record. What happened to her is a case study in what Confucian rationalization did to the Chinese pantheon.

---

## She made humans — but not equally

In the earliest account (2nd century AD, *Explanations of Social Customs*), Nü Kua worked alone to make humans by kneading yellow earth. She worked feverishly but couldn't keep up, so she took a different approach:

> People say that when Heaven and earth opened and unfolded, humankind did not yet exist. Nü Kua kneaded yellow earth and fashioned human beings. Though she worked feverishly, she did not have enough strength to finish her task, so she drew her cord in a furrow through the mud and lifted it out to make human beings. That is why rich aristocrats are the human beings made from yellow earth, while ordinary poor commoners are the human beings made from the cord's furrow. (*Feng su t'ung-yi*)

Social stratification is literally baked into creation. The rich were individually hand-crafted; the poor were mass-produced by dragging a rope through mud.

---

## She mended the broken sky

This is probably Nü Kua's greatest myth. The four poles holding up the sky collapsed. Fires raged. Floods rose. Savage beasts devoured people. Nü Kua intervened:

- Smelted five-color stones to mend the blue sky
- Severed the feet of a giant sea turtle to use as new corner pillars
- Killed a black dragon to save the region of Chi
- Piled up reed ashes to dam the floodwaters

> The blue sky was mended. The four poles were set right. The surging waters dried up. The region of Chi was under control. Fierce beasts died and the people of Chuan lived. (*Huai-nan Tzu*)

This is why Chinese rivers flow east — in another version, the marplot Kung Kung caused the catastrophe by smashing a pillar and tilting the whole sky. The tilt was never fully corrected.

---

## The brother-sister origin myth (late version)

A Tang-era account (7th–9th century AD) tells a very different story. Nü Kua and her older brother Fu Hsi were the only two people in the world, living on Mount K'un-lun. They wanted to marry but were ashamed. So they went to the mountaintop and prayed:

> "Oh Heaven, if Thou wouldst send us two forth as man and wife, then make all the misty vapor gather. If not, then make all the misty vapor disperse."

The mist gathered. They married. They made a fan from plaited grass to hide their faces — which is why, the text says, men and women still hold fans when they marry.

Birrell notes that this is a late, demythologized version: the incest, once divinely sanctioned as cosmological necessity, has become something to be ashamed of. Nü Kua has been demoted from primeval creatrix to mortal woman.

---

## What Confucianism did to her

The trajectory of the Nü Kua myth shows the Confucian rationalization project in miniature:

**Early texts:** Independent primeval goddess who makes humans, mends the sky, rules alone.  
**Han era:** Paired with Fu Hsi as his consort; both depicted with serpentine lower bodies entwined; she becomes the exemplar of the institution of marriage.  
**Tang era:** A mortal woman, married to her brother, "subservient to God in Heaven," ashamed of the incest that earlier was simply what happened at the beginning of the world.

The measuring rope and compass she carries in Han iconography — her most distinctive emblem — are attributes of creation and construction. In the earliest iconography she may have carried them alone. By the Han, Fu Hsi carries the carpenter's square and Nü Kua carries the compass: the tools of creation divided between husband and wife.
