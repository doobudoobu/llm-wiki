# Birrell, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* — Chapters 4–5

**Chapters:** 4 ("Destroyers"), 5 ("Miraculous Birth")

---

## The punchline up front

Chapter 4 is the dark twin of everything that came before. The same gods who gave humanity fire, grain, and music also bring war, drought, plague, and dynastic ruin. The chapter's most unsettling insight: many "destroyers" start as heroes. Yi the Archer saves the world from ten suns and then shoots the river god and steals his wife. King Chou of the Shang starts as "attractive, intelligent, heroic, and charismatic" and slides into sadism. The fall is part of the myth — Chinese mythology is allergic to unambiguous goodness (except Yü the Great, who the tradition explicitly exempts).

Chapter 5 reveals that virtually every dynasty-founding figure in the tradition was born by miracle. What's striking is the consistency: a woman accidentally encounters something divine (a footprint, a bird egg, a worm), a virgin birth results, the child is abandoned by a parent who thinks it's bad luck, it survives through supernatural protection, and it grows up to found a people. This is the hero-birth pattern applied to Chinese history — and it was used to legitimize every major early state.

## Chapter 4 — Destroyers

**Crimes of the Gods / Drum and Ch'in P'i:** Two lesser gods murder the sky god Pao Chiang and are ritually executed on a mountaintop. Their evil lives on: Ch'in P'i becomes a giant osprey — wherever it appears, there is war. Drum becomes a k'un-bird — wherever it appears, severe drought. The myth explains natural disasters as the afterlives of punished criminals.

**Kung Kung and Pu-chou Mountain:** The titan [[figure-kung-kung]] fights Chuan Hsu for the godhead. In his fury he charges into Pu-chou Mountain — one of the eight pillars holding up the sky. The pillar breaks. Heaven tilts northwest, which is why the sun, moon, and stars all move in that direction. Earth gaps in the southeast, which is why all rivers flow there. One act of cosmic rage becomes the permanent shape of the world.

**The Separation of Heaven and Earth:** Three texts give three versions of how ordinary people lost the ability to ascend to Heaven. In the earliest version, the god Chuan Hsu simply orders his officers Ch'ung and Li to push sky and earth apart. In later political reworkings, the separation is presented as a punishment for the Miao people's injustice — a myth of paradise lost through moral failure. Classic dynastic-cycle logic in mythological form.

**Hun Tun — the Apertureless God:** The god of the center has no face. Two kindly gods bore him seven openings (eyes, ears, mouth, nose). On the seventh day, Hun Tun died. Chuang Tzu uses this as a parable against meddling interventionist politics. But earlier texts show Hun Tun also appears as one of the "Four Evil Ones" exiled by Shun, and his name (混沌) later became the Taoist word for primordial chaos. Same figure, three completely different meanings across four centuries of texts.

**Yi the Archer's Dark Side:** See [[figure-yi-archer]]. The short version: Yi shoots the Lord of the River (Lo River) in the left eye — an unprovoked attack — and steals his wife Fu-fei (also known as Lo-pin). Heaven doesn't punish him because the river god had transformed into a white dragon to play on the riverbank, so "of course he was going to be shot by someone." Then Yi's student Feng Meng, realizing he'll never be the best archer while Yi lives, beats his teacher to death with a club of peach wood. Hence: demons are terrified of peach wood.

**The Three Trials of Shun:** Shun's evil father and half-brother try to murder him twice — first by burning him in a granary, second by burying him alive in a well. He survives both using magical bird- and dragon-patterned coats his wives gave him. Then his father gets him drunk hoping to finish the job; Shun had been pre-treated with a bath lotion and drank all day without effect. His sister finally convinces the family to make peace. The Chinese virtue-ideal put through three assassination attempts and it still comes out smiling.

**King Kai and the First Cattle Raid:** King Kai (or Hai), seventh Shang king descended from the mythical Shang founder Hsieh, travels to a neighboring tribe as a diplomatic guest. He inflames the host's wife, they have an affair, she gets pregnant. The neighboring king kills Kai and steals his herds. His successor avenges him. Birrell reads this as the Chinese version of the universal "first cattle raid" myth — the pastoral culture origin story. The Shang were once herdsmen.

**King Chieh of the Hsia:** The archetypal last-bad-ruler. He gathered 30,000 female singers for day-long concerts, carved his lovers' names in jade, made men freeze to death and women drown for his amusement, and released tigers into crowded markets to watch people run. His castoff favorite Mo Hsi defected to King T'ang's side. Yi Yin — a figure born from a mulberry tree, now acting as a double agent — posed as a Hsia ally while staying loyal to T'ang. Two suns appearing simultaneously in the sky presaged the Hsia's fall. "When that sun dies, you and I, we'll all perish!" — Chieh's blasphemy condemned him. See [[dynasty-xia]].

**King Chou of the Shang:** Ssu-ma Ch'ien's portrait of the last Shang king is surprisingly sympathetic — he starts extraordinary: "a man with remarkable qualities, most discerning, forceful and quick-witted... his natural strength exceeded that of other men." He falls through weakness: too fond of women, too fond of praise, unable to accept criticism. He filled the palace with naked parading men and women, threw drunken all-night parties, and had the musician compose "very effete" new dances. King Wen of Chou was imprisoned by him, ransomed himself with two beautiful girls and a white horse with a red mane. When Chou saw them: "How lovely they are!" — and immediately freed Wen. The man who conquered him built his undoing out of his own appetites.

## Chapter 5 — Miraculous Birth

The chapter catalogs birth miracles. The pattern repeats with striking consistency:

| Figure | Miracle | Founded |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Hou Chi | Mother stepped on God's giant footprint | Chou people |
| Ch'i / Hsieh | Mother swallowed a swallow's egg | Shang people |
| Po Yi (ancestor of Ch'in) | Grandmother swallowed a black bird's egg | Ch'in people |
| Yü | Born from Kun's corpse, split from his belly | Xia dynasty |
| Ch'i (Yü's son) | Born from stone womb of mother who watched Yü as a bear | First Hsia heir |
| King Yen of Hsu | Hatched from an egg found by a dog | Kingdom of Hsu |
| Yi Yin | Born from a hollow mulberry tree (mother warned by spirit to flee flood, turned to wood) | Minister to T'ang |

The three great dynasty-founding births are all ornithomorphous hierogamy (women impregnated by birds or their eggs) — which suggests this was an early Bronze Age mythic formula for legitimizing royal descent from the heavens.

**The P'an Hu dog myth** stands apart: a god's old wife develops an earache, a doctor removes a knob-worm from her ear, it becomes a five-color dog called P'an Hu (Plate-Gourd). The king promises his daughter's hand to whoever defeats an enemy commander. P'an Hu delivers the head. The king's youngest daughter argues her father must honor his word. P'an Hu takes her up South Mountain and the two found the Miao and Yao people of south China. Birrell notes the myth covertly encodes two taboos (bestiality and sibling incest among the P'an Hu offspring) as an ancestral myth of southern minority peoples.

**Yi Yin's mulberry tree birth** is the strangest and most haunting: his mother was warned by a spirit in a dream — "when your mortar bowl leaks water, flee east, don't look back." Next day the mortar leaked. She fled. She looked back. The city had drowned. Her body transformed into a hollow mulberry tree. A slave woman found a baby inside it. That baby grew up to be the greatest minister in Chinese history, the spy who brought down the Hsia, the man who delighted King T'ang with perfectly cooked food. See [[figure-yi-yin]].
