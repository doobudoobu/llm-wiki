# Lady Hao (Fu Hao)

**Lived:** c. 1250–1200 BC  
**Dynasty:** [[dynasty-shang]]  
**Role:** Senior consort, military commander, ritual officiant of King Wu Ding (King 21 of Shang, at [[dynasty-shang|Anyang]])

---

## Who she was

Lady Hao (*Fu Hao* 妇好) was the most politically powerful woman of the entire Anyang period. She was a senior wife (possibly the principal wife) of King Wu Ding, the longest-reigning and most powerful king of the late Shang. Her name appears in the oracle-bone inscriptions more than any other individual woman in the corpus — and in contexts that are striking even by modern standards.

The oracle bones document her:
- **Leading armies**: King Wu Ding divined about campaigns and repeatedly put Lady Hao in command; on one occasion she commanded an army of **more than 10,000 soldiers** against the Qiang people to the west of Shang
- **Military recruitment**: inscriptions record her mission to recruit soldiers from a locality away from Anyang
- **Ritual duties**: she officiated at sacrifices and divinations, serving functions normally reserved for the king
- **Her pregnancies**: Wu Ding divined anxiously about whether her children would be male — "she gave birth and it was not good; it was a girl"

She was, as Li Feng puts it, "doubtless the most accomplished royal woman in the entire Anyang period."

## Her tomb

In 1975, archaeologists at Anyang made the discovery that partially offset the disappointment of finding the royal tombs at Xibeigang almost entirely looted. Lady Hao's tomb (tomb no. 5) was found approximately **150 m west of Cluster C** in the western zone of Anyang — close to the palace zone but not in the royal cemetery at Xibeigang. Its position near the royal palaces suggests Wu Ding wanted her spirit close.

What kept the tomb intact: a building foundation was constructed directly above the pit at some point after her burial. This foundation — probably a temple built by her son — inadvertently foiled the robbers who looted every other significant Shang tomb.

### Contents

| Category | Count |
|----------|-------|
| Bronze vessels | 210 |
| Bronze weapons and tools | 258 |
| Bronze vessels + weapons total | 468 |
| Jade objects | 755 |
| Bone artifacts | ~564 |
| Small bronze mirrors, bells, etc. | misc. |
| Human skeletons | 16 |

Total artifacts: nearly 2,000.

This is the richest tomb ever excavated at Anyang. The bronzes represent the highest level of bronze manufacture during the late Shang — technically and aesthetically superior to any comparable collection. Some pieces are inscribed with "Mother Xin" (*mu xin* 母辛) — the posthumous name given to Lady Hao by her son, who cast memorial bronzes for her. Others were probably used during her own lifetime in rituals.

The jade collection (755 pieces) is the largest jade assemblage ever found from the Shang period.

The 16 human skeletons were sacrificed retainers, almost certainly killed specifically for her burial.

## The Mother Wu cauldron

One of the bronzes from Lady Hao's son's ritual production was almost certainly the **Mother Wu** (*hou mu wu* 后母戊) square *ding*-cauldron — though it was not found in her tomb but elsewhere at Anyang (discovered 1939). At **1.33 m high and 875 kg**, it is the largest bronze vessel ever found in the world.

To cast a single piece of this scale required more than **1,000 artisans working simultaneously** in a well-organized production line. The furnaces in the royal workshop at Anyang were relatively small — achieving this output required choreographing dozens of simultaneous pours. It stands as the supreme demonstration of Shang industrial-ritual power.

## Why Lady Hao matters

Most women in ancient Chinese history are visible only through their husbands. Lady Hao appears in the oracle bones under her own name, commanding armies, performing rituals, and being consulted by divination as an independent actor. She is exceptional — but her existence proves the exception was possible.

Her unlooted tomb also serves a crucial evidential function. The royal Xibeigang tombs are mostly empty craters. Lady Hao's "modest" (by Shang standards) tomb contained 468 bronzes and 755 jades. The implication for what the royal tombs once held is staggering.
