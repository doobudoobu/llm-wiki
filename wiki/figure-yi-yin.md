# Yi Yin (伊尹)

**Role:** Greatest minister of the early Shang; the spy who brought down the Hsia; legendary chef  
**Served:** T'ang the Conqueror, first king of the Shang  
**Birth:** From a hollow mulberry tree, after his mother flooded  
**Name:** Yi = from Yi River; Yin = chief

Yi Yin is one of those figures who appears in almost every major Chinese classical text — *Annals of Master Lü*, *Mo Tzu*, *Mencius*, *Hsun Tzu*, *Questions of Heaven* — and is universally praised. Confucius-caliber hero. Mencius elevated him to the model of the good minister who puts virtue above loyalty: Yi Yin famously exiled T'ang's unworthy son rather than serve him. But the mythology that explains *how* he got there is extraordinary.

---

## He was born from a tree

His mother lived near Yi River. A spirit came to her in a dream: *"When your mortar bowl leaks water, flee east. Don't look back."*

The next morning, the mortar was leaking. She warned her neighbors, then ran ten leagues east.

She looked back.

The city was nothing but water. Her body transformed into a hollow mulberry tree.

Later, a slave woman picking mulberry leaves found a baby inside the tree. She brought the baby to her lord, who had the cook raise him. They named him Yi Yin — born of the Yi River's mulberry tree.

> The text contains many motifs with worldwide parallels: Moses in the bulrushes; Lot's wife turned to salt; the divine prohibition not to look back; flood as punishment; dead wood preserving new life. — Birrell

Mulberry trees carried mythic weight in early China — they were world-trees, associated with the rising sun, the place where the ten suns roosted before their daily journey. Yi Yin's birth site was not arbitrary.

---

## He was a double agent

By the time T'ang the Conqueror was preparing to overthrow King Chieh, the last tyrant of the Hsia, Yi Yin was already in play. The sources credit him with infiltrating the Hsia court, gaining the confidence of King Chieh, and feeding information back to T'ang — while simultaneously sabotaging Chieh's government.

This isn't how Mencius tells it (Mencius has Yi Yin as a farmer who was found by T'ang). But the *Bamboo Annals* and other sources make clear: Yi Yin "posed as a Hsia ally while remaining loyal to T'ang." The castoff royal favorite Mo Hsi also worked with Yi Yin to destroy the Hsia from within.

The fall of the Hsia was not just military conquest. It was a covert operation.

---

## He delighted the king with food

When T'ang first acquired Yi Yin's services, the ceremony was elaborate: Yi Yin was incensed with *huan-wei* grass, smeared with ox and pig blood, and formally "cleansed of evil" before being presented at court. (He was coming from another household, and the transfer required ritual purification.)

Then he cooked for T'ang. The king was delighted — tasty dishes to perfection.

This cook-minister connection points to something deeper than biography. The earliest Chinese ritual vessels (*ting* cauldrons) were cooking pots used to prepare food for the gods. Yi Yin's role as cook may reflect his original function as a *ritual* specialist — someone who prepared food for ancestor sacrifice, a position requiring specialist knowledge and close proximity to divine power. The humble cook was the king's interface with Heaven.

---

## The later legend

In post-Han texts, Yi Yin continues to grow. He was eventually credited with:
- Traveling to visit the Queen Mother of the West
- Transmitting the cooking arts as a kind of cosmological system (the balance of ingredients as a metaphor for good governance — this speech appears in *Annals of Master Lü* as a treatise on statecraft in culinary form)
- Exiling T'ang's son King T'ai Chia and then restoring him after three years when he had reformed

In Mencius, this exile-and-restoration story makes Yi Yin the archetype of the minister whose loyalty is to virtue, not to the ruler's person. He is the *zhong* (loyal) official taken to its logical extreme: when the ruler fails virtue, the minister corrects him even at the cost of political crisis.
