# Neo-Confucianism

The dominant intellectual framework of East Asia for seven hundred years. Not just Chinese — Neo-Confucianism shaped the thought of Korea, Japan, and Vietnam as completely as it did China. The system that the Song philosopher **Zhu Xi** (1130–1200) synthesized from existing Confucian commentary became the official state orthodoxy from the Yuan dynasty onward, and the basis of examination questions until 1905.

---

## What changed from classical Confucianism

Classical Confucianism (*rujia*) — the tradition deriving from Confucius and Mencius — was primarily concerned with ethics, ritual, governance, and social relationships. It said relatively little about cosmology, metaphysics, or the structure of reality. By the Tang and Song, it faced intellectual competition from Buddhism (which had a sophisticated metaphysics and psychology) and Daoism (which had cosmological depth). Confucian scholars felt the need to answer at the same level.

The **Song masters** — particularly Zhou Dunyi, Zhang Zai, Cheng Yi, and Cheng Hao (the two Chengs) — developed a Confucian metaphysics in the 11th century. Zhu Xi synthesized their work.

---

## Zhu Xi's system

Zhu Xi's key move was to argue that every thing in the universe has two aspects:

- ***Li*** (principle, pattern): the ideal form or essence of a thing — what it is and what it should be
- ***Qi*** (vital force, material energy): the physical substance through which *li* is realized

The *li* of a ruler is benevolence; the *li* of a son is filial piety; the *li* of a thing is its essential nature. The goal of moral cultivation is to *gewu* (investigate things) until you perceive their *li* clearly — until your own moral nature is transparent to you and you act without distortion.

This was a demanding program. Zhu Xi didn't think it happened through a sudden enlightenment (unlike Chan Buddhism); it required sustained, effortful study — reading the classics, examining historical cases, discussing with teachers and friends, correcting one's conduct daily.

## The Four Books

Zhu Xi elevated four texts to a new canonical status above the older Five Classics:

| Text | Attributed to |
|------|--------------|
| *Analects* (*Lunyu*) | Confucius's conversations, compiled by disciples |
| *Mencius* (*Mengzi*) | Mencius's arguments (human nature is good) |
| *Great Learning* (*Daxue*) | Brief text on self-cultivation and governance |
| *Doctrine of the Mean* (*Zhongyong*) | Brief text on moral equilibrium |

He wrote commentaries on all four. From the Yuan dynasty onward, examination candidates were tested primarily on these four texts with Zhu Xi's commentaries as the orthodox interpretation. To pass the exams — to have any official career — you had to have mastered Zhu Xi.

---

## Political and social conservatism

Zhu Xi's system reinforced existing hierarchies. The *li* of social relationships (ruler-minister, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend) defined proper behavior; cultivating virtue meant acting according to these roles. Women's proper *li* was domestic, subordinate, and retiring.

Neo-Confucianism did not invent foot-binding or the subordination of women, but its intellectual prestige gave social conservatism a systematic philosophical foundation. The elevation of *li* as the principle of things also meant that departures from established pattern were, by definition, wrong.

---

## The Wang Yangming alternative

In the Ming dynasty, the philosopher **Wang Yangming** (1472–1529) challenged Zhu Xi's system. For Wang, you don't need to investigate external things to find *li* — the *li* is already fully present in your own mind. Moral knowledge (*liangzhi*) is innate; the task is to realize it in action. "Knowledge and action are one."

Wang Yangming's school was called **xinxue** (the learning of the mind/heart) as opposed to Zhu Xi's *lixue* (the learning of principle). It was more intuitive, more activist, and potentially more egalitarian — if moral knowledge is already in everyone, the distinction between scholar and laborer is less absolute.

Both schools remained contested throughout the Ming and Qing; Japan, especially, produced brilliant thinkers in both traditions.

---

## Legacy

Neo-Confucianism was the common intellectual currency of East Asian civilization from roughly 1300 to roughly 1900. Its effects:

- Examination orthodoxy: shaped what every literate Chinese, Korean, Japanese, or Vietnamese official had read and how they reasoned
- Social structure: provided philosophical justification for hierarchy, filial piety, and gender subordination
- Historical consciousness: Zhu Xi's *Comprehensive Mirror in Aid of Governance* (the annotated history *Zizhi Tongjian* continuation) established a framework for reading Chinese history that remained standard for centuries
- Intellectual dialogue: Neo-Confucian debates within and between East Asian countries created one of the richest philosophical conversations in premodern world history

Its decline came with the abolition of the examination system in 1905 and the May Fourth Movement's (1919) rejection of Confucian tradition as an obstacle to modernization. The question of what to do with it has never been fully resolved.
