# Wu Zetian (624–705 CE)

The only woman in Chinese history to rule as emperor in her own name. She governed effectively for fifty years, built the Tang empire to its greatest extent, and has been slandered by historians ever since.

---

## Origins

Wu Zhao — her given name — was brought into Tang Taizong's household at age thirteen as a low-ranking concubine, probably as an honor to her recently deceased father (a Tang aristocrat). According to the histories, she was "beautiful and enticing." Whether Taizong was enticed is unclear. His son, the future Tang Gaozong, certainly was. Their affair probably began before Taizong's death and definitely flourished after it.

This was a problem: under Tang law, it counted as incest. Gaozong already had an empress. Wu Zhao had two things working in her favor — she was brilliant, and she had given Gaozong at least one son.

---

## The ascent

In 654, Wu Zhao gave birth to a daughter. The Empress Wang came to play with the baby. Wu Zhao returned to find the infant dead. She convinced Gaozong that the Empress Wang must be the murderer. Whether this story is true — she may well have suffocated the child herself — is disputed, but it worked. The Empress Wang was demoted and eventually killed. All who had supported her were purged, including most of the senior ministers inherited from Taizong's golden age.

By 655 Wu Zhao was Empress Wu Zetian, and "government proceeded from her alone." Gaozong — who may have been complicit in the whole thing, since he benefited from the removal of the old guard — gradually faded as a political force. By 664 "all the great powers of the empire devolved on the empress."

---

## Ruling the empire

The Tang Standard Histories, written by the Confucian bureaucrats she had bested, portray her reign primarily as a catalogue of atrocities, scandalous liaisons, and palace intrigues. Keay notes that "less is known of her half century of dominance than of any comparable period of the Tang" — not because she left no records, but because the historians were determined to discredit her.

What the histories concede despite themselves:
- "She was perspicacious, and rapid and sure in decision. Therefore all the brave and eminent of the epoch were glad to serve her and found opportunity to do so."
- She outfitted "smat, [stymied] rebellion, defended and extended the frontiers, and launched grand dynastic initiatives."
- She completed the conquest of Koguryo in 668 (which Tang Taizong had tried and failed twice).
- In 692, she recovered the "Four Garrisons" of western Xinjiang from the Tibetans and reinforced them with 30,000 permanent troops — a costly but effective commitment.
- She crushed the Khitan rebellion of 695 while simultaneously managing the Eastern Turk threat.
- She expanded the examination system and established the **Hanlin Academy**, which became the empire's most prestigious scholarly institution.

She governed a realm that Keay calls "the greatest power in Asia at this time."

### The feminist angle

She took her gender seriously as a political fact to be managed rather than denied. She imposed ceilings on marriage dowries, equalized mourning periods for deceased mothers and fathers, commissioned biographies of eminent women, and eventually had her daughter (the Taiping Princess) and a lady scholar (Shangguan Wan'er) as practical co-rulers — a "petticoat government" that scandalized contemporaries and intrigues historians.

---

## The Zhou dynasty (690–705)

In 690 she took the final step: she renamed the dynasty Zhou (invoking the ancient Zhou dynasty's aura of orthodoxy — the same move Wang Mang had made in the first century), renamed the capital Luoyang as her seat, and declared herself emperor. She was sixty-five.

To shore up legitimacy, she did everything: courted Confucian scholars, patronized Daoist practitioners (one of whom was apparently also her lover), and showered the Buddhist establishment with patronage — including allowing a monk-turned-cosmetics-salesman to convince her that a text prophesied the arrival of a female Maitreya (the Future Buddha) in China, and that she was it. She adopted "Peerless Maitreya" as one of her titles. When the monk's monks ran riot in Luoyang and he apparently burned down her ceremonial hall, he was found murdered days later. Liabilities had short shelf lives around Wu Zetian.

---

## The fall

In her eighties, ailing and bedridden, she became infatuated with two young half-brothers (both named Zhang). Their orgies disgusted everyone; she pardoned them repeatedly when they were brought before courts. One pardon too many, in early 705, triggered a coup. Senior ministers rallied troops, coaxed the heir apparent out of hiding, entered the palace, and beheaded the Zhangs on the spot.

They found a "disheveled and unsteady empress" barring their path. In her last political act, "she addressed her trembling son and the other plotters in terms of contempt. Then, her half century of power at an end, she returned to bed." She died later in 705.

The Tang dynasty was restored. The histories got their revenge posthumously. In 1966, during the Cultural Revolution, a Communist Party official was disgraced for republishing a biography of Tang Taizong's remonstrating minister Wei Zheng — accused of using it as "a poisonous shaft directed at Chairman Mao." Wu Zetian's more favorable modern rehabilitation had to wait for the late 20th century.

---

## How to think about her

Wu Zetian poses an impossible standard for Chinese historiography. A Confucian minister was supposed to remonstrate with rulers; she had remonstrators executed. An emperor was supposed to cultivate virtue; she cultivated power. A woman was supposed to serve the family; she dismantled the family of her husband and built her own. Every charge against her would be unremarkable if made about a male emperor. The Tang's greatest period of territorial extent happened on her watch. The dynasty was still standing when she died.

"In the words used of Tang Taizong's golden age," Keay notes, "there had been 'nothing quite like her since antiquity.'"
