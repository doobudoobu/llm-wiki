# Buddhism in China

Buddhism's adoption by China was the greatest religio-cultural transformation in pre-modern Chinese history. Beginning as a curiosity in the late Han, it became the dominant religion of Chinese civilization within three centuries, reshaping art, philosophy, politics, urban life, and the visual landscape of the entire country.

---

## The slow arrival (1st–3rd century CE)

The Buddha lived in northern India around the 5th century BCE. He does not appear in Chinese records until AD 65, when a Han prince in Jiangsu was noted as "fasting and performing sacrifices to the Buddha." At the time the Buddha seems to have been revered as a co-opted member of the Daoist pantheon rather than as the embodiment of a separate tradition.

Gradual penetration followed. Buddhist missionary communities appeared in Chang'an and Luoyang in the 2nd century. A half-century later, a man called Zhai Rong in Jiangsu appropriated provincial grain revenues to build a massive Buddhist establishment — a stupa, 3,000-monk monastery, and a gilded Buddha statue that was periodically given a ritual bath. Tens of thousands came for the food and spectacle. Zhai Rong used his Buddhist following as a power base for a brief career as a murderous warlord. "For obvious reasons, he never became the ideal prototype of the liberal donor."

The challenge of transmission was immense. Sanskrit was alphabetic, grammatically complex, highly inflected; classical Chinese was uninflected, concise, tone-dependent, and written without phonetic indication. Key concepts like *dharma*, *nirvana*, and *bodhisattva* had no Chinese equivalents. Early translators borrowed Daoist terms — *wuwei* for nirvana, *dao* for dharma — with predictable distortions. Buddhist monasticism, which required celibacy and loyalty to the sangha over family and state, violated core Confucian values. Reincarnation was offensive to a society that cherished ancestors as permanent spirits.

## The breakthrough (3rd–4th century CE)

The collapse of the Han and the chaos of the Period of Disunion created exactly the conditions Buddhism needed. Established social frameworks — Confucian hierarchy, family networks, bureaucratic career paths — were shattered. Buddhism offered consolation, community, medical care, welfare, and a metaphysical framework that made sense of suffering.

The translator **Dharmaraksha** (c. 230–307), son of a Yuezhi merchant at Dunhuang, produced Chinese versions of over 150 Buddhist texts. His biographer credits him with contributing "more than anyone else to the conversion of China to Buddhism." His near-contemporary, the monk **Dao'an**, created the first systematic taxonomy of Buddhist texts and trained several of the tradition's greatest scholars.

The missionary and miracle-worker **Fotudeng** (from Kuqa on the Silk Road) arrived at the Yellow River in 310, in the middle of the siege of Luoyang by Xiongnu forces. He secured an audience with the Xiongnu general Shi Le by filling his begging bowl with water and conjuring a bright blue lotus from it. Shi Le adopted Buddhism and became its patron; his successor Shi Hu — a genuine psychopath whose rule included having his own heir apparent, heir's consort, and their twenty-six children all killed and buried in the same coffin — continued this patronage enthusiastically. Shi Hu explained his logic clearly: "Buddha, being a foreign god, is the very one we [as outsiders] should worship." Buddhism offered legitimacy to non-Han rulers that Confucianism, rooted in Chinese ancestry and Han ritual, could not.

## Mahayana vs. Theravada

The split between Buddhism's two major schools had occurred in India in the 2nd century CE. **Theravada** (sometimes called Hinayana by Mahayanists) maintained strict monastic practice and offered a demanding path to liberation. **Mahayana** (the "Greater Vehicle") was more accessible: even laypeople could attain enlightenment, and a host of **bodhisattvas** — enlightened beings who had postponed their own final nirvana to help others — were available to hear prayers and grant assistance.

Mahayana won China almost completely. Its visual iconography (elaborate Buddha figures, bodhisattvas in Chinese form) and its devotional richness made it more adaptable to Chinese artistic and religious practice. The bodhisattva Avalokiteshvara changed sex on the way to China, becoming **Guanyin** — the compassionate female figure who became arguably China's most widely worshipped deity. The gender transition was gradual and visible: Tang-dynasty paintings from Dunhuang depict Avalokiteshvara with a **thin moustache and beard**. By Ming dynasty porcelain, Guanyin is clearly female, carries a small child, and has lost the moustache entirely. She was never depicted with bound feet — a deliberate distinction from aristocratic Chinese womanhood. She became associated with salvation and the provision of children. Amitabha became "the Buddha of the Western Paradise," offering rebirth in a beautiful heavenly realm to those who called his name with sincere faith.

## The golden age (4th–7th century CE)

By 311, when the Jin fled Luoyang, the city had 180 Buddhist establishments and nearly 4,000 monks. The sculptor **Fotudeng**'s disciples fanned out across China after the Later Zhao dynasty collapsed in 349, spreading Buddhist scholarship from Shandong to Guangdong.

The greatest translator was **Kumarajiva** (from Kuqa, d. 413), who produced definitive Chinese versions of the most important Mahayana texts. His translations combined philosophical accuracy with literary elegance; some remain canonical today.

In the north, non-Han dynasties competed to be the most lavish Buddhist patrons. The Northern Wei sponsored the carving of the cave temples at **Yungang** (near Datong) and **Longmen** (near Luoyang) — colossal Buddha figures carved directly from cliff faces by tens of thousands of laborers. These remain among the greatest Buddhist monuments anywhere.

In the south, the Buddhist intellectual tradition flourished in Jiankang. The Liang Emperor Wu (r. 502–549) sponsored enormous numbers of monasteries, declared himself a "Buddhist Son of Heaven," and participated in monastic rituals personally. His reign was both a cultural peak and, ultimately, a political catastrophe — when it ended in rebellion and sack, part of the damage was the enormous monastic wealth that the dynasty had accumulated.

## Xuanzang and the India connection (629–645 CE)

The monk **Xuanzang** set off for India in 629, defying an imperial travel ban, with an old horse and a crazed guide. He spent sixteen years traveling through central Asia and India, studying at the great Buddhist universities, learning Sanskrit, and accumulating five hundred trunks of texts, statuary, and relics. His *Record of the Western Regions* is the primary source for 7th-century India, including the first full account of Emperor Harsha-vardhana's empire.

On his return in 645, Tang Taizong — who had previously been indifferent to Buddhism — received him warmly and asked him to write up his journey. Xuanzang spent the rest of his life translating texts and building the **Great Wild Goose Pagoda** in Chang'an (still standing in Xi'an) to house them. He produced over seventy-three works totaling more than a thousand scrolls.

His fictional counterpart — with a monkey, a pig, and a river ogre as companions, fighting demons across a fantastical landscape — is the subject of *Journey to the West* (16th century), still one of the most popular works in Chinese literature.

## The tension with the state

Buddhism's relationship with the state was never simple. Monasteries accumulated enormous wealth and land, exempting their residents and dependants from taxation and corvée. Monks and nuns were loyal to their monastic superiors rather than to the emperor. Some monasteries became effectively independent economic units.

The state struck back periodically:
- **446 CE**: Northern Wei Emperor Tai-wudi proscribed Buddhism, destroyed temples, defrocked monks. The proscription was reversed after his 452 assassination.
- **843–848 CE**: Tang Emperor Wuzong launched the most comprehensive suppression — destroying thousands of monasteries, defrocking up to 250,000 monks and nuns, melting down copper statues for currency, seizing monastic land for tax revenue. The Japanese monk Ennin, who witnessed it, notes the motivation was as much economic as religious. Buddhism never fully recovered its pre-843 institutional dominance in China.

## The lasting transformation

Buddhism's impact on Chinese civilization was total:
- **Art and architecture**: the pagoda (evolved from the Indian stupa), Buddha sculpture, cave temple painting, and the entire iconographic tradition of Chinese religious art are Buddhist
- **Philosophy**: Chan (Zen) Buddhism, Tantric Buddhism, and Pure Land Buddhism — the three major schools that flourished in Tang and Song — each offered distinct and sophisticated responses to Confucian and Daoist ideas
- **Language**: hundreds of Chinese words and concepts entered the language via Buddhist translation
- **Social welfare**: Buddhist monasteries functioned as hospitals, banks, inns, and famine-relief centers
- **Calendar and festivals**: Buddhist festivals were integrated into the Chinese ritual calendar

By the Tang, the boundary between Buddhism, Daoism, and Confucianism in ordinary practice was often indistinct. The same educated person might perform Confucian rituals at court, read Daoist texts in retirement, and pray to Guanyin in crisis. This three-way synthesis — shaped partly by Buddhism's flexibility and partly by Daoism and Confucianism's reciprocal adaptations — became a permanent feature of Chinese religious life.
