# Tao Tao Liu, *The Chinese Myths: A Guide to the Gods and Legends* — Chapters 1–2

**Publisher:** Thames & Hudson  
**Author:** Retired Professor of Modern Chinese, Oxford (Wadham College)  
**Chapters:** 1 ("A Brief History of Chinese Mythology"), 2 ("China's Literary Traditions")

---

## What this book is

A popular guide — broader and more accessible than Birrell, less technical about individual mythic variants. Where Birrell focuses on the classical era (pre-Han through early Han) and treats texts with great philological care, Tao Tao Liu covers the full sweep from classical mythology through the imperial era (ending 1911), including Buddhist, Daoist, and Ming popular religion. The book is illustrated and designed for a general reader.

Key differences from Birrell's *Chinese Mythology*:
- Covers Buddhist and Daoist mythology as full chapters, not footnotes
- Covers the Ming and Qing deification of historical figures (Guan Yu, Guanyin's gender transformation)
- More coverage of living mythology — festivals, temples, annual cycles still practiced today
- Less coverage of mythic variants and structural analysis

## Chapter 1 — A Brief History of Chinese Mythology

### "Shenhua": a borrowed concept

The Chinese word for mythology — *shenhua* (神話), literally "words/talk on the subject of gods/deities" — was **not a native Chinese concept**. It was introduced at the beginning of the 20th century from Europe, via Japan, along with other humanities vocabulary. China had no prior generic term for "myths" in the Western sense.

Tao Tao Liu's working definition: *shenhua* refers mainly to the myths of the classical era (roughly 6th century BCE to mid-3rd century CE), with the imperial era (221 BCE–1911 CE) covered separately.

### The key inversion: historicizing instead of mythologizing

The Greeks mythologized their history — real places and people became gods and legends. The Chinese did the reverse: **they historicized their myths**. Confucianist scholars took mythological figures (Yellow Emperor, Yao, Shun) and wrote them up as historical emperors, complete with genealogies, ministerial appointments, and administrative achievements. The Greek term *euhemerization* (treating myths as imperfectly remembered history) applies, but in China the impulse is inverted.

Sima Qian is the exemplary case. He opened the *Shiji* with the Five August Emperors treated as real historical rulers — and frankly admitted his editorial method: *"Wherever I went, all the village elders would point out sites of Huang Di, Yao and Shun. The traditions were certainly very different from each other…. I edited and selected those words which were the most appropriate."*

### Living mythology

The mythology was never purely textual or academic — it was embedded in temple life, festival cycles, and popular worship. Tao Tao Liu's figures: between 1400 and 1900, Beijing alone had approximately **2,500 temples**, mostly Buddhist but also Daoist, Confucian, and imperial cult, all actively used. In the small town of Shaoxing today there are thirteen Buddhist temples, one Daoist temple, a local shrine to a woman who drowned in 151 CE saving her father's life, and the tomb of Yü the Great — which Qin Shi Huangdi visited in 210 BCE and erected a (now lost) stele to commemorate.

The **Cultural Revolution (1966–76)** tried to destroy all of this: temples wrecked as "old superstitions," myths forbidden. After Mao's death (1976) it revived. Now temples are both genuine pilgrimage sites and tourist attractions — "as long as they buy incense and bow low, no one bothers to question their beliefs — they bring in good money."

Mythology's persistence into the present: China's first Mars rover is named **Zhurong** (the God of Fire); the lunar exploration program is named **Chang'e** after the moon goddess. The moon missions Chang'e 1 through Chang'e 5 (the last launched 2020) carry the name of a woman who stole immortality and has been living alone on the moon for three thousand years.

### Guanyin: gender transition from Tang to Ming

The compassionate Bodhisattva Guanyin (full name: "the one who sees and heeds the cries of the world") was an Indian Buddhist icon, Avalokiteshvara, who underwent a sex change in China. Tang-dynasty paintings from Dunhuang depict him with **a thin moustache and beard** — clearly male. By Ming dynasty porcelain, she carries a small child and is recognizably female. She was never depicted with bound feet (a mark of aristocratic femininity from the 12th century onward). She became associated with salvation and the provision of children.

Guanyin is arguably the most universally worshipped deity in Chinese history. See [[concept-buddhism-china]].

### Guan Yu: from general to God of War

The historical general **Guan Yu** (162–220 CE) fought in the Three Kingdoms conflicts, was famous for martial skill and loyalty, and was mythologized in the *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*. The Ming Wanli Emperor formally declared him the God of War in **1594 CE**. His fierce red-faced image was placed in temples as protection against evil spirits.

Tao Tao Liu uses him as the exemplar of "the Chinese habit of deifying real people" — history feeding mythology rather than the other way around. See [[dynasty-three-kingdoms]].

### The annual festival cycle

Key annual points still observed:
- **Lunar New Year / Spring Festival**: third day — worship Caishen, the God of Wealth (rode a black tiger helping Zhou defeat the Yin)
- **Lantern Festival (Yuanxiao)**: brightly coloured lanterns carried through the dark streets
- **Qingming (Tomb-Sweeping Day)**: early third month; clean and weed ancestral graves, pour wine, lay food, picnic in the graveyard; began in the Song dynasty
- **Dragon Boat Festival (Duanwu)**: fifth day of fifth month; racing boats to summon rain-bringing sky dragons; also commemorates Qu Yuan's drowning
- **Zhong Kui festival** (fifth month): the demon-slayer who destroys centipedes, scorpions, toads, snakes, lizards; his image posted on doors
- **Festival of the Cowherd and Weaver Girl** (seventh of seventh month): Altair and Vega closest to Milky Way; girls float needles in water to read their embroidery fortune
- **Festival of Hungry Ghosts** (fifteenth of seventh month): paper boats with lanterns launched onto rivers to save souls of the dead
- **Mid-Autumn Festival** (fifteenth of eighth month): mooncakes; debt-settling; family gathering; nearly as important as Lunar New Year
- **Double Ninth** (ninth of ninth month): visit ancestral graves, climb hills, chrysanthemum wine
- **Lunar New Year preparations** (twelfth month): houses cleaned; demon-frightening gods pasted on front doors; the **Stove God's** image in the kitchen smeared with honey (either so he makes a sweet report on the household to heaven, or to glue his mouth shut so he can't say anything bad)

### Daoism: the five directions and five elements

Daoism is the native Chinese religion — hard to define, based on the natural world, the numinous, the things before humans have altered them. Its core structures:

**Yin and Yang**: opposing forces in everything. Yang = masculine, light, heat, active, positive, south and east. Yin = feminine, dark, cold, receptive, north and west. Neither exists without the other.

**The Five Directions** (north, south, east, west, center) + **Five Elements** (wood, fire, metal, water, earth) govern all fundamentals of life. The emperor "reigns facing south" to receive warmth; subjects face north in his presence; the dead are interred facing north (toward cold, disease, and winter).

**The Five Gods of the Five Directions** (from the *Huainanzi*):

| Direction | Element | God | Assistant |
|-----------|---------|-----|-----------|
| East | Wood | Taihao | Goumang — compasses, governs spring |
| South | Fire | Flame Emperor (Zhurong) | Zhuming — steelyard beams, governs summer |
| Centre | Earth | Yellow Emperor (Huang Di) | Houtu — ropes, governs four directions |
| West | Metal | Shaohao | Rushou — carpenter's square, governs autumn |
| North | Water | Zhuanxu | Xuanming — steelyard weights, governs winter |

Confucianist scholars later historicized these five gods into the **Five August Emperors** — "real" historical rulers. The two versions (Huainanzi and Sima Qian) give different sets; Huang Di and Zhuanxu always appear in both.

### The Three Pure Ones of Daoism

The highest Daoist deities:
1. **Yuanshi Tianzun** ("the Original One, Respected of Heaven") — the Jade Pure One; supposedly the instructor to Laozi
2. **Taishang Daojun** ("the Lord of the Dao") — the Upper Pure One; knows all that is known about the Dao
3. **Laozi / Taishang Laojun** ("the Great Old Lord") — the Extreme Pure One

Each rules a heaven and manifests a form of *qi* (celestial energy). Laozi appears in *Journey to the West* as a member of the Jade Emperor's court, imprisoning the Monkey King — though without actually harming him.

### The Epic of Darkness

In 1982, an old farmer in Shennongjia, Hubei Province, gave a writer named Hu Chongjun a rare manuscript recording the oral tradition of the region. Over the following years Chongjun collected multiple further manuscripts, each slightly different. Together they form an edition of the Tang-dynasty **Hei An Zhuan** ("Epic of Darkness"). Because of its length, this tale was seldom performed except at funerals — hence the name. It tells the story of the founding of the world, along with other early myths. A dynasty-era oral epic, still alive in manuscript form in 1982, in a mountain village.

## Chapter 2 — China's Literary Traditions

### The key sources, briefly

**The *Shan Hai Jing* ("Classic of Mountains and Seas")** is best understood as captions — the text that survives is what was written on a set of geographical maps of pre-Qin China. The maps themselves are lost. This explains why the *Classic* reads the way it does: laconic, descriptive, cataloguing the monsters and gods of each region as if annotating a picture. Multiple authors, possibly compiled from the Warring States period (476–221 BCE) to the Han. Its date is genuinely uncertain.

**The *Tian Wen* ("Heavenly Questions")** from the *Chu Ci* ("Songs of Chu") — attributed to the Chu poet Qu Yuan (c. 340–278 BCE), though probably pre-dates him. ~160 questions without answers, because the answers were presumably common knowledge. Written on bamboo strips: the ties binding the strips together may have perished, leaving them out of order. Much of the esoteric content is permanently inaccessible.

**The *Huainanzi*** — compiled at the court of **Liu An**, ruler of the Huainan Kingdom, a Daoist aristocrat who "allegedly gathered one thousand scholars to collect these tales." Presented to his nephew, Emperor Wu of Han, in the 2nd century BCE. Contains some of the best-known classical myths in their fullest form.

**The *Soushen Ji* ("In Search of the Supernatural")** by Gan Bao (c. 350 CE) — the main Jin dynasty source for myths drawn from oral tradition. Lost since about the 14th century; known only through later quotations.

### The scribal class and oral tradition

The *shi* class ("knights" or "gentlemen") — the lesser aristocracy of the Warring States period — gradually shifted from military to educational and administrative roles, becoming the scribal class. Key insight from Tao Tao Liu: "it became the order of the day to convince the ruler of what they said, rather than what their opponents said; a practical rather than a philosophical solution, and the Chinese have been very practical people all along."

Because most of the population couldn't read or write, the tales of gods, ghosts, and spirits continued in the oral tradition even as the scribal class historicized them in writing. The two streams coexisted for centuries. Festival performances — troupes of actors at rural fairs — were almost certainly the mechanism by which oral myths survived and spread; "undoubtedly the origin of many of the stories that have come down to us."

Sima Qian's admission is the best single illustration of how the written tradition worked: he didn't record what he found, he *selected* what he thought was most appropriate.
