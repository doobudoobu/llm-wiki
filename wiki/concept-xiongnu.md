# The Xiongnu

**What they were:** A nomadic confederacy based on the Mongolian steppe — the primary northern threat to the Han dynasty, and the defining "other" against which Chinese civilizational identity was sharpened
**Period of peak power:** ~209 BC (Maodun's rise) – ~91 AD (Northern Xiongnu dispersed)
**Sometimes called:** "Hun" in English, though the equation with Attila's Huns is debated

---

## The problem they posed

The Han empire was an agrarian civilization: settled, registered, taxed, built on a sedentary population that could be conscripted and counted. The Xiongnu were mobile pastoralists: tents, livestock, horses, no writing, no fixed cities. They came "like a sudden wind" and left "like a mist, but with the speed of lightning." No wall could be long enough; no army could reliably catch them; no treaty could bind people who had no cities to threaten in return.

The relationship was also economically entangled in ways that made simple hostility counterproductive. The Xiongnu needed Chinese grain, silk, and ironware — especially in bad years. The Han needed Xiongnu horses, and the frontier markets that developed were genuinely useful to both sides. The question was always whether the Xiongnu could be made dependent enough on Chinese goods to behave — or whether the dependency would run the other way, with the Han paying tribute disguised as gifts.

## Origins and rise

The Qin First Emperor's general Meng Tian had pushed the Xiongnu out of the Ordos desert (the Yellow River's great northern loop) in ~221 BC and garrisoned the region with walls and forts. But the costs were unsustainable, and when Qin collapsed, the garrison withdrew. The nomadic peoples beyond the frontier — deprived of grazing land and trade — made common cause.

**Maodun** (also spelled Modu, r. 209–174 BC) unified the Xiongnu confederacy after killing his own father to claim the title of *shanyu* 单于 (supreme king). He trained his horsemen by ordering them to shoot at whatever he aimed at — first animals, then his favorite horse, then his favorite wife. Those who hesitated were executed. Those who didn't were proven loyal. He tested the obedience on his father; the shot was taken; the conquest of surrounding peoples began.

Under Maodun, the Xiongnu swept east, west, south, and north, reclaiming all the territory Meng Tian had taken and penetrating into what are now Hebei and Shanxi. While Liu Bang and Xiang Yu were fighting their civil war, Maodun was building a steppe empire of 300,000 crossbowmen.

## The humiliation of Pingcheng (200 BC)

Han Gaozu led a personal campaign against the Xiongnu in 200 BC. The Han forces were surrounded at Pingcheng (near Datong) in winter, frost-bitten, unable to fight. The emperor himself would have been captured but for the intercession of Maodun's queen, who argued that holding him was more trouble than it was worth. Gaozu beat an ignominious retreat.

The result was the first "peace-through-kinship" (*heqin* 和亲) treaty. Its terms:
- The Xiongnu were accorded "brotherly" — equal — status
- An imperial princess was sent as bride to the *shanyu* 单于
- Annual gifts of silk, grain, and "other foodstuffs" were delivered

Effectively tribute. Face was saved by calling it gifts. The Xiongnu kept raiding anyway, on a somewhat reduced scale. With each treaty renewal, the tribute increased. By the mid-second century BC, "gold, ironware and liquor" had been added, while silk and grain components "soared to astronomical proportions."

In 192 BC Maodun wrote to the Dowager Empress Lü suggesting that, as two lonely widowed rulers of a certain age, they might find "agreeable consolation in one another's company." The dowager empress was all for war. Cooler heads prevailed, and she replied with magnificent strategic humility that she was too old and infirm to be worthy of his attention and hoped he would spare her country.

## Han Wudi's wars (129–119 BC)

After sixty years of appeasement and raiding, [[figure-han-wudi|Han Wudi]] ended the *heqin* policy. From 129 BC, systematic campaigns pushed the Xiongnu north. General Wei Qing reclaimed the Ordos in 127 BC; by 119 BC Han armies had crossed the Gobi into Outer Mongolia.

The price was enormous — some expeditions suffered 90% casualties — but the Xiongnu confederacy was gradually broken. Their livestock herds and pasturage were destroyed or seized. Without fixed cities to shelter behind, the nomads' only advantage was mobility; once the Han learned to sustain armies in the field for months, that advantage narrowed.

After Wudi's wars, the Xiongnu never again posed an existential threat to the Han. But they didn't disappear.

---

## The split and the Southern Xiongnu (51 BC onward)

Succession disputes fractured the confederacy. In 51 BC one claimant turned to the Han for recognition. His "Southern Xiongnu" became subsidized clients: 10,000 bales of silk fabric, 2.5 tonnes of raw silk, 500,000 kilos of rice, 36,000 cattle — just the first installment. By AD 91 the annual subsidy ran to 100 million cash. The Southern Xiongnu settled in large numbers within the frontier.

The Northern Xiongnu, still hostile, were eventually driven off by their former subjects the **Xianbei** — who then built their own confederacy and proved an even greater threat to the Later Han.

## The pattern and its significance

Sima Qian recognized that "Han versus Hun was just round one." In measuring *zhongguo* 中国's stability and cultural superiority against the nomadic "other," he was setting a pattern that would repeat for a thousand years: Tibetan, Khitan, Jurchen, Mongol, Manchu — each in turn threatening from the north, each eventually either repelled, absorbed, or conquering outright. The Xiongnu were the template.

What's striking about the relationship is how economically intimate it was. The frontier markets weren't a convenience; they were a structural feature of both economies. Silk flowed north; horses flowed south. The "tribute" system was partly tribute, partly trade, partly extortion — and all parties knew it. The Han called it tribute to maintain the fiction of superiority; the Xiongnu called it trade to maintain the fiction of equality. Both were right.
