# Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms (907–960 CE)

The Tang collapsed not with a bang but a slow hemorrhage. After the An Lushan rebellion (755–763), the dynasty spent 144 years managed by autonomous regional warlords — technically Tang emperors reigned, but actual power lay with military governors (*jiedushi*) who collected their own taxes, passed commands to their sons, and fought each other. When the last Tang emperor was finally deposed in 907, nothing changed except the label.

What followed was fifty-three years of the fastest dynastic churn in Chinese history: five dynasties in the north, ten kingdoms in the south, all simultaneously. Average reign of a dynasty: about ten years.

---

## The prelude: Huang Chao (874–884)

The Tang's death blow came not from a warlord but from a salt smuggler. **Huang Chao** failed the imperial examinations multiple times, took to trafficking contraband salt, and in 874 joined a peasant uprising. Within a few years he had turned it into something different: a massive, mobile army that spent a decade marching the length of China.

The march was extraordinary. Huang Chao's forces swept south through the Yangzi valley, crossed into modern Guangdong, sacked Guangzhou (killing 120,000 according to Arab merchants' accounts), then reversed north through Sichuan, crossed the Yellow River, and captured Chang'an in 881.

The sack of Chang'an marked the end of China's most cosmopolitan city. Huang Chao held it for three years before Tang loyalists, backed by Shatuo Turk mercenaries, drove him out. He retreated east, was surrounded, and in 884 — rather than surrender — had his concubines killed, then rode north alone into the dawn with only two servants. His nephew cut off his head shortly after. The exit consciously echoed Xiang Yu at Gaixia a thousand years before.

Tang formally limped along until 907, when the warlord Zhu Wen forced the last emperor's abdication.

## The five dynasties (907–960)

All five northern dynasties were founded by generals — most of them Shatuo Turk or other non-Han commanders who had served the Tang as mercenaries. The throne passed through violence.

| Dynasty | Period | Founder's origin |
|---------|--------|-----------------|
| Later Liang | 907–923 | Han Chinese (Zhu Wen, the Tang's destroyer) |
| Later Tang | 923–936 | Shatuo Turk |
| Later Jin | 936–947 | Shatuo Turk |
| Later Han | 947–951 | Shatuo Turk |
| Later Zhou | 951–960 | Shatuo Turk/mixed |

The Later Tang explicitly declared Later Liang illegitimate and expunged it from the dynastic record. Legitimacy claims and counterclaims proliferated — see [[concept-mandate-of-heaven]].

The most consequential act of the period was the worst: Later Jin's founder **Shi Jingtang** ceded sixteen northern prefectures — including modern Beijing — to the Khitan **Liao dynasty** in exchange for military backing. This strip of territory included the main passes through the northern mountain range. Without it, no subsequent Chinese state could defend the north. The Song spent its entire existence trying and failing to recover these prefectures.

## Feng Dao: the man who served everyone

The strangest career of the era belongs to **Feng Dao** (882–954), an official who served nine emperors across four of the five dynasties and also managed to serve two Liao (Khitan) emperors during the brief period when Liao occupied the north. He rose, was dismissed, rose again. He outlasted everyone.

Later historians — particularly Ouyang Xiu writing under the Song — condemned him savagely: a man without loyalty or shame who had prostituted his talents to whoever held power. Feng Dao's own answer was that he served the people, not the emperor — that continuity of administration was itself a form of service. He was not obviously wrong. During his tenures, famine relief was organized, libraries preserved, texts printed.

It was under Feng Dao's supervision in 953 that the **first complete printed edition of the Confucian classics** was produced — a project begun in 932 and completed by the Later Zhou. This was woodblock printing on a massive state scale. Combined with the private printing industry already booming in Sichuan and the south, it meant that by the Song, books were cheap and literacy was spreading fast.

## The commercial revolution and paper money

The Tang–Song transition involved something larger than political change: a shift in China's entire economic geography. Population migrated south. The Yangzi basin and the coastal southeast — previously secondary — became China's agricultural and commercial core. By the Song, 60% of the population lived south of the Yangzi where previously 60% had lived in the Yellow River basin.

Maritime trade overtook overland trade. Arab, Persian, and Southeast Asian merchants thronged the southern ports. Silk and porcelain went out; silver, spices, and horses came in. The Silk Road remained important, but the sea lanes were more reliable.

In Sichuan, copper cash was so heavy that merchants deposited it with private exchange houses and received paper receipts — *jiaozi* — instead. The government nationalized this practice in 1023 as the world's first state-backed paper currency. The Song economic revolution was underway.

## The Song founding (960)

The Later Zhou's best general was **Zhao Kuangyin**. In 960, on the eve of a campaign against the Liao, his officers draped the imperial yellow robe over him before he could object — or so the story goes. He "reluctantly" accepted the throne, founding the Song dynasty.

Then he did something no Chinese dynasty-founder had managed: he systematically dismantled the warlord system that had made the Tang's fragmentation possible. He gathered his top generals for a famous banquet ("the design at a cup of wine"), pointed out that the empire was fragile and they were powerful, and offered them a choice: accept generous pensions and retire to country estates, or remain in service and risk future conflict. They took the pensions. The military was brought under civilian control, paid well, and kept politically weak.

It worked. The Song never had a warlord problem. It also never had a particularly effective military.

## The Liao empire and the Shanyuan treaty (1005)

The **Khitan Liao dynasty** (907–1125) ruled the Manchurian and Mongolian steppe, the Sixteen Prefectures gifted by Later Jin, and a large swathe of northern China. It ran a dual-administration system: Mongol-style tribal governance for its northern nomadic subjects, Chinese-style bureaucracy for the agricultural south. Two systems, one empire — a template later dynasties would refine.

The Song and Liao fought intermittently for decades. In 1005, after a Liao incursion reached almost to the Yellow River, the two sides negotiated the **Shanyuan treaty**: Song would pay an annual tribute of 200,000 bolts of silk and 100,000 taels of silver; in return, Liao and Song would address each other as equal sovereigns rather than suzerain and vassal, and the border would be fixed.

Keay calls it "the first genuine international frontier in the modern sense" — fixed, recognized by both parties, regularly patrolled. The Song paid. The trade-off was real: the tribute was less than the cost of one military campaign, and peace enabled Song's commercial revolution to proceed uninterrupted for over a century.
