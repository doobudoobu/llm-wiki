# Birrell, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* — Chapter 12

**Chapter:** 12 ("Love")

---

## The punchline up front

Chinese love mythology is noticeably sanitized compared to every other world tradition. No Aphrodite, no Eros, no Zeus's serial assaults. Birrell's opening argument: this isn't accidental. Han-era censors trawled the classical texts, and Han social philosophy demanded love narratives that depict courteous exchanges between lovers, subordinate women, and carefully skirted physical realism. What survived is both beautiful and a little muted — except when it's not, and then it's devastating.

The chapter's six myths run a spectrum: cosmological origin myth (Nü Kua marries her brother), gender war (Lord of the Granary vs. Salt Goddess), courtly divine romance (Son of White Emperor and Huang O), stellar separation (Weaver Maid and the Sky Voyager), conjugal bereavement (Shun's wives), and the most haunting: Han P'ing and his wife, whose love outlives their bodies and turns into trees.

---

## The censorship problem

At the end of chapters 9 and 13 of the *Classic of Mountains and Seas*, two identical endnotes state that in **6 BC** officials Wang, Kung, and Hsiu "corrected" chapters 1–9 and 10–13. This happened in the first year of the reign of Han Emperor Ai, who had just abolished the entire government entertainment office as "unethical and unorthodox." The word *correct* could mean mild amendment or wholesale excision. The result: classical Chinese mythology has no goddess of love, no god of love, and even the mildest suggestion of physical sexuality is buried in indirection and euphemism.

## Nü Kua marries her brother — the first marriage

Tang-era text by Li Jung (fl. AD 846–874). Long after Nü Kua had been reduced from primeval creatrix to domestic female, this author produced the etiological myth of the institution of marriage:

Brother and sister alone on K'un-lun Mountain, no other people in the world. They want to marry but feel ashamed. They climb the mountain and pray:

> "Oh Heaven, if Thou wouldst send us two forth as man and wife, then make all the misty vapor gather. If not, then make all the misty vapor disperse."

The vapor gathers. They marry. They plait grass into a fan and hold it to screen their faces. "Even today, when a man takes a wife, they hold a fan" — that's where the wedding fan comes from.

Birrell's reading: this is neomythology — a late reworking of Nü Kua into an ordinary woman ashamed of what earlier was simply cosmic necessity. The "sociological charter" function (Malinowski): myths of divinely sanctioned sibling marriage appear in extreme survival scenarios — flood, epidemic, decimation — where traditional taboos are suspended for survival. The awkward sentence structure ("there were two people, Nü Kua and her older brother") betrays the seams of the reworking.

## Lord of the Granary and Goddess of Salt River

Covered in chapter 9 discussion as a goddesses myth; here Birrell frames it as a love myth — specifically the **battle between the sexes**. The Salt Goddess is the trickster, the lord the hero who outwits her.

Key re-emphasis here: the Salt Goddess's power over the sun (she blots it out for ten straight days) suggests a vestige of a female sun deity in the local Pa tribe tradition, parallel to Hsi-Ho. The silk girdle the lord offers her — which is actually a hunting weapon — plays on her vanity. The myth reads as a patriarchal grain culture defeating a matriarchal salt culture, with the sun clearing again as a symbol of restored male order. See [[src-birrell-ch9-11]] for the full narrative.

## Weaver Maid and Draught Ox: the romantic version

Chapter 9 gave the literary history; here Birrell presents the actual text of the love myth (as opposed to the original *Classic of Poetry* poem about futile labor). The source is *Erh ya yi*:

> "East of Sky River is Weaver Maid, the daughter of God in Heaven. Year by year she toils and slaves with loom and shuttle till she finishes weaving a celestial robe of cloudy silk. God in Heaven pitied her living alone, and allowed her to marry Draught Ox west of the river. After they married she neglected her weaving work. God in Heaven grew angry and punished her by ordering her to return to the east of the river, letting her make one crossing each year to be with Draught Ox."

Short. Stark. The romantic elaboration (magpie bridge, tears on the seventh night) comes from later accretion over centuries.

### The Sky Voyager

The most beautiful myth in the chapter. A man who lives on a small island notices that every year in the eighth month, a floating raft passes. He builds a compartment on it, stocks provisions, boards it, and leaves. For ten days he can still see the sun and moon and stars. Then it goes blurry — he can't tell if it's day or night. After another ten days he arrives somewhere with what seem like city walls and well-ordered houses. He can see weaver women in a palace. He meets a man leading an ox to drink:

> "How did you get here?" The man explained his purpose and asked where this place was. "Go back to Shu commandery and ask Yen Chün-p'ing, then you will know."

He returns. He asks the astronomer Yen Chün-p'ing of Shu. The astronomer checks his records and says:

> "On a certain day of a certain month in a certain year, a **stranger star** trespassed into the Draught Ox constellation."

He calculates: it was precisely when the man had arrived in Sky River.

The man's journey to heaven registered as an astronomical anomaly. A mortal's raft trip left a trace in the stars. This is the kind of mythological detail that makes you put the book down.

(In a later version, this story was attached to the historical figure of Zhang Qian — Han Emperor Wu's great western explorer — "to give greater glory to a narrative by investing it with the name of a famous person." See [[figure-han-wudi]].)

## Son of the White Emperor courts Huang O

A courtly divine romance, 6th-century AD text (based on 4th-century source). The setting is Ch'iung Sang (Exhausted Mulberry) — a mythical western shore. The participants:

- **Huang O** — a goddess who weaves by night and sails a wooden raft by day
- **Son of the White Emperor** — the essence of T'ai-po (the planet Venus); a child-god whose "appearance stood out from the ordinary"

He descends to the riverbank and feasts and plays music with her until "he forgot to go home." Their courtship takes place on a raft with a cassia mast, banners of scented reeds, and a jade-carved pigeon as a weathercock on the masthead (because a pigeon knows the four seasons). They play paulownia lute and catalpa zither. She sings:

> The sky is clear, the earth is wide and immense,  
> Ten thousand images turn to fading, changing into nothing.  
> We drift through skies so vast and gaze into space.  
> We ride in a light boat, companions of the sun.  
> *My heart knows such joy, yet our bliss is not yet full.*

He replies with a song about cutting catalpa for instruments. Their son is **Shao Hao** — also called Ch'iung Sang (the name of the place where they courted) and Sang Ch'iu. He "ruled by the power of metal" and presided over the east. His tomb is in Ch'ü-fu, Shandong — the birthplace of Confucius.

Note: the mulberry grove at Ch'iung Sang connects to the *Classic of Poetry*'s "Airs of Wei," which uses "sang-chung" (among the mulberries) as a euphemism for illicit trysts. Mulberry trees in the Chinese tradition are associated with both silk labor and clandestine love — the same tree that produced the silkworm myth also hosts lovers' meetings.

## Yü and the T'u-shan girl

Brief recapitulation of the myth already detailed in chapters 5 and 8. Yü transforms into a bear, accidentally drums on a stone, T'u-shan girl sees him as a bear and flees in shame, turns to stone, gives birth to Ch'i/K'ai through the stone's north flank. Birrell notes: "No love is expressed between the parents" — it's a primitive myth, rich in graphic action, empty of sentiment. A "love" myth where no love is present.

## Han P'ing and his wife

The darkest and most beautiful myth in the chapter. From Kan Pao's 4th-century AD collection:

Prince K'ang of Sung has a manservant called Han P'ing. P'ing's wife is so beautiful that the prince takes her away. The prince imprisons P'ing and forces him to do dawn labor on the city walls.

P'ing's wife sends him a coded letter: *"When the rain pours in torrents and the river rises and gets deeper, our hearts will come together at sunrise."* None of the prince's courtiers can decode it. His official Su Ho does: *"'rain pours in torrents' means to grieve and pine; 'the river rises' means unable to go out or come in; 'our hearts will come together at sunrise' means a death wish."*

P'ing kills himself.

His wife deliberately makes her clothes worn and rotten. When the prince takes her up a tower, she throws herself off. The courtiers grab her — but her rotted clothes tear away in their hands and she falls to her death. A suicide note in her belt: *"The prince would have preferred me to live, but I preferred to die. I request that my body be buried with P'ing."*

The prince refuses. He buries them facing each other but far apart, mocking: *"If you can make your burial mounds come together, I won't stand in your way."*

That night, two huge catalpa trees grow from the mounds. In ten days they are full-grown. Their trunks bend toward each other — **roots entwined below, boughs embraced above**. Two mandarin ducks perch in the trees day and night, entwining their necks and singing sadly. The people of Sung call them the "loving-you trees."

Later versions add: the rags torn from her hands as she jumped **turned into butterflies**.

Birrell notes the parallels with the long verse narrative "A Peacock Southeast Flew" — enforced separation, villainous prince (there a scheming mother-in-law), double suicide, twin trees over the graves, mandarin ducks. The structural similarities are not coincidental: they reflect a mythic theme powerful enough to generate multiple independent expressions. The story was dramatized in the Yuan dynasty; Han P'ing's song before suicide was added by a Ming author.

## What the chapter is really about

The chapter's opening and closing together suggest that Chinese love mythology is a record of what censorship and social philosophy left behind. The sanitized myths are everywhere. But what survived the sanitizing — the Sky Voyager accidentally appearing as an anomalous star, Huang O's song on the raft, the catalpa trees growing toward each other — is strange and beautiful enough that the loss of the original bawdier tradition doesn't feel like the whole story.
