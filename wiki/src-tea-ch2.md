# Benn, *Tea in China: A Religious and Cultural History* — Chapter 2

**Author:** James A. Benn (McMaster University)  
**Publisher:** University of Hawai'i Press, 2015  
**Chapter:** 2 — "The Early History of Tea: Myth and Reality" (pp. 21–41)

---

## The punchline up front

Tea is, in cultural terms, a **Tang dynasty invention**. The word *cha* (茶) didn't exist until the 8th century CE. The origin myth attributing tea to the mythical emperor Shennong was invented by Lu Yu in his *Classic of Tea* (c. 760 CE) and had almost no basis in earlier sources. Everything that came before Lu Yu was scattered, inconsistent, and often frankly weird.

This chapter is essentially a detective story: Benn reads through every claimed "early reference" to tea and shows how shaky the evidence actually is.

---

## What's most interesting

See [[concept-tea-culture]] for the full picture. The headline items:

- The word "tea" (*cha*) was invented by removing one stroke from the older character *tu* sometime in the mid-Tang; inscriptions show *tu* still being used in 779 CE, *cha* appearing by 841 CE
- The most famous early evidence for tea — a Han dynasty slave contract listing "brew tea" among a slave's duties — is probably unreliable; the oldest surviving version of the text doesn't mention tea at all
- The earliest reliable reference to tea is a courtier secretly swapping his emperor's alcohol for tea at banquets (Three Kingdoms period, ~3rd century CE) — not exactly Shennong discovering it in a mountain garden
- Lu Yu's *Classic of Tea* is partly built on anomaly literature: ghost stories, demon encounters, Taoist immortals — the pre-Tang cultural world around tea was strange and otherworldly, not the refined aesthetic experience Lu Yu was constructing
- Before the mid-Tang, tea was primarily a medicinal substance associated with south China (especially Sichuan); it became a mass cultural phenomenon only after the *Classic of Tea* and Buddhist monks spreading it as an alternative to alcohol
